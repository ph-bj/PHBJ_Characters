Precious Mirror for Grading Flowers
Chapter 13
**Two hearts secretly imprint deep feeling;
lustful demons and lecherous ghosts continue crooked entanglements.**
After Zhongqing had provoked Chunhang into anger,
he sent the hundred taels
that Wang Xun had prepared to Gao Pin,
to cover Chunhang's daily expenses of firewood and food.
Chunhang sat brooding for two days;
pressed on all sides for rice, coal,
and the repayment of debts,
with nowhere to turn for a loan,
he accepted Gao Pin's kind offer
to stay and was thus obliged to take his meals there for the time being.
One day, after eating, Gao Pin went out to pay calls,
and Chunhang went to the theater, his heart entirely fixed upon Su Huifang.
Having no money to watch the opera,
he could only stand at the entrance,
waiting to catch a glimpse of Huifang going in or coming out.
Just as the performance was about to begin,
he did indeed see Huifang arrive in a carriage
and step out at the entrance.
But just then a crowd of people pressed in to see the opera,
and in the jostling Chunhang was shoved to the rear,
making it impossible for them to come face to face.
Chunhang was deeply vexed;
he pushed his way out again,
but Huifang had already gone inside.
He could only stand there motionless,
waiting for him to come out.
While waiting he also saw many of the celebrated performers, every one the equal of Huifang.
Chunhang thought to himself
that he never would have expected the Lianjin troupe to have such fine performers,
proving their fame was no exaggeration.
He waited a full three hours before seeing Huifang emerge with his head lowered.
Two splendidly dressed beautiful young men walked ahead of him,
and two manservants carrying cushions and clothing bundles followed,
and they all boarded his carriage and were gone.
Chunhang, knowing Huifang had not seen him, returned home in a mood of dejection.
He passed the night,
and the next day went again
to wait at the theater entrance.
Yet again he failed to see him,
letting the day slip by in vain while sighing endlessly.
Chunhang had been caught in this obsession for so long
that he could not easily awaken from it,
going every day to seek out the Lianjin troupe and wait for Huifang.
For more than ten days in a row,
Huifang did notice the man who had previously fallen in the mud.
Every time he got in or out of his carriage,
that man was always standing by the theater entrance,
staring at him with unblinking, entranced devotion.
This struck Huifang as deeply strange.
He looked closely at his face
and saw that he possessed a pure and refined grace.
Although his features bore a trace of haggard weariness, his elegant bones
and poetic charm were vividly apparent.
Huifang already knew in his heart
that this person had come for his sake,
and he could not help but feel touched,
casting many a lingering, affectionate glance his way.
Chunhang, overjoyed to the point of dancing, would follow Huifang's carriage every day,
walking alongside it until they reached the gate of Huifang's lodgings in Jixiang Alley,
where he would pace back
and forth for a long while before finally departing.
One day, Chunhang's luck finally turned, perhaps by the hand of destiny.
He was following Huifang's carriage as usual when Huifang,
paying special attention, felt a stir of tender pity in his heart.
As soon as he entered his gate,
he told his manservant to invite the gentleman inside.
The manservant went out.
He looked Chunhang up
and down and asked if the gentleman was looking for their master,
adding that their master had invited him in for tea.
Chunhang was overjoyed beyond expectation,
yet he stood rooted to the spot, unable to step forward.
The manservant invited him a second time,
but Chunhang remained overwhelmingly shy and hesitant.
Suddenly someone else came out from inside
and said to invite the gentleman who had been following the carriage to come in.
Chunhang had no choice
but to straighten his clothes and follow the manservant through the main gate.
He found himself in a courtyard lined on both sides with delicate double bamboo fences.
It was late in the second month,
and a hundred flowers were in full bloom,
filling the entire courtyard with vibrant crimsons
and purples and a dazzling, fragrant beauty.
Above sat a small, three-room reception hall, furnished with ancient bronzes
and zithers and books, exquisite and elegant in every detail.
Before long, Su Huifang emerged wearing a plain-colored pearl-trimmed fur garment,
and stepped forward to offer a courteous greeting.
Chunhang immediately took his hand,
finding it as soft as fresh spring shoots, delicate and slender.
The two stood side by side, almost exactly the same height,
for Huifang was also seventeen years old.
Huifang looked at Chunhang with a smile,
remarking that they had seen each other every day,
yet he still did not know his esteemed name and native place.
He expressed his deep gratitude for Chunhang's generous forgiveness regarding the incident
in the mud the other day,
and added that to be favored
with his continuous attention was a blessing accumulated over three lifetimes.
Chunhang was deeply astonished by his elegant speech
and graceful choice of words.
He replied that ever since gazing upon his lovely face,
he had been haunted by him day and night.
He said his heart was filled with admiration,
which he could only swear to in secret.
To be invited in rather than rebuked was a sign of true understanding
and deep feeling that he, Tian Xiangzhi, would never forget until the end of his days.
He then detailed his native place and family name,
and spoke further words of yearning.
They looked at each other in silence for a long moment, communicating without words.
Huifang then invited Chunhang inside.
They left the reception hall,
passing through the western bamboo fence into a small courtyard containing a row of five rooms.
The easternmost room was set aside as a guest room for unexpected visitors.
The middle two were kept open as a small study,
and the western suite served as Huifang's bedchamber.
Chunhang first sat on the heated bed in the middle room.
He saw eight meticulously detailed paintings of 'Immortals Gathering' by Qiu Shizhou hanging on the walls.
The sides were lined with nanmu-wood frames set with glass windows,
and the floor was covered with a pure blue velvet carpet, completely spotless.
After sitting for a moment, Huifang led him into the western suite.
The space was divided by a floor-to-ceiling wooden partition carved in an 'ice-plum' pattern.
In the outer room stood two small bookshelves and a curio cabinet.
Above was a small wooden heated bed, spread with a beige, finely embroidered cushion.
On the bed-table rested a rectangular Ding-ware porcelain basin,
from which five or six pure-hearted orchids bloomed.
On the main wall hung six pieces of small regular script on gold-flecked paper,
each written by a different hand in balanced, elegant calligraphy.
The signatures included 'Master of Duxiang', 'The Recluse Jingyi', 'The Lyricist Zhujun',
'The Mountain Man Jiantan', 'The Unofficial Historian Qianzhou',
and 'The Lay Scholar Yongan'.
They appeared to be matching seven-character regulated verse poems.
Looking closer at the dedication, it read:
'By Meixiang's request, matching the original rhymes
of the six seven-character verses on the Long River Purification Ceremony.'
Chunhang's respect deepened even further.
He thought to himself:
"So he knows how to compose poetry."
He then asked to see the original poem,
noting that his knowledge of poetry must be exceedingly profound.
Huifang laughed and replied
that it was mere scribbling, just cobbling together a few colloquial phrases, not true poetry.
Chunhang asked why the original verses were not displayed.
Huifang explained that Yuan Baozhu had written them out for him the previous year,
but someone had borrowed them
to look at and lost them.
Chunhang looked at Huifang more closely,
observing that his refined bearing
and elegant speech were entirely unlike those of an ordinary performer.
He asked when he had come to the capital to learn the opera.
A look of shame passed over Huifang's face,
and with a sigh he said
that since he asked of his origins,
he would tell him
that he too came from a respectable family,
and his father had once been an official.
Chunhang stood up in apology,
saying he had indeed felt he did not look like someone from a humble background,
and asked why he had been forced
to take up this profession.
Huifang's eyes grew red, and he said:
"Please sit, and I will tell you."
Chunhang sat down, and Huifang continued.
He explained that as a child he had followed his father
to his official post in Yunnan.
His mother had died when he was eight.
When he was twelve, his father was impeached by a superior,
fell ill from the shock and anger,
and died within a month.
Having always been an upright official with empty sleeves,
they had no private savings.
What little remained was quickly divided up
and plundered by relatives and attendants,
leaving him with only one old servant.
They stayed in Yunnan for over a year.
It was pitiful to be entirely without family,
and in that world of snobbery, no one was willing to help;
they survived solely by the old servant taking on menial labor.
When they could no longer sustain themselves,
they had no choice but to head home.
On the road they endured every kind of bitter hardship,
walking for a year
and two months before finally reaching Suzhou.
There they found only overgrown weeds and ruin.
The world had changed completely,
and relatives and neighbors looked upon them with cold eyes,
refusing to lift a finger.
They could not even borrow a single branch for shelter or beg a single meal.
After staying in a temple for a few days,
they found out that a relative was working as a secretary in Zhili.
Expending the effort of nine bulls and two tigers,
they managed to board a grain transport boat and travel north.
They first went to Baoding
to seek out this relative,
only to discover he had been involved in some trouble
and had already been exiled beyond the Great Wall,
with his family's whereabouts unknown.
He asked Chunhang if he did not think this was a wretched fate.
Chunhang agreed that it was indeed a case of the mountains
and rivers ending with no path in sight,
and asked what had happened next.
Huifang said they could not stay in Baoding,
so they decided to come to the capital
to find a way to survive.
Just as they arrived outside the Front Gate,
they fortuitously met a good man,
a fellow townsman who had also been his childhood tutor, Mr. Gu.
He was a scholar.
Seeing their miserable state,
he took them to his lodgings
and asked them about everything that had happened.
He asked Chunhang if he could guess what this tutor was doing in the capital.
Chunhang replied that he naturally assumed he was teaching.
Huifang said he was not teaching,
and that his behavior was somewhat like Chunhang's own.
He was only twenty-seven this year.
Upon arriving in the capital,
he had spent every day listening to the opera until his money ran out,
but by then he had learned the operas by heart
and befriended many of the performers, eventually becoming an opera instructor.
When he failed the provincial examinations that year,
he burned all his essays
and joined the Lianjin troupe as a young male lead.
Chunhang remarked that this was the action of a truly liberated man,
completely free from conventional constraints.
Huifang continued, explaining that Mr. Gu had taken them in.
In his free time,
he taught Huifang to read and write,
and discussed poetry and lyrics with him,
allowing them to live in peace and stability.
Tragically, the old servant,
having suffered the wind
and frost of the journey and carrying deep sorrow in his heart,
fell ill upon entering the capital and died two months later.
At that point, Huifang felt even more isolated
and alone, caught in a dilemma with nowhere to turn,
and could only rely entirely on his teacher for survival.
It was not until the spring of the previous year
that his teacher earnestly urged him to learn the opera.
He had been unwilling at first,
but realizing he had no other path,
he followed his teacher's advice.
He learned a few roles,
and gradually, day by day,
he naturally grew into the profession.
His teacher loved to compose poetry,
and whenever he wrote a poem,
he would explain it
to him and teach him how to write.
Though his attempts were clumsy,
he began to enjoy it himself.
But his fate was unfortunate
and his deep debt of gratitude remained unpaid.
In the summer of the previous year, his teacher contracted a seasonal illness and passed away.
Left entirely alone, he could only look at his shadow and pity himself.
Speaking to this point,
he choked back tears.
Hearing this, Chunhang also felt a deep sorrow,
remarking that in five years the stars had shifted and things had changed,
and he had tasted so much of the ways of the world.
He offered a few words of comfort.
Then, drinking two cups of tea, Huifang asked Chunhang if,
since he enjoyed the opera so much,
he had come to admire any particular performers in the various troupes.
Chunhang laughed and confessed that he prized beauty over artistry,
paying no attention to the technical aspects of the opera
and having no understanding of how to rank performers.
He simply took those who had fine looks
and treasured them as the supreme.
He admitted that at first he was reckless, his eye untrained,
and upon meeting any seductive face he would be quite swept away.
But having now gazed upon Huifang's jade-like countenance,
he felt as though the wondrous dwelling Bodhisattva had appeared upon the lotus throne,
beyond what a creature of the lower world could ever hope to approach.
He said his past experiences were like Wang Xizhi studying calligraphy under Lady Wei,
a futile expenditure of years,
and his shame and remorse had no end.
Huifang, having heard these words, was already half won over.
He gazed at Chunhang for a good
while without speaking, then smiled again and asked:
'You say you treasure those with fine looks as supreme,
but I would like
to know, what specifically is it that you treasure?'
Chunhang stood up, and in high spirits, arms
and feet moving expressively, face glowing with animation, he said:
'Meixiang, you are a person of understanding, try to guess!'
Huifang blushed slightly and said he would not know how to guess.
Chunhang replied that it was for no other reason at all.
Huifang then asked with a serious expression what the reason was.
Chunhang declared that if the looks were fine and the temperament fine,
he would willingly die for that person.
Huifang asked why someone else being fine had anything to do with him,
and why he should die for them.
He urged him to explain what it was that deserved such treasuring.
Chunhang asked him to listen.
He explained that for those who travel thousands of miles from home,
aside from two or three intimate companions, there are four other kinds of fine friends
that are most difficult to obtain,
and even when obtained they commonly have some unsatisfying imperfection.
So while one can call them treasures, one cannot call them the supreme treasure.
Huifang expressed surprise and asked
to be enlightened about these four kinds of fine friends.
Chunhang said the first was fine weather:
the evening sun, the bright moon, gentle rain, clear breezes, light mist, or crystalline snow.
Even sitting alone, one's heart
and spirit become open and glad.
One feels the beauty of spring and autumn days,
lingering over the scenery and surroundings.
Whether in the open countryside or a pavilioned courtyard, elegant bamboo
and scattered blossoms present themselves.
Under the shade of parasol trees or willows, one recites verse and strolls slowly,
savoring the fragrant season and forgetting the mundane world.
Huifang nodded, agreeing that this was indeed fine,
and guessed that the second must be fine scenery.
Chunhang confirmed this, describing a hill, a valley, the mountains
and waters serene and secluded,
with every step revealing a changed view
and drawing one deeper in.
The third, he said, was fine books, those
that lack a single dead sentence or a single idle brushstroke,
leaving the reader endlessly exploring.
Huifang nodded again.
Chunhang said the fourth was fine poetry arising from the spirit's depths,
noting there was no need
to confine oneself to worshipping Du Fu and venerating Han Yu.
If one or two lines could express what others could not express,
one might contend with the ancients for supremacy.
Huifang praised him as a person of refined and universal understanding.
Chunhang countered that while these four friends are indeed fine, each has its imperfection.
He asked how many days of fine weather there are in a month.
Often the morning is beautiful and by afternoon it changes,
spoiling even the morning's beauty.
When one's mood flags, one begins
to dread wind and rain and dares not linger.
He asked how many places of fine scenery exist in an entire province.
Some can only be reached on foot by treacherous or obscure paths, over sand
and rocks and thorns, miles away,
leaving one exhausted, often knowing its beauty yet unwilling to make the journey.
As for books, aside from the few volumes recited in every household,
the ocean of writing is vast,
yet most of it wastes wood and paper.
Even where something new emerges from the old,
it reflects individual hands and styles,
and cannot fully satisfy every taste.
Poetry is small in scope yet difficult to perfect.
Sometimes a newly composed poem seems like refined gold,
yet upon rereading it tastes like chewing wax,
leaving one regretting having let the brush fall too easily.
These four friends are difficult to obtain,
and finding them perfectly satisfying is even more difficult,
so while one can call them treasures,
they are not the supreme treasure.
He concluded that only those
of Huifang's distinguished profession form a perfectly beautiful friend of unmatched quality,
a treasure friend beyond these four.
Huifang laughed, finding the term 'treasure friend' quite remarkable,
and confessed they had no idea they possessed anything worth treasuring.
Chunhang elaborated:
'Soft as jade, warm as perfume,
flowering with richness, gleaming like snow,
that is the treasure of beauty.
Rounded like Lady Huan, slender like Lady Yan, flesh smooth and bone fragrant,
that is the treasure of the body.
Bright eyes that glance bewitchingly, a coquettish smile and an artful pout,
that is the treasure of the face.
A thousand charms gathered
to one side, a hundred allurements born across the whole,
that is the treasure of manner.
Tender crying that sips the dew, lovely speech that chides the flowers,
that is the treasure of feeling.
Pearl hairpins carved in kingfisher hues, gold hair ornaments with flying rosy clouds,
that is the treasure of adornment.
Add to all this the pure song
and the exquisite dance, the sandalwood clappers and the golden goblets,
the trills of the voice-box and the light steps on the verge of falling,
and one calls this the treasure of artistry, the treasure of a person.'
Huifang replied that his argument was truly excellent,
though in some places it set the standard too high.
Even as Huifang said this,
he was in fact deeply moved
and grateful toward Chunhang within his heart.
He could not help casting languid sidelong glances
and allowing his powdered cheeks to soften with sweet tenderness.
He studied Chunhang carefully,
and the more he looked the more fine qualities he found.
In his eyes, all the wealthy princes
and elegant young gentlemen were quite eclipsed.
Chunhang continued:
'Tea-smoke and the sound of the qin, wind
and rain and the cock's crow at night,
thinking of one's old friends with a heart
that reaches a thousand miles.
Without a kindred spirit to share morning and evening,
where is there joy to be found?
And then in the desolate temple, loneliness
and sorrow, a fading lamp and solitude.
Where indeed can one find two or three intimate companions
to endure the bleakness together?
Only you people of refinement can offer a word or half a phrase
that sinks into the bones of the heart,
turning a hundred-tempered blade of steel into soft silk wrapped around the finger.
And then a heavenly beauty like you.
Even if you could only be seen and not approached,
I would be like the pilgrim who,
upon catching sight of the Bodhisattva, prostrates himself at every step,
and I would do that willingly.
How could I dare to harbor any other hope?'
Hearing this, Huifang could not restrain the tears
that flowed down his face.
He said he now understood Chunhang's heart
and told him to say no more.
He then asked Chunhang
to give a full account of everything that had happened since he arrived in the capital.
Chunhang recounted everything from the previous winter to the present day.
Huifang smiled again, praising him as a person of utterly devoted feeling and wild fancy,
yet admiring his two legs for running every day
and standing motionless at the theater entrance.
Chunhang declared that if it were not for Huifang,
he would not come even if invited.
Huifang smiled, went out,
and directed someone to bring in several kinds of fruit, several dishes,
and two pots of wine,
inviting Chunhang to drink a little.
Chunhang made no show of refusal.
The two sat across from each other at the small huali-wood table
and drank, each pouring out what was deepest in their hearts.
By this time Huifang's own heart was completely drawn in,
without a trace of calculating self-interest.
When the meal was finished,
they moved to the inner room
and sat for a little while.
Huifang then told the manservant
to hitch up the carriage outside and take Master Tian back to his lodgings.
Huifang clasped Chunhang's hand
and declared that they had become friends today,
and must not betray this friendship for the rest of their lives.
He swore that if he, Su Huifang, spoke a single false word,
the bright sun would be his witness.
He told Chunhang he need not come out
to look for him anymore,
promising to visit him early or late, every single day.
He urged Chunhang to take good care of himself
and make an effort toward his prospects,
pleading with him not
to lose his good name and give others occasion to talk for their sake.
Hearing this, Chunhang was so moved
that his admiration turned to reverence, the feeling penetrating to his very marrow,
and tears streamed down his face.
The two looked at each other
and choked with emotion for a while.
Only the manservants and other attendants did not understand what this was about
and found it quite puzzling.
Still talking as they went, they came outside.
Huifang saw Chunhang into the carriage
and gave him a few more parting words,
and Chunhang went directly back to his lodgings.
Huifang then went to bed, carefully recalling everything Chunhang had said
and pondering his situation for a long time.
When he fell asleep,
he dreamt of Chunhang appearing before him, dressed in magnificent robes
and an imposing crown, his expression so stern it inspired awe.
Then Chunhang transformed into a middle-aged man wearing the robes of a first-rank official.
He had a restless night of confused dreams
and woke up late the next morning.
It was already breakfast time
when he finally washed his face and ate a light meal.
His manservant came in
and announced that there were guests outside.
Huifang asked who it was,
and the manservant replied
that it was Master Zhang from Fuhu Bridge,
accompanied by Third Master Pan of the Qisheng Silver House.
Huifang had no choice
but to dress and go out to meet them.
This Master Zhang was none other than Zhang Zhongyu.
The Master Pan was Pan Qiguan, a wealthy man of the capital with a vast fortune.
He owned three silver houses, two pawnshops,
and a spice shop,
and had purchased a sixth-rank official title.
His family originally hailed from Shanxi
but had lived in the capital for two generations.
He was a petty, vulgar,
and stubbornly foolish man, incredibly stingy,
and an insatiable lecher who pursued his desires relentlessly day and night.
He had one redeeming quality:
he was entirely terrified of his wife.
His current wife, whom he had married after his first wife's death, was quite beautiful,
but she was exceptionally fierce and jealous.
Though she had little affection for Pan,
she strictly forbade him from making trouble outside.
If he failed to return home for a single night,
she would take a carriage
and lead her servants to search through all the brothels;
if she caught him,
she would create a tremendous, earth-shattering scene.
Left with no other options, Pan had recently conceived the idea of purchasing a young boy,
and had been keeping an eye out among the various theater troupes.
He had set his sights on Su Huifang.
Today he had dragged Zhang Zhongyu along
to act as a go-between.
Zhongyu thought to himself
that a person of Huifang's refined character was unlikely to agree to follow Pan Qiguan,
so he was evasive and reluctant to help.
However, after Pan pleaded with him repeatedly
and promised a heavy reward of gold and silk,
he had no choice but to come along,
intending to just see how things played out.
They arrived at Huifang's house, sat down,
and made idle conversation.
Consider the appearance of this Pan Qiguan:
He was short and stout,
with a round, dark-complexioned face
and a mouthful of yellowish, bristly hair resembling pig bristles.
He was over forty years old,
with a protruding belly and flat buttocks,
exuding a vulgar and foul air.
He wore a padded jacket of black silk,
a soft cap edged with velvet trailing a small marten tail, black satin socks,
and grey trimmed shoes.
A short tobacco pipe hung from the lapel of his jacket,
revealing half of a green leather tobacco pouch.
His pale yellow eyes were laced with red veins.
He smiled ingratiatingly, speaking in a low, obsequious voice,
putting on a grand show of humble warmth.
Huifang had no choice
but to sit and keep them company.
Zhang Zhongyu looked at Huifang,
appearing as though he wanted to speak but holding back.
Huifang lowered his head, stood up at one point
to look at the orchids by the window, his mind entirely on Tian Chunhang.
Unable to simply ask them to leave,
he sat in restless, listless discomfort.
Pan Qiguan sat motionless, not speaking a word, his eyes fixed entirely on Huifang.
Zhang Zhongyu suggested they not bother finding another place,
but simply arrange a small banquet right there
and have a few dishes prepared.
Pan Qiguan enthusiastically agreed,
noting it was peaceful in the house.
Huifang replied that while it was a fine idea,
he could not keep them company for long
because he had to perform at the Gusu Guildhall,
and his act was the second on the program.
Pan Qiguan dismissed this,
saying it did not matter if he skipped it.
Huifang insisted he had to go.
Pan Qiguan argued that since he had no master binding him,
he had nothing to fear,
and as such a popular star,
he could not possibly offend anyone.
Huifang remained silent and finally ordered his manservant to quickly prepare wine.
Before long, the wine and dishes were set out.
Huifang offered them seats,
and Pan Qiguan pushed Zhongyu
to take the seat of honor.
After drinking a few cups, Pan Qiguan began a relentless stream of nauseating, overly familiar chatter.
Huifang was thoroughly disgusted and hatched a plan.
Feigning attentive hospitality, he stood up
to offer several toasts and played a few rounds of drinking games,
hoping to get Pan completely drunk so he could make his escape.
Little did he know
that Pan Qiguan was a seasoned drinker who only grew more obnoxious with every cup.
As the wine went down, his words grew fouler.
He reached out with his short, fat, stiff hand and grabbed Huifang's hand,
calling him a good child
and asking why he never came to see him,
claiming he missed him terribly.
He said that every time he saw him perform,
he dreamt of him at night,
playing together intimately, only to wake up feeling exhausted.
He accused Huifang of being the death of him,
and since he had no son,
he questioned what use his vast fortune was.
He proposed that Huifang become his adopted son so they could enjoy themselves together every day.
Hearing this, Huifang was so furious he nearly cried.
His eyes reddened as he thought
to himself that this wretched slave had no sense of his own standing,
and decided to trick him thoroughly.
Suppressing his anger, he put on a smiling face
and told Pan he was speaking nonsense.
He claimed a clumsy child like himself could never hope for such a connection.
He pointed out that Pan listened
to operas every day but never even cast a glance his way,
nor had he ever cheered for him,
and accused him of lying in front of Master Zhang just to deceive him.
These few words left Pan Qiguan feeling light as a feather, entirely swept away.
Zhang Zhongyu was inwardly astonished.
He thought it strange
that Su Huifang actually seemed to like Pan Qiguan.
He wondered if money truly could move the gods,
and rejoiced that his own luck had arrived,
anticipating a massive windfall.
He quickly chimed in,
assuring Huifang that Third Master Pan truly praised him to everyone he met,
admiring his looks, his temperament,
and his skills, speaking of him at least twice a day.
He claimed Pan often said
that if Huifang would only favor him,
he would give him an entire silver house.
He then turned to Pan Qiguan
and asked if those were not his very own words.
Pan nodded vigorously.
Huifang laughed and asked how many silver houses he had.
He pointed out that
if he gave one to every performer,
with hundreds of performers in the capital,
he would need hundreds of silver houses.
Pan Qiguan declared that
while others could not get a single copper coin from him,
if Huifang was willing,
he would give him anything.
Huifang, his plan firmly in mind, cast a sidelong glance at Pan Qiguan
that completely captivated his soul.
Zhongyu was also immensely pleased,
tapping his fingers on the table
and continuously shouting his approval.
Huifang challenged Pan, saying
that since he held him in his heart,
he must drink freely today
and not hide his true capacity.
Pan Qiguan immediately called for large cups.
Huifang personally fetched two large cups, filled them with wine,
and offered one to each of them.
He then poured two more cups
and told Pan that he had originally intended to drink a paired toast with him,
but his tolerance was too low,
so Pan must drink the pair himself.
Pan Qiguan bobbed his head and drank them down.
Huifang poured two more cups and turned to Zhongyu,
urging him to also drink a paired toast.
Zhongyu laughed and asked who he was supposed to pair with.
Huifang suggested they pair together,
but insisted he first pair with Pan.
Zhongyu flicked Huifang's forehead,
joking if he was even worthy.
