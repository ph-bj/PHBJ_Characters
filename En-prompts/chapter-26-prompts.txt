Precious Mirror for Grading Flowers
Chapter 26
**Slander is advanced to satisfy old grudges;
valuing beauty above all, Lord Hua purchases celebrated performers.**
Word has it that by the time Young Master Hua returned to his city mansion,
the evening lamps had long been lit.
He rested briefly in the main hall
while Lady Hua inquired about the affairs at Pleasant Garden.
After sharing a few details,
he summoned two young maids
to carry lanterns and guide him to his Star-Lattice bedchamber.
Beneath the soft lamplight,
he found the ten maids waiting, all clad in identical unlined gowns of pristine white silk.
Their hair was elegantly swept into Magu chignons,
with long braids trailing behind
and single jasmine blossoms pinned proudly at the crown.
Below, their trousers were trimmed with dark silk borders, faintly revealing their delicate three-inch lotus steps.
As he entered, they formed two graceful lines,
greeting him with beaming smiles.
Young Master Hua looked them over
and asked, "Why the change of attire today?"
One stepped forward and replied, "Today,
Madame visited the Guanyin Pavilion at the family temple to offer incense.
She asked us to change our clothes and accompany her."
The Young Master took a seat.
The Ten Pearls, all fifteen or sixteen years of age,
were remarkably similar in beauty, each as lovely as the last.
Mingzhu first offered him a bowl of iced plum broth,
while Zhangzhu stood by, gently fanning him with a goose-feather fan.
Chengzhu (Pearl) came forward
to remove his boots and help him into his cloud-stepping slippers,
as Hezhu draped a light gauze robe over his shoulders.
Baozhu then asked, "Has the Master dined?
Shall we order the inner kitchen to prepare anything?"
"I drank too much today
and feel parched," the Young Master replied.
"After the first watch of the night,
follow the recipe I gave you the other day for the ledebouriella porridge.
Mix it with the fruit powders and floral dews,
and simmer it slowly over alternating high and low heat.
It must not touch copper for even a moment.
You must make it yourself;
do not let those clumsy old women in the kitchen handle it.
In this sweltering summer heat,
it is a time for dispersion,
so use less ledebouriella and peppermint dew,
and add more rose, sweet rice, lotus, and osmanthus dew.
Portion out the poria, lotus seed, qiongmi,
and swallow's nest powders exactly as prescribed."
Baozhu agreed and pulled Huazhu along with her.
Having prepared the ingredients
and fetched a bowl of sweet rice and a jar of snow water,
they avoided the kitchen altogether.
Instead, under the eaves of the octagonal glazed pavilion just outside his room,
they lit a small copper brazier
and began to slowly simmer the porridge in a silver pot.
Huazhu squatted nearby, a long crimson sash trailing onto the ground,
and remarked, "The Master seems quite drunk today.
He keeps staring at us,
smiling to himself for no reason."
"I heard Madame say yesterday
that she plans to take you in as a concubine by autumn," Baozhu teased.
Huazhu spat lightly in mock disdain.
"If she takes anyone,
it will be you!
You have a sharp mind
and know exactly how to curry favor.
I doubt you'll even have to wait until autumn!"
Baozhu shoved her playfully,
sending Huazhu tumbling to the ground.
Her legs sprawled, knocking against the silver pot
and nearly tipping it over.
Scrambling up, Huazhu grabbed Baozhu by the shoulders,
trying to wrestle her down,
and the two collapsed into a fit of laughter.
Presently, Aizhu emerged carrying an amber silk lantern
and said, "It is almost the first watch,
yet we are still supposed to summon Lin Shanzhi!"
"Why call for Lin Shanzhi?"
Baozhu asked.
"How should I know?
It must be something urgent," Aizhu replied.
Wearing wooden-soled bow shoes, her rapid steps clattered sharply against the floor.
Reaching the side gate,
she relayed the message to the older women on duty,
who passed it to the inner gatekeepers, until it finally reached the outer servant quarters.
Finding Lin Shanzhi enjoying the cool evening air in the courtyard,
attended by two young pages preparing his pipe and fanning him,
they informed him of the Master's summons.
Shanzhi had no choice
but to don a long gown, fasten his belt, light a small horn lantern,
and follow the inner gatekeeper.
He halted by the octagonal glazed pavilion and, after exchanging greetings with Aizhu
and the others, asked, "What business does the Master have?"
Aizhu lifted the amber lantern to Shanzhi's face, smiling playfully.
"Your face is flushed red from drinking;
you are sure to meet a rebuff when you go in."
Shanzhi chuckled, "When did I drink?
It's your red lantern reflecting on my face
that makes you think I'm drunk."
Aizhu smiled again and led him slowly into the inner chambers,
where the Young Master was chatting
and laughing with the maids.
Aizhu entered first to announce his arrival,
and the Master called him forward.
Standing by the window, Shanzhi saw the Young Master sitting cross-legged on a Drunken-Elder couch,
flanked by four of the Pearls.
Upon seeing him, Young Master Hua instructed, "Go
and invite Secretary Wei to the Quiet-Green Retreat.
I will head over from here;
there is something I wish to discuss."
Shanzhi replied, "The first watch has already sounded,
and the East Garden gates are locked,
as are the three main gates.
If it is nothing urgent, perhaps it would be better to tell him tomorrow.
Furthermore, opening several sets of doors
to fetch him from the East Garden will take us well into the second watch,
and the secretaries have likely already retired."
Knowing the summons was merely
to discuss the day's opera, Shanzhi deliberately dissuaded him.
Realizing it was indeed not pressing, the Young Master conceded,
"Since the gates are locked, tomorrow will not be too late."
After a pause, he added, "It was my first time seeing that boy Baozhu perform,
and I never expected him to be so magnificent.
Why does their troupe never enter the mansion?"
"They always arrive early on the first
and fifteenth of the month," Shanzhi explained.
"But the gatekeepers, seeing the Master is not yet awake, turn them away.
When actors come to pay their respects,
it is not recorded in the ledger.
Even that boy Qinyan was once brought here by his master,
though he never made it inside."
"Whose apprentice is Qinyan?"
the Young Master inquired.
"He is Changqing's apprentice."
"Changqing... wasn't your master also named Changqing?"
"Yes," Shanzhi replied.
"This servant originally belonged to the Lianjin Troupe before joining the Dengchun Troupe."
"Why did you switch?"
"Changqing has a terrible temper,
and I offended him.
As a result, he traded me for Xiufang of the Dengchun Troupe.
Only after Xiufang completed his apprenticeship did Changqing buy Qinyan—
he has only been with him for a little over half a year."
"And what do you think of this Qinyan?"
the Young Master asked.
Shanzhi remained silent.
When pressed again, he finally answered, "He is quite good,
but he is the favored darling of Second Master Xu.
Word has it he refuses to entertain anyone else."
"Second Master Xu favors Yuan Baozhu, not him," the Young Master countered.
"I hear Second Master Xu cherishes them both equally," Shanzhi replied.
"They say he has already spent thousands of silver taels on Qinyan."
The Young Master fell silent.
After a moment, he spoke again.
"The other day, Secretary Wei mentioned that Qinyan was extraordinary,
yet I only saw him for myself today.
There is a young master named Mei whom Qinyan truly favors;
his attachment to Second Master Xu is merely an act."
"This servant is unaware of such details," Shanzhi said cautiously.
"I only just saw Qinyan today as well."
"You are of the theater yourself," the Young Master continued.
"Tell me, in their joint performance of Searching the Dream today,
who was truly the better of the two?"
Shanzhi thought for a moment before replying.
"If we judge strictly on acting, one must consider expression and bearing.
In terms of appearance,
they are quite matched.
However, as for the performance, Baozhu's singing is far more seasoned.
Qinyan seemed slightly raw in the first scene,
though he improved in the second."
The Young Master nodded in agreement.
"He is but a beginner,
while Baozhu has been performing for two or three years;
naturally, he is more polished.
Yet, to my eyes, Qinyan possesses the finer appearance.
He moves with an ethereal grace,
as if set apart from ordinary mortals."
Shanzhi lowered his head and offered no reply.
Zhangzhu, meanwhile, had been fanning the Master
while listening to their conversation.
Her mind wandered, and the fan slipped from her grasp,
tumbling to the floor.
Mingzhu let out a muffled giggle,
and Zhangzhu, flushing deeply, hurriedly retrieved it.
The Young Master laughed aloud.
"Have you never heard opera before?
Is mere talk of it enough
to steal your hearts away?
Someday, I will summon the entire troupe
to perform for Madame for a full day,
and you shall all reap the benefits."
Aizhu chimed in, "What outside troupe could possibly compare
to our own within the mansion?"
Smiling, the Young Master replied, "There are ten of you as well.
If you were to play the male leads
and they the female leads in a joint performance,
you would soon realize their brilliance."
The maids blushed and lowered their heads.
"We do not know how to perform," they murmured.
"If you wish for an opera, there is always the Eight Ling Troupe."
"You could learn," the Young Master laughed.
"Female actors are not so uncommon."
Shanzhi offered a faint smile.
After standing for a moment longer without receiving further orders,
he took his leave,
finding three or four of the maids still gathered around the brazier.
He exchanged a few words with them before departing.
Once the fragrant porridge was ready,
it was served alongside several delicate, handmade pastries.
After cooling himself for a while, the Young Master retired for the night,
and the Ten Pearls returned to their respective quarters.
The following morning, still sluggish from the previous night's wine, Young Master Hua rose late.
It was not until the hour of Si
that he finally washed his face, allowed the maids to comb his hair,
and dressed for the day.
Lady Hua, concerned that the wine might have harmed his constitution,
sent a maid with a bowl of medicinal ginseng soup, which he drank.
Baozhu then entered to report, "Shanzhi is waiting outside
to ask for your instructions.
Do you still wish to invite Secretary Wei,
as you ordered last night?"
The Young Master hesitated briefly before replying, "Tell him
to invite Secretary Wei to breakfast at the Quiet-Green Retreat."
Baozhu agreed and left.
The Young Master then visited Lady Hua's chambers,
where her morning toilette was complete,
with maids attending her on both sides.
Seeing her delicately drawn brows, faint rouge, glossy green tresses,
and a high chignon piled like clouds,
he realized she bore a striking resemblance to Su Huifang.
He sat down and exchanged pleasantries,
announcing he would dine in her quarters.
He excitedly recounted the previous day's opera,
declaring the Eight Ling Troupe lacking even a tenth of their skill,
and casually mentioned her resemblance to Huifang.
Lady Hua was inwardly displeased but held her tongue.
They were normally a deeply devoted couple,
harmonizing like the qin and se lutes.
Though the Young Master loved luxury and extravagance,
he was by no means debauched.
Lady Hua had several times suggested taking Huazhu and Baozhu as concubines,
but the Young Master always refused.
"Once they become concubines, their charm will fade," he would say.
"It is better to let them serve for a few years
and then arrange marriages with handsome youths—
a truly meritorious deed.
Furthermore, if you were plain-looking,
I might naturally seek comfort among the maids.
But since you are so beautiful, even the loveliest maids pale in comparison.
They are like miscellaneous wildflowers—
pretty enough on their own,
but if planted upon the peony terrace,
they become mere awkward mimicries, maids attempting
to play the mistress, only worsening their own embarrassment."
Lady Hua was delighted by these words,
and thus, no matter how lavishly the Young Master lived,
she never harbored jealousy.
Even with the Ten Pearls tumbling together in his presence daily,
she felt no envy.
As for Lin Shanzhi and Feng Zipei,
they were mere shadows of suspicion without any real interference.
After all, everyone's temperament is different,
and he was not obsessed with carnal indulgences like Xi Eleven.
The Young Master breakfasted with Lady Hua,
and their laughter and conversation carried them past noon, completely forgetting
that Wei Pincai was waiting at the Quiet-Green Retreat.
When Lin Shanzhi had gone
to summon him earlier, Pincai had been awake for quite some time
and was just about to eat.
Hearing that the Young Master had invited him to breakfast,
he was as elated as a scholar hearing his name called at the palace examinations.
He hurriedly pulled on his boots, changed into a fresh gown,
and, grabbing a round fan, strutted confidently out of the garden alongside Shanzhi without even pausing
to inform Zhang or Gu.
Along the way, he endlessly flattered his companion,
calling him "Old Shan"
and "Little Brother," walking shoulder to shoulder until they reached the Retreat.
Believing this to be an urgent summons from his patron,
he paid no attention to the garden's scenery and walked straight inside.
Four young lackeys sitting beneath the gallery rose at the sight of him.
Shanzhi asked, "Is there any word on
when the Master will emerge?"
"Silence," they replied.
"We do not know
if he will come out at all."
Shanzhi turned to Pincai, "Secretary Wei, please wait here.
I will go and inquire."
With that, he left.
Pincai took the opportunity to study the room's appointments,
finding them unspeakably sumptuous—
even finer than the west salon.
The windows, railings, and screens were intricately carved with hollowed miniature landscapes,
the figures inlaid with precious gems,
creating the illusion of jade pillars and beams.
It was an eye-opening sight.
However, a full two hours passed,
and still the Young Master did not appear.
The lackeys refreshed his tea repeatedly,
washing his intestines hollow until his stomach rumbled like a hungry kite,
yet he could only sit and endure.
Meanwhile, Lin Shanzhi waited outside the Crimson Wash Pavilion,
chatting with the Ten Pearls, unable
to ascend and disturb the Master.
Zengzhu remarked, "Earlier, I heard the Master dining with Madame upstairs.
Their conversation was quite lively,
so he is unlikely to emerge anytime soon."
Shanzhi sighed, "What is to be done?
Secretary Wei has been summoned since dawn
and has waited over two hours.
I am famished myself.
Have you all eaten?"
Mingzhu replied, "We finished long ago.
There are leftovers, if you don't mind eating them.
It's impossible to guess when he will come out."
"Dear sister, your scraps are far beyond my meager fortune," Shanzhi laughed.
"I would be honored;
how could I possibly disdain them?"
Aizhu teased, "Such a silver tongue!
I wager your eyes are hungry too."
She led him to a side room where summer dishes requiring no heating—
both meat and vegetables—
were laid out.
Shanzhi ate his fill, wiped his hands,
and resumed joking with the maids,
but the Young Master still did not appear.
As the afternoon wore on, Shanzhi grew anxious.
"Will he come out or not?
Keeping someone waiting like this... Dear Aizhu, please go
and tell him that Secretary Wei is still waiting at the Quiet-Green Retreat."
"I won't carry your messages," Aizhu retorted.
"Go tell him yourself."
"Dear sister, if I could step inside,
I wouldn't need to beg you."
After much delay and teasing from Aizhu, Hezhu finally relented and went inside.
Half an hour later, footsteps sounded,
and the Young Master emerged.
He and his wife had talked until they were exhausted,
and he had lain down on her bed to rest.
Naturally, no one dared to wake him,
and he only stirred as the afternoon waned.
Seeing Hezhu inquire, the Young Master suddenly recalled his morning appointment.
Realizing it was already late,
and that he could only offer supper now,
he slipped through a side gate
that opened directly onto the courtyard of the Quiet-Green Retreat,
which was just one courtyard away.
Shanzhi hurried ahead to receive him,
and Pincai stepped forward to the window as the Young Master arrived.
Pincai bowed, but the Young Master seized his hand.
"I meant to speak with you this morning,
but yesterday's wine made me oversleep, keeping you waiting.
Have you had breakfast?"
Pincai lied and said he had eaten.
Shanzhi, pitying the man's half-day fast, interjected, "The Secretary arrived at the crack of dawn;
he likely hasn't had breakfast yet."
The Young Master scolded Shanzhi, "What is your job,
if not to feed a guest?"
"This servant also entered at dawn
and has been waiting inside ever since," Shanzhi explained.
The Young Master immediately ordered pastries to be fetched.
Shanzhi sprinted off, and soon, eight refined dishes filled a heated kang table.
The Young Master urged Pincai
to eat, then enthusiastically recounted the entrance of the eight female leads from the previous day,
especially praising the duet of Searching the Dream by Qinyan and Baozhu.
"I lavished great effort in buying those eight,
intending to form a troupe
that would eclipse all outsiders," the Young Master lamented.
"Who would have guessed that outsiders would eclipse us instead?
Have you seen their acting?"
Catching his drift, Pincai flattered him further.
"I have seen them.
Eclipsing outsiders is not difficult;
fine flowers need not crowd a garden.
Simply purchase the choicest few,
and that will suffice."
Inwardly, Pincai plotted:
'He fancies Qinyan, little knowing
that Qinyan's heart belongs to Mei Gengxiang and is unlikely to favor him.
Furthermore, Qinyan's snobbery scorns me.
If admitted to this mansion,
he might change, or he might not,
but once inside, he would never glimpse Gengxiang again.
The Young Master is unaware of Qinyan's eccentric temper;
in time, disgust will surely follow.
Better to drag him in, make him suffer for two years,
and then my spite will be fully vented.'
Aloud, he suggested, "Why not purchase both Baozhu and Qinyan?
Join them with your eight to make ten—
would that not easily surpass the outside troupes?"
"I hear both are Duxiang's favorites;
it would be hard
to wrest them away," the Young Master replied.
"Duxiang truly cherishes Baozhu, not Qinyan," Pincai countered.
"If the Master desires him,
I can assure you it is possible.
I know his master,
Changqing, quite well, and often patronize his troupe.
Changqing loves silver above all else.
If he learns the Master wants him,
he will crawl to oblige and deliver the boy gift-wrapped."
The Young Master hesitated, weighed down by the thought of Xu Ziyun.
Furthermore, acquiring Qinyan alone would only bring the troupe to nine,
and obtaining Baozhu seemed impossible.
Seeing him waver, Pincai argued
and cajoled until the Young Master was finally swayed.
"Go tomorrow and probe the matter," he commanded.
"If they are unwilling, drop it.
Even if we purchase him later,
we will trail Duxiang and always be second best."
Driven by pride and a loathing
to appear as though he were copying Duxiang,
he supped with Pincai, their conversation overflowing with praise for Qinyan's talents,
and even commending Qiguan, until they parted late in the evening.
The next morning, after breakfast, Pincai took Si'er
and a large saddle-cart out into the city to find Ye Maolin.
Maolin rode with him to Changqing's residence.
Along the way, they ran into Zhang Zhongyu.
Both carts halted, and Maolin yielded the right of way as Pincai stepped out to speak.
When Zhongyu inquired of their errand, Pincai explained the Young Master's commission.
"No wonder he was so generous the other day," Zhongyu remarked.
"He bestowed over a hundred gold pieces
and even gave his own jade pendants to Qinyan and Baozhu."
Zhongyu then whispered at length into Pincai's ear.
Ye Maolin could not hear the words,
but saw Pincai nod emphatically.
"I know my own mind," Pincai muttered.
"Once he is inside, will he not have to bend?"
After exchanging a few more words, they parted ways.
Arriving at Qinyan's door, Maolin alighted first and entered alongside Pincai.
Fortunately, Changqing was home and invited them to sit.
Sizing Pincai up and noting Maolin's modest escort,
Changqing assumed he was a person of minor importance
and offered a cool greeting.
Maolin interjected, "This is Secretary Wei, the closest confidant of Young Master Hua.
Hearing of your fame,
he has come especially to discuss a matter of business."
Changqing instantly bloomed with obsequious warmth,
calling him "Secretary this" and "Secretary that."
Recognizing Pincai's cleverness, he eagerly courted him.
"Young Master Hua favors me beyond measure,
and with two of my disciples already in his mansion,
his grace is as high as heaven
and deep as the earth—
truly inexpressible."
Tea was served, and after more pleasantries,
Changqing prepared his opium lamp,
inviting them to lie down.
"I do not partake," Maolin declined.
"You entertain Secretary Wei;
it will make talking easier."
Pincai added, "I am
but a beginner and cannot prepare the pipe myself."
Changqing roasted a prime bowl and passed it to Pincai,
who smoked it and returned the pipe.
"I am an outsider;
I won't offer a courtesy smoke in return."
When Pincai asked about Qinyan's recent situation,
Changqing boasted, "The lad is doing wonderfully,
and he is quite clever.
Just the other day,
he sang at Second Master Xu's garden,
and your patron, the Young Master, rewarded him with ten gold ingots—
over fourteen taels, worth some seven hundred strings of cash!
Second Master Xu also piled gifts upon him,
and the Young Master even gave him his own purse ornament, a yellow-red jade.
I asked Sha Huizi at the Degu Studio,
and he appraised it at two hundred strings.
Without the boy's flattery and charm,
who would pamper him so?"
Changqing offered another puff,
which Pincai accepted before continuing.
"Today's business is exactly what I wish to consult you about.
Yesterday, our patron saw his performance of Searching the Dream and was completely besotted.
He praised him all day
and wishes to bring him into the mansion.
Could you bear to part with him?"
Changqing listened, pondering for a moment.
"Secretary, it is not that I refuse the honor,
but the lad is truly pitiful.
He only arrived in the capital last October.
I bought him, and he has been a quick study with a handsome face.
Even Huifang and Baozhu, at the height of their fame, can barely rival him.
You can judge for yourself:"
"Since his stage debut on the first day of the twelfth month,
a mere seven months have passed.
Not to mention others, Second Master Xu alone has poured oceans of wealth upon him."
Changqing stretched out his hand.
"This much!
My purse is vast,
but the money is spent as fast as it is earned.
You see my household of over twenty mouths?
We rely entirely on him.
If he goes to the mansion,
he will thrive, but I will founder.
He is only fifteen;
with five years left until he completes his apprenticeship,
would he not earn tens of thousands for me?
How can I relinquish him?
He is not like Lin Shanzhi,
who had a surly temper, ate too many oil cakes,
and constantly vexed me until I traded him for Xiufang.
When Xiufang graduated, he brought in eight thousand strings of cash—
everyone knows this.
Qinyan surpasses Xiufang many times over.
Secretary, I beg you to plead with the Young Master.
He is my only fine disciple, my lifelong reliance.
The other boys are worthless;
they only cost me money for costumes, attend a few wine banquets a month,
and constantly cause trouble..." Pincai was about
to reply when Ye Maolin stroked his beard and smiled.
"Old Qing, everything is negotiable.
You know Young Master Hua's ways.
Others crawl to enter his doors,
yet he seeks you out.
This is your great fortune!
If he catches the Master's fancy,
it secures not just one lifetime, but two.
Later, you could enter the mansion yourself,
obtain a minor post, a minor rank, and great face—
surely that beats eating opera rice?"
"Maestro Ye speaks plainly," Pincai added.
"A mere glance from him rewards one with gold.
For a serious hire, would he ever stint?
Your entire life would be secured.
An early graduation pays more;
a late one pays less.
He is fourteen or fifteen,
and his body is still developing.
What if he takes a bad turn in a year or two?
Then what?
Thousands of strings wasted!
I urge you to reflect on this.
Anything is negotiable, and you will face no injustice."
Having smoked over a dozen bowls, Changqing sat up.
"That sounds fair enough.
We can discuss it,
but I must also ask his will."
Pincai laughed coldly.
"Old Qing, bright folks hide nothing.
You know Qinyan's temper as well as I do.
Aside from Second Master Xu,
who else favors him?
Who else will he oblige?
Offend Second Master Xu..." He paused with a sneer, "And your lifelong reliance will reverse.
At home, you surely know why he fell ill.
It was all over Young Master Mei!
Everyone knows they click.
When he drinks with Mei, he is pleased;
without Mei, there are only tears,
and everyone tires of it.
You are not blind;
you merely protect your disciple's face,
though the truth is ugly.
If you are banking on Young Master Mei to lift him up,
you are waiting for a rooster to lay eggs!
Think carefully:
is my counsel kind or cruel?"
Changqing resented Qinyan's temper,
which was hindering his ability to inflate the price,
and the truth of Pincai's words struck hard.
Unable to argue, he conceded, "The boy is indeed stubborn.
You are a glass lantern,
seeing right through it all.
Whatever the case, I must lean on him.
If the numbers don't work, there is no deal."
Maolin pulled Changqing toward the window
and whispered, "Name your price,
so Wei can report back."
Thinking the Young Master was a legendary sucker,
Changqing wanted ten thousand
but settled on whispering a figure of five or six thousand to Maolin.
Maolin relayed this to Pincai,
who shook his head.
"Impossible.
A boy for ten thousand?
He is not some miraculous good.
Four or five thousand is suitable."
"It is a ballpark figure," Maolin reasoned.
"The best actors graduate for three or four thousand strings;
this is more than double that."
Changqing shook his head repeatedly before finally saying, "At
that price, it is impossible.
He has only been here half a year.
Money aside, my heart cannot bear to release him.
I never wished him gone."
He wiped his eyes, feigning tears.
Pincai thought to himself, 'Crafty devil!
Asking for a sky-high price!
How can I report this to the Young Master?
Even if he doesn't suspect graft, others will.
I must crush him with bluster.'
Feigning outrage, he stood up.
"Splendid!
Go grow rich yourself, then!
I meant to show you kindness,
and you bite my hand.
In a few days,
you will crawl begging to me, entirely worthless."
With that, he stormed out.
Ye Maolin eyed Changqing,
who, seeing the rage, smiled placatingly.
"Secretary, please calm down.
Sit, let us discuss this."
"Discuss what?"
Pincai retorted.
"I have no patience for hollow talk.
Maolin, you may sit,
but I am leaving."
He marched out, and Maolin chased after him, clutching his arm.
"Wait for me at the Six Harmonies Inn," Pincai whispered,
shaking him off and vanishing into the cart.
Unable to escort him,
Changqing dragged Maolin back inside.
"He left in wrath," Maolin warned.
"Speak plainly!
Offending Young Master Hua is ill-advised.
When Wei returns with gossip, a storm will brew.
What will you do then?"
"I am not dull," Changqing replied.
"But he is my cash tree;
I cannot bear it.
I must ask his will.
Give me two days to reply,
and I will curry favor.
Perhaps yes, perhaps no."
Hearing little flexibility, Maolin took a cart and left.
He found Pincai and repeated Changqing's words verbatim.
Helpless, Pincai returned and had Shanzhi report
that he could not locate Changqing,
deciding to try again later.
Whether this would decide Qinyan's fate remains
to be seen in the next installment.
