Precious Mirror for Grading Flowers
Chapter 36
**In intimate talk, all mouths curse Shanzhi;
amid treacherous scheming, a jade bracelet is smashed in anger.**
The last chapter told of Baozhu’s birthday
and their joyful day at Yi Garden,
showing how the joys and sorrows of human life vary.
Meanwhile, at the Hua estate, on the day of the Lantern Festival, Young Master Hua had ordered Qinyan
and Baling to perform a play.
Qinyan, feeling unwell and grieving over the past, wore a desolate expression
and lacked any spirit for the performance.
On stage that day, moved by the tender sentiments of the play,
he could not help but weep real tears.
Young Master Hua, assuming he was mourning for no reason, was deeply displeased;
he called Qinyan down
and sharply reprimanded him, not summoning him again for several days.
Qinyan remained alone in his room without visitors.
Finding the tempers of the servants and attendants incompatible,
he felt entirely out of place.
Behind the wing of the Lingering Green Cottage,
he occupied a small three-room suite with a single servant to wait on him.
In the courtyard stood a few Taihu rocks, two green-calyx plum trees,
and one red plum tree, still in full, brilliant bloom.
It was the twenty-seventh day of the first month.
Facing the plum blossoms, Qinyan could not help
but long for the Plum Blossom Ridge of Yi Garden.
He recalled how Duxiang had treated him;
compared to the present day,
they were worlds apart.
Even if the servants fell short in their duties, Duxiang not only refrained from showing anger
but harbored no resentment, instead offering every comfort and consideration.
On the other hand,
when Young Master Hua was in a good mood,
he seemed somewhat like Duxiang,
but the moment things displeased him,
he would grow irritated and fault-finding.
On the Lantern Festival,
he had even resorted to scolding,
treating Qinyan like any other servant.
The sycophantic attendants rejoiced in this,
as if feeling that Qinyan had finally met his downfall
and would sink into the cycle of reincarnation, never to rise again.
Every time the master summoned someone, the others hated him;
every time the master gave a reward, the others cursed him.
Their mocking, sarcastic remarks were hard to bear.
He deeply resented that scoundrel Xi Eleven for causing trouble at his door for no reason,
which had led to his current plight.
He also thought of Wei Pincai;
though not a good man,
he had at least occasionally spoken words that touched Qinyan's heart,
yet now he was gone too.
Then there was Lin Shanzhi,
who acted like half a master;
one had to flatter him carefully
to please him, otherwise he would stir up trouble.
“If only they would just cast me out entirely,
I would be free,” Qinyan thought.
“Then, naturally, I wouldn't have
to return to my teacher's place either.
If Heaven preserves me
and lets me live a clean life,
I would gladly endure hunger
and cold for the rest of my days.
Otherwise, people will say I was first an actor, then a servant—
how would that sound?
Would anyone still respect me?” The more he thought about it, the angrier he grew,
and tears fell naturally.
Sitting all alone under the plum tree,
he grieved for a long time.
Then he heard Lin Shanzhi’s voice calling, “Yunong!” as he walked in.
Qinyan stood up.
Seeing his sorrowful face, Shanzhi asked, “Do you know already?” Qinyan, confused, asked, “Know what?” Shanzhi replied, “Your teacher has died.
Someone just came to deliver the news
and informed the young master,
who told you to go back for the encoffining.” Hearing this, Qinyan felt a wave of sorrow and wept continuously.
“When did he die?” he asked.
Shanzhi said, “The messenger said he wasn’t sick;
he went to sleep last night
and was found dead this morning.” Qinyan grieved a while longer, then asked, “How am I to go back?” Shanzhi told him, “Someone is waiting for you outside.
The young master ordered you not to delay too long;
return once the funeral matters are settled.” Qinyan thought for a moment and agreed.
After Shanzhi left, Qinyan had the servant pack a bundle of clothes, roll up his bedding,
and took a pouch of silver.
Locking his door, he stepped out.
Poor Qinyan did not even know the way;
the servant pointed the direction.
Passing the gatehouse, he was glad
to see everyone already knew,
so no one asked questions.
Reaching the main gate,
he saw a carriage waiting by the screen wall—
the very carriage he always took.
He also saw Wu Mazi, his teacher’s wife's cousin,
who had come along.
Qinyan went up to greet him,
and the two boarded the carriage,
talking all the way out of the city.
As they neared the door, a group of people was already setting up a mourning canopy.
Qinyan entered and went straight in.
Tianshou ran out, saw him,
and ran back in.
Inside, he could hear his teacher’s wife weeping bitterly.
Approaching the bed, Qinyan saw his teacher already dressed in grave clothes, a cloth covering his face.
Overcome with bitter grief,
he knelt by the bed and wept profusely.
His teacher’s wife pulled him up
and urged him to stop crying.
“What illness did he catch

She replied, “He wasn't ill at all.
He was perfectly fine last night.
He smoked opium until after midnight, talked a great deal before sleeping,
but when I woke up
and touched him, he was cold.
If it was coal gas, why am I perfectly fine?” Qinyan asked about the funeral arrangements.
She sighed, “Your teacher earned money his whole life,
but heaven knows where he spent it.
By the new year, things were already tight.” Sighing again,
she added, “It’s far worse than when you were here.
Your two junior brothers are idle eight days out of ten;
I can’t think of a way forward.
Your teacher died so suddenly he left no final words,
and he never told me about his affairs outside.
Now I know nothing of who owes us or whom we owe.
Those two houses beyond the alley won’t fetch much rent.
We’ve managed to put together the grave clothes, coffin,
and canopy, but when it comes
to buying a burial plot and arranging the funeral, things will likely get very tight.” Sighing, Qinyan walked to his old room, had the servant set things up,
and opened the silver pouch he had brought.
It contained fifteen ingots of various sizes,
weighing perhaps fifty or sixty taels.
He took it in and gave it to her,
saying, “I don’t know exactly how much this is—
it’s the New Year’s reward from the young master and his wife.
It can help with the expenses.” She took it, weighed it in her hand, untied it to count,
and said, “I heard you have a high-ranking position in the Hua estate.
Have you managed to save much?
I know you’re inexperienced;
don’t let people cheat you.
You must be careful and save a little.
I have no sons or daughters;
I’ll have to rely on you in the future.” Qinyan replied, “The young master mostly gives small trinkets.
The silver rewards are few,
and I have no use for them anyway.
If I get more in the future,
I will bring them to you.” She nodded, “That’s good.
You’re a conscientious child.” She placed the silver in a drawer,
and Qinyan went out.
Outside, amidst the bustling crowd, Wu Mazi brought in a bundle of mourning clothes.
Then Yuan Baozhu, Su Huifang,
and Lu Sulan arrived.
Qinyan quickly welcomed them,
and they sat down together.
They first asked about his teacher’s passing, then inquired about his life since the New Year.
Qinyan briefly recounted recent events.
Baozhu asked, “Since you’re back, how many days of leave did you get?” Qinyan replied, “Lin Shanzhi came
to tell me this morning.
I didn’t see the young master myself.
He only said to return after the funeral,

He only dares to throw his weight around

Don’t you remember when Wang Jingfang almost beat him up at the Yanqiu Hall?
Now, when he sees Jingfang,
he completely ignores him, still holding a grudge.” Qinyan mused, “Young Master Hua’s temper isn’t entirely eccentric, but it’s unpredictable.
Yet whenever Shanzhi is summoned, whatever he says is right;
he never hits a snag.
I suppose it’s just his luck.
He’s truly adaptable;
there’s never a question he can’t answer.
You have to give him credit for that.” Sulan noted, “I heard

Wei Pincai calls him ‘Third Brother,’ ‘Old Third,’ or ‘Third Grand Master.’”
Qinyan asked, “Wei Pincai has moved out.
Do you know if he’s with Yuxiang?” Huifang scoffed, “Wei Pincai?
He’s even more arrogant now.
He lives at Hongji Temple
and causes trouble every day with a bunch of scoundrels like Xi Eleven, Pan Third, and Yang Eight—
barbers, monks big and small—
gambling and visiting prostitutes.
It’s a disgrace.
Even Zhang Zhongyu no longer associates with him.”
When Qinyan asked about Ziyu, Baozhu recounted, “The day before yesterday,

and used Cixian’s newly designed wine pots and cups,
making Qinyan deeply envious.
He also mentioned the poem Duxiang had engraved
and described Yuxiang’s sorrowful expression upon seeing it,
detailing every bit to Qinyan,
which moved Qinyan to melancholy as well.
Huifang suggested, “Since you’re back,
we must find a few days to gather properly.
Would tomorrow be alright?” Baozhu confirmed, “He has no obligations tomorrow either.”
But Qinyan demurred, “My teacher has just died.
It’s contrary to propriety;
we should wait a few days.” Sulan countered, “If we wait a few days,
you’ll have to return to the city.
Besides, a quiet gathering for conversation
and relaxation won’t hurt anything.
You’re not a filial son in formal mourning—
what are you afraid of?” Baozhu promised, “I’ll go ask Duxiang;
tomorrow or the day after will both work.” The three sat for a long time,
but when they tried to leave, Qinyan clung to them,
and, reluctant to part,
they sat back down.
Later, Wang Guibao, Li Yulin,
and Jin Shufang arrived.
They all stayed until the coffin was sealed, paid their respects, and then departed.
Since Qinyan was wearing mourning robes,
it seemed improper to go out the next day,
so they agreed to meet again in three days.
He also sent Wu Mazi to the Hua estate
to ask Shanzhi to request a one-month leave for him,
waiting until after the burial to return to the city.
Young Master Hua approved
and sent back a chest of clothes,
which finally put Qinyan’s mind at ease.
On the third day of mourning rites, many people came.
Jin San and Ye Maolin were invited to help manage things.
Aside from actors in the same troupe, members of other troupes, neighbors,
and restaurant owners crowded the house, only dispersing after the paper offerings were burned.
Qinyan, exhausted, returned to his room and slept.
The next morning, Baozhu sent a message:
“We originally planned for today,
but Duxiang has matters to attend to,
so we’ve changed the gathering at Yi Garden
to tomorrow morning.” Qinyan agreed.
After washing up, he sat alone in thought:
“Since I have nothing
to do today, why not go see Yuxiang?” Remembering Lady Mei’s kind treatment last year,
he figured there would be no harm in going.
Mind made up, he changed into plain clothes, ordered the carriage to be readied,
and told his teacher’s wife he was going
to thank his fellow actors.
When he reached the outer courtyard, however, he hesitated:
“It’s been half a year.
Before, Pincai took me,
so I didn’t have to speak to the gatekeepers.
If I go alone now, even
if the lady treats me well and invites me in,
I still have to beg the gatekeepers.
What if they refuse me entry?
Besides Yun’er, I don’t know anyone there.” He pondered
but couldn’t decide, standing frozen.
The servant came in and asked, “The carriage is ready.
Where to?” Qinyan remained silent, thought a bit more,
and said to himself, “I should go find Pincai
and have him take me,
saving a lot of explanation.
Since he’s moved out,
it’s only right I visit him,
and he’ll appreciate the gesture.”
He told the servant, “I’ll go
to Hongji Temple first to see Master Wei.” He went out and boarded the carriage;
the servant climbed onto the shaft.
After a few turns
and less than a mile, they arrived.
Qinyan saw a large, fine carriage with a sleeping driver resting at the temple gate.
Assuming it was Pincai’s,
he felt relieved to have arrived before he went out.
The servant went in
to ask and reported, “He’s home and invites you in.” Qinyan stepped down and entered the east gate.
Following the servant’s directions,
he passed two halls
and entered a courtyard behind the eastern corridor.
Keeping his head down,
he paid no attention to his surroundings until he reached Pincai’s courtyard.
Pincai’s servant, Si’er, came out, nodded to him,
and opened the wind-door.
When Qinyan looked up, he was startled—
the room was full of people.
His heart pounded, and his face flushed.
He wanted to retreat,
but Pincai was already coming out to welcome him.
Gathering his wits, Qinyan stepped forward to greet him.
Pincai exclaimed, “What brings you here today?
I could never have dreamed it.” Blushing, Qinyan couldn’t find the words.
Pincai turned to the room,
saying, “This is the famous Master Du I talk about every day;
he’s now called Master Du Qin.” To Qinyan,
he added, “These are my closest friends:
Master Xi, Third Master Pan, my landlord Monk Tang,
and his little novice.
Those two are from the troupe;
you probably don’t know them,
so let’s all get acquainted.” With no choice, Qinyan bowed to them.
The monk, knowing he came from the Hua estate, pressed his hands together, bowed repeatedly,
and smiled broadly, saying, “Too polite, too polite!
Please sit, Master Qin.” Pan Third merely bowed shallowly;
Qinyan, focused on the monk, didn’t notice.
Pan Third, his lust already roused, took the chance
to step forward and grabbed Qinyan’s hand.
Qinyan tried to pull away but couldn’t.
Pan Third, bearing his teeth, his eyes red
and staring, smiled lasciviously and said, “You are Master Qin, my grand Master Qin!
I’ve longed to meet you but couldn’t.
Today is truly a fated encounter from a thousand miles away.” Ashamed
and furious, Qinyan desperately yanked his hand free.
Knowing Qinyan was shy
and prone to tears when anxious, Pincai quickly pushed Pan Third away and pulled Qinyan down to sit.
Before Pincai could ask anything, Xi Eleven spoke up:
“Are you doing well in the Hua estate?” Qinyan had
to reply, “Yes.” Xi Eleven asked, “Do you recognize me?” Qinyan looked up and saw a dark, imposing man;
feeling somewhat intimidated, he said, “We are not acquainted.” Xi Eleven burst into laughter
and stepped close to Qinyan.
Qinyan tried to stand,
but Xi Eleven pressed both hands heavily on his shoulders.
Qinyan lowered his head, his heart racing.
Xi Eleven continued, “You should thank me.
Last summer I came looking for you.
You were clearly home
but wouldn’t come out to see me.
Later, when I argued with your teacher,
you ran out the back door,
and from there you entered the Hua estate.
Didn’t I make that happen for you?
Now that we meet,
you ought to thank me.” Realizing this was Xi Eleven, Qinyan grew even more panicked;
he leaned away, stood up, and hastily retreated.
Xi Eleven laughed loudly, “You’re not that young anymore.
Why are you so bashful,
acting like a little girl?” Fearing Xi Eleven would get rough, Pincai explained, “The rules in the Hua estate are very strict.
He hasn’t seen a stranger in a year,
so naturally, he’s restrained.” Meanwhile, Pan Third was scratching his ears and rubbing his cheeks,
drooling with desire but not daring to act,
while Monk Tang could only harbor wild fantasies.
Pincai asked Qinyan, “How did you manage
to come out today?” Qinyan explained his teacher had died and he was on a month’s leave.
“I came to see you today,
and I also want you
to go with me to…” He was too embarrassed to finish,
but Pincai understood and asked, “Where do you want me
to go with you?” Qinyan finally said, “I want you to go with me to see Lady Mei and Yuxiang.” Pincai smiled, nodded,
and said, “Of course, of course.
We’ll go in a little while.” Seeing so many people present, Qinyan couldn’t urge him.
Xi Eleven was a coarse
and vulgar man who cared only for raw desires,
but he could easily distinguish a beautiful face.
Seeing Qinyan now, he felt he had laid eyes on an unprecedented treasure,
and his heart was deeply stirred.
But considering Qinyan had left the profession
and was now a personal attendant in the Hua estate, Xi knew he couldn't just casually lay hands on him,

to keep him company over wine.
He had to think of a way
to have his sport with him.
As he watched and listened
to them discussing Pincai accompanying Qinyan to the Mei residence, an idea formed.
After scheming for a moment, Xi Eleven stood up
and announced, “I’m leaving.” Thrusting out his belly,
he strode out the door in a few paces.
Pincai and the monk hurried to see him off,
while Pan Third remained seated, his eyes wide and fixed on Qinyan,

that filled Qinyan with an unexpressible rage.
Xi Eleven pulled Pincai into the monk’s room and bowed to him,

I am truly enamored with that person we just saw.
But if I asked him to drink with me,
he would definitely refuse.” Before he could finish, Pincai shook his head rapidly, “He won’t, he won’t!
Absolutely not.” Xi Eleven continued, “And since he’s left the profession,
it’s hard to force him.

Let’s go back in,
and you keep him for a meal,

When you start eating,

Even if I can’t do much,
it will satisfy my craving,
and it shouldn’t count as disrespecting him.
Then I’ll give him some gifts

My dear brother, we are good friends;
if you do this for me,
I’ll be grateful to you for the rest of my life.” Pincai pondered for a moment.
He knew well that Qinyan’s temperament could not be forced,
but he couldn't refuse Xi Eleven’s plea.
So he finally agreed, “Your plan is fine,
but you must not act coarse or rough.
He is not like the others;
say one wrong word and he’ll burst into tears.
I’ve hit that wall many times myself.” Xi Eleven reassured him, “Don’t worry,
I absolutely won’t be rough.
I just want to sit with him;
how could I dare hope for anything more?
I have some dishes being sent over.
Get Pan Third out of there, send Tianxiang and Cuiguan away,
and set the table.
I’ll be right back.” With that,
he hurriedly got into his carriage and left.
Pincai went in and told Pan Third, “The monk wants
to speak with you.” Reluctantly, Pan Third dawdled out,
looking back several times.
Pincai then sent Tianxiang
and Cuiguan away and feigned relief to Qinyan, “Ah, finally some peace and quiet.
I was getting dizzy with all their noise,

Let’s have a nice, quiet chat.
We’ll go after breakfast;
a little delay won't matter.” Qinyan thought it over;
eating at Pincai's place wasn't a problem,

Meanwhile, Xi Eleven climbed into his carriage

Upon arriving at his residence,

He told the servant

and Pan Third entered with the monk.
Pan Third shouted, “Perfect timing!
I’ve crashed a fine feast.” The monk chimed in, “Master Wei hosts a banquet
and doesn't even offer a polite invitation to me.” Pan Third hunched his back, hitched his shoulders,
and hurried forward, saying, “Let me offer a toast.” He snatched Qinyan’s cup,
saying, “This wine is cold,
I’ll drink it for you.” He downed it in one gulp, wiped the rim of the cup around his own lips, poured another half cup,
and offered it with both hands,
shoving it right to Qinyan’s mouth.
Qinyan twisted away, trying to leave,
but with Pan Third on one side
and the monk blocking the other, he was trapped.
Forced, he took the cup.
Pan Third refused to let go,
trying to force it into his mouth.
Qinyan flared up, “I truly cannot drink!
Let go, and I will sip it slowly.” Pincai made Pan Third sit down,
saying, “He really can't.
Just let him drink it slowly.” Pan Third finally let go and sat.
Pincai and Monk Tang pulled up two stools and sat lower down.
Seeing that Pan Third had rubbed the cup on his mouth, Qinyan was immensely disgusted.
He knew now they were in league to humiliate him,
but feared that if he openly turned on them,
he would suffer for it.
Forced to endure this bitter humiliation,
he picked up the cup and,
feigning clumsiness, let it slip.
With a sharp crash,
it shattered to pieces on the floor,
splashing a few drops of wine onto his clothes.
He wiped it with his handkerchief
and said to Pincai, “How clumsy of me.” Pincai, perfectly understanding his intent, replied smoothly, “It matters not at all.”
When they called for a replacement cup, Qinyan flatly refused.
“There is no need, no need at all.
Even if you bring it,
I will not drink.” Xi Eleven insisted, “That won't do.
We won’t press you too hard—
just three cups from each of us.” Pan Third had been eagerly anticipating
that if Qinyan drank from that cup,
it would be as if they had kissed;
seeing it smashed utterly ruined his mood.
He tried to press another toast
but was blocked by Pincai.
Monk Tang, completely lacking in tact, poured half a cup and declared, “Amitabha!
The noble Hua estate is a great patron of our humble temple.
The old lady adorned the Three-World Buddha with gold, the young mistress commissioned the Guanyin who brings sons,
and they donated three years of lamp oil.
Now that the gentlemen of the estate grace us with their presence, this monk has nothing
to offer but borrowed flowers for the Buddha.
Young Master Qin, please drink this cup.” Holding the cup,
he bowed deeply and chanted, “Namo Greatly Compassionate
and Merciful Guanyin Bodhisattva who saves from suffering and distress!” The room erupted in laughter.
Qinyan, both annoyed and amused, found his expression softening slightly.
“I truly cannot drink, please do not force me,” he said.
Monk Tang smiled obsequiously, “My dear Grandpa Qin,
I have just chanted a prayer over it;
there is a Buddha inside this cup.
If you drink it, the front door will welcome a hundred blessings, the back door will gather a thousand auspicious signs,
and I pray you make a great fortune,
taking in a bar of gold every day.” The others roared with laughter,
but Qinyan remained resolute in his refusal.
The monk then rubbed his own face, took off his felt hat,
and said, “Little Master Qin, take a look at my monk’s face—
doesn’t it look less like a human face
and more like a dick-head?” Confronted with this bizarre display, Qinyan couldn't help but let out a laugh.
The monk cheered, “Wonderful, wonderful!
The heavens have opened their eyes.
It seems my dick-head is better than a human head after all.” At these vulgar words, Qinyan’s face immediately darkened again.
The monk persisted, “My ancestral grandpa,
if you don’t drink this cup, this monk will lose all face
and will have to return to secular life tomorrow.” He balanced the wine cup on his bald head, dropped to both knees, rested his hands on Qinyan’s lap,
and continuously chanted prayers, refusing to rise.
The others laughed until their sides ached.
Harassed beyond endurance, Qinyan finally conceded, “Please get up, please get up.
I will take one sip,
and never again.” He took the cup from the monk’s head and took a sip.
Then, fearing someone else would drink his leftovers,
he resolutely downed the rest.
He stood up to leave,
but Xi Eleven pushed him back down,
while the monk threw his arms around Qinyan’s legs
and kowtowed against his knees.
Forced to sit back down
and genuinely angered, Qinyan spoke with stern severity:
“May I ask what exactly you gentlemen intend today?” Pincai hurriedly intervened, “No more drinking, let’s just chat,”
and hauled the monk to his feet.
Qinyan insisted, “I have matters to attend to;
I can stay no longer.” As he tried
to leave again, Xi Eleven blocked his path,
saying, “If you won’t drink, fine.
Just sit for a while.
What's the rush?” Pincai could only urge, “Serve the food quickly,
we have business to attend to.” Qinyan was forced to sit once more,
overflowing with rage yet violently suppressing it.
Xi Eleven thought to himself, “This boy is truly strange;
we just can't fit our joints together.
If it were anyone else,
I would have cursed them out
and forced myself on them by now.
But out of pity for him,
he acts this stubborn.
It’s absolutely hateful.” He reconsidered:
“They always said he was proud,
and it’s clearly true.
If I use force,
it will offend the Hua estate.
Besides, he’s no longer an actor,
so it’s inappropriate to make him serve wine.
I'll give him the gift;
perhaps once he receives the reward,
he'll change his mind.” Pan Third thought, “This boy is even tougher than Su Huifang.
If only I had brought some bank notes
to reward him, perhaps he would fawn over me if he got some money.” Xi Eleven spoke up, “I have something to give you,
and I hope you won't find it too meager.” He pulled a brocade box from his robe, opened the lid,
and revealed a pair of translucent, flawlessly green jadeite bracelets, their brilliance dazzling the eyes.
Pan Third clicked his tongue
and said, “A treasure like this, only you would have.
Where could anyone else find one?
You couldn't find a pair of bracelets like this in the whole city for a thousand strings of cash.” He continued clicking his tongue in awe.
Pincai and the monk stared wide-eyed.
Pincai thought, “What an opening move—
offering such a fine thing right away.
Let's see if he takes it.” Qinyan didn't even look,
keeping his head bowed.
Xi Eleven urged, “Try them on,
I guarantee the size is perfect,”
and told Qinyan to put them on.
Qinyan stood up and said sternly, “I absolutely dare not accept this.
Furthermore, I never wear bracelets.” Without thinking, Qinyan held out his hand to show them—
meaning to demonstrate that he wore no bracelets.
Xi Eleven misinterpreted this as an invitation
to put them on him.
He grabbed Qinyan's arm and yanked him forward;
using too much force, the delicate
and frightened Qinyan lost his balance and tumbled directly into Xi Eleven’s chest.
Xi Eleven simply wrapped his arms around him, unable
to hold back any longer.
He buried his face in Qinyan’s cheek, deeply inhaling his scent, then grasped his hand
and forcefully slid a bracelet onto his wrist.
As Xi Eleven reached for the second, his grip loosened for a moment.
Qinyan tore himself free, tears streaming down his face.
Sobbing uncontrollably, disregarding all consequences,
he cried out, “I don't even know you!
I've left the profession,
yet you still treat me like a gigolo and humiliate me.
I will go back and tell my master,
and then we will speak again!” He bolted out the door.
Reaching the courtyard, he frantically tore the bracelet from his wrist
and hurled it to the ground with all his might.
With a sharp crack,
it shattered into three pieces.
He ran for his life.
Xi Eleven erupted in fury,
cursing, “You ungrateful little bastard!” He lunged forward
to chase and grab him.
Pincai desperately held him back.
Xi Eleven refused to yield,
stomping and raging, screaming curses.
With his great height
and strength, Pincai could hardly restrain him, finally pressing his own head against Xi Eleven’s chest,
pleading repeatedly, “It's all my fault!
If you must hit someone, hit me!
Hit me!” Pan Third and Monk Tang stood by,
adding fuel to the fire and aiding the tyrant.
Blocked by Pincai, and realizing Qinyan had likely run out the temple gates, boarded his carriage,
and gone too far
to catch, Xi Eleven finally gave up.
His eyes bulging with anger, his belly heaving,
he sat down, panting heavily.
Ba Yingguan stood by, suppressing a smile.
He went into the courtyard, gathered the shattered pieces of the bracelet—
three in total—
pieced them together in his palm,
and remarked, “If I spend three mace of silver
to mount these three sections,
I can show off a fashionable three-section inlaid jadeite bracelet.
I wonder if anyone would be willing
to reward me with it?”
He brought them over
and placed them in front of Xi Eleven,
adding, “A bracelet worth a thousand strings is now worth three thousand.” Seeing this, Xi Eleven cursed even more viciously.
Pan Third and Monk Tang kept repeating what a pity it was.
When Xi Eleven returned with only one bracelet left, Juhua would surely raise a massive uproar.
It was precisely the case of a toad longing for swan meat, completely unaware of its own meager lot in life.
Meanwhile, Qinyan boarded his carriage, lowered the curtain,
and wept into his hands all the way back.
Upon arriving home, he stripped off his outer coat, collapsed onto his bed,
and the more he thought about it, the more bitterly he hated himself for foolishly seeking out Pincai
and bringing this disaster upon himself.
He pulled the quilt over his head
and cried for half the day, nearly driven to thoughts of suicide.
