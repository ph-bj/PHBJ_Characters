Precious Mirror for Grading Flowers
Chapter 39
**In wedding-chamber revelry, quick wit yields elegant jokes;
with false hair arranged, white locks become youthful color.**
To return to our story:
Wang Xun had been unable
to keep his appointment at the Yi Garden the other day,
for Sun Lianggong had engaged him
to help arrange the wedding,
and he had spent several days running about on the matter.
The ceremony was set for the tenth day of the second month,
which was not many days off.
At the New Year, Li Xingquan had sent several hundred taels of silver
to Yuanmao, along with a letter to Wang Wenhui asking him to arrange his son's marriage.
Wang Wenhui had no patience for matchmaking
and delegated everything to Wang Xun.
Li Yuanmao then sought out Wei Pingcai
and begged him to take charge of all the arrangements.
Wei Pingcai had been carousing for over a month,
spending, losing at cards,
and frittering away a good deal of money.
As the theft case had still not been resolved,
so his spirits were rather low
and he was at a loss for what to do.
When Li Yuanmao came
to him, Pingcai reflected that he himself had originally served as co-matchmaker with Wang Wenhui,
and that this would give him a convenient pretext for visiting Wenhui again.
So he agreed, then added, "It is time you returned
that bracelet you borrowed from me last year.
Every day you delay, the interest mounts."
Yuanmao said, "Father only sent three hundred taels,
and I'm afraid that's barely enough to cover the wedding.
I've nowhere else to borrow,
so if you press me on that debt,
you'll be the death of me."
Pingcai said, "What a strange thing to say.
How does asking for what's mine put you to death?
What did you tell me last year?
That you'd repay me the moment you heard from home.
Now I find it hard even to ask."
Yuanmao said, "Don't worry.
Once I've moved into the house,
I'll redeem it for you."
Pingcai said, "Once you've moved in,
you'll have even less money."
Yuanmao said, "I may not have money,
but she ought to."
Pingcai said, "She?
Who?"
Yuanmao laughed.
"My wife, of course.
Not just this one debt,
but I have all sorts of plans that depend on her."
Pingcai laughed.
"Your wife is going to be a source of funds?"
Yuanmao said, "What an absurd idea!"
Pingcai said, "But you just said so yourself
that you were going to draw on her."
Yuanmao said, "I only mean that she'll have a dowry,
and once she's married
to me it'll all be mine anyway.
Would I really be unable
to get a single bracelet back?"
Pingcai said, "A man who lives off his wife's purse is no man at all."
Yuanmao said, "What's the harm?
It's not like living off someone's shame."
The two men joked and laughed for a while,
and then Yuanmao left.
The following day Pingcai called on Wang Wenhui,
but Wenhui had gone into the yamen;
Wang Xun received him instead.
They then went together
to pay their respects to Lianggong, exchanged the usual pleasantries,
mentioning that they were currently sojourning away from home with no one to look after things,
and begged for his forbearance.
Lianggong said, "We are joining hands
because we love the same family;
all this formality can be dispensed with.
I don't want to cost the other side a single cash.
Leave everything to me."
He then kept Pingcai to dine.
Three days before the wedding came the exchange of betrothal gifts,
and Pingcai had no choice
but to go and find Yuanmao,
which meant calling on Madam Yan as well.
Having stayed away for several months
and having caused that unpleasant scene the previous year,
he was quite ill at ease.
Madam Yan, for her part, asked nothing about past events
and exchanged only a few bland words with him.
When Pingcai went to see Ziyu, the latter still remembered what Qinyan had said some days earlier
and bore him a quiet grudge and was noticeably less warm toward him than before.
All the arrangements for Yuanmao's affairs were handled by Mei Jin,
who procured hairpins and bracelets, embroidered silks and gowns,
and borrowed from Madam Yan a pearl crown, jade sash, official rank-badge robes with court beads, embroidered dragon skirt,
and other finery, arranging everything in sixteen lacquered boxes
and erecting a ceremonial bower to make a respectable display.
The two matchmakers escorted the gifts over.
The Sun household accepted them
and sent back the return-boxes with gifts of commensurate value,
nothing particularly rare or precious.
On the auspicious day the household of the Mei family saw to all the preparations,
laying on two tables of wine, one for Madam Yan
and one for the matchmakers,
while inviting Ziyu and Yan Zhongqing to keep them company.
Zhongqing said, "Brother Yuan, tonight you truly arrive at the summit of the Jade Mountain."
Wang Xun said, "A life of glory
and splendor all the way to white-haired age."
Ziyu said, "'Still they say the lamplight shows two shadows face
to face, the more you rub your eyes, the blurrier they grow.'
That is a poem about a nearsighted man in the bridal chamber;
it would do nicely for Brother Yuan this evening."
Yuanmao said, "I've always thought nearsightedness had its advantages;
even if the bride is a bit plain,
you can't see her clearly."
Zhongqing said, "But if she should turn out
to be beautiful, wouldn't that be a waste?"
Yuanmao laughed.
"I rather doubt my bride will be a beauty.
I've heard a few things,
so I'll just hope she doesn't resemble the two brothers in looks."
When the auspicious hour arrived,
they all escorted Yuanmao to the Sun residence,
where he was welcomed with drums and music.
The young lady in question was the daughter of Lianggong's first wife;
the current Madam Sun did not dote on her especially,
and so the whole affair was conducted simply.
Among the female guests, only the sister-in-law of Madam Lu, the wife of Lu Zongyuan, had come,
bringing her young daughter.
The groom's family had offered their congratulations in the morning.
It was Ji Lianghuan who was making a show of festivity;
he was privately hoping to rowdy up the bridal chamber,
and had the two useless brothers to receive him.
Yuanmao and his new bride performed the wedding rites before the bridal candles, were escorted to the bridal chamber, sat upon the bed
while wedding verses were scattered over the curtains, drank from the crossed cups,
and then the groom was called back out
to sit at the great banquet table.
Sihui and Siyuan kept them company for a while, until Wang Xun
and Zhongqing proposed moving the party into the bridal chamber to drink more freely.
Everyone filed into the bridal chamber.
Zhongqing said, "Tonight we get
to look at the new bride," and made as if to approach the bed.
By the bed stood two elderly escort-women and two small maidservants.
Siyuan, afraid that Zhongqing might lay eyes on his sister, ran to the bed
and seized hold of the curtain,
managing to push out a few "look" sounds and then,
with a great effort, the two words "not permitted," which made the whole company burst out laughing.
Wang Xun pulled Zhongqing over and made him sit down,
but Siyuan still wasn't reassured
and kept his grip on the curtain door,
so that everyone continued to laugh under their breath.
Sihui said, "The marriage chamber is the foundation of the greatest human bond;
how should outsiders intrude?
We may count ourselves fortunate
that a brother guards the bedside against all insult.
Otherwise, that which is white as snow would be viewed by ten pairs of eyes."
Ziyu heard this and broke into laughter.
Wang Xun said to Zhongqing, "It is as the old text says:
'Arouse not my temper, lest even the dragon bark.'"
Zhongqing smiled faintly.
Li Yuanmao drank his wine with an air of supreme self-satisfaction.
Ji Lianghuan had met Wang Xun
and Zhongqing on several previous occasions,
but this was his first meeting with Ziyu,
whom he eyed with private admiration:
"This young master Mei is a fine-looking man.
Compared to that old Sihui of the Sun family,
it's like comparing the heroic generals Cen Peng
and Ma Wu to some stage ruffian."
Pingcai asked Ji Lianghuan, "I haven't seen your master come out in days, what's he doing at home?"
Lianghuan said, "My master has been indisposed these past couple of days
and hasn't been able to get about."
Pingcai said, "What sort of indisposition?"
Lianghuan said, "I hear it's a boil on his leg;
that's why he hasn't gone out."
The table that evening was divided into three separate clusters:
Ziyu, Zhongqing, and Wang Xun formed one group;
the Sun brothers Sihui and Siyuan another;
and and Pingcai and Lianghuan yet a third,
so the gathering never warmed into real conviviality.
Wang Xun, always amiable by nature, saw how the company was failing
to mix and made a determined effort to draw everyone together.
By this time the seating in the bridal chamber had been taken without any ceremony of deference.
Pingcai and Lianghuan sat on one side;
Zhongqing and Ziyu on another;
Yuanmao sat alone at the head;
and Wang Xun sat below with Sihui.
Someone called Siyuan to come over,
but he would not leave;
he pulled up a stool
and planted himself in front of the bed-curtain.
Ji Lianghuan turned to Ziyu with a grinning smile
and said, "Elder Master Mei rarely goes out in company,
and today is the first time I have had the honor of your acquaintance.
If you're inclined, why not come visit my master one of these days?
He is a most agreeable person."
Ziyu, not knowing whose household was being spoken of, gave a vague reply, then quietly asked Wang Xun,
who answered that it was Xi Shiyi.
A sudden surge of indignation filled Ziyu
and he said nothing more.
Lianghuan then turned to Yuanmao:
"Your future wife, my cousin's cousin on the other side, is a paragon of virtue, Brother Li is truly fortunate
to have arranged such a fine match.
Our mutual kinsman will be posted to a province before long, either Kuizhou in Sichuan or Chenzhou in Hunan.
As the son-in-law of the family,
you'll travel together to his posting.
It won't just be as a secretary,
for I should think the entire household, inside
and out, will have to rely on you."
Zhongqing found this amusing
and could not help asking, "And how exactly are you related to the Sun household?"
Lianghuan said, "The legitimate sister-in-law of Elder Sun's mother-in-law is the elder cousin on my mother's sister's side, through her legitimate niece on the paternal line,
which is the recent connection.
Going back to the older tie, the maternal grandfather of the late mistress of the house was the father-in-law of my father-in-law."
Zhongqing started laughing.
Pingcai said, "That's a connection so light you'd call it eggshell blue."
Lianghuan said, "Though slender,
it still beats having no kin at all.
I've heard a couplet that goes:
'What writings could astonish all within the seas,
when not a single friend or kinsman stands at court.'"
He continued, "But that's a careless thing to say.
All of you gentlemen here have the court full of close friends and worthy associates.
Take the two members of our host's family,
who speak for themselves.
Brother Li, the new son-in-law, is the son of the prefect;
Elder Master Mei is the son of the School Inspector;
Elder Master Wang is the son of the Vice-Minister;
and Elder Master Yan is the Vice-Minister's own son-in-law.
Even Master Wei was once an honored guest in the household of the Lord Hua, practically a deputy to the young master.
You are all men of distinction.
Only this humble nobody here is a mere secretary's secretary,
and I shall have
to beg your generous patronage and assistance in future, your support and kind mention."
This speech fairly turned everyone's stomach.
Zhongqing could not contain himself
and said, "The way Master Ji works out these connections,
we all become kinsmen,
as it merely requires a few more turns in the pedigree."
Lianghuan agreed heartily.
Ziyu smiled.
Yuanmao said, "Not only can I not follow the connections,
I couldn't even hear them properly.
It's like a pumpkin vine tangled up with a bean vine."
Pingcai laughed and said, "And in the middle of it all you run into a man with his legs all wrapped in creepers."
Sihui said, "Kin love is the way of kin, whosoever has blood
and breath cannot but honor his kin.
To love one's kin is humanity;
humanity is humanity itself."
Siyuan, hearing his elder brother open his mouth, immediately set about refuting him.
"Th-th-that s-s-saying," he stuttered, "is n-n-not right.
You s-say whoever has b-b-blood and breath honors their k-k-kin,
meaning everyone is your kin.
I th-think only the c-c-crab has no blood,
while the turtle still has blood,
so I suppose the t-t-turtle is your kinsman too.
I haven't g-g-got so many relations myself."
He burst out in a loud laugh,
and the whole room laughed with him.
Sihui said, "What a fool, not worth speaking to."
Siyuan said, "I'm not a f-f-fool,
for you're the one who's d-d-dead.
'The dead man prizes nothing,
but the humane man prizes his kin.'"
The company laughed all the louder at this.
Zhongqing said, "I have a ready-made joke for the occasion.
One day the Dragon King of the Sea was releasing the various monsters
to be reborn into the world,
and had already freed a great many.
At last a night-patrolling Yaksha dug two creatures out of the mud
and begged the Dragon King to let them go.
The Dragon King looked:
one was a soft-shelled turtle, the other a toad.
The Dragon King said, 'I'm not entirely easy about letting these two go,
so have them bring a guarantor.'
The turtle, hearing this, pointed to the Minister Tortoise standing nearby
and said, 'He is my kinsman,' and then pointed to General Snake and said, 'And he is my relation.'
The Dragon King said, 'If the Minister is your kinsman,
that's quite sufficient, why bring in a General as a relation as well?'
The turtle replied, 'Not merely a relation,
but something of a true kinsman as well.
We turtles cannot father our own young;
we must invite a snake to do it for us.
Though the lineage is tortoise, the seed is serpent,
so the snake counts as both a relation and a kinsman.'
The Dragon King laughed
and said, 'Since you have such distinguished kinsmen and well-connected relations,
I'll let you go.'
He then called the toad forward
and asked, 'And do you have kinsmen and relations?'
The toad said, 'Every man is my kinsman;
every person is my relation.'
The Dragon King said angrily, 'How can that possibly be?'
The toad said, 'Our kind is born from the spare essence
that men leave in the privy,
so we resemble men in our form.
If that doesn't make every man my kinsman
and every person my relation, what does?'
The Dragon King cried out in alarm:
'Release him at once!
Should he linger, he will soon claim kinship with me,
and I have no desire for a toad as a cousin.'"
At this, Pingcai, Wang Xun,
and Ziyu nearly fell off their seats laughing.
Sihui and Lianghuan, knowing themselves
to be the butt of the joke and having no reply, had to swallow their indignation.
Siyuan, however, seeing those two mocked, laughed all the louder.
"A f-f-fine turtle kinsman!"
he cried.
"A f-f-fine toad r-r-relation!"
Wang Xun said, "I have a joke of my own.
There was a blind prostitute,
and when men came to her she couldn't see them,
but she could somehow tell them apart by rank.
One day she received three clients,
and her procuress afterward asked her:
'Miss, can you guess what manner of men your three visitors today were?'
The blind girl said, 'The first was a scholar, the second was a criminal-law secretary,
and the third was a nearsighted simpleton.'
The procuress said, 'How could you tell them apart?'
The blind girl said, 'The first came up very properly
and carefully separated my two parts, then brought them together again, made me face one way and then the other,
and I heard him murmur:
softer here, firmer there;
one deep, one shallow.
He was composing an eight-legged essay.
So I knew he was a scholar.
The second came in, poked about for a while, then examined me closely.
I heard him say:
a scar on the left temple, a fingernail mark on the right breast, an inch
and two-tenths long at a slant.
Presently he said:
both legs stretched straight, both arms spread wide.
Wasn't that the style of a coroner examining a body-in-evidence?
So I knew he was a criminal-law secretary.
The third was the strangest of all.
The moment he came in he peered at my private parts,
and his eyebrows were like a brush,
and they tickled me dreadfully.
He peered, then sniffed;
sniffed, then peered again.
From that I knew he was a nearsighted simpleton.'"
The whole company burst into laughter, even the old escort-women
and the little maids laughed.
And from behind the bed-curtain came a barely perceptible tremor of suppressed merriment:
the bride herself was laughing,
covering her mouth tightly.
Siyuan said, "Th-th-that n-nearsighted one sounds just like Brother Li,
and that c-c-coroner one is just like B-Brother Ji."
Lianghuan laughed and said, "Not at all, not at all.
I am quite certain it wasn't the coroner,
it must have been the one who examines the body after the inquest."
Li Yuanmao said, "I don't believe eyebrows can actually tickle."
Ziyu laughed.
"Your own eyebrows are by no means light."
Sihui said, "Of the three,
I am studying the one who composes eight-legged essays."
Pingcai said, "I have a joke too.
Two brothers, both nearsighted,
yet neither would admit it.
The elder always said the younger's eyes were bad;
the younger always laughed at the elder's poor sight.
Next door there was a local-deity shrine
that had just hung a new plaque.
The two brothers agreed
to go test their eyes, to see which of them could read the plaque most clearly.
The two of them were unfortunately both quite short.
The elder first made the younger crouch down, then climbed onto his shoulders
and had him stand up,
so they could get close to the plaque.
The elder squinted carefully at it, then came down
and said to the younger:
'Let me lift you up to look.'
The younger went up in the same fashion, looked,
and immediately asked his brother:
'What characters did you read?'
The elder said, 'I read it as a pawnshop sign,
so there must be a pawnshop inside.
I clearly made out the characters tu ye dang,
meaning even earth can be pawned.
Let's go home and carry over a couple of loads of earth to pawn.'
The younger laughed:
'Brother, you've misread it.
I read it as shang ta dang,
meaning you've been taken in.
If we haul over the earth
and they won't accept it, haven't we been made fools of?'
The elder thought his brother had a point,
so they both went home.
Another day the two brothers decided
to have another contest of eyesight.
The younger said, 'Brother,
you shouldn't compete with me.
Say you describe my appearance and I describe yours,
but since we can't see ourselves,
we wouldn't believe each other.
Now, my sister-in-law's face I can clearly recall,
and you must know your younger sister-in-law's face well enough.
Let's each describe our wife's appearance
and see whose eyes prove sharper.'
The elder thought this was sound reasoning and nodded,
but when he tried
to recall his wife's face it was a blur and he couldn't bring it to mind.
The younger thought it over at length
and couldn't picture her either;
so each hurried off indoors.
The elder went home and couldn't find his wife anywhere;
after some searching he found her in the mill-room, just sifting flour, her whole face powdered white as snow.
He leaned in close
and peered at her face very carefully, then walked out and sat down to wait for his brother.
The younger ran to his own room, found the door shut, pushed it open.
His wife had just taken off her trousers
to step into the basin for a bath;
seeing her husband enter,
she was embarrassed and grabbed the nearest thing
to cover her lower parts.
There was a fly-whisk within reach,
so she held it up over herself.
The younger, seeing those trailing silky threads, was quite astonished,
and stooped down to examine them closely before going out.
He found his elder brother sitting there grinning,
and asked, 'What's so funny?'
The elder said, 'Brother,
I confess my eyes are worse than yours.
I've been married five years
and only today have I seen her clearly.
Who would have thought
that your sister-in-law is an albino with a whole head of white hair.'
The younger also sighed
and said, 'Brother, sister-in-law's white hair is nothing remarkable.
I just now got a good look at your younger sister-in-law,
and all her intimate hair is white as well.'"
The company let out a roar of laughter.
Then suddenly from behind the curtain the bride's voice rang out in fury:
"Who is that shameless wretch over there talking his filth!
Your mother's an albino!
Your grandmother's an albino!"
Before she had finished,
something flew out from behind the curtain
and smashed two dishes of food.
Everyone was so startled they looked at one another in mute bewilderment.
Siyuan, seeing his sister had started cursing, leapt to his feet and joined in.
The party broke up at once in embarrassment;
everyone scrambled to their feet and hurried out,
going their separate ways as fast as they could.
Yuanmao and Sihui were left with no way
to rein things in and could only see the departing guests to their carriages before going back inside.
As it happened, Pingcai's joke,
though he had aimed it at Li Yuanmao's nearsightedness, had unwittingly landed on a tender nerve where the bride was concerned.
As had been mentioned before,
she was an albino:
her hair, eyebrows, and body hair were all white.
In the north such a person is called a "Heaven's Elder";
in the south, a "white lamb."
On top of this, her temper was fierce and imperious.
She was already thirty years old, famed throughout the surrounding area,
and no one had been willing to take her,
so she had been given
to Yuanmao for want of a better match.
Yuanmao had had no idea.
He came back into the room in high spirits, thinking to himself:
"Just now Pingcai's joke was aimed at my nearsightedness,
and she went and cursed him for it,
and threw something besides.
We haven't even been properly intimate yet,
and already she's defending me like that.
Whoever heard of a wife as devoted as this?"
He hurried the maids
and escort-women out, took off his outer robe, bolted the door, trimmed the candle to a good bright flame, lifted the curtain, drew aside the red veil,
and held the lamp close.
Yuanmao felt his bones go soft
and his sinews melt with delight.
There before him was a face like a silver basin, peach-blossom fair;
a head of black hair like dark clouds;
two curving, vivid black brows;
a small cherry mouth, red as crimson;
a fragrance of powder
and oil, a breath of orchid and musk.
Yuanmao was beyond himself with joy.
He helped her out of her outer garments,
and the new bride slipped first into the bedclothes.
Yuanmao hastily undressed and slid in after her,
and what followed between them was a matter
that need not be described;
suffice to say the bride yielded by degrees
and the first union was duly accomplished.
The new bride handed him a handkerchief.
Yuanmao remembered something about the practice of the "original red"
and used it to wipe himself, then tucked it under the pillow to examine the next morning.
He lay there thinking
that this was truly a very satisfying experience,
and tried to think of something to say,
but nothing came to mind.
He slept for a while, then rose again for a second union.
The whole bed of quilts was newly padded cotton;
moreover it was the tenth of the second month
and the air had already turned warm.
The exertion left Yuanmao steaming like a pot, sweat pouring down his head without stopping.
He came to rest at last,
and in the cooling-down happened to feel the handkerchief;
forgetting he had already used it,
he took it up
and wiped his face and head with it.
Then he pulled back half the quilt
to let the heat escape and drifted off to sleep.
Very early in the morning the new bride rose first
and went to comb her hair in another room.
Yuanmao got up, washed his face, and dressed;
then, quietly slipping the handkerchief into his pocket,
he tried to go
and watch his bride at her morning toilette but was caught by an escort-woman who dragged him out to pay his respects to his father-in-law,
and then there were various senior relations to greet,
and the delay stretched on.
By the time the bride had finished her dressing
and sat in the room in her finest attire with her head bowed, Yuanmao sidled up and managed to squeeze out a few words;
the bride could only smile without replying,
but she stole a glance at him, a broad-featured face with good proportions,
which, though except for the eyes
and brows nothing really remarkable, was far better-looking than her two brothers,
and she felt not displeased.
Then she looked again
and noticed a dusky, mottled darkness on his face, patches and streaks of it, deep and light in different places, patchy and uneven, like a man who had rubbed coal-black onto his face and not wiped it off clean.
She thought to herself:
"He must wash without soap.
Tomorrow I'll tell him to scrub harder."
Yuanmao stared at her with extreme satisfaction, thinking:
"Life is good now.
No more wandering about outside."
He also thought of the handkerchief
and stepped into the outer room where no one could see, drew it from his breast,
and held it stretched out between both hands for inspection.
He stood stock-still.
After a moment he thought:
"I must have taken the wrong one."
He turned back inside, lifted every corner of the bedclothes but found nothing;
searched under the quilt
and under the pillow and found nothing either.
A maidservant nearby asked, "Son-in-law, what are you looking for?
Let me help."
Yuanmao looked around and saw the room full of maids and old serving-women;
there was no way he could say.
He had to go out again to a place with no one about,
and hold the handkerchief up for close examination.
He saw a series of streaks
that were not quite lacquer, not quite oil,
and not quite ink.
He truly could not guess what it was.
He sniffed it:
a faint oily fragrance,
with a trace of perspiration;
he gave a sudden snort of laughter
and thought, "No wonder her younger brother is full of learned quotations, even inside his sister's parts such dark fluid abounds."
Then he reconsidered:
"That's impossible."
He turned the handkerchief over
and examined it again very closely,
and at one spot, at the edge of the black oil, there seeped a faint reddish tinge, like diluted rouge-water, odorless.
He thought it over carefully for quite some time,
and was suddenly struck by enlightenment.
"That's it, that's it!
This faint red shadow is undoubtedly the original red.
As for all the black, someone must have been playing a prank yesterday
and smeared some dark oil on my hat or onto my head,
and with the sweating I wiped my head by mistake with this cloth."
He tucked it back into his sleeve.
He went in and ate the morning-after wedding feast,
and in the warmth of new marriage the two were
to each other as brother and sister.
After a few days,
when Yuanmao was making the rounds of thanks to the matchmakers
and paying calls on well-wishers, Wang Xun and Zhongqing asked him how he found his new wife.
He said nothing about anything else,
but kept talking about hair, one person said it was white silk-fine hair, another said it was silver thread and crane feathers,
and no one could make out what he meant.
People saw how delighted he was and were thoroughly puzzled.
Then Yuanmao recalled the incident
when Pingcai had been cursed, the joke had touched on white hair and white intimate hair,
and that was what had made the bride so furious.
His suspicions began to stir.
And furthermore:
every day his own face was becoming stained with a dark oiliness,
and there was the strange matter of the handkerchief;
and she rose very early
and combed her hair in a separate room, always behind a closed door.
What did all that signify?
He could not resolve his doubts
and dared not ask directly.
One day he came into the room
and saw her in high spirits, bedecked in pearls and jade, her hair plainly full and black, blacker than lacquer.
He tried to get close enough to smell her head,
but she pushed him away.
He suddenly thought:
perhaps her hair was naturally black
while her intimate hair was white,
and that was what people's remarks had been about.
He thought again:
even if she had a few white intimate hairs, how would outsiders know?
But if that were truly the case, it wouldn't do.
Then he thought:
I must not entertain such ideas.
Tonight I'll pull one out and look at it tomorrow,
which will tell me all I need to know.
He waited impatiently for dusk,
and when they had gone to bed,
he felt about for
that precious thing, a lush, woolly patch of growth,
but he could not bring himself to pull any,
for fear of causing her pain.
So he went ahead with his usual carrying-on,
and afterward, feeling about with his hand in hopes
that something might have naturally come loose and would put his mind at rest,
he suddenly found that he did indeed have one.
He was overjoyed;
he pinched it tight between two fingers, reached out one hand
to grope for a piece of paper under the mat, wrapped it up carefully,
and then, having nowhere safe
to put it, tucked it into the fold of his braid at the very top of his head for fear she might find it.
Madam Sun could not imagine what he had been doing.
Yuanmao had spent half the night at his maneuvers,
and in the morning slept in.
Madam Sun had long since finished combing her hair.
When Yuanmao finally got up and was washing his face,
he remembered the paper packet in his braid, hurriedly put on his hat, ran into the outer room, opened it up
and found a single jet-black strand.
Yuanmao was delighted.
"I've been suspecting for nothing these several days,
for those sharp-tongued gossips were just talking nonsense."
And his mind was at rest.
What a fool that Yuanmao was,
spending half the night on his scheme
and obtaining a single hair,
which all along was one of his own
that had rubbed off onto his hand.
He attributed it to his wife's
and considered his suspicions entirely dissolved.
About half a month passed,
and then one day the truth came out.
While Madam Sun was combing her hair she felt a chill
and called a maidservant to fetch her a sleeveless jacket from the other room.
Yuanmao was already up,
and seeing the maid carrying clothes into that other room,
he followed along without enough time for the door to be closed.
There, seated, was a figure dressed in a scarlet close-fitting garment,
with a cascade of silver-silk hair over three feet of it hanging loose,
and two eyebrows of a pale gold color.
Li Yuanmao's heart gave a tremendous lurch;
he thought he had encountered a ghost
and almost turned and fled.
But then he thought:
the clothes are clearly hers;
could she really be some white apparition?
He hurried forward, and Madam Sun herself was so startled
that before she could conceal anything her face had flushed scarlet.
Li Yuanmao looked closely,
and a rush of indignation rose straight to his head.
"So that's how it is!"
he cried.
"My luck has well and truly run out,
for I've married a goblin.
This is the Ageless Crone from Journey to the West.
She wants a husband too.
Laughable, utterly laughable!"
Madam Sun heard this,
and in a torrent of shame
and fury burst into tears and shouted:
"We've treated you so well,
I am a young lady of a good family,
and we opened our doors
to receive you as a son-in-law, a man lacking rank or gleaming prospects,
yet you remain unsatisfied and leap to find fault!
So what if my hair is a bit white?
In every other respect, am I not your match?
Or are you too fine for a mere cross-eyed man to aspire to?
If you find fault with me,
you can just divorce me!"
She flew into a rage,
and pots and ornaments flew about crashing on the floor.
Li Yuanmao retreated to the other room muttering and cursing,
and the two of them kept it up the whole morning.
As it happened, Madam Sun had for some time been mixing sesame oil with lampblack
and then adding persimmon lacquer.
She would first comb her hair out neatly
and then carefully paint on the mixture,
and the effect was sleeker and glossier than natural hair.
The process required washing once a day;
otherwise the hair became impossible
to comb and the lacquer would not go on again.
For washing she used a bucket of soapberry water with borax
and alum to clean it thoroughly, then dried it to half-dampness before combing and setting,
which took the better part of an hour.
That morning she had been running a little late,
and so the truth came to light.
Yuanmao stormed out in a fury
and went to stay at Wei Pingcai's lodgings for a couple of days.
Pingcai asked him what had happened,
and Yuanmao had no choice
but to tell him the whole story.
Pingcai was immediately enlightened:
he now understood how his joke the other night had hit exactly on the mark.
Madam Sun, seeing her husband fail
to return for two or three days, grew anxious and reported the matter to her parents.
Lianggong was furious;
Madam Lu too was displeased,
and she sent someone to the Mei household to inquire,
but he was not there.
After searching everywhere, he was found at Pingcai's.
Yuanmao still refused to go back;
only after Pingcai pressed him earnestly did he agree
to return with the messenger,
though he still would not go into the inner chambers
and sat in the study chatting idly with Sihui instead.
When Lianggong returned that evening he gave Yuanmao a talking-to,
and Madam Lu reproved him as well,
though at heart she felt they had somewhat wronged Yuanmao,
and between scolding and coaxing persuaded him to go in.
Yuanmao had no choice but to comply,
but the memory of
that apparition was still with him and he could not bring himself to be cheerful.
Madam Sun, seeing him come in, wanted him
to speak first and apologize,
and sat without stirring.
In the lamplight Yuanmao looked again
and saw her distinctly black and white, a real beauty,
and his heart softened a little.
He spoke first.
Madam Sun slowly replied.
Yuanmao sat with head bowed and eyes shut,
pondering for a while,
and then suddenly a brilliant idea struck him;
he jumped up and grinned at her.
Madam Sun, seeing him beginning
to come around, made a show of playing hard to get,
she regarded him coolly and said nothing,
studying her own reflection in the mirror.
Yuanmao could not help saying:
"Why not just tell me the truth?
What's the point of hiding it from me?
Now that we're married it's not as
if we can undo it.
I see you have to lacquer your hair every day,
which is a great deal of trouble and very inconvenient,
and every day it rubs off on my face
and people laugh at me.
I've thought of a solution that's simpler, more attractive,
and won't leave oil on my face.
Would you care to hear it?"
Madam Sun, curious about what scheme he had devised, asked, "And what would that be?"
Yuanmao said, "Like a young female role in the opera, put on a hairnet and tie it up.
Who would ever see through that silver hair underneath?
Or better yet, just shave it off entirely,
which would be cleanest of all."
Madam Sun said, "Shaving it off won't do.
A hairnet, as you suggest, would be convenient enough.
And I am tired of all that lacquering.
Tomorrow morning I'll send someone to buy one."
Yuanmao said, "You ought
to scrape your face with a razor every day too, otherwise a few pale fine hairs will come out.
If you shave your down and wear the hairnet,
you could easily pass for a great beauty."
Madam Sun was pleased by this,
and her expression softened into smiles.
She said, "It's still early,
so why not send someone
to buy it now so it's ready for tomorrow morning?"
Yuanmao said, "Buy it now
and we can use it tonight,
saving my face from being smeared again."
Madam Sun called a maidservant
to go tell the steward to buy one hairnet, one hairpiece,
and one swallow-tail ornament,
and to bring them back promptly.
In short order everything was purchased.
Madam Sun was overjoyed,
and at once she put a pot of soapberry water on
to boil, then scrubbed the lacquered oil from her hair until the washwater ran dark as the blue vats in a dyer's shop.
Not even waiting for it to dry,
she let Yuanmao blot it with a cloth,
blotting and then blotting again.
Yuanmao then suggested she trim
and shave back the temple-hair and the hairline all around so nothing would show.
Madam Sun had an old serving-woman take a razor
and shave all around the edges,
and shaved off the eyebrows entirely as well;
the fine down on her face was shaved smooth too.
Then the hairnet was fitted on, the real hair coiled once around the base, the false hairpiece pinned in place with a clasp, the swallow-tail ornament tied in, a jade kingfisher ornament set on the forehead,
and eyebrows painted on,
and she truly became several degrees more handsome.
By evening she looked for all the world like the Tipsy Lady Yang.
Madam Sun had two large candles lit
and used two mirrors, one in front and one behind, to admire herself from all angles,
and she found the effect breathtaking.
Yuanmao, looking at her, felt his heart blossom with delight;
he stepped close, smelled the top of her head, rubbed his cheek against her face,
finding only the faintest trace of oil, nothing like before.
In high good spirits he dismissed the maids, took her by the hand to bed,
and under the quilt together they bloomed like the night-opening flower.
Then Yuanmao suddenly recalled the hair-pulling episode of the other night
and asked Madam Sun, "I've heard that those with albinism have white hair all over their bodies,
so why is your intimate hair dark?"
Madam Sun said, "It isn't exactly dark."
Yuanmao said, "Dearest, let me have a look."
Madam Sun refused.
Yuanmao said, "Can you still be keeping things from me?
After all I've done
to fix things for you, what is there to hide?"
Madam Sun said nothing.
Yuanmao climbed naked out of bed, took up the candle, folded back the quilt.
Madam Sun tried to shield herself,
but Yuanmao could already see
that her body was truly white as frost and snow,
which he found quite charming.
When he came to that most intimate area,
it was as if he had ridden upon a silver-maned horse,
and indeed it bore out Pingcai's joke,
looking for all the world like a fly-whisk held over herself.
Yuanmao could not suppress a laugh,
and gave her a pinch.
Madam Sun said, "What do you think you're doing?
Aren't you yourself a nearsighted man?
Why not have a good sniff as well?"
Yuanmao, moved by what he had seen, set down the candle
and climbed back into bed.
What followed between them need not be told.
