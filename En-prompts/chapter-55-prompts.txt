Precious Mirror for Grading Flowers
Chapter 55
**At Phoenix Mountain they pay respects to a literary altar;
beside Emerald Nest they seek an old tomb.**
After departing from the capital, Qinxian was consumed by ceaseless longing,
his tears falling like rain along the way.
Traveling through eighteen overland relay stations,
they finally reached Wangjiaying, crossed the rolling Yellow River,
and took up lodgings at a riverside inn in Qingjiangpu.
There, they hired a riverboat, fashioned their flags and official boards,
and paused for three days' rest.
Though Qu Daosheng had old acquaintances in both the grain-transport and salt-monopoly offices,
he chose not to disturb them and paid no visits.
Daosheng had a longtime attendant named Liu Xi, an honest
and loyal man in his forties who had served him for half a decade.
Having previously worked under the eminent Hou Shiweng in Jiangning,
Liu Xi was remarkably skilled at cooking,
and Daosheng now assigned him to look after Qinxian.
This Liu Xi proved as fussy
and attentive as an old matron,
always asking if Qinxian was hungry when it was time to eat,
or if he was cold when the wind blew.
Qinxian found great comfort in his care.
Setting sail once more,
they arrived in Yangzhou in three days.
Fearing that local merchants would incessantly pester him for poems and paintings,
or drag him to endless banquets
and musical performances, Daosheng decided not to go ashore.
However, they were obliged to wait for customs clearance,
leaving them no choice but to linger at the checkpoint.
Early that morning, Daosheng thought of Pingshan Hall
and decided to take Qinxian there for a leisurely stroll.
After taking their breakfast aboard the ship,
he had Liu Xi hire a small skiff,
which carried them from the Little South Gate along the winding river toward the west.
Fortunately, the day was refreshingly cool, the sky veiled by clouds without a glaring sun.
Qinxian gazed upon the winding stretch of jade-green water,
where duckweed leaves drifted in uneven patches.
A gentle breeze swept from both banks, lightly rippling his sheer gauze robe,
bringing him a quiet delight.
After traveling for several miles,
they came upon a garden.
Its enclosing walls had half crumbled, its pavilions and towers leaned precariously,
and the air was filled with the cawing of crows in ancient trees
and the shrill cries of cicadas in the dense shade.
It was exactly as the old poem goes:
"Where red towers and carved windows once echoed with song, now brambles
and broken tiles spread across the wasteland."
Daosheng sighed, "This is the Lesser Rainbow Garden.
In days past, I gathered here
with famous scholars for the spring purification festival at Rainbow Bridge.
I saw with my own eyes the magnificent temples, the jade-like grass,
and the fairy blossoms,
yet now it has been reduced to this state.
It truly moves one to sigh.
Further ahead lies the Greater Rainbow Garden,
which is much the same, perhaps only slightly better."
Qinxian remarked, "If we speak of this garden's former glory,
I imagine it must have been comparable to Yiyuan."
Daosheng replied, "Originally, it could not match Yiyuan.
But if these two gardens were combined
and connected to Pingshan Hall,
they might rival it."
After a while, they saw scattered spirit stones covering the ground.
A few elegant rockeries still stood intact,
while the rest had collapsed
and tumbled into heaps of ruin.
They passed several pavilions where corners had caved in,
leaving only a few bare pillars standing upright—
a desolate and mournful sight.
Crossing a stone bridge bearing the carved name "Rainbow
Bridge," they spotted another garden on the opposite bank.
Though slightly better preserved
and not entirely ruined, its winding corridors and secluded chambers—
once surely gleaming with vibrant green and vermilion—
had now faded to a single, pale and deathly gray.
From the high ground within the garden,
they could see the upper windows of a tower.
Of the original twelve panels, only seven or eight remained,
and half of those hung crookedly on their hinges.
Only the trees still thrived,
growing so dense they blocked out the sky,
while the cicadas hissed so loudly they grated on the ears.
It took a long time to finally pass this stretch.
They then crossed another stone bridge, open on three sides,
bearing the complete and elegant name "Lotus Bridge."
Here, the river widened slightly,
and from the shade of green willows
on both banks emerged the red walls of a Buddhist temple,
looking exactly like a painting.
A few wine-shop banners fluttered in the breeze,
leading down winding paths to secluded spots.
Qinxian felt he could never take in enough of the beautiful scenery.
Suddenly, they saw two pleasure boats approaching from ahead.
When Qinxian looked up,
he spotted two bare-backed men with large, protruding bellies.
On one of the boats sat two women, thickly powdered
and elaborately dressed in gaudy colors.
To his surprise, Qinxian noticed his foster father quickly lower his head
to look at the water,
using his fan to shield his face,
though he did not understand why.
Qinxian then saw the two women staring fixedly at him,
one of them smiling radiantly in his direction.
As the two boats scraped closely past theirs, the women stared even more intently,
looking as though they wanted to speak to him.
Feeling deeply embarrassed, Qinxian lowered his head and looked away.
Just as the boat glided past,
something suddenly struck Qinxian's body.
Startled, he watched it fall onto the deck boards
and saw it was a square of white silk wrapped around some fruit.
Daosheng chuckled, picked it up,
and untied it, revealing loquats, bayberries, water chestnuts, lotus roots, peaches, and pears.
Still not realizing where it had come from,
Qinxian asked Daosheng where the bundle had dropped from.
Daosheng replied, "It was tossed over
to you from that boat.
It seems you've become Pan An, receiving thrown fruit!"
Only then did Qinxian understand
that the two women had given it to him,
and his face instantly flushed red.
Daosheng said, "There's no need to mind them.
Since they sent it,
it is a gesture of goodwill;
we might as well enjoy it."
He took a loquat and ate it himself,
though Qinxian adamantly refused to have any.
Daosheng explained, "Those two men just now were employees
of a salt merchant family who recognize me.
I was afraid that if they saw me,
they would go back and gossip,
and then come to pester me.
Fortunately, they didn't spot me."
When the boat reached a landing, Daosheng took Qinxian ashore to explore.
Qinxian saw it was a temple.
Entering the mountain gate,
they found a small garden with balustrades
and a pavilion, centered around a three-room hall bearing the plaque 'Pinghu Cottage.'
After walking around briefly and finding it of little interest,
they returned to the boat.
Upon reaching Pingshan Hall, the scenery vastly improved.
At the foot of the mountain,
a path lined with green pines offered a rustling, pure breeze
that soaked a cool chill into their garments.
Step by stone step,
they climbed to the mountain gate
and went in to pay their respects.
The main hall was towering and majestic,
with winding corridors weaving through layers of courtyards, each higher than the last.
Everywhere there were layered spirit stones
and lush, heavy foliage, alongside countless exquisite chambers and pearl-adorned doors.
Though it bore the marks of age,
it did not appear desolate.
Passing the Imperial Library Tower,
they finally stepped into Pingshan Hall,
where they saw the authentic calligraphy of Ouyang Xiu.
A monk emerged, and upon recognizing Daosheng,
he hurried forward with a beaming smile and bowed.
"When did the honorable Lord Qu arrive?
This poor monk has been looking forward to this till his eyes nearly wore out!"
Seeing the monk—
a fair-skinned man in his fifties with high cheekbones
and a three-inch black beard—
Daosheng remembered him as a guest-prefect
but had forgotten his name.
He clasped his hands in greeting
and replied, "I just arrived.
We are currently waiting
to pass through customs and will set sail tonight."
The monk protested, "Why the rush?
You must naturally linger for a few days."
His eyes rolled slickly as they darted over Qinxian's face several times.
Seeing Qinxian dressed in a white gauze robe with small black boots,
he guessed this was Daosheng's young master.
The monk brought his hands together in a respectful greeting,
and Qinxian returned the bow.
The monk hastily offered them seats, inquired about Daosheng's destination,
and ordered tea to be served.
Smiling broadly at Qinxian,
he said, "The young master is visiting for the first time,
so you must not know we have a 'Second Spring' here.
Please, taste the water of this Second Spring."
He then instructed the servants
to quickly brew Longjing tea with the spring water,
adding, "Tomorrow when you reach Zhenjiang,
you will taste the First Spring,
but it cannot surpass this one."
Daosheng remarked, "Drawing water from
that First Spring is truly exhausting.
Often, what is brought up does not seem to taste very good."
The monk explained, "You have
to measure the rope to the exact length.
During high tide, twenty-four feet and five inches is needed;
during low tide, sixteen feet is enough.
Only when you reach the precise depth should you pull up the bucket's lid.
"If you pull the lid open before reaching the exact spot where the spring emerges,
the river water will flood in,
and the spring water won't be able to enter.
"That is why what people pull up often tastes poor—
they simply didn't measure the depth correctly."
Daosheng realized, "Ah, so that's it.
I only knew the First Spring was at the foot of Jinshan,
but I never knew the exact measurements for high and low tides.
No wonder what we pulled up was still just river water;
we completely wasted the First Spring."
The monk offered, "It's easy.
Tomorrow we can row across the river
and fetch some for you;
the bucket is already prepared."
Daosheng replied, "Let it be.
Tasting this Second Spring is certainly no inferior to the First."
The monk then said, "Lord Qu,
we missed you to death!
Last year you said you would return within the third month.
In the fourth month, Lord Bao the Seventh
and Lord Yu the Third were here admiring the peonies and poppies,
and they spoke of you.
They mentioned that for the Salt Commissioner's birthday on the fifteenth of the third month,
aside from the usual gifts from the salt monopoly,
they needed to make a ceremonial screen.
For a time, they couldn't find a single capable writer in all of Yangzhou city.
In truth, there are many Hanlin scholars and Jinshi here,
but the merchants said their work wasn't good enough.
They had no choice
but to go to Jiangning to find Lord Hou Shiweng,
presenting him with twelve kinds of gifts
and six hundred taels of silver,
and then they invited the great Lord Wang Mengshan
to write the calligraphy for another three hundred taels.
They said that if you, venerable sir, had been here,
it would have taken only a single table of wine.
You would have done it quickly and flawlessly,
composing and writing it all within two days,
saving them so much trouble.
Waiting in vain for you forced them
to scramble east and west looking for people,
causing them no end of difficulty."
Daosheng laughed and said, "It doesn't matter
if those merchants spend a few extra coins."
The monk then turned
to Qinxian, "Young master, there is another garden over there.
Please, go and take a stroll."
Qinxian wanted to explore the garden
but did not dare to say so,
looking instead at Daosheng.
Daosheng agreed, "Very well,
we might as well take a look."
The monk had the gates opened and led them inside.
Unfortunately, it was summer.
Though there was no sun today, the air remained sweltering,
and the dense, tangled trees formed a canopy that stifled the breeze.
After walking through the various spots, the monk pointed to a well,
claiming it was the Second Spring.
Pingshan Hall was a famous scenic spot in Jiangnan,
and every passing traveler made a point to visit.
In his life, the monk had seen thousands of men and women,
yet he had never encountered beauty like Qinxian's.
It was not necessarily that he harbored wicked intentions,
but those slick, oily eyes set upon a shaved head made him seem exceptionally unsavory.
He constantly hovered around them,
pushing forward and squeezing close,
displaying an excessive eagerness.
Under the pretext of pointing out the scenery,
whenever they encountered a difficult stretch of stone path,
he would reach out to support and guide him,
managing to grasp Qinxian's delicate hand several times.
Qinxian hated him deeply in his heart,
and a look of anger began
to show on his face.
He turned to Daosheng and said, "Let us go back.
I fear it is going to rain."
The monk protested, "It does not matter.
If it rains and makes the return difficult,
our humble temple has plenty of rooms where you can rest."
Daosheng also feared the rain,
and hearing the faint rumble of thunder,
he too decided it was time to return.
The monk still tried to urge them to stay,
but Daosheng was resolved to leave.
At the garden gate, Qinxian saw the gatekeepers asking Liu Xi for money.
Liu Xi gave them one hundred large cash,
but they still complained it was too little.
The monk shouted them down
and escorted the guests straight out of the mountain gate.
Daosheng and Qinxian boarded their skiff and rowed back.
There were many pleasure boats coming and going,
and the round trip had taken the better part of the day.
By the time they returned, their main ship had already passed through customs.
Once Daosheng and Qinxian were aboard, the crew struck the gong three times,
pulled up the gangplank, and set sail.
Catching a light breeze,
they reached Guazhou, where they had
to pass through customs once again.
As Guazhou had no spots worth exploring
and Daosheng had no old friends there,
they were delayed for another half-day.
After clearing customs, they traveled for a day
and a half to reach Jiangning,
where they dropped anchor at the Longjiang checkpoint.
Daosheng thought of Hou Shiweng
and decided to linger here for a few days to visit him.
Early the next morning,
taking Qinxian and Liu Xi along,
he hired a shaded skiff.
They rowed from the city moat to the Dry West Gate,
entered the city, hired sedan chairs,
and proceeded to Phoenix Mountain to call upon Hou Shiweng.
This Hou Shiweng was a veritable immortal of the mortal realm, now seventy-four years of age.
Having entered the Hanlin Academy at twenty,
he was by now a senior elder spanning twenty-three examination cohorts,
with none of his same-year peers left in the imperial court.
He had retired from office in his thirties
and spent the next three decades roaming the realm.
At Phoenix Mountain, he built a garden of exquisite elegance.
He read voraciously, loved composing poetry
and prose with the ambition of passing his work down through the ages,
and was hailed in his time as the foremost talent under heaven.
Yet despite being past seventy,
he retained a romantic and amorous nature;
his halls were filled with beautiful concubines in vivid array.
Those who came to him bearing texts
to seek his guidance were not only brilliant young scholars
but also many talented beauties of the boudoir.
Shiweng was playful and witty,
mastering every form of jest.
While his prose was truly his greatest achievement,
the world ironically prized his poetic reputation far more;
anyone receiving a single word
of his praise considered it as glorious as wearing an imperial brocade.
In discussing poetry, Shiweng focused entirely on innate spiritual resonance.
Even if women or children happened
to produce a fine line or two,
he would praise them to the skies.
Thus, his contemporaries called him the 'Poetry Buddha,'
implying his gates were broad enough to accept all.
However, critics who favored strict formal rules looked down on him for this very reason.
Upon arriving at the garden, Daosheng had Liu Xi send in his calling card first.
Qinxian noticed that the garden was enclosed entirely by fences of woven hibiscus
and bamboo, interspersed with various trees.
Looking inside, he saw scattered pavilions and courtyards,
exuding a clear and open atmosphere, entirely devoid of solid enclosing walls.
Shortly after, Liu Xi emerged with a servant who invited them
to have their sedan chairs carried straight in.
Looking through the sedan window, Qinxian saw a landscape
that rose and fell, twisting and turning:
paths lined with tall pines, forests of slender bamboo, waterfalls cascading like curtains,
and clear springs winding like ribbons.
Two or three pavilions were linked together,
while trees so thick it would take a dozen men
to encircle them intertwined their branches.
Pure white cranes stood elegantly within balustrades,
while deer bleated softly inside their enclosures.
Reaching a certain spot,
they alighted from their chairs and walked forward.
From beside the pines
and rocks, an old man came out to welcome them.
With an ethereal bearing
that seemed ready to pierce the clouds,
he was unconcerned with his dress,
wearing neither formal robe nor proper shoes.
Stepping forward, he firmly grasped Daosheng's hand, then glanced at Qinxian
and grasped his hand as well,
leading them both into a three-room study.
After exchanging formal greetings, Daosheng instructed Qinxian to bow.
Shiweng asked, "What relation is this young gentleman to you?"
Daosheng replied, "He is my son."
Shiweng burst into a roaring laugh.
"A man with an empty belly trying
to pass as a great scholar, a beggar pretending to be a rich man,
a remarried widow trying to act like a blushing bride!
You're a tailless monkey—
how could you suddenly have a son?
Did you spit this jade-like boy out of your mouth?"
Daosheng chuckled, "Nonsense.
He is my adopted son."
Shiweng laughed again, "So he's an adopted son!"
Holding Qinxian's gaze with fixed eyes,
he said, "Please rise, please rise.
What a beautiful jade boy!
What sort of old crone could give birth
to such a precious child?
Rare indeed, truly rare."
The two old friends caught up on their past separation, then launched into lofty debate.
Standing to the side, Qinxian listened to Hou Shiweng,
whose voice boomed like a great bell.
With bright, piercing triangular eyes
and a sparse white mustache, Shiweng's tongue lashed out vertically and horizontally,
his words flowing like a rushing river.
Qinxian heard him challenge Daosheng with difficult classical passages,
asking how a specific line in the Classics should be interpreted,
how a point in the Histories should be resolved, how a philosopher's text should be read,
and how a passage in the *Book of Han* should be understood.
Yet Daosheng answered every single one clearly,
causing Shiweng to nod continuously.
Later, Daosheng also quizzed him on several texts,
and Shiweng answered them all with absolute clarity.
The two men debated back
and forth for a while before clapping their hands and bursting into hearty laughter.
Shiweng then ordered his servants to prepare a meal.
Though Shiweng did not drink wine himself,
he brought some out to accompany Daosheng.
Qinxian declined the wine,
while Daosheng, a fine drinker, poured for himself.
Shiweng said, "I advise you, there's no need
to pursue officialdom any longer.
Though you've obtained the post of Vice-Prefect,
it is ultimately too small a stage for your talents.
You have plenty of close friends;
if you cast aside this sixth-rank prospect, would you really go hungry?"
Daosheng sighed, "It isn't
that I'm an old horse clinging to the stable,
but I have my difficulties.
You know that for decades, not only have I remained exactly as I was,
I have ended up utterly alone,
without even a patch of land to stand on.
If I possessed your immortal talent and deep blessings,
I would naturally enjoy them in peace.
But my fate is ruled by the Scorpion star—
there is nothing to be done."
Shiweng replied, "With your literary prowess,
you can stand proud.
Why must you clutch an official tablet
and drift in the sea of bureaucracy?
To my mind, it is entirely unnecessary.
Whether serving on a patron's staff or roaming famous mountains, wouldn't you be free and unrestrained?"
Daosheng answered, "Have you not read Tang Linchuan's reply to Mei Guozhen?
'To serve a lord shoulder-to-shoulder with you all in my youth was fine,
but to be a dependent guest in my old age is something I cannot do.'
Having failed to establish myself at court in my youth,
and now relegated to a low rank in my old age,
how can I bear the thought of relying on others?
Moreover, my literary powers have waned, my withered thoughts are hard
to wring out, my empty fame is limited,
while powerful enemies are constantly many.
If Yang Youji, the legendary archer, were
to miss a single shot, the critics would swarm.
I fear this deeply.
From now on, I will burn my brushes
and inkstones, hide my blade away,
and harbor no thoughts of posthumous glory.
To drift aimlessly as a minor official until the end
of my days will be enough for me."
Shiweng also sighed deeply a few times, then asked, "Are Wang Zhifu
and Liu Jingzhi doing well?"
Daosheng replied, "Very well!
I have seen the descendants of their circle,
and every one of them is of outstanding character."
Shiweng asked, "All of them?"
Daosheng said, "The foremost is Mei Tie'an's son, named Ziyu, styled Yuxiang.
He is truly a phoenix among men.
If he sits for the Erudite Scholar exam this year,
he is certain to pass."
Shiweng laughed, "The Erudite Scholar exam is nothing special.
Anyone who thoroughly memorizes the three volumes of the *Shilei Fu* can pass it."
Daosheng teased, "That is only
because you, old sir, failed to pass it yourself,
and now your old grievances are showing."
Shiweng retorted, "Not at all,
I speak from public consensus.
But what makes Mei Tie'an's son so exceptional?"
Daosheng explained, "First, his appearance is outstanding, gentle and warm as jade,
and his learning in all fields is complete."
Shiweng smiled, "If his appearance is outstanding, his mind must naturally be brilliant;
that is a given.
Are there any others who are exceptional?"
Daosheng listed the young scholars one by one.
Shiweng remarked, "The Tian boy who took top honors this year—
his father could be considered my disciple.
He became a Jinshi but is now gone.
His uncle Zhang Tongsun is also a good friend of mine.
Young Master Xu goes without saying;
Minister Xiaoshan's goodness naturally blesses his descendants."
Daosheng then recited the matched couplets composed by the various scholars at Yiyuan.
Shiweng praised several of them,
saying, "I never expected a group of children
to be capable of such work.
Truly, heroes emerge from the youth.
We old men had better retreat a safe distance!"
Daosheng then recited the preface he had written.
Shiweng praised it a few times,
saying, "It is truly a piece of Tang-style prose;
the parallel prose of the Song writers lacks such rigor.
However, there are two lines within it
that require a bit more deliberation."
Daosheng asked, "Please instruct me, which two lines?"
Shiweng explained, "'Glazed inkstone case, kingfisher brush-rest' is taken from the *Yutai Xinyong* preface.
But combining one dense phrase with one light phrase
to form a parallel structure places it in the ancient style.
His original reads:
'Glazed inkstone case, kept by his side all day;
kingfisher brush-rest, never leaving his hand.'
Such lines are fine as they are.
But when you changed it
to 'Placing the star-eyed Duan inkstone,
resting the mouse-whisker Xiang brush,' the tone slips into contemporary convention.
Although the piece contains beautiful phrasing,
it carries an ancient elegance.
Only these two phrases feel slightly modern
and fail to match the tone of the whole piece.
If you simply remove the four specific words 'star-eyed'
and 'mouse-whisker,' it can be saved.
'Glazed inkstone case, often holding Duan stone;
kingfisher brush-rest, frequently cradling Xiang bamboo.'
That becomes the syntax of the Six Dynasties.
What do you think, my friend?"
Daosheng declared, "You are truly a 'one-character teacher';
how could I not bow in admiration!"
After drinking a few more cups of wine, Daosheng added,
"Elder brother, your poetic power has grown ever more unrestrained recently,
like the nine tributaries of the Xunyang River overflowing their banks;
I have long admired it.
However, it seems inappropriate that you include your playful,
jesting verses in your complete works.
Why not divide them into an inner and an outer collection?"
Shiweng replied, "Playful verses capture a great deal of natural charm.
The *Book of Songs* did not discard the erotic poems of 'Sangzhong' and 'Zhenwei'.
Why didn't the Sage compile those into a separate collection
when he edited the *Odes*?"
Daosheng argued, "That was
to preserve the local folk customs of the states
and to serve as a moral warning to curb the idle passions of readers.
If you, elder brother, use yourself as the example,
it seems as though you are treating idle passion as the orthodox tone
and playful jest as high elegance.
It is as if the immortals had gathered at the Queen Mother's Jade Pool, only
to have the bewitching maids of the back alleys
and brothels arrive arm-in-arm to sit beside the divine maidens Cailuan and Shuangcheng.
The blind might think them equally beautiful,
but the discerning would wash their cups and leave,
unwilling even to look.
Moreover, since it harms works of orthodox morality,
it is especially fitting that you part with them.
A man like you,
with a romantic flair surpassing Zhao Feiyan or Zhuo Wenjun, naturally refuses
to be bound by petty constraints.
But your posthumous reputation rests in the mouths of others;
surely you must know this yourself.
It is only because your talent is so unrestrained
that you disdain living within the strict bounds of convention."
Shiweng nodded, "I deeply respect your excellent admonition.
From now on, I will certainly take care
to redeem my past faults."
Suddenly, looking at Qinxian,
he remarked, "This jade branch is far too艳 (alluringly beautiful)."
He then laughed and quoted, "'Climb not over my garden wall, break not my sandalwood trees.'"
Hearing himself called 'too alluringly beautiful,' Qinxian felt somewhat displeased.
Daosheng looked out at the garden
and changed the subject, "Your garden is wonderfully tranquil.
It perfectly matches the lines:
'In the deep woods, one sometimes sees a deer;
beside the midday stream, one hears no temple bell.'"
At first, Shiweng did not find this strange,
but suddenly he caught the meaning and cried out, "Abominable!
Absolutely abominable!"
Daosheng laughed as well.
Shiweng demanded, "If you give me a couplet,
it must be piercing
and true, not some vague scratching-through-the-boot nonsense."
Daosheng recited, "'All poets under heaven are my juniors.'"
Shiweng roared with laughter, "I can hardly bear the weight of that,
though my added years might just justify it."
Daosheng smiled, "The second line is harder to match."
He then added, "There is a Lu Mochou in this region;
let's borrow her for the match:
'The young wife of the Lu family is a hometown kin.'"
Shiweng laughed wildly, "That won't do.
That line could be used on a personal seal,
but it's terrible for a couplet.
Think of something more dignified."
Daosheng said, "I've thought of another,
but you'll suspect me of malice again."
Shiweng urged, "Just say it.
Even if you curse me,
as long as the curse is apt, it's fine."
Daosheng recited:
"'My belly does not betray me;
I do not betray my belly.
The writing is like the man;
the man is like his writing.'"
Shiweng pondered for a moment
and said, "Though the couplet was not born of good intentions,
it suits me quite well.
The writings are here for all to see and hear;
my place in eternity will naturally be fixed.
Let us use this one."
Noticing Qinxian's delicate, jade-shoot-like fingers holding a fan, Shiweng asked to see it.
Taking the opportunity to examine Qinxian's hand as well,
he praised, "This boy has literary patterns in his palm;
he possesses innate intelligence from a past life."
He turned Qinxian's hand over and over,
squinting his blurred old eyes
to study it twice before handing his own fan to Qinxian.
Qinxian saw that the painting on the fan was excellent and admired it,
unwilling to put it down.
Shiweng examined Qinxian's fan, realized it bore Daosheng's painting of 'Plum Wife
and Crane Son,' and began fanning himself with it.
After conversing a while longer, Daosheng prepared
to return to the boat.
Shiweng invited him to tour the famous sights early the next morning.
Daosheng agreed, took his leave with Qinxian,
and they rode their sedan chairs back along the old route,
exiting the Dry West Gate and returning by boat.
It was already late.
On the way, Qinxian realized the fans had been swapped and deeply regretted it.
Back on the ship,
he informed Daosheng, who replied, "I am going back tomorrow;
I will just swap it back for you then."
The night passed.
Early the next morning, Shiweng sent a man
to invite Daosheng and Qinxian.
Qinxian stubbornly refused to go,
and Daosheng did not force him.
The messenger presented a fan,
saying the wrong one had been taken yesterday.
Daosheng accepted it without looking, handed over the fan Qinxian had brought back the day before,
took a servant, boarded the messenger's boat, and departed.
After he left, Qinxian picked up his own fan
and saw a poem inscribed upon it:
"Who sings of branch-high cold in hand?
For cloud-youth holding inkstone, surely hard...
I envy the lone crane flying in the wild,
By jade forests day by day, stealing a glance."
Reading this, Qinxian grew suspicious, vaguely recalling some anecdote about a 'cloud-youth holding an inkstone.'
Thinking carefully, anger surged in his heart,
and he wanted to tear the fan to pieces.
But then he stopped himself:
"I will wait for Foster Father
to return and show it to him.
Why should he associate with such a person!"
Furious, he flung the fan aside
and threw himself onto the bed in gloom.
Suddenly, memories of the capital rushed back,
making him feel even more wretched.
Except for the circle of famous scholars at Yiyuan,
it seemed every stranger he met subjected him to mockery and harassment.
It was utterly detestable.
The more he thought about it, the angrier he became,
and unconsciously, tears began to fall.
When Liu Xi brought breakfast in, Qinxian refused to eat.
Seeing him so depressed, Liu Xi urged him to go sightseeing,
saying, "Sitting on the boat is stifling for you, Young Master.
It would be better
to go into the city for a stroll.
The best places to visit are Mochou Lake, the Qinhuai River, Bao'en Temple, Rain Flower Terrace,
Jiming Embankment, Xuanwu Lake, and Swallow Bluff.
I'll accompany you into the city to relieve your boredom;
the Master won't be back until evening anyway."
Qinxian replied, "I have no spirit for it.
The weather is frightfully hot,
and I cannot bear to walk."
Liu Xi said, "For other places you might have to walk a bit,
but for Mochou Lake, the Qinhuai River,
and Swallow Bluff, it is entirely by water.
We can go by boat without walking at all.
We missed docking at Swallow Bluff the day before yesterday due to the wind,
which was a pity,
but we might pass it tomorrow,
so we can visit it when we set sail again.
Today, let's go visit the Qinhuai River;
the banks are lined with pearl-adorned beauties and emerald ornaments—
it's incredibly lively and fascinating."
Qinxian asked, "How far is Mochou Lake from here?"
Liu Xi answered, "Not far at all, right around the Water-West Gate."
Qinxian suddenly recalled the planchette reading at Yiyuan:
'On a future day, gaze upon Mochou Lake;
lotus fragrance guards the maiden's tomb.'
It had prophesied that his grave from a past life lay there.
Emotion swelled within him,
bringing deep sorrow, and he said
to Liu Xi, "I have a relative whose grave is at Mochou Lake.
If we go touring the lake,
I wish to offer some sacrifices there."
Liu Xi replied, "That isn't difficult,
but we haven't prepared any sacrificial dishes."
Qinxian said, "We don't need dishes.
A cup of wine
and a stick of incense will suffice."
Liu Xi agreed, "That makes it even easier."
He went and hired a shaded skiff, packed a fruit box, brought incense
and wine, instructed the crew to watch the ship carefully,
and helped Qinxian board the smaller boat.
With twin oars flying, they set off.
Qinxian saw they were traveling along the same river as yesterday,
and after about ten li,
they arrived at Mochou Lake.
Liu Xi suggested, "Let's tour around first, then go looking for the grave."
He led Qinxian into the Guanyin Convent.
Inside, they passed through two sets of heavy doors.
The layout was open on all sides, elegantly furnished,
with walls entirely covered in books, paintings,
and inscriptions by famous figures.
Among them, he spotted poetry and prose by Hou Shiweng,
as well as a couplet by Mei Shixie, the Education Commissioner of Jiangxi.
Qinxian noticed that the visitors strolling about included scholars, merchants, farmers, and women.
Several tea tables were set up,
and just beyond the balustrade lay a lake entirely blanketed in lotus flowers.
A monk brewed two bowls of tea;
Liu Xi invited Qinxian
to sit, then took his own bowl to sit elsewhere.
Qinxian saw the people walking back
and forth constantly staring at him.
A few village women—
with faces as broad as clay basins, feet as wide as bream fish,
protruding bellies, and stiffly starched summer blouses hanging empty from their shoulders—
chewed on sweet melons
while fixing their wide, yellow eyes upon him.
They seemed to think the handsome scholar Zhang Sheng had leaped straight off the opera stage.
Qinxian became extremely impatient under their gaze, had Liu Xi pay for the tea,
and walked straight out.
Seeing the boatman come forward carrying the wine box,
Liu Xi asked, "Where exactly is this grave located?"
Qinxian replied, "How should I know?
We have to look for it!"
Liu Xi asked, "Which family does it belong to?
We need a surname to even begin searching."
Qinxian thought for a moment,
realizing the planchette had never revealed a surname.
He sat in stunned silence before saying, "I don't know the surname either."
Liu Xi laughed, "How can you forget your own relative's surname?
Then we might as well give up—
where would we even start looking?"
Qinxian confessed, "To tell you the truth,
I once participated in spirit-writing,
and the planchette revealed
that my grave from a past life is here at Mochou Lake,
but it never gave a surname."
Liu Xi replied, "That sounds incredibly nebulous.
How can you know if it's true or false?"
Qinxian insisted, "It is absolutely true;
it predicted many other things perfectly."
Unwilling to argue, Liu Xi let the matter drop.
Qinxian walked to the edge of the lake
and saw a vast expanse of lotus flowers.
The red ones looked like Yang Guifei slightly flushed from imperial wine,
while the white ones resembled Zhao Feiyan freshly emerged from a scented bath.
Among them drifted a few lotus-picking boats, some carrying young girls,
while several naked children played and splashed in the water.
Qinxian prayed silently in his heart:
"Supreme Immortal, Supreme Immortal!
You graced me by pointing out my former self,
yet you gave no surname.
Now you have me standing here in person, unable to find it,
which is a great sorrow.
Show me some miraculous sign
to guide me from this lost path."
As Qinxian prayed, he looked around at the vast open spaces
on all sides but saw no graves.
Suddenly, a small skiff drifted out from the thickets of lotus.
On it stood a young girl in red with her hair hanging down in twin tufts,
about fourteen or fifteen years old.
With long brows, lovely cheeks, bright teeth,
and clear eyes, her exquisite beauty was utterly captivating as she rowed toward them.
Qinxian stared intently, thinking she must be a celestial maiden at play—
how could the mortal dust produce such a stunning beauty?
Feeling profoundly mundane in both body and spirit,
he stood in reverent awe.
He noticed several lotus flowers resting on the girl's boat,
while a flock of kingfishers clustered at the bow,
chirping and preening their feathers without the slightest fear of humans.
Qinxian found this deeply strange.
The girl fixed her clear gaze upon Qinxian,
and he looked back at her.
In a moment, the boat reached the shore,
and with a collective rustle, the flock of kingfishers took flight toward the north;
Liu Xi even clapped his hands to shoo them along.
Liu Xi asked the girl, "Are there any fun places across the lake?"
She replied, "Over there is just the city wall
and the tomb of Du the Fairy—
it's the best place to see orchids and kingfishers.
That flock of kingfishers just now came from her tomb;
they were too lazy to fly,
so they hitched a ride on my boat."
Hearing of Du the Fairy's tomb, Qinxian's heart was deeply stirred,
and he immediately asked, "From what era was this Du the Fairy?"
The girl answered, "That I don't know.
I've only heard she lived seventy or eighty years ago.
She was the daughter of an official family
and was buried here when she died."
Qinxian asked, "Why is she called a fairy?"
The girl explained, "People see this area is home
to only a few prominent families,
so how could it produce a young woman of such extraordinary and radiant beauty?
Yet she is often seen rowing a small boat,
appearing and vanishing among the lotus flowers.
If anyone tries to chase her, she disappears.
Later, they found a small grave over there, ringed by many mottled bamboos,
with a massive trumpet vine growing behind it—
its canopy spreads as large as a house.
Countless kingfishers nest inside it,
and strange, wonderful orchids bloom all year round.
If anyone picks them
and takes them home, they fall ill.
So the locals, seeing these miraculous signs, don't dare desecrate the place.
Instead, they often go to repair and tidy it,
and no cattle or sheep are allowed to trample it.
On the first and fifteenth of the month, people even cross the lake to burn incense."
Qinxian said, "I would also like
to cross the lake and take a look.
Are you willing to ferry me over?"
The girl replied, "Just step onto the boat."
Qinxian immediately had Liu Xi bring the wine box and incense,
and told their hired boatman
to return and wait at the main ship.
Once they boarded, the girl began
to row with her oar,
and Liu Xi took another oar to help her.
The girl asked Qinxian, "Where are you from?"
Qinxian replied, "I am originally from Suzhou,
but I am now coming from the capital."
She asked again, "Where are you going now?"
Qinxian answered, "To Jiangxi."
As she asked and he answered,
they reached the shore of the lake.
The girl said, "I will lead the way."
Qinxian replied, "Very good."
Holding a lotus leaf
and a single lotus blossom, the girl led Qinxian through the woods.
The city wall was built along the contours of the mountain.
Walking into the thicket of mottled bamboo,
they saw two silk tree flowers in full bloom, along with several crape myrtles and hibiscuses.
There, indeed, was a small tomb.
A secluded fragrance assailed the senses,
and countless orchids were in full bloom.
At the foot of the mountain, a mass of trumpet vine entwined around the rocks,
forming a rounded dome like a lush green umbrella canopy.
Inside, kingfishers chirped and fluttered about.
When a pure breeze blew past, the fragrance pierced straight into the bone.
Qinxian had felt sorrowful at first,
but upon reaching this place,
he suddenly felt all worldly dust washed away, feeling light and airy,
as if he were about to ascend as an immortal.
If he could build a small hut here,
it would be better than anywhere else.
He gazed at the grave mound for a long time,
gently brushing away the moss and lichen.
He then had Liu Xi strike a fire with his flint,
light the incense, pour the wine,
and arrange the various fruits they had brought before the tomb.
The girl said, "Let me help you."
She peeled a petal from her lotus flower, placed it before the grave,
filled it to the brim with wine,
and arranged the fruits on her lotus leaf.
She told Liu Xi
to take the box away, then asked Qinxian, "Why aren't you bowing?"
Qinxian replied, "I am her,
and she is me."
The girl laughed, "What do you mean by that?
Such silly talk.
If there is you, there is no her;
if she still exists, there is no you."
Hearing a spark of spiritual truth in her words, Qinxian looked at the girl,
and she looked back at him.
Qinxian said, "You don't know me;
you only know her."
The girl replied, "I haven't seen her,
I've only seen you.
Why are you offering sacrifices to her for no reason?"
Qinxian said, "There is a reason,
but even if I told you, you wouldn't understand."
The girl retorted, "If I wouldn't understand, then there is no need to speak of it."
Qinxian sat down on the ground,
and the girl sat beside him.
Qinxian felt deeply reluctant to leave,
but the girl urged him, "You can go back now."
Left with no choice, Qinxian rose
and offered the fruits to the girl.
She smiled and said, "I don't eat these things.
But since you gave them to me,
it wouldn't be right to refuse.
I will plant them here for you,
so wait until you return in the future to see them."
Pulling a hairpin from her head,
she dug a few small pits in front of the grave
and planted the seeds of the peach, plum, apple, and pear.
The rest she packed back into the box for Liu Xi to take away.
Qinxian watched in great astonishment.
The girl urged them to set off,
so they boarded the boat and crossed the lake.
When Liu Xi tried to pay her the boat fare,
she laughed and said, "No need, no need,
I am not a ferrywoman."
Qinxian found this even more incomprehensible,
and could only offer his thanks and bid her farewell.
The girl gave a radiant smile
and rowed back into the thicket of lotus flowers.
Qinxian watched her intently,
but amidst the shimmering light of the flowers
and the haze of the lake water, everything blurred together.
Unable to see clearly where she had gone,
he returned to the boat, his heart heavy with melancholy.
As it was still early, Liu Xi wanted
to visit the Qinhuai River,
so he had the boat rowed into the Water-West Pass.
Arriving at the Qinhuai River,
they indeed saw painted mansions
and embroidered curtains lining both banks, the air thick with fragrant perfumes.
On the balconies stood many courtesans—
some leaning alone against the railings, others resting shoulder-to-shoulder in twos
and threes, gently waving their singing fans to reveal slender jade hands,
or whispering softly through parted red lips.
They varied in beauty
and charm, each distinct from the last.
Seeing Qinxian's striking beauty, the courtesans began calling out 'sister'
and 'brother,' leaning out to gaze down at him.
Their stares made Qinxian so intensely embarrassed
that he could only complain that Liu Xi shouldn't have brought him.
Just as he urgently demanded
to turn the boat around and go back, more pleasure boats crowded in from both ends.
Courtesans accompanying their guests squeezed close,
winking and flashing their eyes at him.
Qinxian truly felt like Wei Jie,
the handsome man of antiquity who was 'stared to death' by crowds.
With great difficulty, they squeezed the boat through.
Suddenly, a window ahead banged open,
and an older courtesan appeared.
Seeing Qinxian, she stared unblinking and called out, "Zhang Laobao,
where are you rowing to?
Why not come over to our place?"
Zhang Laobao, the boatman, looked at Liu Xi
and pursed his lips upward in inquiry.
Liu Xi shook his head, "Let's go back.
Our Young Master won't go."
The old courtesan continued to call out from above,
but Zhang Laobao waved his hand and rowed straight past.
Exiting the Water-West Pass,
it took them a long time
to reach the main ship.
By the time they boarded, night had fallen.
They were met by two household servants who cried out in panic,
"Young Master, why have you only returned now?
Disaster has struck!
The Master took a fall on the mountain and fainted.
Though we revived him,
he hasn't stopped groaning since!"
Hearing this, Qinxian broke into a cold sweat
and rushed into the cabin.
