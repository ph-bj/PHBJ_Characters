Chapter 1 — Shi Nanxiang compiles a floral register of celebrated beauties; Mei Ziyu, catching their fragrance, is stunned by peerless allure.

The theatrical scene of the capital surpasses all under heaven. Set at the very edge of the sky, stage pavilions and dance terraces appear at every turn; amid the vast multitudes, people spend their days drunk on moonlight and judging beauties. Truly the splendor defies description, the spectacle defies depiction. What one hears and sees in a moment — strange and wondrous — falls within the bounds of reason and touches the full range of human feeling. And so, with a playful brush, I have portrayed these playful souls. The rarest among them are the few gentlemen who love with restraint and hold to propriety, and the few performers who keep themselves upright — truly embodying the line from the Guofeng: "fond of beauty yet not licentious." I first divide the young men of the gentry into ten types, all under the single word: feeling.

The first is feeling-in-rectitude; the second, feeling-in-eminence; the third, feeling-in-loftiness; the fourth, feeling-in-ease; the fifth, feeling-in-elegance; the sixth, feeling-in-boldness; the seventh, feeling-in-wildness; the eighth, feeling-in-wit; the ninth, feeling-in-harmony; the tenth, feeling-in-joy. I then divide the celebrated dan performers of the theater into ten types — also under the single word: feeling.

The first is feeling-in-depth; the second, feeling-in-wisdom; the third, feeling-in-grace; the fourth, feeling-in-purity; the fifth, feeling-in-virtue; the sixth, feeling-in-ardor; the seventh, feeling-in-integrity; the eighth, feeling-in-abandon; the ninth, feeling-in-radiance; the tenth, feeling-in-charm. These are all figures of the highest order. As for those of the lower order, the word feeling cannot apply to them at all — they too may be classified: one, lewdness; two, depravity; three, cunning; four, licentiousness; five, greed; six, demonic; seven, malevolent; eight, parasitic. In general, from ancient times to the present, those who have given their feelings to the pleasure quarters fall under one of two paths — the crooked or the straight. What my eyes and ears have taken in, I have set down in writing, forming sixty volumes in all, entitled Pinhua Baojian — also called Yiqing Yishi. The book has both lead and supporting figures, neither too close nor too distant, like lotus stems that break yet keep their threads, like flowers thick with gathering clouds. Dead expressions are thoroughly avoided — one wonders how many pangs of effort it cost the author; new ground is broken at every turn, so that the reader feels instantly gratified. As the saying goes: the mandarin ducks are embroidered for all to see — do not let slip the golden needle.

This book bears no name, and one truly cannot know in what era, what year, what place, or by what person it was written. The story opens with a man utterly indifferent to feeling, who fathers a son utterly devoted to feeling. This man's surname is Mei, personal name Shixie, courtesy name Tiean — a native of Jinling in Jiangnan. He is of an illustrious family, currently serving as a Reader-in-Waiting at the Hanlin Academy, residing in Mingke Lane in the southern part of the city. His grandfather, Ding, served as President of the Board of Civil Appointments; his father, Gengtiao, served as Grand Secretary of the Hall of Literary Glory — three generations of only sons. Shixie passed the jinshi examination at seventeen and entered the Hanlin; twenty-nine years have passed since then, and he is now forty-six. His family background ranks with the Jin and Zhang clans of old; in classical learning he follows in the tradition of Ma and Zheng. Noble blood inclined him toward Confucian simplicity; a court official, he turned entirely away from ornament and show. His manner is gentle as spring warmth; his bearing, stern as autumn severity. People compare him to the likes of Sima Junshi and Zhao Qingxian. His wife, Lady Yan, is also from a great Jinling family — daughter of Left Censor-in-Chief Yan Yaochen and younger sister of Hanlin Compiler Yan Zhuang, both of whom have already passed away. Lady Yan is forty-four this year, truly combining virtue and beauty, matchless in her goodness and grace; she and Academician Mei have been devoted companions for more than twenty years. At twenty-nine she dreamed that a divine being presented her with jade, and gave birth to a son — a jade lad — named Ziyu, styled Gengxiang. Mei Ziyu is now seventeen years old, his countenance pure gold and fine jade; his nature a lustrous pearl still in its shell, a brilliant moon rising over the peaks. Moreover, his gifts are heaven-bestowed and remarkable: though he has studied for only ten years, he has already mastered ten thousand volumes. Two years ago Shixie took leave to return home for ancestral rites, and Ziyu accompanied him; there he passed the prefectural examination and sat once for the provincial examination without success. He then followed his father back to the capital and, it being inconvenient to return south, enrolled as a senior student at the Imperial Academy, now studying under a celebrated scholar from Zhejiang, Li Xingquan. This Li Xingquan is a former student of Shixie's from the provincial examination cohort — a strict Confucian teacher of upright words and correct conduct. Lady Yan treasures her son as a jewel, and aside from his studies keeps him constantly at her side. The household has many maidservants and female attendants; those among the serving women under thirty, and maidservants above fifteen, are none of them permitted to wait on Ziyu, for fear of leading him astray. And Ziyu himself guards his character like jade: though surrounded by silks and satins, he has none of the dissolute habits of a pampered youth — he needs no scented pouch to be beautiful, no powder or rouge to be radiant. He devotes himself only to choosing worthy friends and honoring his teacher, studying with diligent effort; he discusses the present and debates the past, his ambitions reaching to the clouds. Even now he already bears the reputation of an auspicious star, and people count it a pleasure to catch a glimpse of him.

One day the teacher dismissed class early for some errand, and Ziyu was sitting alone when two close friends came to call. One was surnamed Yan, given name Zhongqing, styled Jiantan — currently twenty-three years old — the son of the late Hanlin Compiler Yan Zhuang, and Lady Yan's nephew.

While Yan Zhuang lived, he had been both a close in-law relation and a bosom friend of Shixie's. To everyone's sorrow, he was called to the jade tower at thirty, and his wife, Madame Zheng, starved herself to death in loyal devotion. At the time Zhongqing was barely three years old; Shixie took him in and raised him, and also petitioned for Madame Zheng to be formally honored for her fidelity. At nineteen, while at the Mei household, Zhongqing passed the provincial examination as a runner-up. That same year Shixie arranged his marriage into the household of a fellow-provincial and examination-cohort acquaintance, the current Transmission Office Commissioner Wang Wenhui, as a son-in-law by adoption. Wang Wenhui is Lady Yan's cousin — so the family ties became doubly close — and father- and son-in-law got on extremely well. The other visitor was surnamed Shi, given name Nanxiang, styled Zhujun — a native of Hanyang in Huguang, currently twenty-four years old, who had already passed his provincial examination as top candidate. His father, Shi Zengwang, currently serves as a Supervising Secretary in the Office of the Scrutiny of Civil Appointments. Both men were abundantly talented and deeply learned, yet their characters were quite different. Zhongqing was proudly self-contained and straightforward in heart. Comparing his learning to Ziyu's: Ziyu pursued one pure and thoroughgoing path; Zhongqing took one broad and open-minded path. In all matters of human nature and the principles of things, Zhongqing observed the broad outlines and sought no further; Ziyu probed and investigated hidden depths, striving always for greater precision.

Often Zhongqing would dismiss a field of study with contempt, only for Ziyu's careful exposition to reveal layer after layer of subtle meaning.

And often Ziyu would struggle to find the key to some principle, only for a single word from Zhongqing to bring sudden, brilliant clarity.

These two had been inseparable for more than ten years, their bond deeper and more intimate than that of brothers born of the same mother. As for Nanxiang, he was brilliantly unconventional — pure and wild, utterly beyond the commonplace, disdaining the whole world — with ten thousand words pouring from him at a moment's notice; and of all people, he admired only Ziyu and Zhongqing.

That day they came together to call on Ziyu. The gateman recognized them as frequent visitors and the young master's closest friends, and led them directly to the study to see Ziyu. Zhongqing then accompanied Ziyu inside to pay respects to his aunt, after which they came out and sat down with Nanxiang. The three conversed for a while, and a page boy brought fragrant tea. Nanxiang, pleased by the refined elegance of the room and the clean scent of the tea, said with sudden enthusiasm: 'Is there anything entertaining to hand?' Ziyu took out a jade flute and played the piece 'Three Variations on the Plum Blossom.' When he finished, Nanxiang beat time in appreciation and sighed: 'A fine sword for a hero, rouge for a beauty — sweet music for my friend. This jade flute was a gift from me to you, and this piece was the perfect choice. Today I have something even finer to give you.' Saying this, he noticed the immaculate elegance of the room, every furnishing refined and antique. Knowing Ziyu's exceptional mind and seeing his noble, radiant bearing—even those selected recently in the Flower Register of the Theater, despite their beauty, lacked this spiritual bone. Yet seeing his humble modesty and seeming reserve, he wondered what his tastes might be. If he were rigid and inflexible, he could not be considered a complete talent. So he decided to test him, asking: 'Yuxiang, let me ask you, of all the things in the world that please the ear and eye, and move the heart and soul, what is the greatest?' Ziyu, taken aback by the question, looked at Nanxiang and thought: 'He is a pure, wild, and unrestrained man; his views surely differ from the vulgar, and there must be a reason for this.' He replied: 'This question is too broad. People's ears and eyes are the same, but their temperaments differ. Some love prosperity, while others despise it. Some love tranquility, while others loathe it. For instance, Xie An found pleasure in music at the Eastern Mountain, while another might find it a noisy disturbance in his humble dwelling. Some dismiss beautiful women, while others keep concubines by their side. Since the pleasures of the ear and eye vary so greatly, the things that move the heart and soul are even harder to agree upon. How can one person's temperament and senses represent everyone's?' Nanxiang said: 'That is not what I mean. I am referring to a specific kind of person.'

In the capital today, the streets teem with multitudes. When you meet a man of high dignity and great prestige, he speaks nothing but official language and official virtue, every inch of him conforming to official decorum — and you feel a kind of dread. When you meet a sour and pedantic scholar, with cramped hands and twisted feet, hunched back and hunched shoulders, groaning as he affects the pose of weighing his words — you feel like laughing. When you meet a market ruffian chasing rank smells, with unkempt hair and bare feet, calculating over every last copper — you feel contempt.

"When you meet a vulgar performer or a common prostitute, hair slicked with oil and face caked with powder, utterly shameless—you feel revulsion. Think about it: of all the people one encounters, once these are removed—what sort remains?" Ziyu had been unable to guess what Nanxiang was driving at, and could only say: "If pleasure is not to be found in sound and color, then what of two or three kindred spirits sharing heartfelt conversation morning and evening—how would that be?" Nanxiang said: "True, such kindred souls with pure hearts exist; but once you have been together long enough, everyone reveals their particular habits, and at every turn something gives you cause for dissatisfaction—that is not the highest joy either. Moreover, most such companions have already settled into their lives, sitting stiffly over their books with no interest in anything—rather dull." Ziyu said: "Then what about roaming freely in mountains and streams, finding contentment among fishermen and woodcutters—might that not be worthy of a lifetime?" Nanxiang said: "Mountains and streams are serene and free from the world's dust—pleasant enough for a leisurely wander, yet in the end too solitary and lonely, and not real joy." Ziyu said: "If none of these bring joy, can it be that only the young men of the theater constitute life's highest delight?"

"Does pure wine not intoxicate the heart? Does an ancient sword not light up one's soul? Can there truly be no place in friendship that moves and stirs one deeply?" Nanxiang laughed: "You're still speaking from inside your one little room — your experience is narrow, your travels have not yet transformed you." He then drew a book from his boot and presented it to Ziyu: "This is a recent work I have compiled, a Catalogue of Flowers — also called the Flower Register. It is not yet fully carved; this is a handwritten copy. In it I have selected eight celebrated dan performers in the theater today, assessed and characterized each one, with poems and verses to describe their appearances. You may as well enjoy a preview — read it first and then speak."
