Chapter 4 — Three scholars compose by a snowy window; a young man inscribes a lyric on a powder-white wall.

To return to our story: Ziyu was brooding over the state of Qinguan's heart when he heard Yuanmao open the vestibule door and cry: "Something terrible has happened!" This gave Ziyu and the others quite a start. "Why are you making such a fuss?" Yuanmao said: "Look at the ground — already covered a layer deep, and with flakes as large as cotton bolls coming down, a night of this and we'll have more than a foot." Ziyu and Pincai went to the door to look; sure enough, snowflakes were drifting and swirling down. Ziyu said: "This winter solstice snow is the finest kind. All winter long the wind has been dry; now that it snows at last, it is thanks to our speaking of Qinguan — we have moved Heaven to harmony, and auspicious signs appear on every side." Pincai said: "If it snows all night, tomorrow we may go out to admire it." Yun'er had already brought out cloaks and wind-hoods; he helped Ziyu put them on and led him inside.

That night a full five inches of snow fell, and by daybreak sharp gusts of the north wind came in waves — cold beyond measure.

The snow had just stopped. It was truly a world of jade and glass, the heavens and earth carved in white — a scene of surpassing beauty. Master Li Xingquan had risen early, braved the cold, developed a dizzy head and cough, and gone back to bed, feeling unsettled; he told Ziyu and the others not to study. Being himself well versed in pharmacology, he had his page-boy fetch several doses of dispersing medicine, took them, covered his head, and slept. Ziyu instructed the two page-boys to attend carefully outside the study, then went himself to a small three-room studio named the Twenty-Four-Qin Study — its plaque bearing the hand of his grandfather, the late Duke Wenmu. With nothing to do, Ziyu took out Xie Huilian's Ode on Snow and began to read.

When he reached the lines "The white crane loses its freshness, the silver pheasant its pallor" — he paused to admire the ancients' skill at rendering scenes in words. Just then the sky darkened again; snow began to drift down once more, and the snow on the rooftops and branches was blown by the wind like pear blossoms scattering in confusion. He at once told Yun'er to instruct the kitchen to prepare extra dishes, and invited the young gentlemen Wei and Li to come admire the snow. Before long a table of fine food was brought over; Pincai and Yuanmao came to enjoy the scene together.

Ziyu could not drink wine, but made a show of accompanying the others. He again turned to Pincai to ask after Qinguan; seeing how deeply Ziyu's mind was engaged, Pincai shifted his approach and spoke of Qinguan's bearing and temperament entirely in terms of praise, leaving Ziyu all the more ardently admiring. He thought to himself: on such a snowy day, to behold one who stands like a jade branch amid ice blossoms — how different from gazing at flowers on a fairy isle! Truly one could laugh at the brocade-tented banquets of the warlord's house, where fine wine and roast lamb never quite shake off the martial swagger. When the meal was done, they boiled snow to brew tea, chatted for a while, and Pincai and Yuanmao each returned to their own rooms.

Suddenly Juner arrived carrying a letter, the envelope inscribed "For the hand of Master Mei," with a line of smaller writing beside it. Inside were one sheet of note-paper and four sheets of verse-paper. Recognizing Zhongqing's hand, he asked Juner who had brought it. Juner said: "It was sent by Young Master Yan's personal attendant." Ziyu said: "Ask him to wait a moment." He opened it and read: the note paper read: "Yesterday Yong'an and I sat together in the empty room. Only when jade cups grew heavy with cold did we know the six-pointed flowers had begun to fly; only when the silver candle-light guttered did we see the snow's full radiance. Ice mountains piling upon ice mountains, ranged like mica folding screens; pagoda upon pagoda lit from within, gleaming like glass lanterns. Beauties arranging themselves for play, toying with jade cats; arhats piled and assembled, tossing balls at lions. At dusk we chose rhymes, fighting in white verse and setting topics; ashamed at the poverty of our jewelled lines, we offer these bricks to invite jade in return. Respectfully presented for your keen judgment; we await your pearled reply. To my worthy younger brother Yuxiang. Yong'an sends his regards as well. Written in haste by Jiantan."

Ziyu read it and said: "What a beautifully wrought letter!" He then turned to the verse sheets, which bore the title Eight Verses on the Snow Window.

Snow Mountain
This peak has surely flown here from afar—
one lotus of white jade, burst into bloom.
In clogs, how fine to chant the catkins by the pavilion;
on a donkey, how hard to find the plum along the ridge.
Look again: it seems to drip, yet holds no green;
though much may melt, how could it be the ash of ruin?
Deep in the night of cloud and rain the cold locks shut—
how could the goddess now come down from her terrace?

Snow Pagoda
The flower-scattering maiden nears the Brahma palace;
these tiers of treasure, all this splendour, are Heaven’s own craft.
Four eaves, at times, will stand in the sun’s way;
seven storeys, at every turn, will startle the wind.
In the moon, the holy relic—how it blazes;
on the water, the floating spire—its colour emptier still.
Were I but granted the mood to climb its highest peak,
I’d carve my name up there, and put my question to the sky.

Snow Screen
Last night the Liang Garden gave word that spring had come;
jade tables, pearl blinds—all vie, brand-new and bright.
Mica would serve to screen the flower-loving censor;
crystal should be bestowed on the lady of the tiger’s house.
The silver candle, unstirred, sheds a light gone cold;
paint the golden geese—still the dream will not hold.
How strange—the ring of singers all in mourning white;
should the chancellor draw near, he would surely take offence.

Snow Lantern
Trimming the wick, how often I have chanted the sharp-rhymed snow;
tonight the burning oil outshines the nine-flower lamp.
Weaving white silk, one need no longer face the wall;
whose is the house that reads with never a lamp alight?
The pure cold has burned three candles to their ends;
lighting my sleep, I still can see the six-fold flowers.
Remember now the Lantern Feast draws near—
you festive moths and withered willows, boast not against this glow.

—first draft by Wang Xun of the Yong’an studio.

Ziyu read them and said: “Splendid poems. Of these four, Snow Pagoda must rank first, Snow Screen second, Snow Mountain next, and Snow Lantern last. Now to see Jiantan’s:”

Snow Lion:
Uncanny form, taking shape as a long-haired beast;
called by name, the Bai Ze claims lordship by chance.
Riding the air, can it truly leap beyond the seas?
Driven by wind, it only roars along the eastern river.
A high golden throne is not hard to make brilliant;
clad in red trees and new finery—the workmanship, I fear, falls short.
If the Recluse of Longqiu were but to see it,
he would surely cast aside his staff and speak again of emptiness.

Ziyu thought: The topic of Snow Lion was not easy to handle; yet look how he uses his allusions with effortless ease, finely suited to the subject — only a master's hand could manage this. He read on:

Snow Cat:
Playing go at random, pillowed on two dressing-cases;
the jade-like creature crouches by the carved eaves.
Coaxed away—how could fish threaded on willow bring it back?
Led off—it is best lured by rice wrapped in salt.
Resembling a tiger's head—there truly was an original;
but those mouse-like things only bask in others' warmth.
Today the red of peony has all flown away—
cold-eyed, there is no need to add a single thread.

Ziyu said: "This one is even better; the third couplet is delightfully pointed." He read further — the next topics were Snow Arhat and Snow Beauty. Ziyu reflected that these two topics were even more barren and still than the preceding six, and difficult to get one's brush around. He found:

Snow Arhat:
Who at dawn pushed open the gate of Chan?
Gautama faces the wall, his staff-ring returned.
Deliverance—the mind is like still water;
wandering freely—the intention settles on Cold Mountain.
Turning sutras: in the misty dimness, palm-leaf texts;
forming a society: lotus flowers arise in an instant.
This very body is the same as a phantom image—
do not sigh and shake your head at the stubbornness of stone.

Snow Beauty:
Jade bones, fine and flawless, without a single flaw—
is it she, in the end, or is it not she?
A spring heart already like floss clinging to mud;
my face should be like flowers blooming in the rain.
In the deep of night, pondering—it has become flowing water;
in a former life, its flavor I recall as brewed tea.
Where now is the pearl-selling maidservant who attended her?
Leaning against the bamboo without a word, the sun slopes once more.

—Jiantan Zhongqing, final draft.

Ziyu read through to the end, softly intoning the verses several times. He felt that of Zhongqing's four poems, Snow Lion was gilded and richly patterned; Snow Cat was jade-carved and finely chiselled; Snow Arhat exhaled a clean fragrance, like a courtyard full of lotus; Snow Beauty was deft in both strokes at once, graceful and free as jade camellias — surpassing Wang Xun's. He thought he would compose eight matching poems in the same rhymes, but feared they could not equal the seamless completeness of the originals. Better to choose four new topics and avoid the same grooves. Those eight topics of theirs all conceived from the end backward, turning vacancy into substance, defining the host through the guests. He would instead conceive from a moment before the snow had fallen — and simply use four empty words joined to the character for snow as his topics. He thought: before the snow falls, when heavy clouds mass and a misty indistinctness prevails, the feeling of impending snow is already there. He would make Snow Feeling his first topic. When snowflakes begin to drift, indistinct and shadowy, Snow Shadow emerges — that would be his second. When the snow first begins to fall, the granules pattering and rustling, it has a sound — Snow Sound would be his third. Once snow has fully fallen, all is a brilliant expanse of white — that must be Snow Color, his fourth topic. Novel and original — and with that he set to composing. He had just completed two when Yuanmao and Pincai came in and saw him; Ziyu therefore invited them to compose a few verses as well. Yuanmao said:

"With a single empty character appended to the word 'snow' as my topic, I simply cannot manage it. All I can do is write a plain snow poem." Pincai said: "Even a plain snow poem — making it rhyme properly is hard work. I can only manage a quatrain." Yuanmao said: "Seven-character lines are too unwieldy; all I know is how to write a five-character regulated poem." Ziyu said: "Either will do perfectly well." They each went off to rack their brains.

Before long, Ziyu had completed all four of his poems and written them out on a sheet of cold-gold notepaper. He wrote a reply letter as well, and was just about to seal it when Pincai came over smiling, carrying a sheet of verse:

"It has not come out well — please help me correct it." Ziyu took it and looked: the topic was On Snow, and the poem read:

"Swirling toward the plum tips, petal by petal, slanting;
moths and powdered butterflies fill all the heavens and earth.
Clearly these are immortal products from the Jade Terrace above—
commanding alone the foremost flower of the human world."

Ziyu said in surprise: "I had no idea you had such skill. You must have put in a great deal of work on poetry." Pincai said: "Where would I find the time for that? I just remembered a few arias and patched them together." Ziyu said: "What arias?" Pincai said: "The lines about swirling toward the plum tips, and moths and powdered butterflies, are from the Treading the Snow sequence in River-Sky Snow. " Ziyu said: "And the last two lines?" Pincai said: "The third line is padded out, and the final line borrows from the Occupying Alone aria in the drama The Flower Queen — I just commandeered it and used it as though it were a classical song." Ziyu said: "You have managed to patch it together without any visible seam, which is no mean feat." By this time Yuanmao had also finished; he came with it written out neatly and properly. Ziyu looked it over — it was rather difficult to follow — but could only say in praise: "Very polished and well-balanced. Why not write both of them out and send them over so the others can have a look?" Yuanmao, hearing Ziyu's praise, was sure it must be very good indeed, and said: "Yes, let us invite their instruction." It was Pincai alone who knew his own worth, and quickly said: "Mine had better not go — it would only make a spectacle of itself." Ziyu said: "What does it matter? I will write them out for you." He copied them onto a separate sheet of paper, and added two more lines at the end of his reply letter. He sealed the whole thing, and sent Yun'er to go with the attendant.

On the other side, Zhongqing received the reply note and read it together with Wang Xun. It read: "Your letter arrived like a drift of cloud; the words fell like fragrant snow. The lotus-lantern gutters in the Buddha-pagoda's delicate glow; the kingfisher screen is cold, as one traces the fairy mountain's dim silhouette. White ground, and the golden body of an arhat manifests; a form robed in white comes — jade bones of a beauty, a cat dozing by the chess game beside her. New dew to wash the hands, ancient snow to cleanse the heart; the bright moon comes of itself; the melodies of the bright spring find few to echo them. My seven-character verses, ashamed before the jade and pearls that preceded them; these four vulgar stanzas, abashed that they cannot repay the琼瑶 they have received. I write this in haste to acknowledge; regard it with a smile."

To Jiantan and Yong'an, both brothers, for their joint perusal. Ziyu, with clasped hands. Attached are four rough compositions of mine, and one seven-character quatrain and one five-character regulated poem; please kindly correct them.

Zhongqing and the others then looked at Ziyu's poem titles: Snow Feeling, Snow Shadow, Snow Sound, Snow Color. Zhongqing said to Wang Xun: "These four topics are entirely abstract — even harder to get a brush around than ours. Yuxiang is sure to have produced something fine." With that they looked at the poems; they read:

Snow Feeling:
Three thousand worlds gaze, brimming and full—
one knows there are jade flowers a-brewing, a-gathering.
Before becoming flower, it first cuts water into shapes;
already, cloud-like, it is about to take wing and fly.

Zhongqing said: "The opening couplet builds fine momentum before the topic; the second couplet carves out the character for 'feeling' — truly a stroke of divine transformation." He read on:

People on earth await the planting of a flawless jade wall;
beyond the heavens a sleepless city is about to open wide.
Where is it now—the jade tower frozen solid?
The assembled immortals, imagining—rank on rank at Penglai.

Snow Shadow:
Six-petalled, dimly drifting, the craft of embellishment complete;
beyond the jade balustrade, a delicate tracery is written.
Dim and low it mirrors the water, rippling with empty whiteness;
vaguely perched in the dust, it sways with soft redness.
Flying into plum blossoms—its trace grows faint;
dancing back among willow catkins—its color is all void.
In the clear cold it matches a dream of the Jade Pool—
the jade-tree branches glowing plainly in the moon.

Wang Xun applauded after every line. Zhongqing said: "This one has summoned the very soul of the topic." He read on:

Snow Sound:
In the cold void, jade and jasper scatter;
entering the night, one burns incense to console the loneliness.
On the path the pellets gather, rustling first, collecting sleet;
at the window they are blown in a whisper, riding the gusting wind.
Threading through pine trees—in the stillness I sense pearls ricocheting apart;
sifting through bamboo—lightly, aptly, fine flakes drift.
Waiting until at dawn the clearing scene opens wide—
the dripping of the cold water-clock has nearly worn away its mark.

Snow Color:
Who has been dazzled by that silver sea of jade radiance?
Alone at the summit of the Mountain of Assembled Jade he gazes outward.
The plantain leaf, without intent, somehow touched by green;
the pear-blossom cloud, lost in a dream, ends up piled in yellow.
Richly the pearly dew floats—three parts brilliant;
faintly it borrows from ice-plum—one thread of fragrance.
Dazzling the eyes, this pellucid emptiness is hard to examine closely—
white sands and pale moon—both fade into endless haze.

When they had finished reading, Zhongqing struck the table in admiration, and he and Wang Xun chanted the poems aloud several times. Zhongqing said:

"These poems surpass all of ours." They then looked at Pincai's quatrain. Wang Xun said: "This one is also quite good — but what made Yuxiang compose this extra one on his own?" Zhongqing said: "This one is passable, but it is certainly not Yuxiang's work." They then looked at Yuanmao's five-character regulated poem; the opening couplet read:
"In the sky, vermillion clouds spread;
my thoughts come—rain and snow fill to overflowing."
Wang Xun said: "What do those two words 'my thoughts come' mean?" Zhongqing suddenly burst into laughter: "Read on." Wang Xun looked at the second couplet:
"White people, both eyes draw near;
long horses, four hooves grow light."
He pondered: "'Horse hooves grow light' — I suppose this uses the image of light horse-hooves when snow has thawed.

But why add the character for 'long'? And the first line is so profoundly obscure I simply cannot decipher it."

He looked at the third couplet:
"Burrowing mayflies resemble—
digging out hollow lions, made complete."
Wang Xun said: "These two lines are very strange indeed — how do they come into play? The first line probably uses the Book of Songs — since there is the character for snow in 'linen garments white as snow,' the 'burrowing mayfly' passage gets dragged in. And this 'digging out hollow lions' — what allusion is buried there?" Zhongqing said: "It is simply saying that the snow-sculpted lion has been hollowed out, that is all." They read the closing couplet:
"Going out, one presents world-treasures;
six auspicious signs—the great steps are made smooth."
Wang Xun said: "Is such flattery really called for? This poem must be that old Wei's work. Seeing those slick and pat turns of phrase, naturally this sort of joke would come out." Zhongqing said: "Not so. I look at old Wei and, though he is no conventional scholar, he seems a clever man — he would not be this inept. I suspect the seven-character quatrain is his, and this one must be the fine composition of that Young Brother Li — a poem very like the man." Wang Xun said: "Young Brother Li should not have produced this; looking at him, so proper and literary, he still seems to have some scholarly spirit about him." Zhongqing said: "Precisely because he has scholarly spirit, he lacks poetic spirit." Wang Xun said: "Yuxiang asks us to comment on them — shall we or not?" Zhongqing said: "You might as well give them a comment." Wang Xun said: "Why should I make enemies?" Zhongqing said: "Let me begin by marking Pincai's quatrain with a full circle." He wrote a critical note: Captures the spirit of Heaven's sport with jade.

For the first two couplets of Yuanmao's poem he drew single circles; for the last four lines he drew full circles. His critical note read: Couplets are polished and well-matched; classical allusions are dissolved into the new composition; fully worthy of carrying on the tradition of Yuan and Wei. Wang Xun used a needle to pin Ziyu's poems inside a translucent gauze cabinet, then read Zhongqing's comments and laughed: "The comments are well done — only slightly too barbed." Zhongqing said: "What a pity the day is not long; and this snow will not hold — otherwise we could go and chat with Yuxiang. " Wang Xun said: "Let us go tomorrow! If we went now there would be no time to talk properly." That day it snowed on for another full day and night, accumulating to about a foot's depth. The next morning it cleared; one blast of the north wind and the whole world was frozen solid as a jade box, dazzling and bright. After breakfast Zhongqing and Wang Xun rode together in one carriage, their two attendants on horseback, and came to call on Ziyu. Halfway there they met another carriage coming toward them; both parties' attendants dismounted.

Wang Xun looked and saw it was Sun Sizhui. The two carriages drew up face to face; Wang Xun asked: "Where are you headed?" Sizhui said: "Owing to the fact that my father and his wife have been at cross purposes — consuming and obliterating, with blood nearly flowing in rivers — a physician surnamed Wang has been treating man with man's own means to eradicate the old taint; looking askance at the situation, he says there is no injury. Now that the illness is somewhat better, one cannot but soothe and bring them around." Wang Xun smiled and said: "I will come by when I return." Sizhui agreed and hurried away. Zhongqing said: "This gentleman deploys his literary style in every conceivable context — truly absurd and laughable. This 'worm-eaten Thousand Character Classic' style is fit to be his name in life and his posthumous title in death; I doubt there is a second person in the world who resembles him in this." Wang Xun laughed: "As I see it, I fear that when it comes time for him to perform his conjugal duties he will still have to compose a couplet or two for the occasion."

One does feel sorry for that sister-in-law of ours — though not especially beautiful, she is dignified and chaste, silent and unsmiling. Having married this sort of man — truly she will harbor regret for the rest of her life." Zhongqing laughed: "Perhaps he has one virtue that makes up for it — one never knows." Talking and laughing all the way, they arrived at the Mei residence.

The gatekeeper announced them; Ziyu came out to welcome them inside, and said: "The two brothers have written excellent poems — my admiration is boundless. My own rough scribbles are inevitably a case of a minor shaman meeting a greater one." Zhongqing said: "What my brother and the others have done — how can it compare with your fine compositions, which repay repeated murmured recitation? Refined and fitting in every respect." Wang Xun said: "What I love most are these two poems — Snow Feeling and Snow Color — fresh and vigorous, combining the virtues of Geng Jing and Bao Zhao at once." Ziyu said: "Jiantan's four poems breathe ice and snow throughout, with pearls and jade in hand. The opening lines of Snow Mountain and Snow Pagoda arrive with the force of open sky — especially transcendent. As for Jiantan's finest individual couplets — 'but those mouse-like things only bask in others' warmth,' and 'in the deep of night, pondering — it has become flowing water' — the depth of feeling, the care of expression, a single-minded ardor going forward: as Dongpo put it, one who does not eat the food of this human world — surely work destined to endure." Zhongqing said: "One borrows from the scene to give expression to feeling on a whim — how lightly you say the word 'endure.'" Wang Xun said: "That seven-character quatrain and that five-character regulated poem — whose hand are they?" Ziyu smiled: "Have you not tried to guess?" Wang Xun then reported what had been said the day before; Ziyu said: "Jiantan's eye is as sharp as ever. Have you added your comments?" Wang Xun took the poems from his sleeve; Ziyu read the comment on the five-character regulated poem but could not make out what the allusion meant — who were Yuan and Wei?

Wang Xun then also told him about the Sun brothers; Ziyu laughed, and had someone summon Pincai and Yuanmao to come out. Everyone met. Ziyu returned each person's poems and said: "These assessments were made by Elder Brother Yan — he was most complimentary." Pincai read the comment and thought to himself: "This Yan Zhongqing is truly versed in everything ancient and modern — even the opera tunes I borrowed were spotted by him." He then went and thanked Zhongqing. Zhongqing said: "Brother Wei's poetic style is quite fine — both sound and rhythm are excellent; you must compose frequently, as though you have an aria perpetually on your lips." Pincai said: "I have no foundation to speak of, and have neglected it for these several years — what can I call a poem at this point? So long as the tonal pattern is not violated, that is all." Ziyu said to Zhongqing: "Brother Pincai's poem is not too far off the mark." Zhongqing nodded. Yuanmao, meanwhile, pressed the circled couplets and the critical note up close to his face, reading them over and over again for quite some time; then he put the verse sheet on the tea table, folded it twice with both hands, undid the buttons of his fur coat, and tucked it inside his lapel. Wang Xun said: "Elder Brother Li, you are carrying a great deal more, aren't you." Li Yuanmao, thinking he was remarking on the moth-holes in his fur coat, answered without thinking: "The moth-holes are still manageable. Coming by the water route, it was shut away in the hold of the boat and absorbed the moisture — got a bit moth-eaten as a result. I have been wearing it through this one winter; next year I will have to have it seen to." Everyone heard this and had no idea what he was talking about. Pincai, realizing he had misheard, said: "Elder Brother Wang is saying you have written a great many poems — not referring to your fur coat." Everyone then understood; seeing Yuanmao's face flush deep red, unable to say a word, they all had to suppress their laughter. Zhongqing asked: "In your composition, 'long horses' and 'white people' — I imagine these use Mencius; but the three characters 'both eyes draw near' — is there a source for this?" Yuanmao gave Zhongqing two long looks and said: "I never rely on sources.

I have seen in the poems of the ancients that they sometimes write themselves into the poem — it was that intention." Wang Xun suddenly understood. After more idle conversation, Zhongqing and Wang Xun said their farewells; Ziyu and the others escorted them to the door. Zhongqing said: "Why not come out with us to see the snow scenery?" Yuanmao, hearing this, was quite willing to go. Ziyu said: "The teacher has not fully recovered today — we must stay home to attend to him. We will accompany you another time." Yuanmao stuck out his lip but said nothing. Zhongqing and the others bid farewell and departed; Ziyu saw them out the front gate, came back inside, and chatted with Pincai and Yuanmao for a while about the poems, then suddenly again brought up Qinguan. Seeing that Pincai had developed a certain intent, he quietly threw out a hint: "Another day, why not steal a moment to come with me and make Qinguan's acquaintance?" Yuanmao said: "Let us go tomorrow — I shall just say I am going to see friends who came down the same route." He pointed at Ziyu: "And you say you are going to the Wang family to return a call. Once we are out of these two confining doors, what is there to fear?" Ziyu laughed: "In a few days' time." But let us leave this side of the story for now.

To return to Zhongqing and Wang Xun: they walked out of South Small Street to the Low Hollow to take in the view. All was a vast expanse of white; fields and paths were indistinguishable. Far off they could see Xu Ziyun's Garden of Ease, with its jadeite trees rising in uneven lines, mists and mountain vapors swirling and gathering, layer upon layer of gleaming multi-storied buildings, and the faint outline of high pavilions touching the clouds. They gazed for a while, then saw a carriage coming toward them from ahead. The person perched on the running-board spotted them first and immediately jumped down; then a young performer stepped down from the carriage as well. Zhongqing and the others quickly urged him not to bother; the young performer then leaned out of the carriage and settled himself — carved and powdered like jade, gilded and ornamented — it was none other than Lin Chunxi, the youngest of the performers listed in the Flower Selection. Wang Xun asked: "Where are you coming from?" Chunxi said: "I am coming back from the Garden of Ease. Are you going there too?" Zhongqing said: "We are just looking at the snow scenery, and will be turning back soon." Wang Xun said: "Why do we not stop at that restaurant on Small Street and sit a while? One can also take in the distant scenery from there." Chunxi said: "If you are in the mood, I will be happy to accompany you." Zhongqing said: "Excellent." They turned their carriages about; arriving at Small Street, they found a restaurant with two facing balconies, east and west. Zhongqing and the others went up to the east balcony; though the day was cold, there was no wind up there.

Zhongqing had the windows thrown open; the view extended a great distance. They ordered food and chatted idly for a while. Chunxi said: "This month the eight of us have been holding gatherings at the Garden of Ease every three days — a Winter Solstice club. Today is the fifth meeting. At each meeting there must be one entertainment — sometimes a drinking game, sometimes a parlor game. Today Duxiang wanted us to write poems; he set the topic Ice Bed, with each person writing one seven-character regulated poem, and Su Meixiang was judged first." Zhongqing said: "Do you remember his poem?" Chunxi said: "I only remember the middle four lines." He then recited:

"Oars and punts have quite become a bed most stable;
storms and waves are no different from a level road.
Who says the Blue Sea is hard to fill to fullness?
I refuse to believe that Penglai's path cannot be passed."

"Everyone said his use of figures was wonderfully inspired, without a single flat line — which is why he surpassed the others." Wang Xun said: "And yours?" Chunxi said: "Mine was not good — I cannot remember it." Zhongqing said: "I imagine you came eighth." Chunxi laughed sheepishly: "You guessed it on the first try." Wang Xun said: "No wonder — he is only fourteen years old; if he were to study for two years, do you think he could not catch up with them?" Chunxi said: "I was not going to compose at all, but they absolutely insisted I do. Today none of the poems were anything remarkable; it was only that Ruixiang and I got the tonal pattern wrong — as a result Ruixiang was placed seventh and I was placed eighth. Never again shall I write such incompetent poetry. Let me study for a year and then come back and compete with them." Then he added: "Two new performers have come to our company — one is called Qinguan, one is called Qiguan. Have you met them?" Zhongqing said: "The other day Ruixiang mentioned the two of them — but just as he was starting to say something, someone interrupted and he never finished." Wang Xun asked: "What are these two like?" Chunxi said: "Excellent. That Qinguan is on a par with Yaoqing. That Qiguan — it is hard to say who ranks higher, him or Ruixiang. Neither has appeared on stage yet, but every day there are already three to five requests for them. The other day Duxiang saw them and praised them tremendously, immediately giving them fine gifts and having several complete new sets of costumes made for them.

These two are going to make a great name for themselves. Only Qinguan's temperament is a bit cool — he is not very easy to talk to." Just as they were deep in conversation here, suddenly the window on the opposite west balcony opened with a sound. Zhongqing and the others looked up to see a beautiful young man, splendidly dressed — wearing a white heron-frost fur cloak, a violet sable cap on his head, a face like polished jade, lips red as if painted, his gaze bright with something of a cloud-piercing spirit, his bearing most refined, his manner extraordinary. To judge by his age, he appeared to be a little over twenty; he had four young performers in his company, and was leaning against the balcony window gazing out. Zhongqing and Wang Xun were privately astonished: looking at his appearance and bearing, he was fully a match for Yuxiang — truly a phoenix among men. From his accent, he seemed to be from Jiangning, yet there was also something of a Yangzhou intonation mixed in. Looking at the four young performers, they were none of them celebrated names or proven talent — no more than common blossoms of the flower world. Wang Xun recognized one as Rongguan; the other three he did not know, and so asked Chunxi. Chunxi said: "The one in tinted sable is Yu Mei; the one in Japanese-sword-patterned fur is Si Xi; the one in otter is Quanfu. All three are from the Jianchun company." Just then the young man glanced over at this balcony once, and turned his back and sat down again. They could hear the young performers on that side chattering like orioles and swallows, with cups and counters flying about — quite lively. By comparison, the three on this side felt rather like the cold and lean poets of the Jiao and Jia school. They heard the beautiful young man say: "I hear people say that of the theater companies, Lianjin and Lianzhu are the finest. But listening to those two companies, all the performers are old; not a single fine-looking young dan in the Kunqu roles. What circulates through the gardens is nothing but defeated soldiers and broken remnants — I truly cannot understand why people say they are good."

Rongguan said: "Our two Lian companies mostly do private banquet performances; the few good performers who sing Kunqu are always at banquets and rarely come to the public gardens. A refined person such as yourself — how is it that you do not appreciate Kunqu and instead prefer the clapper operas?" The young man laughed: "I pay attention to people, not to opera; I would rather have a vulgar opera with refined people than a refined opera with vulgar people." They also heard Yu Mei say: "It is all opera-singing — why distinguish between Kunqu and clapper style? Even granting that Kunqu has finer verse, it was still composed by the ancients and not by yourselves. The clapper style is merely rougher; in terms of conveying feeling and spirit it is fundamentally the same. What is most laughable is those people who talk only of Kunqu and do not like erhuang. In your two Lian companies, once those few people leave the company and stop performing, there will be no one left who can hold things together — and audiences will likely dwindle. The company might even disband." Si Xi said: "As I see it, it is all the same — erhuang is opera, Kunqu is opera; learn whichever you learn and sing whichever you sing." Rongguan laughed: "Enough — let us not talk about opera; let us drink." They could then be heard playing finger-guessing games and singing drinking songs for a while. Then the young man spoke again: "When I listen to opera I do not listen to the words; I listen entirely to the tonal melody. It is not that I am unaware of Kunqu's moral dignity and musical elegance — but listening to it is like reading Song dynasty poetry: the rhythms are gentle and peaceful, yet the feeling lacks excitement and urgency. When I listen to the sounds of the zheng zither, pipa lute, and string instruments — with their dense and rapid notes and rhythms — there is a fullness and richness of feeling left over, capable of stirring a man to ardor and exaltation, to a vigor of all four limbs and an outpouring of all seven emotions. Even the clapper style, though it is certainly not orthodox music, has something of a quality of rises and falls, pauses and turns — something that moves one to contemplation, to dwell on the present and recall the past, like Ma Zhou passing through Xinfeng or Wei Su crossing the river south, with a chest full of sorrow and lament, feeling tangled and lingering — able to stir the deepest emotions of the lone traveler and the wandering scholar. People say that the sound of the huqin fiddle is the most licentious. But when I hear it I am overcome by sorrow, and it often moves me to tears — just as Dongpo in his Ode on the Cave Flute said: 'like weeping, like complaining, like sobbing, like beseeching' — like the grief of a banished official ten thousand li from home, like the weeping of a widow in a lone boat on the river, each note entering the very depths of the heart. I cannot understand why people call it a licentious sound. Or is it that my ears differ from other ears, that my feeling is out of accord with ordinary feeling? As for the sounds of plucked strings and drums — I hear them and my heart and breath grow calm; there is nothing that moves me. That is how it is when I listen — I wonder whether others find it the same?" The four young performers could none of them answer.

Zhongqing said quietly to Wang Xun: "This man's views are partial, yet he has his own private understanding — his unwillingness to follow the common herd is already evident. Moreover his chest must hold much accumulated resentment, which is why he dislikes the peaceful and prefers the stirring. String music is inherently sorrowful; his saying that the huqin is not licentious — now that is indeed a view that breaks from convention, one that no one has ever perceived before. I look at this man and he seems precisely our sort — certainly not a mediocre person. Someday I would like to seek him out." Wang Xun said: "From what he has said and his manner and bearing, one can already form a general impression." Just then the young man asked one of the restaurant's people for brush and inkstone, and on the plastered wall he wrote a few lines; then, with his four young performers, he descended the stairs and left. Zhongqing and the others also stopped drinking, told the attendants to settle the bill, took Chunxi with them, and came over to the west balcony. There, in flowing strokes of dense ink, in a fine bold cursive hand, he had written a ci poem to the tune Langtuasha:

The red sun has already sloped to the west—
I smile, watching cloud and rosy haze.
The jade dragon's scales have scattered and spread across all the sky.
I yearn for the spring wind to come ten thousand li
and blow away every jade flower.

Do not boast of worldly things—
no thought of them is without error.
The immortal of Penglai reaches down for the cloud carriage.
Drunk, I ask the guest dwelling in the Great Luo Heaven above—
the colored phoenix—whose home does it grace?

Zhongqing and Wang Xun read it and both nodded in admiration. Chunxi said: "This ci poem seems written by an immortal — there is a transcendent, unearthly quality to it." Zhongqing said: "This man is one of pure and unconstrained spirit, free and unrestrained — yet the ones he appreciates are that company of young performers. Truly baffling." They then looked at the signature: "Xiangfan — written drunk." They could not tell his family name or given name; so they called the restaurant keeper and asked whether he recognized the man. The keeper answered: "This lord came here for the first time today; when they settled the bill, his second attendant paid in cash, and we never asked his name or address." Zhongqing said: "This ci poem is very fine — the brush of a man of exceptional talent. It illuminates your humble establishment; by all means preserve it and do not scrub it off." The keeper agreed and went off. Chunxi said: "As for this man's background, Rongguan ought to know — when I see him I will ask, and then we will know what manner of person this is."
