Chapter 5 — Yuan Baozhu introduces Du Qinyan; Third Master Fu gives a detailed account of Lord Hua.

The previous chapter told of Lin Chunxi and Zhongqing discussing the poetry-gathering at the Yiyuan garden. Now let us set the stage for that garden. There was a prince among young noblemen, a leader of literary men, by the name of Xu Ziyun, courtesy name Duxiang, a native of Shanyin County in Zhejiang. Speaking of his family lineage, it was truly among the foremost of the age, boasting seven generations of official distinction, with grandfathers and grandsons serving as prime ministers, fathers and sons as Ministers of State, and brothers as governors-general. Of Xu Ziyun's own line, his father, named Zhen, rose through the Hanlin Academy and now served as Grand Secretary and Governor-General of Guangdong. His elder brother, Zirong, also a Hanlin graduate, had served as a censor and been appointed Inspector of the Huai-Yang Circuit.

His mother had gone to Guangdong to accompany his father's posting; only Ziyun himself remained in the capital. This Ziyun was cultivated and elegant in appearance, distinguished and peerless in character, magnanimous beyond measure, and deeply versed in the classics and histories. He was twenty-five years of age, holding a sinecure post as a departmental secretary through hereditary privilege, and at twenty-two had also passed the provincial examinations as a juren. His wife, née Yuan, was twenty-three years old, daughter of Yuan Hao, the current Governor of Yunnan. She was surpassingly beautiful, supremely virtuous, and possessed literary gifts of the orchid and cassia. She and Ziyun were perfectly matched, a talented man and a beauty, loving and mutually respectful, as lute paired with jade se. They already had one son and one daughter.

Ziyun lived amid luxury and splendor. He had no trace of dissolution or extravagance. He disliked the constraints of official dress and decorum. He preferred the pleasures of hills and valleys as his enjoyment. Though still in his twenties, he already possessed the refined taste for music of Xie An of Dongshan, and the pleasures of lute and wine-cups of Kong Rong of Beihai. In front of his residence lay a large open plot of land, five or six li in circumference, with naturally rolling hills, natural hollows, ancient trees, and gnarled pines. It had originally been a ruined garden belonging to some former family.

Ziyun purchased this plot and expanded it, buying up the nearby residences at generous prices.

He had a close friend from Xiangtan County in southern Hunan, a man named Xiao Cixian, courtesy name Jingyi. Thirty-two years of age, he was a renowned man of letters who had come to the capital to sit for examinations as a distinguished tribute student. Yet he had no desire for trifling titles and no ambition to advance. In astronomy and geography, in the writings of all the philosophers and the hundred schools, there was nothing he had not mastered. He and Ziyun had sworn brotherhood, and over the course of three or four years spent enormous effort supervising the construction of the Yiyuan garden. The result was a place of driving clouds and shifting mountains, a sweep of soaring towers and layered pavilions, secluded recesses and winding depths. In the magnificence of its flowers, plants, and scenic walks it surpassed all others in the capital, comprising twenty-four scenic areas with over four hundred rooms. Great hills linked together, streams wound in curving courses; its wonders were beyond description. Ziyun's reputation and connections were vast, and scholars from all directions gathered like stars and massing clouds.

In character, Ziyun was noble and sincere, responsive to every request and sparing no effort. He cherished the choosing of friends, not by power or status, but by virtue and principle. Though every day his seats were full of guests and wine cups never empty, only a few true kindred spirits were his daily companions; the rest were merely received with proper courtesy, their wishes accommodated as best as possible. The eight famous dan performers from Shi Nanxiang's Flower Register came and went daily. Ziyun treasured them all, but singled out Yuan Baozhu for particular affection. His feeling of love and appreciation for beautiful performers was quite different from others. He regarded these fine young opera players as he would rare treasures, fine birds, and celebrated flowers. He held only a caring and admiring heart, with no thought of vulgar intimacy. And so these famous performers all forgot the distances of social rank with him, treating him as a kind father or caring mother, able to speak openly of anything, free to follow their hearts. Xiao Cixian was even more pure and detached, taking no pleasure in worldly amusements.

Thus these two men not only sharpened each other in virtue and letters, but in temperament and spirit had no barriers between them whatsoever.

One day, at a theatrical gathering, Ziyun encountered two newly arrived performers, Qin Guan and Qi Guan, and was greatly impressed. He marveled that such talent was unprecedented, seeing that they were perfectly suited to stand alongside the eight famous dan performers. This kindled his desire to cultivate their acquaintance. He asked Yuan Baozhu to bring them to the garden another day. Seeing that their clothing was not yet fine enough, he immediately had several new outfits made overnight and presented them as gifts. The two performers were naturally grateful. But this Qin Guan was different from ordinary people. Let us first briefly describe his background.

Qin Guan's surname was Du. His father was known as Du Qinshi, Master Du the Qin-player, who made his living by crafting and performing on the qin zither. The sons of gentry throughout Jiangsu vied to invite him to give lessons, and so everyone called him Du Qinshi. When this son was born, he was named after the qin, and called Qin Guan.

Qin Guan bore auspicious markings on his palm and was clever from earliest childhood; his parents loved him as their most precious treasure. When he reached ten years of age, Du Qinshi was suddenly humiliated and beaten by a powerful lord, smashed his qin in rage and grief, and died. His mother, a year later, also fell ill with sorrow and died. Left without support, the orphaned Qin Guan was taken in by a clan uncle. When he was thirteen, this uncle too died. His widowed aunt could not maintain her chastity and remarried, so she sold Qin Guan into a theater troupe. Just then Ye Maolin happened to see him and bought him out of the troupe, taking him along to the capital. Qin Guan had begun to learn characters at six, was exceptionally gifted, and could memorize anything upon a single reading. By thirteen he had read many books, including poetry collections, ci-lyrics, miscellaneous essays, tales and popular fiction, and understood them all clearly. His heart aspired to lofty things and his nature was one of great purity; he possessed the ambition of a mountain pheasant dancing before a mirror, a vermilion phoenix perching on its proper plane-tree. When he was forced into the theater troupe, he had tried several times to hang himself, but failed each time to die, and so he used the troupe's disdain for him as a means to preserve his integrity. When Ye Maolin brought him to the capital and treated him with fresh care and regard as a rare treasure, who would not have found peace? Yet he had another worry: having climbed out of the bottom of a well, he feared he had only fallen into the bottom of the sea. He worried that the coral net would be hard to find, that bright pearls might be wasted in darkness, that his jade, like the fabled gem of Bian He, might go forever unrecognized, and that drawing his sword might only bring suspicion. So he was always melancholy. The night before entering the capital, he had a dream: he dreamed of a place where ten thousand plum trees bloomed, their fragrant snow filling the air like a sea. He was strolling in delight when suddenly his body sank into a pit.

The earth was about to reach the top of his head. He was in desperate peril. Suddenly a beautiful youth appeared, his face as luminous as jade and divine. With one hand he lifted Qin Guan out. Qin Guan was overwhelmed with gratitude and was about to bow in thanks. The young man turned gracefully, walked into the plum grove, and vanished. Qin Guan went in to search and found a large plum fruit growing on a plum tree; looking closely, he saw it was made of jade. Then he awoke. The next day upon entering the city, jostled in a traffic jam on the road, he saw Ziyu, who was the very person who had saved him in the dream. He was greatly astonished and could only stare at him dazedly for a while. But they were strangers on the road; he did not know the young man's name or address, and had no way to make inquiries. Whenever he attended theatrical gatherings or visited the theaters, he looked everywhere, but did not see him again. When he later met Xu Ziyun, who greatly appreciated him and gave him many clothes and objects, he became uncertain and wary. Knowing Ziyun was a nobleman of great wealth and rank, he feared such a man must carry the haughty airs of the powerful, and was very reluctant to approach him. But unable to escape his master's command, he had no choice but to go and pay his respects.

That day, Qi Guan had caught a cold and could not get up, so Yuan Baozhu came first to Qin Guan's lodgings. Baozhu's appearance had already been described in the Flower Register: his nature gentle and yielding, his looks like a maiden's. He too was attracted to Qin Guan, whose appearance resembled his own. Though they had just met, it was as if they were long-standing friends. After several heart-to-heart conversations, Qin Guan had also come to value Baozhu's character, seeing him as someone who kept his own purity and self-respect. Baozhu then described Ziyun's fine qualities in detail, and Qin Guan felt somewhat reassured. The two of them rode in a carriage together, Qin Guan in front, Baozhu behind. By happy coincidence, they reached the South Small Street intersection. They met Ziyu returning from Shi Nanxiang's, in a carriage with two horses, coming directly toward them. Ziyu had not drawn his curtain; Qin Guan had drawn his, but through the glass window he had already seen clearly.

Unable to help himself, he raised the curtain, revealing a face of unparalleled beauty. Ziyu caught a glimpse and recognized the very Qin Guan he had encountered before, the one Pincai had told him about, the one he had been thinking of day and night. Joy spread across his face and he smiled. He glimpsed Qin Guan's clear bright eyes and slightly parted crimson lips. Then the curtain fell, and in an instant both carriages had sped away from each other like wind and lightning. Ziyu saw that the carriage today was finer and more beautiful than before, and thought admiringly to himself: "Truly, a night-luminous gem cannot be concealed, a bright moon shines of its own accord. Naturally he has met a true connoisseur. But I wonder what sort of person that connoisseur is." He also thought: "Pincai said his temper is peculiar and he is extremely proud. He must be one who chooses whom to follow, and would certainly never drift with the current to seek a momentary meeting." On Qin Guan's side, he was thinking: "This young nobleman, his elegance is in his bones, his beauty in his spirit; his warmth and gentleness overflow naturally. He must be a righteous man of genuine feeling, with not a trace of selfish or improper intent. In my dream he lifted me from the mud, surely in the future he is the one meant to raise me up. Otherwise, why would I meet him so soon after dreaming of him? But in the dream, I saw him walk beneath the plum trees and disappear, and then I saw a jade plum. What does that signify?" He kept turning this over in his mind, lost in reverie.

Not long after, they entered the Yiyuan garden. Baozhu inquired and learned that Ziyun was that day at the Begonia Spring Court. This Begonia Spring Court had open terraces and winding covered galleries, intimate chambers and secluded rooms, all connecting in a continuous chain of over twenty bays. Baozhu led them inside to a suite of three rooms, where Ziyun and Cixian were sitting around a charcoal brazier drinking together. Seeing the two arrivals, both welcomed them with warm, smiling faces.

Qin Guan timidly went forward, made two bows of greeting, expressed his thanks, and stood with bowed head. Ziyun and Cixian saw that today, in fine and colorful dress, his appearance was even more beautiful than before. But they saw his tentative, bashful, nervous bearing. Feelings of tender regard arose in them unbidden, and they invited him to sit. Qin Guan sat close beside Baozhu. Ziyun smiled warmly and said: "When we first met the other day, we could not speak at length. Please tell me in detail about your background, your family, and how you came to enter the theater troupe." When asked about his background, Qin Guan's accumulated sorrow was stirred; without realizing it, his face flushed rose-red and his eyes brimmed with pearly tears. He steadied himself, but could not refuse to answer, so adopting the Mandarin pronunciation and setting aside his Suzhou accent, he recounted his family history. When he came to his parents' deaths, his uncle's care, his uncle's death, and his aunt's remarriage and abandonment of him, his voice became like a gentle breeze playing a flute, softly lamenting. Ziyun and Cixian were quite moved and earnestly consoled him.

They then asked what operas he had studied, which pieces he knew, and Qin Guan answered them. Cixian said:

"In what way does he resemble a performer at all? What a pity that such spiritual luminosity as heaven and earth has produced, instead of being bestowed upon a fragrant boudoir, is given to the singing stage. Wearing a cap instead of hairpins, shoes instead of skirts. It is truly a lamentable matter." Ziyun said: "He and Yaoqing truly could be called a matched pair soaring under auspicious clouds, racing a thousand li abreast. If their hats and shoes were exchanged for hairpins and skirts, I fear even the Two Beauties east of the River could scarcely be compared with them. It must be that the Creator intends to allow a few such extraordinary figures to emerge from this world, washing away all the commonplace and vulgar habits of former times, who can say for certain?" He then said to Qin Guan: "Here is different from other places, you need not feel like a stranger. Follow Yaoqing in everything; whatever he does, you do likewise. If you wish to know what we are like, simply ask him in detail.

"Here Yaoqing is not treated as an ordinary performer. All forms of address are different from the outside world: everyone addresses each other by courtesy names, and formal salutations are not required. If you are inclined, please come here often in your free time. There is no need to stand on ceremony, for ceremony creates distance." Qin Guan could only agree. He looked at the two men again. Both were of extraordinary bearing, serene and approachable, and he knew they were not ordinary men. Cixian said to Ziyun: "What you have said is exactly right. At this moment he still does not know what our temperament is like; he may suppose that wealth and rank must bring with them proud and extravagant airs, not knowing that we most dislike such things. As for your natural gifts, nothing need be said. But a person's elegant demeanor and refined bearing all come from books; without having read and understood the principles of learning, one's speech and bearing will inevitably fall short of true refinement. Have you studied any books?" Before Qin Guan could answer, Baozhu laughed and said:

"His mind is far stronger than ours! If we were tested right now, I'm afraid even Meixiang could not surpass him." Ziyun was even more pleased and asked Qin Guan: "Tell me honestly, have you studied books?" Qin Guan replied: "I have studied for five or six years." Cixian asked: "What have you read?" Qin Guan replied: "Besides the Four Books, I read through the Shi Lei Fu, a rhapsody on classes of things, and two volumes of Tang poetry." Ziyun said: "That is quite enough. Can you compose poetry?" Qin Guan said: "I cannot." Baozhu said: "That is only because he has never been taught; once he begins studying he will certainly be able to do it at once. The other day he told me about the operas, which pieces are fine, which are not, and he explained every point without error. With such innate gifts, how could he fail to learn?" Qin Guan lowered his head and said nothing. Ziyun said:

"His name is not suitable. Jingyi, please give him a new character, let us change this word 'guan'. And give him a courtesy name as well." Cixian thought for a moment and said: "What about changing it to Qin Yan, meaning Lute-Words, with the courtesy name Yunong, meaning Jade Youth. Would that do?" Ziyun said: "Very good. The two characters 'Qin Yan' are both fresh and elegant; and 'Yunong' as a courtesy name suits the person perfectly." Baozhu told Qin Guan to express his thanks; Qin Guan rose and made two bows. Cixian said: "We have already said all this, why are you bowing again?" Ziyun said: "Let us establish a rule: on festival days and occasions for congratulations you may bow; in all other ordinary meetings it is entirely dispensed with. The word 'Master' is forever forbidden from your lips. Address me simply as Duxiang, and him as Jingyi." Qin Yan stood and said: "How could I dare?" Ziyun said: "If you insist on refusing, it is as if we were the same as common men, that would not be honoring us but distancing yourself from us. Besides, what weight does 'Master' carry? Everyone outside, without exception, is called 'Master.' When you address others, naturally you must do the same; but here, such forms are unnecessary." Qin Guan still dared not agree, and Baozhu laughed: "Since Duxiang has already given his word, just call him Duxiang." Qin Yan saw that Baozhu called him by his courtesy name directly. Since this was still really their first meeting, he himself felt somewhat awkward, and so he gave a small smile. Ziyun, seeing this smile, lips like cherries, teeth like strung pearls, beauty blooming on fragrant cheeks, brightness rippling in clear eyes, felt dazzled and stirred to the depths of his heart, and knew a great blossoming of joy. He ordered a servant to set out wine, and the four of them sat together at the table. During the meal, Baozhu guided and instructed Qin Yan in all manner of things. Seeing that neither Xiao nor Xu showed any teasing or flirtatious words or intentions, that their speech was refined and their bearing proper, they were truly men one could approach and trust. Qin Yan gradually relaxed, his spirit settling and his breath becoming easy. Baozhu also went through several drinking games with him and told him about many famous scholars and men of letters of the day, and how he should conduct himself when he met them. Qin Yan listened to everything attentively. In his heart, he also thought again of the young nobleman he had glimpsed in the carriage, wondering whether Baozhu knew him, whether Duxiang was acquainted with him. He still did not know the man's name, and had no way to inquire. That day they lingered in the Yiyuan for half a day; after the wine was done, Ziyun and Cixian led him through a tour of the garden. The rooms and furnishings, antiques and curios were all things he had never seen in his life, and he took pleasure in looking at each one carefully. Ziyun gave him many more gifts and told him to come and ask for anything he desired. Qin Yan expressed his thanks and took his leave. From this point on, he came often to the Yiyuan together with Baozhu and the other famous performers; after a few visits he felt quite at home there. But let us set this aside for now.

Now let us return to Ziyu, who had again encountered Qin Guan that day and was in excellent spirits. Returning home, he immediately sought out Pincai to tell him what had happened. Pincai was somewhat pleased as well, and proceeded to describe to Ziyu the scene on the road. It turned out that when Pincai and Ye Maolin had been traveling together and reached Jining Prefecture, all the performers had gone ashore, but he found Qin Guan alone in the boat, in tears. He asked about his troubles, but Qin Guan would answer nothing. When Pincai suggested he might not want to go onstage for fear of disgracing his parents' memory, Qin Guan gave him a single glance. Pincai then tried to press further, not pausing to consider whether he was overstepping, and took out a handkerchief to wipe Qin Guan's tears. Just as he was about to do so, Qin Guan snatched it away with one hand and threw it into the river, then covered his face and wept. Pincai had resented him for this ever since. Now seeing Ziyu's delight, he carelessly let this episode slip out. Ziyu felt only deeper admiration and respect, admiring the chastity and reserve, the inviolable dignity. From respect grew love, from love grew longing; these two feelings turned like a waterwheel in his heart.

And so it is that among all the treasures in the world, true beauty is the foremost. If it is genuine beauty, there is no one in the world who does not love it. Ziyu's experience at the theater the other day had been such that Baozhu seemed to have made some impression on him; his heart ought to have been forever unmoved. Yet who would have known that after only two encounters with Qin Guan, it was like a flash of lightning or a spark of flint, passing in an instant without a trace, and yet his heart was now constantly thinking of him? How much more so for others, whose self-restraint was not as great as Ziyu's, and who could be with beautiful people day and night. Naturally their love and longing would be even more intense, transforming tempered steel into pliant gold thread. Pincai knew that he and Qin Guan had no karmic affinity and could get nowhere; though he admired Qin Guan's looks, he genuinely resented his temperament. Now seeing Ziyu's infatuation, viewing the inside situation as an outsider, he merely offered encouraging words and a flattering manner, but he himself was not particularly invested. Shortly after, Ziyu went out, and Pincai set the whole matter aside.

One day the weather turned clear and mild. The snow had melted, and Pincai thought of Fu Sanziye and wished to go into the city to visit him. He had his boy Sier hire a carriage and set off toward the Eastern City. Coming from the opposite direction was a crowd of horses and carriages rushing forward like the wind. First came a mounted outrider, then a pair of lead horses, then a carriage with its side-curtain hanging open. Pincai leaned out to look and caught, quick as a lightning flash, a glimpse of a beautiful young man with fine brows and bright eyes. His bearing was magnificent as a divinity, like the morning sun glorious among clouds and mist, like a crane in flight over the Isle of Penglai. He was clearly about twenty years of age.

He wore an embroidered python-robe with sable cloak, a magnificent official hat, and court shoes. Behind him came twenty or thirty mounted attendants, all in freshly made uniforms of matching color. Then followed over ten cloth-curtained heated carriages, inside which sat some powder-and-jade-carved children, also resembling small dan performers. Behind these came four or five large carts laden with trunks, clothing bundles, tea stoves, wine boxes, portable kitchens, and other such articles. The drivers were all dressed in short silk jackets, brocade trousers, damask stockings, and satin shoes, striding along in magnificent, vigorous fashion. The procession passed for quite some time. Pincai thought: "What sort of person is this, with such a grand retinue?" Just then he heard his driver say: "Sir, do you know who this person is?" Pincai replied: "I have no idea. What person is this, so wealthy?" The driver said: "This is the magnificent young nobleman of Jinchun Garden. There is a saying in the capital that everyone repeats: 'In the city, one star; beyond the walls, one cloud. Two great young noblemen, more grand than all the world.' As for this young nobleman's family background, I don't know the full story. I only know his father is a duke and currently serves as Commander-General of the Western Frontier.

"His residence takes up three or four li in circumference. They have a Master of Stables called Master Lu, and I once heard him say they have a hundred-odd horses and seventy or eighty large mules. Would you say that's wealthy?" Pincai asked: "What is his surname?" The driver replied: "His surname is Hua, people all call him Young Master Hua." Pincai asked: "The men on horseback must be his household servants; but those children in the carriages, who look like opera performers, what are they?" The driver said:

"They are xiangong, opera boys."

"He keeps his own opera troupe at home. Whenever he is invited out to drink and watch a performance, he insists on bringing his own troupe to perform a couple of scenes. Even for performers outside, if one catches his eye, he spares no expense to buy them and take them home. I hear one of his personal attendants is also an opera boy, whom he bought last year for eight thousand taels of silver. Just think of that extravagance! Who could possibly match him?" Pincai said: "Truly magnificent. But do his parents just let him carry on like this without interfering?" The driver replied: "His father and mother are ten thousand li away! Besides, his family has boundless silver. So what if he spends a little more, what does it matter? Today he must be going out to a banquet, which is why he has his troupe with him." As they spoke, they entered the Eastern City and arrived at the Gold Pailou. Finding a large doorway opposite a tea shop, the carriage stopped. Pincai had Sier take in his calling card, waiting in the carriage himself. He saw two sealed notices on the wall: one for a former Vice-Minister of War, the other for the Jiangnan Bureau of the Ministry of Revenue. Someone in the gatehouse took the card and went inside; shortly after, he returned and said, "Please come in." Pincai stepped down and followed the gatekeeper inside. Passing through the second gate, they entered a courtyard leading to a covered passageway.

Through the passageway was the main hall, flanked by six wing-rooms on either side. Fu San was already standing beneath the eaves of the main house, coming forward to welcome him. Pincai hurried up and clasped his hands in greeting. Fu San led him behind the main hall to sit in a pair of small study rooms, exchanging polite inquiries about each other's health. Pincai said: "The snow delayed me these past few days, otherwise I would have come to pay my respects the day before yesterday. I felt so restless at home, thinking of you constantly, Third Brother." Fu San replied: "The feeling is mutual." This place was Fu San's study, situated close to the inner chambers, separated only by a courtyard. Pincai briefly took in the room's furnishings: the space was divided by a floor-to-ceiling partition of nanmu wood carved with ice-crackle patterns. In the upper room was a wooden kang, adorned with a set of four small landscape screens, and a striking clock rested on the kang table. Nearby stood a square table with several chairs, and in the center sat a large copper coal-brazier. On the wall hung a silk-scroll couplet, and beside it an intricate Western painting. The kang cushions were of sapphire-blue satin. A servant entered, wearing a plain silk fur-lined jacket and a fur hat pulled down over his eyebrows, revealing half of a large braided queue at the back. Carrying a tea tray, he first handed a cup to Pincai. Pincai said: "Please convey my respects to the Madam." Before the servant could reply, Fu San said: "Your sister-in-law is not home today; she has gone to visit her parents. Stay and have a meal with me today, and we can chat." Pincai readily agreed, then asked: "Will Eldest Brother be coming today?" Fu San replied: "I'm not sure. I heard him say yesterday he had business and needed to go to Jinchun Garden to ask Young Master Hua to put in a good word. I imagine he has gone there by now." Hearing of Young Master Hua of Jinchun Garden, Pincai asked: "I was just going to ask about that Young Master Hua." He then recounted what he had seen on the road and what the driver had told him. Fu San said: "What does a driver know! This Young Master Hua's name is Guangsu, courtesy name Xingbei. His father is a hereditary first-class duke, currently serving as Commander-General of the Western Frontier. Because of the great merits of his ancestors, when he was eighteen and entered official service, he was immediately awarded the rank of a second-class idle grand minister. This year he is twenty-one, accomplished in horsemanship and archery, and quite capable in literary affairs as well. His intelligence goes without saying, even those young performers could not match it. The only fault is that he spends too lavishly. But in truth he is not arrogant or haughty at all; people who see his imposing retinue simply do not dare approach him. His family wealth is beyond reckoning. A few years back he married the daughter of Marquis Jingbian and Vice-Minister Su, whose dowry alone came to a million taels. His wife is truly beautiful as a heavenly immortal, her looks may be the finest in all the realm. She is supremely virtuous too, accomplished in calligraphy, painting, chess, and music. She also has ten maidservants called the Ten Pearl Maids, all bearing names with the character 'pearl', all as beautiful as flowers and jade, literate, and able to sing and play instruments. In his residence, Young Master Hua truly passes every day in delight. He is the foremost noble young man in the city, the supreme wealthy patron. I am distantly connected to him, for his wife's maternal great-uncle is a relation of my own sister-in-law.

"This year I hosted him for one meal and spent over a thousand strings of cash. He does not go to restaurants or theaters; when he comes to someone's home, this crowd of twenty or thirty horses and twenty or thirty people must be accommodated. If the house is small there is simply no room to fit them all. Besides, his tastes are demanding: he wants both quality and abundance. His own appetite may be limited, but when hosting him you must devise special preparations. You need thirty or forty excellent dishes, twenty or thirty varieties of fruit, and more than ten kinds of fine wine. When his spirits rise, one day is not sufficient; he stays until midnight. Naturally there must be a theatrical troupe, and he brings his own as well.

"Getting performers to serve as wine companions is also difficult. One moment he thinks of this one, the next moment that one, and you must have all the famous ones present in attendance. Even having performers is not enough: there must also be ensemble troupes, eight-beat drum performers, acrobats, and all manner of miscellaneous entertainments. All must be arranged beforehand to cater to his fancy. When pleased, his tips are several gold ingots. But if anything goes wrong, or someone's face displeases him, he rewards them all the same, and then has them flogged thirty or forty lashes and driven out. Can you imagine such standards? He does not care whether people find it tolerable or not, he simply follows his own whims. That day I was somewhat careless: I love the shibo xian variety performance, and there is a young performer named Xiaoshun'er who is the absolute master of that genre. I thought surely he would like him too. When Xiaoshun'er came out in full costume and walked on, Young Master Hua's face instantly turned furious. He let out a cold laugh, and his attendants at once drove Xiaoshun'er off, leaving me extremely mortified. After that he said not a word, gave not a smile. Barely ten dishes had been served when he grew eager to leave, and no amount of urging could detain him. I had to let him go. It was generous of him to save my face and not have the whip used on anyone. For that brief visit, counting the main courses, the middle courses, and the late courses, plus all the various tips and incidental costs, it came to over a thousand strings. Not only did I fail to please him, he went away declaring me vulgar and intolerable. Since then I have never dared invite him again. He has a personal attendant named Lin Shanji who genuinely cost eight thousand taels of silver." Pincai nodded with a slight smile and said: "Befriending this wealthy nobleman would indeed be no easy matter." Fu San said: "Difficult indeed, unless one possesses true abilities that earn his admiration. Otherwise, however assiduously one flatters and courts him, this is precisely the sort of person who most enjoys being flattered." Just as they were saying this, the meal arrived, a flask of wine, four side dishes, and a hot pot. Fu San said: "Today is quite simple, nothing worth eating." The two drank and chatted at ease. Then Pincai heard some women's voices from within, quite animated. After a moment it seemed as if two women had gotten into an argument, growing noisy and confused, mixed with the voices of maids and older maidservants trying to mediate, and scattered laughter.

Fu San tried to ignore it, but with Pincai present he felt embarrassed, and went inside.

Pincai listened quietly and could make out Fu San's voice saying "There's a guest, there's a guest" once or twice. The women's voices dropped slightly, trailing off in sporadic murmuring. Fu San came back out and drank a cup of wine with Pincai; then the noise inside started up again.

Fu San could not sit still and ran back inside; this time the commotion was very loud. Even with Fu San there trying to suppress it, it only grew worse. They could also hear Fu San shouting: "Have some consideration for me, this is simply not done!" Then a woman's voice was heard, half-crying and half-shouting, her words too hurried and rapid to make out clearly from outside.

Pincai had no heart for drinking, and it would have been awkward to ask questions, so he simply asked for the meal to be served. Fu San came out again; Pincai could see that his composure was quite unsettled. He took his leave and expressed thanks for the meal. Fu San saw that Pincai had already eaten and the commotion inside was still so bad; he could hardly urge him to stay. He could only say: "Today things have been rather informal, please do not laugh at us. The moment my wife steps out of the house, the others lose all restraint and start quarreling." Pincai could say nothing in response, and went straight out. Fu San accompanied him to the main gate, watched until he had got into the carriage, then turned back inside.

Pincai then called on Gui Daye's house; he was not at home, so Pincai left his card and departed. Riding in the carriage, Pincai reflected:

"The other day at the theater, Rong Guan mentioned that the Green Concubine and the White Concubine had come to blows, throwing this, smashing that. I took it for a joke. Today it turns out to be quite true." The hour was still early to return home; he left the city, dismissed the carriage, strolled past the theater gates and various other places, then made his way home.

Time passed quickly; after a few days, it was already the end of the year, and the Mei household was full of festive bustle.

Master Li the tutor had finished teaching for the season and went out often, gathering with fellow provincials and examination classmates for conversation and amusement. On New Year's Eve, Pincai and Yuanmao sat idly in the study, feeling very much the melancholy of guests far from home. Presently Ziyu came out and said to both of them: "Yesterday I heard that Uncle Wang, on the day of the group-visit ceremony, will especially prepare two tables of wine to host us, and also the Sun brothers." Yuanmao said: "I will not go. I am not even from the same province." Ziyu said: "That does not matter. First, Uncle Wang has invited us specifically, and we will not be seated with the others; second, it is also Yong'an's wish. If you do not go, it will be awkward for everyone." Pincai smiled and said: "If that is the case, that day we should be able to watch Qin Guan perform in the opera!" Ziyu smiled and said: "There is still something else I must attend to." And he went back inside. That evening, when Li Xingquan returned home, entering the gate he already saw the whole hall illuminated with lanterns in brilliant splendor. Looking toward the main hall, he saw Scholar Mei with his wife and Ziyu, surrounded by a group of servants and their wives, offering sacrificial foods before the deity images.

He hurried to the study and found two pairs of red candles and four plain glass lanterns burning within. Yuanmao went forward to bow in greeting. Pincai also came to offer the New Year's salutation. Xingquan hastily returned the courtesies, then led them to pay their New Year's respects to their Teacher and Teacher's Wife. Scholar Mei stopped them from the full formality of bowing. Lady Yan immediately instructed Ziyu to go out and offer New Year's greetings to the Teacher. Scholar Mei then personally escorted Ziyu to the study; they exchanged congratulations, and wine and food were set out.

Scholar Mei with great reverence filled a cup of wine for Xingquan; Xingquan kept demurring that he could not accept such an honor. Mei then made to fill cups for Pincai and Yuanmao as well. Pincai quickly took the wine cup himself, set it in place, and they all took their seats in proper order.

Scholar Mei was a man of strict speech and upright conduct, and now paired with Li Xingquan, the conversation at the table was nothing but discourse on self-cultivation and moral rectitude, all words of exhortation to Ziyu. Li Yuanmao sat stiff and constrained, hardly daring to eat, in considerable discomfort. Only Pincai managed to pretend at being earnest, affecting pedantic speech to converse with them. After not too long, the banquet dissolved. Scholar Mei sat outside a while longer, speaking at length on various matters, then went inside together with Ziyu. Xingquan, Yuanmao, and the rest also retired for the night. But for what follows, please await the next chapter.
