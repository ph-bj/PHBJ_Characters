Chapter 8 — Theater thieves steal silver yet again; amid merriment at the wine shop, disorder erupts.

Ziyu returned from drinking at Liu Wenze's home past the second night-watch. He first paid his respects to his parents, changed his clothes, and went to find Pincai and Yuanmao for a chat — but found everything quiet with the door closed. The serving boy Tiger'er came and said: "After Young Master went out, the private secretary was invited away and won't return tonight. Masters Li and Wei went out after breakfast." Ziyu said:

"Where did they go? Still not back at this hour?" And with that he went inside. Now, after Ziyu went out, Pincai and Yuanmao both felt bored. Since the sixth day, Yuanmao had seen some beautiful performers and longed to enjoy himself; on one hand, his father kept a tight rein on him; on the other, he had no money and could not do as he pleased. He said to Pincai: "Today you really ought to treat me to a show." Pincai said: "If I had money, wouldn't I take you without your even needing to ask?" Yuanmao frowned and paced back and forth with his arms folded, then said: "Just the two of us listening to the opera wouldn't cost more than three hundred big coins." Pincai said: "If it were only three hundred coins, I could still manage — but just sitting there coldly watching a few pieces of opera isn't much fun. Haven't you seen how others bring cushions for the best seats, surrounded by a circle of performers, all laughter and chatter, so lively? After listening to a few pieces, they take the performers to a restaurant for wine. You haven't seen what it's like with performers attending the table — the scent of cosmetics and face powder, the mingling aromas of wine and flowers, swallows chattering and orioles singing, feigned anger and artificial smiles — all these charms never fail to bewitch and enrapture one. In truth the expense is limited — only seven or eight strings of copper cash — which in silver is only three or four qian. Back home down south, that wouldn't even cover one table of flower-wine entertainments. I just don't have those few strings, so I can't foot such a bill." Yuanmao listened and his heart burned with impatience, and he said: "I have nothing left to pawn, but you still have a few pieces — why not pawn your things to treat me?" Pincai said:

"If I pawn them, I'll have nothing to wear." Yuanmao said: "Go borrow from the accounts room — you're quite friendly with the bookkeeper." Pincai said: "How could I face that? We've only been here a few days. Borrowing money to go watch opera would make us look ridiculous." Yuanmao said: "Then there's nothing to be done — can't pawn, can't borrow. Just forget it; I can't think of any solution." Pincai said: "You have a solution — you have silver and won't use it." Yuanmao said: "I have silver? I ran short on the road and no one has given me any since arriving in Beijing — where would I get silver?" Pincai said: "Your honorable father's trunk must surely have silver in it. Why not borrow a few taels temporarily, use them, and repay when I figure something out? You needn't tell him." Yuanmao said: "That might not do — what if he finds out?" Pincai said: "If he doesn't find out, so much the better. If he does inquire, just say we borrowed travel expenses from Ye Maolin on the road, and he came today to collect, and it was too embarrassing not to repay him at once." Yuanmao said: "That does sound plausible, but since it was never mentioned before, he might not believe it." Pincai said: "Why wouldn't he believe it? Just say you always thought I had already repaid it, which is why it was never brought up." Yuanmao thought it over, went straight to his father's room, opened the trunk, reached inside, and found a large bundle — several tens of taels in all. Opening it, he saw many small pieces, so he selected five or six. He stopped there and was about to rewrap it. Pincai said: "For opera and wine combined, it will run to about twenty strings of cash — you'd better pick out a couple more." Yuanmao picked out two more — totaling about eight or nine taels — put everything in a waist-purse and tucked it into his belt, rewrapped the remaining silver, replaced it, and locked the trunk. After eating, with his boy Sier carrying a horse-blanket, they hired a carriage and hastened to the opera theater.

As they approached, Yuanmao asked: "Which troupe shall we see?" Pincai said: "The Lianjin troupe, of course." They checked the billboard: the Lianjin troupe was performing at the Taihe Garden. Pincai was familiar with the area from the previous year's wanderings and led Yuanmao straight in. Pincai walked quickly ahead; Yuanmao, dazzled by the colorful decorations at the theater entrance — painted clouds, tigers, and flower displays, all in vivid color — kept looking about and walking forward without watching his feet. He didn't notice a low bench lying across the path just inside the threshold, tripped over it, and fell forward like an upended plant. Sier was just coming to help him up when a man stepped over, lifted Yuanmao to his feet with both hands, brushed the dust from his clothes, and said with a grin: "Watch where you're going! That was quite a fall — lucky I pulled you up fast. If you'd hurt your shoulder or knocked your head, what then? You came for pleasure and would have found nothing but trouble."

Yuanmao, embarrassed, mumbled a word of thanks and went in to find Pincai. They sat at a small table upstairs. The performance had already begun; two pieces had been done, and at this moment they were performing 'Picking Up the Gold.' Yuanmao, seeing no young female-role piece, wasn't interested and looked around in all directions — not only Qin Guan and the others were nowhere to be seen; even Ye Maolin was not on stage.

He was sitting there without enthusiasm when suddenly a man walked over and nodded at him; Yuanmao felt the face was familiar but couldn't place it. The man went around behind Pincai and gave him a pat on the shoulder, saying: "Having a fine time!" Pincai turned and saw it was Zhang Zhongyu; his face lit up at once and he hurried to offer him a seat. He asked: "Elder Brother, did you come alone, or with others?" Zhongyu said: "When would I find time to come watch opera? This morning I paid a call at the Hua residence at Jinchun Garden, then went to Master Xu's place at Yiyuan to discuss something, and then had lunch at Tianxiang Lou with Old Third Pan from the Qisheng Bank. Yesterday the Tang Monk from Hongji Monastery asked to meet me here about some business." As he spoke, he produced a jade snuff bottle and handed it to Pincai, who accepted it. Yuanmao now remembered seeing Zhongyu on the sixth day and they exchanged a few pleasantries. Zhongyu then passed the snuff bottle to Yuanmao; Yuanmao, not knowing what it was, treated it as medicine to be inhaled against heatstroke and immediately sneezed seven or eight times in succession, with eyes streaming and nose running, making Zhongyu and Pincai both laugh. Zhongyu asked Pincai about life at the Mei household; Pincai answered casually. Zhongyu said: "Brother, if you are ever in need, come and talk to me." Pincai thanked him. Zhongyu didn't watch the opera but just chatted with Pincai. Pincai brought up Qin Guan; Zhongyu said: "I've seen this person — the looks are fine, but he's rather cool in manner. Now he's at Xu Duxiang's Yiyuan every day. There's also Qi Guan, who is somewhat friendlier." Pincai said: "This Qin Guan is the one our Mei Yuxiang values most." Zhongyu said: "He likes Qin Guan too? I don't often see him out and about." Yuanmao had been listening in a daze; just then he saw a performer walk over, bow to Zhang Zhongyu, acknowledge Pincai, and also bend at the waist toward Yuanmao. Yuanmao rubbed his eyes, focused his gaze, and took a look — it was a performer he had seen at the guild hall the other day, whom Sun Sihui had praised extravagantly. The performer sat down beside Zhang Zhongyu, who remained rather cool. Pincai asked Zhongyu:

"What is his name?" Before Zhongyu could answer, the performer eagerly replied: "My name is Erxi — Second Joy." He then asked: "What is your honorable surname?" Pincai told him. He also asked Yuanmao: "The other day at the Suzhou Guild Hall when you were watching the opera, you were talking with Elder Sun Master — do you know each other well?"

Yuanmao thought to himself: "This performer is very attentive — he met me once and kept me in mind. How rare." And so he sat gazing at the man with both his amber eyes, studying him carefully. Erxi simply came over and sat on the same bench as Yuanmao, asking: "Do you come to the opera often? Which troupe do you prefer?" Yuanmao mumbled some vague reply. Erxi filled Yuanmao's short pipe, lit it, and passed it over; Yuanmao was greatly pleased — his two eyes grew even more watery and dewy, his heart pounded with joy, his throat itched with words he couldn't quite bring out. The performer stayed seated and didn't move. The next piece was "Visiting a Brothel," and another performer came to sit beside Zhang Zhongyu and also didn't leave. Pincai asked his name: it was Baozhu. On stage the next piece changed to "Woman Ballad-Singer." The moment the performer came on, Pincai recognized it was Qi Guan — beautifully dressed and fragrant, with the charm of a flower holding morning dew, the moon reflected in a quiet stream. Applause from both galleries was unending. Zhongyu said: "That is Qi Guan." Pincai nodded with a smile: "Qi Guan has become even better than last year." Yuanmao couldn't get a clear look, being busy talking with Erxi and glancing at Baozhu, with no attention left for the stage. Baozhu, seeing that Yuanmao was pleased with him, edged closer; Erxi blocked him and wouldn't let him approach. Baozhu went around and sat on the other side.

Two dark-complexioned performers flanked one timid country patron, and had Li Yuanmao looking left and right, unable to attend to both. Baozhu and Erxi competed to stuff his pipe, competed to pour his tea — one pressing close to his shoulder, the other squeezing against his leg. Li Yuanmao was in seventh heaven, his heart blossoming with delight.

When Qi Guan's piece was finished, he came off stage, removed his makeup, stood for a moment at the edge of the curtain, spotted Pincai, and gave him a slight smile. Pincai nodded back. He also noticed that Qi Guan was now dressed in fine furs with fashionable boots and hat — a completely different appearance from before — and thought he would surely come over to say hello. Indeed Qi Guan went into the dressing room; then after a wait, Pincai suddenly looked up and saw him already seated across the way in the gallery, together with the performer who had played 'Inscribing the Tune' the other day, in attendance on two magnificently dressed men. After a short while, those two men took them and left. Pincai was not a little deflated. Just then he heard Erxi ask Yuanmao:

"Where are we going today?" Yuanmao, not understanding, simply nodded his head. Then he heard Baozhu ask: "Which restaurant shall we go to today? I'll be in attendance on you." Yuanmao mumbled vaguely without answering. Erxi said: "Today there are only two or three places open; if we go too late there may be no seats." Yuanmao thought to himself: "These two are both very agreeable — and it looks as if both want to come. But I'm afraid the silver I brought may not be enough." He thought further: "Giving the two of them twelve strings — and spending five or six strings on food and wine — should be sufficient." He said to Pincai: "Shall we go?" Baozhu took hold of Yuanmao's hand: "Which restaurant?" Pincai looked at the two performers and wasn't particularly pleased with either of them; but since Yuanmao was paying and it was nothing to do with him, he was happy enough for some lively company. He said to Zhongyu: "Elder Brother, please come along — let's have a cup together." Zhongyu said: "You go ahead; I still need to wait a while." Pincai said: "Come with us — at this hour whoever he's waiting for probably isn't coming." He pulled Zhongyu along and they all came downstairs — only to realize he had forgotten to pay the theater fee. An attendant ran up and grabbed Sier: "Not so fast — you haven't paid for your seats." Pincai stopped, asked Yuanmao about it. Zhongyu said: "That's mine — just settle it with the manager." The attendant agreed.

They had just left the theater when the two attendant-boys came trailing behind. Pincai asked Zhongyu: "Which restaurant is good?" Zhongyu said: "The Chunyang Restaurant just ahead is quite fine." In a few steps they entered; the manager and staff all stood up calling out: "Master Zhang, Happy New Year! May you gain promotion and fortune!" and bowed; Zhongyu exchanged a few pleasantries. They chose a quiet corner table; Zhongyu sat first, Yuanmao second, Pincai third; Erxi and Baozhu shared a bench. A waiter brought tea and invited them to order. Zhongyu deferred to Yuanmao and Pincai; both in turn deferred to Zhongyu, who ordered braised tile-cut fish and stewed duck kidneys; Pincai ordered fried gizzards and ham; Baozhu ordered clam tofu and stir-fried shrimp; Erxi ordered stir-fried fish slices, soy-braised meats, and red-braised pork. Yuanmao said: "I like chicken — I'll just have chicken." The waiter and Erxi both laughed. Two pots of wine were brought, along with several plates of fresh fruit and small side dishes; everyone drank a few cups. First to arrive were the fried gizzards, duck kidneys, ham, and fish slices. Pincai suggested playing finger-guessing games. Zhongyu said to Erxi: "You set the game." Erxi said: "Joy-in-joy, sorrow-in-sorrow. First cup lost — you must sing a little song; second cup lost — you must tell a joke; third cup lost — receive the skin-cup, a drink passed mouth-to-mouth." Yuanmao said: "None of those three I can do." Pincai said: "That won't do. Since that's so, you go first." Erxi filled three cups to the brim, set them out, and said: "Master Li, your turn!" Yuanmao squinted his eyes and said: "Keep a close watch for me — my eyesight is poor and I'm afraid I'll make mistakes." He extended his hand, played one round against Erxi — and lost. Zhongyu said with a laugh: "Please sing." Yuanmao said: "Singing is completely beyond me — I'd rather drink an extra cup." Baozhu said: "If you agreed to sing, you must sing." Yuanmao drank a cup and begged Baozhu to sing on his behalf. Erxi said: "Singing by proxy earns a fine of ten cups." Baozhu didn't dare act as proxy. Yuanmao bowed to him and said: "Good fellow, please sing for me — I don't know a single line of anything." Everyone saw he truly could not sing, and so Baozhu sang a 'Silver Button Silk' on his behalf.

They played for the second cup — Erxi lost. Erxi said: "There was once a host who invited guests but had no money to buy wine, so he placed an empty cup before each guest. The host said: 'Please drink.' The guests didn't move.

He said 'Please drink' again. A guest said: 'The wine hasn't come yet — please drink what?' The host walked over, picked up the cup and peered into it, and said: 'Ah, I see — this cup of wine is bone-dry. Just drink it as it is.'" Erxi then took the cup and brought it to Yuanmao's lips; Yuanmao was greatly amused and drained it in one go. Zhongyu and Pincai both called out: "Bravo!" Baozhu said: "That joke is truly entertaining!" and immediately filled a cup, brought it to Pincai's lips, and called out: "Dear papa, drink this cup!" Pincai was pleased and drained it.

Baozhu filled another cup, brought it before Zhongyu, addressed him as "Dear papa" as well, and Zhongyu also drained it.

They played for the third cup — Yuanmao won again. Erxi held a mouthful of wine, cupped Yuanmao's face in both hands, and poured it mouth-to-mouth into him. Yuanmao was happy but embarrassed; he had swallowed half of it when he suddenly lowered his head with a laugh — and that mouthful of wine shot back out through both nostrils, spraying like two streams, drenching the whole table. Li Yuanmao's forehead was both tickling and burning; he buried his face on Erxi's shoulder and couldn't raise his head. Baozhu laughed so hard he couldn't hold his seat and slid off the bench onto the floor. Zhongyu laughed so hard he spilled wine all over himself. Pincai laughed until his stomach ached and had to hold his belly with both hands.

Erxi, still laughing, patted Yuanmao on the chest. Yuanmao finally raised his head, closed his eyes, opened his mouth — his nostrils still itching — and let out several more sneezes. After quite some time, he finally said: "What's so funny?" Seeing his expression, everyone laughed again for a while, then returned to eating.

Erxi filled a cup and played finger-guessing against Zhang Zhongyu. Zhongyu lost; Yuanmao urged him to sing. Zhongyu said: "That's not difficult." He drank a cup and sang a 'Ma Tou Diao' tune — everyone applauded: "Excellent!" The second cup — Zhongyu lost again and had to tell a joke. Raising his eyes, he noticed a small spirit shrine nailed to the wall, bearing a picture of the Wealth God Zhao Xuantan riding a black tiger. He said to Erxi: "When you see a wealthy patron, you rejoice and say: 'The God of Wealth has arrived — he likes to spend money!' And when a poor patron sees a dark-complexioned performer, he goes pale and says: 'A tiger is here — he'll eat anyone he meets!' Now look up there — is it the God of Wealth riding the black tiger, or a poor patron straddled on a dark performer?" Pincai slapped the table in delight; Yuanmao covered his nose trying not to laugh; Baozhu looked up at the shrine. Erxi filled a cup, brought it before Zhongyu, and said: "You deserve a fine — that remark cut quite deep." Zhongyu took it and drank, saying: "There are no poor patrons afraid of performers here." They played for the third cup — Erxi lost again and had to offer the skin-cup to Zhongyu. Zhongyu said: "We don't need to do it that way — a moment ago Master Li didn't get to enjoy his cup properly; please offer this one to him instead." Erxi held the cup, took a small sip, then brought it to Yuanmao's lips. Yuanmao shook his head and clamped his mouth firmly shut. Erxi then straddled Yuanmao's lap, sat upright, cupped Yuanmao's head firmly, and tilted it upward; Yuanmao's face turned up accordingly. Erxi then pressed those pearl-like lips to that wide mouth and slowly let the wine trickle through — three slow mouthfuls in succession.

Yuanmao was like one receiving the nectar of the gods poured over his crown — indescribably blissful. Everyone could hear his throat: gurgle, gurgle, gurgle — three swallows in succession.

Erxi filled wine again; now it was Pincai's turn. First round — Erxi lost and sang a 'Nine Interlocking Rings.'

Second round — Pincai lost. Pincai gave a small laugh first and said: "A husband was away from home, so his young sister asked to sleep in the same bed as his wife. The wife, thinking of her husband, couldn't sleep, and asked the young girl to imitate her brother and press against her for a while. The wife was wonderfully pleased and said: 'Good as it is, you're not yet skilled — and it's all wet.' The young girl said: 'This is the first time — the second time I'll be more practiced. We should give it a name.' The wife said: 'It already has one — it's called polishing mirrors.' The young girl said: 'That doesn't fit — a mirror is round. Better call it offering the skin-cup.'" This brought a wave of laughter quite delightful to hear; Yuanmao laughed until tears ran down his face and called out: "You terrible person — you'll turn into a mute tomorrow!" Baozhu laughed so hard he tumbled into Pincai's lap; Erxi came over and gave Pincai a tap, saying: "Where does such a wicked person come from — the mockery cuts to the bone!" Third round — it was Erxi who lost again. Erxi held the cup and said: "How shall I sing? Give your instructions." Pincai immediately put on a stern face and said: "You listened to Master Zhang's words but not mine — you look down on me. I won't let you off today." Erxi was startled: "I haven't offended you." Pincai said: "Even though you haven't offended me, you must still follow my word." Erxi said: "Then tell me what you want." Pincai said: "I want this skin-cup offered to Master Li again." Erxi brought the cup to Yuanmao; Yuanmao shook his head: "Oh no — you've all been using me for your entertainment all day. This time I'm not falling for it again." Zhongyu said: "Master Li, if you don't take this cup, I'll make up another joke about you." Pincai said: "Pah! So it's just silver foil over a wax spearhead after all — utterly useless! The moment someone says something, you back down." Yuanmao thought: "I can't outargue them. Never mind — nothing in the world is too hard if you're thick-skinned enough. What brings an advantage is always the better choice." He said: "I'm nothing like you people, scared of shame. Come, come, come! Watch me drink!" He turned the tables, cupped Erxi's face in his own hands, and drank the cup himself — and nobody could reasonably laugh at him for it. Erxi's game was done; Baozhu played the same game with Yuanmao, and Baozhu sang a 'Full River Red.'

Pincai suddenly saw a monk walk in, saying: "My dear Second Master! So you're here — I've searched seven or eight theaters without finding you anywhere!" Erxi and Baozhu saw the monk and bowed in greeting; Pincai and Yuanmao also rose to welcome him. The monk bowed all around and sat down beside Zhongyu. Pincai observed the monk's appearance: a square face of dark purplish complexion, a thick two-strand mustache, about forty years of age; he wore a velvet monk's cap and a sapphire-blue silk fox-fur monk's robe, a yellow silk cord tied at his waist, cloud-cutout green satin soft boots on his feet — nothing of the look of a man who had renounced the world. He was clearly a meat-and-wine monk.

And yet his manner was pleasant and genial, most skilled at being sociable. He immediately asked Pincai and Yuanmao their names and where they were lodging, then said to Zhongyu: "Second Master, when tomorrow's business is settled — whether at the Suzhou Guild Hall or Tianqing Tang — let's invite your two companions and these two young performers, and have a thoroughly merry day together. Today I truly cannot linger — everyone on the other side has gathered and is waiting for you to come settle things." Zhongyu said: "No need to rush — have a cup yourself and then we'll go." The monk smoothed his mustache, grinned broadly, drank a cup of wine, and ate a slice of ham.

Baozhu said with a smile: "Master Tang, you haven't brought your 'young master' along today?" The Tang Monk laughed: "Outrageous! A monk has no wife — where would a 'young master' come from?" Erxi said: "Your 'young master' looks just the same as a wife." The Tang Monk immediately reached a hand toward Erxi's face. Erxi laughed: "I only said he looks like a wife — there's nothing wrong in that." The Tang Monk, seeing Pincai and Yuanmao present, pretended to be decorous, withdrew his hand, and said: "Mocking a disciple of the Buddha incurs great sin." Zhongyu and Pincai burst out laughing. The Tang Monk urged Zhongyu again to get up and leave. Zhongyu said: "A moment or two more won't matter." Erxi, seeing a gourd hanging on the wall, pointed at it and asked the Tang Monk: "What does this look like?" The Tang Monk laughed: "It looks like your mouth." Erxi said: "Wrong, wrong! How can it look like my mouth? It clearly looks like your head — smooth and bald, not a single hair." The monk said: "Indeed it's bald. Haven't you heard it said that in heaven there are 'three luminosities,' while in the human world there are 'four luminosities'? These are: the monk's head — shaved smooth; the married woman's leg — polished smooth; the wealthy patron's money-purse — spent smooth; and the performer's mouth — eaten smooth." He burst out laughing, pulled Zhongyu to his feet and made to leave, then bowed to Pincai with a grin: "I have been talking nonsense — please do not laugh at me, sirs." Zhongyu said: "Let me settle the account first." Pincai said: "Elder Brother, since you have business, please go — the bill is this younger brother's." Zhongyu said: "Please have a few more cups, gentlemen; I'll be back shortly." With that he bid farewell and went out with the monk.

Pincai and Yuanmao played another round of finger-guessing with Baozhu; Baozhu also offered the skin-cup twice. Erxi had more dishes brought; they made merry for quite a while longer. Half a candle had already burned down — it was past the first night-watch. The two performers were growing tired; the two attendant-boys stood by the outer door. Li Yuanmao, having entirely lost track of himself, had been gulping soup and wine; he had taken off his hat and his head was steaming like a rice-steamer. Pincai said: "We should be wrapping up." He leaned close to Yuanmao's ear and said quietly: "Take out that thing and go settle the bill at the counter." Yuanmao felt about his waist with both hands, then with a flustered expression said: "How strange!" He stood up, lifted the back hem of his gown, and said to Pincai: "Look — is it there?" Pincai said: "Is what there?" Yuanmao said: "The waist-purse." Pincai said: "No." Yuanmao's face went blank at once: "How extraordinary — where did it go?" Baozhu said: "Lost something?" Yuanmao didn't answer and rummaged through his breast again — nothing. His face went pale in waves. He undid his belt and shook it — nothing there either. Pincai grew anxious: "Think carefully." Yuanmao said: "What do you mean? You saw me with it." He lifted his robe again and felt all around his trouser waistband — nothing. Pincai pulled Yuanmao aside to the window; the two attendant-boys were also standing there, so they went out to the courtyard and said quietly: "This is dreadful! Think — where did you lose it?" This prompted Yuanmao: "Oh! I know now. When I was going into the theater, I fell — and someone came and pulled me up and brushed off my clothes. That person must have taken it." Pincai said: "I didn't see you fall — when was this?" Yuanmao said: "There was a low bench lying across the path at the entrance; I wasn't watching where I was going and went down just inside the door." Quick-witted as Pincai was, he could think of nothing to be done.

Erxi came out and said: "What are you two discussing in the courtyard?" The two came back inside and sat down. Erxi said: "It's getting late." He leaned close to Yuanmao's ear and said: "Come back to my place — I'll take care of you." Yuanmao heard this and felt simultaneously happy and flustered, his face burning; he just stared blankly at Pincai. Baozhu and Erxi couldn't make out what was the matter. Pincai had no choice but to say to Yuanmao: "Now that this purse of silver is lost, what are we to do?" Yuanmao said: "There was also something else inside — it was all taken together." Baozhu said: "What happened?" Yuanmao said: "The silver — at the theater entrance, a pickpocket cut it away." Erxi said: "I came out with you and didn't see any pickpocket." Yuanmao said: "It happened going in." Erxi said: "It happened going in, yet you watched the opera for so long and drank for so long without noticing? Only now, about to leave, you bring it up? Isn't it more like you forgot to bring it and it's still at home?" Yuanmao grew agitated: "That's outrageous — do you think I'm making excuses?" Erxi gave a cold laugh. Pincai said: "That's not what we mean — it's not as if we came here without money planning to cheat you. Master Li was careless and lost it; naturally we couldn't say so at the time. Trust us — tomorrow when we come to watch the opera, we'll bring everything we owe, Baozhu's share included." He asked Baozhu at once: "Do you believe us?"

Baozhu said: "I have no particular reason not to believe you. Besides, this is our first acquaintance — you would certainly not deliberately cheat us. But when we go back we must give an account to our master. And especially at New Year's, it's even harder to go back empty-handed — not only would it be difficult to face master, it would be unfair to the boys who attended us as well.

"We only ask that you can somehow manage something to spare us this difficulty." He then said to Erxi: "Brother Xi, isn't that right?" Yuanmao said: "I'm telling you the truth, but you won't believe me. Today I had eight pieces of silver — well over ten taels in total — loose in a waist-purse, which was taken, purse and all. To ask us to go borrow from somewhere at this hour — where would we go? This is a truly unfair position to put us in." Erxi sniffed and said: "It's not all that late yet. Why don't you send your boy back to fetch more? We can wait here a bit." And he pressed Yuanmao back into his seat. Yuanmao shook his head: "That's absolutely impossible." Erxi said: "If it's impossible, then you planned this from the start. We spent the whole day in your company as complete strangers, serving wine, offering the skin-cup — Master Li, if you can think of that as sufficient friendship, well and good. But we too are driven by necessity — wanting to please our patrons, earn a few strings of cash, make a showing for our master. If things always go this way, we won't be able to make our living. Master Li, since you refuse to send someone home, here is what I ask — please be kind enough to come back with me and say a word to my master yourself. As for whether you give anything — that doesn't matter at all." Baozhu said: "What he says is perfectly right — as long as our master knows what happened, that's all we ask; we don't need any money." Li Yuanmao was reduced to utter helplessness — face flushed deep red, not a word he could say. Pincai had no choice but to say: "We've now made each other's acquaintance — is this to be the only time, with no future dealings? You want him to go back with you and explain to your master in person — your master would certainly not refuse, but I have never before seen the case of a performer calling out his own master to settle the account." Baozhu said: "This is only because we don't know each other yet. Once you've been a patron for three or four years and we're well acquainted, it would never come to this." The two of them were at an impasse. Then Sier came in and said: "Elder Sun Master is here too — he just stepped out." Pincai saw an opening: knowing Sihui was acquainted with these performers, he said: "Go and ask Elder Sun Master to come in." Sier hurried out; Sihui was still at the counter in conversation, accompanied by a performer who had already gotten into a carriage and gone. Sihui couldn't quite recognize Sier, but hearing someone asking for him, he came back in, and now recognized Yuanmao and Pincai. Seeing Erxi and Baozhu, he laughed: "Today you two gentlemen — what merriment this is!" Yuanmao and Pincai bowed; Erxi and Baozhu made their salutations; everyone sat back down. Pincai explained the whole situation: how Yuanmao had lost his silver today, had nothing to pay with now, had promised to bring it tomorrow, and the performers were refusing to accept this. Sihui pushed up his hat, rubbed his red nose, pointed at Li Yuanmao, and delivered a long improvisation stringing together phrases from the Thousand Character Classic — his packet of 'gold born of beautiful waters' had become 'fallen leaves adrift in the wind'; instead of going to 'execute the thieves and robbers' he sat here 'dispersing his cares in ease'; he must think Sihui an 'old acquaintance' and so sent for him urgently 'to requite gladness and joy'; seeing the two performers in their 'vigorous and resplendent' condition, something had suddenly been 'pushing forth new shoots' inside Sihui as well; and so on through a parade of absurd classical phrases culminating in suggestions of what Yuanmao might do with the performers to ensure everyone's joyful satisfaction. Wei Pincai and Li Yuanmao were already convulsed with laughter; the two performers could not fully follow — it sounded like reciting opera lines — and they too laughed uncontrollably. Yuanmao spluttered incoherently; Pincai had no choice but to say he himself would take on the obligation and settle with them tomorrow.

Sihui heard this and was startled: "One whose strength cannot lift a hundred jun cannot bear such a heavy burden on a long road — I fear I am unable." Pincai could only entreat him again at length; Sihui reluctantly agreed, saying: "What can be given tomorrow shall be given — a man without integrity, who knows what becomes of him?" He then addressed Erxi and Baozhu: "Come, let me speak with you — why not take your leave now? Tomorrow I shall personally deliver to your door — do not press people too hard." The two performers could not follow all of this; Sihui had to repeat it in plain speech. Baozhu and Erxi, seeing Sihui had taken on the matter, were left with no recourse and made a reluctant show of gratitude and took their leave. Sun Sihui, afraid they might also try to make him pay the restaurant bill, hurried away immediately.

The waiter came in and read through the account item by item; everything together came to fifty-six strings seven hundred and eighty copper cash. Yuanmao, hearing this, stuck out his tongue and said: "What discount can you offer?" The waiter said: "Full price — no discount." Li Yuanmao counted on his fingers and said: "At ten-tenths that's five thousand six hundred seventy-eight coins — two thousand eight hundred thirty-nine hard-cash coins — very fair indeed. I shall certainly come here often to patronize your establishment." The waiter laughed: "We don't offer discounts — it's fifty-six strings seven hundred and eighty copper cash." Yuanmao said: "How can there be so much?" The waiter said: "We dare not overcharge." Pincai said to Yuanmao: "You're drunk — don't talk so much. Let's go settle at the counter." They went to the counter; the waiter recited the total again; the manager worked his abacus and looked at Pincai and Yuanmao: "You two came with Master Zhang, and Master Zhang left already — are you his fellow-provincials or what?" Pincai said: "We are relatives; he had business and left first." The manager asked: "May I ask your honorable names? Where are you lodging? What brings you to the capital?" Pincai answered briefly, then asked for a credit slip. The manager hesitated and said: "At New Year's money is tight — today being the very first day of business — and by long custom one always hopes for a few cash payments in the first month. And besides, today is the first time we have had the pleasure of your company.

"Master Wei is a relative of Master Zhang, and it would be awkward for me to refuse you a credit account. But please remember — don't let it drag on too long." He handed a slip to Pincai; Pincai felt deeply indignant inside but wrote out the full amount, added two strings for the wine-service, and noted down: 'Mingke Alley, Mei Residence, Wei surname.'

The manager glanced at it and filed it away. A waiter brought out a lantern; Sier took it, and they left the restaurant. The two walked home step by step, heads bowed in silence. It could truly be said: they had come in high spirits and departed in deflation. But as for what happened next, please await the following chapter.
