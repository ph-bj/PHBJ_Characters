Chapter 8 — Theater thieves steal silver yet again; amid merriment at the wine shop, disorder erupts.

Ziyu returned from drinking at Liu Wenze's home long past the second watch of the night. After paying his respects to his parents and changing his garments, he went to seek out Pincai and Yuanmao for a chat. Yet he found their quarters perfectly silent, the door closed fast. The servant boy Hu'er approached and said, "After Young Master went out, the private secretary was invited away and will not return tonight. Masters Li and Wei departed right after breakfast."

"Where could they have gone? To still be out at this hour!" Ziyu remarked, turning back inside. Now, once Ziyu had left the house earlier, Pincai and Yuanmao found themselves quite bored. Ever since the sixth day of the month when Yuanmao laid eyes upon those beautiful performers, his heart had been set on merrymaking. However, his father kept him under strict discipline, and his own pockets were empty, leaving him unable to fulfill his desires. He complained to Pincai, "Today you really must treat me to an opera." Pincai replied, "If I had the silver, would I not have invited you before you even asked?" Frowning, Yuanmao folded his arms in his sleeves and paced aimlessly for a while before declaring, "For just the two of us to listen to an opera, three hundred large cash would be plenty." Pincai sighed, "If it were merely three hundred cash, I could scrape it together. But to sit there coldly listening to a few arias holds little charm. Have you not seen the others, reclining on fine cushions in the premium seats, surrounded by a lively circle of performers, laughing and chatting in high spirits? Then, after the show, they bring those same performers to a fine restaurant to drink. You have yet to experience such a banquet—the intoxicating scent of cosmetics and powder mingling with wine and flowers, their voices sweet as swallows and orioles, their playful rebukes and enchanting smiles. Such delights never fail to bewitch the soul. In truth, the expense is quite modest, barely seven or eight strings of capital cash, which comes to only three or four mace of silver. Down south, that would not even cover a single table of such entertainment. I simply do not have those few strings of cash, so I cannot play the host." Hearing this, Yuanmao's heart burned with impatience. He pressed, "I have nothing left to pawn, but you still possess a few decent garments. Why not pawn them to treat me?"

"If I pawn them, I shall have nothing to wear," Pincai replied. Yuanmao urged, "Then borrow from the accounts room! You are quite friendly with the bookkeeper." Pincai shook his head. "How could I show my face? We have only just arrived a few days ago. To borrow money just to watch operas would make us laughingstocks." Yuanmao sighed, "Then we are at a loss. You will neither pawn nor borrow, so we must abandon the idea. I have no other solutions." Pincai countered, "You do have a solution, you simply refuse to use your silver." "I have silver?" Yuanmao protested. "I ran short on the journey, and no one has given me a single coin since arriving in the capital. Where would I get silver?" Pincai smiled knowingly. "Your honorable father's trunk must surely hold some. Why not borrow a few taels for the time being? We can use it now, and once I find a way, I will repay you exactly what we spent. You need not breathe a word to him." Yuanmao hesitated, "That might not end well. What if he questions me?" "If he never asks, all the better," Pincai reasoned. "If he does, merely say we borrowed travel funds from Ye Maolin on the road, and when he came to collect today, it would have been too embarrassing not to repay him at once." "That sounds plausible," Yuanmao admitted, "but since it was never mentioned before, he might doubt the tale." "Why should he?" Pincai pressed. "Just say you always assumed I had settled the debt, which is why it was never brought up." After a moment's thought, Yuanmao marched straight into his father's room. He unlocked the trunk and reached inside, feeling a heavy bundle that contained several dozen taels. Opening it, he found a multitude of small pieces and selected five or six. As he was about to tie the bundle closed, Pincai interjected, "For both the opera and the wine, we will need about twenty strings of cash. You had best take out two more pieces." Yuanmao obliged, selecting two more to total about eight or nine taels. He slipped the silver into a pouch and tucked it securely into his waistband, carefully rewrapped the remainder, and locked the trunk. After finishing their meal, they hired a carriage and, with his servant Sier carrying a riding blanket, hurried off to the theater.

As they approached, Yuanmao asked, "Which troupe shall we patronize?" Pincai answered without hesitation, "The Lianjin Troupe, naturally." Checking the playbills on the wall, they confirmed the Lianjin Troupe was performing at the Taihe Garden. Having frequented the area the previous year, Pincai knew the way well and led Yuanmao straight inside. Pincai walked briskly ahead. Yuanmao, bedazzled by the colorful decorations adorning the theater entrance—painted clouds, fierce tigers, and vibrant floral displays—gazed around in wonder, letting his feet carry him blindly forward. He failed to notice a low wooden bench lying across the threshold. Tripping heavily over it, he fell forward like an uprooted plant. Just as Sier rushed to help him, a bystander stepped forward, lifted Yuanmao by both arms, and brushed the dust from his robes. With a broad grin, the man chided, "Mind your step! That was no light fall. Fortunate I caught you so swiftly. If you had injured your shoulder or cracked your skull, your pursuit of pleasure would have found nothing but sorrow."

Deeply embarrassed, Yuanmao muttered his thanks and hurried inside to find Pincai. They settled at a small table upstairs. The performance was already underway, with two acts concluded; they were currently staging 'Finding Gold.' Noticing it was not a play featuring young female roles, Yuanmao lost interest. He let his gaze wander in every direction, searching the hall. Yet not only were Qin Guan and the others nowhere to be seen, even Ye Maolin was absent from the stage.

As he sat there listlessly, a man approached and nodded in his direction. Yuanmao found the face familiar but could not immediately place him. The man stepped behind Pincai, patted him on the shoulder, and declared, "In high spirits, I see!" Pincai turned, his face instantly lighting up with a wide smile as he hastily offered his seat. "Second Brother!" he exclaimed. "Are you alone, or did you come with company?" Zhang Zhongyu replied, "When do I ever have the leisure to watch operas? Early this morning I visited the Hua residence at Jinchun Garden, then went to Master Xu's place at Yiyuan to discuss a matter. Afterward, I dined at Tianxiang Pavilion with Third Brother Pan of the Qisheng Bank. Yesterday, Monk Tang of the Hongji Monastery arranged to meet me here on business." As he spoke, he produced a jade snuff bottle and handed it to Pincai, who accepted it graciously. Yuanmao suddenly recalled having met Zhongyu on the sixth day of the month, and they exchanged brief pleasantries. Zhongyu then offered the snuff bottle to Yuanmao. Not recognizing it, Yuanmao mistook it for medicinal powder meant to clear the head and inhaled deeply. Instantly, he fell into a fit of seven or eight violent sneezes, his eyes watering and nose running, sending Zhongyu and Pincai into peals of laughter. Zhongyu inquired about life at the Mei residence, and Pincai offered a casual reply. "My young friend," Zhongyu offered, "should you ever find yourself in need, do not hesitate to seek out your elder brother." Pincai thanked him. Zhongyu paid no mind to the stage, preferring to chat with Pincai. When Pincai brought up Qin Guan, Zhongyu remarked, "I have met the young man. His features are striking enough, though his temperament runs rather cold. These days he is constantly at Xu Duxiang's Yiyuan. There is also Qi Guan, who is somewhat more amiable." Pincai noted, "This Qin Guan is the one our Mei Yuxiang treasures above all." Zhongyu raised an eyebrow. "He fancies Qin Guan as well? I rarely see him out and about." Yuanmao sat in a daze, listening quietly, when a young performer approached. The youth offered a respectful greeting to Zhang Zhongyu, acknowledged Pincai, and offered a slight bow to Yuanmao. Yuanmao rubbed his eyes, focused his gaze, and realized it was the same performer he had seen at the guild hall days earlier—the very one Sun Sihui had praised so extravagantly. The performer seated himself beside Zhang Zhongyu, though Zhongyu's demeanor remained icy. Pincai turned to Zhongyu and asked:

"What is his name?" Before Zhongyu could answer, the performer eagerly chimed in, "My name is Erxi." He then turned to Pincai, "And your honorable surname?" Pincai provided his name. Erxi then addressed Yuanmao, "The other day at the Suzhou Guild Hall, you were conversing with Young Master Sun. Are the two of you well acquainted?"

Yuanmao thought to himself, "This performer is exceedingly attentive. To remember me after a single meeting is rare indeed." And so, with his amber eyes fixed intently upon the young man, he observed him closely. Erxi unabashedly shifted closer, sharing the same bench as Yuanmao. "Do you often attend the theater?" he asked. "Which troupe do you favor?" Yuanmao mumbled a vague, noncommittal reply. Erxi deftly prepared a pinch of tobacco in Yuanmao's short pipe, lit it, and handed it over. Yuanmao was utterly captivated; his eyes grew increasingly misty and dewy, his heart fluttering wildly with a joy so profound it left his throat itching with unspoken words. The performer remained seated by his side, making no move to leave. The next play, 'Visiting the Courtyard,' commenced, and another performer approached, seating himself beside Zhang Zhongyu and lingering there. Pincai inquired after his name and learned it was Baozhu. On stage, the scene shifted to 'Female Ballad-Singing.' The moment the actor made his entrance, Pincai recognized Qi Guan. His attire was exquisitely alluring, exuding the delicate grace of a blossom kissed by morning dew or the moon reflected in a tranquil stream. A thunderous ovation erupted from both galleries. "That is Qi Guan," Zhongyu noted. Pincai smiled and nodded, "He has grown even more refined than last year." Yuanmao, however, could not catch a clear glimpse. Entranced by his conversation with Erxi and stealing glances at Baozhu, he had no attention left to spare for the stage. Seeing that Yuanmao was pleased with him, Baozhu attempted to sidle closer, but Erxi firmly blocked his path, refusing to yield any ground. Undeterred, Baozhu circled around and took a seat on Yuanmao's other side.

Flanked by two dark-complexioned performers, the timid, country-bred patron found himself entirely overwhelmed, unable to attend to both at once. Baozhu and Erxi vied for his attention, competing to prepare his pipe and pour his tea. One pressed warmly against his shoulder, while the other squeezed intimately against his leg. Li Yuanmao was in seventh heaven, his heart blossoming with absolute delight.

When Qi Guan concluded his performance, he exited the stage, removed his makeup, and paused at the edge of the curtain. Catching sight of Pincai, he offered a faint, graceful smile. Pincai returned the nod. He noted that Qi Guan was now arrayed in splendid furs, complete with fashionable boots and a stylish hat—a stark contrast to his former appearance. Pincai assumed the young man would surely come over to pay his respects. Indeed, Qi Guan disappeared into the dressing room, but after waiting a while, Pincai glanced up to find him seated in the opposite gallery. There, alongside the performer who had sung 'Inscribing the Song' a few days prior, Qi Guan was attentively serving two lavishly dressed gentlemen. Before long, the two men departed, taking the performers with them. Pincai felt a profound sense of disappointment. Just then, he heard Erxi ask Yuanmao:

"Where shall we go today?" Unsure of what was being asked, Yuanmao simply nodded. Then Baozhu chimed in, "Which restaurant shall we visit today? Allow me to wait upon you." Yuanmao stammered, unable to form a coherent reply. Erxi pressed, "Only two or three establishments have opened today. If we delay, there may not be any tables left." Yuanmao thought to himself, "Both are delightful company, and it seems they both wish to come. But I fear the silver I brought will not suffice." He reasoned further, "If I give them twelve strings of cash, and we spend five or six on food and wine, it should be enough." Resolving this, he turned to Pincai, "Shall we go?" Baozhu immediately caught Yuanmao's hand and asked, "Which restaurant?" Pincai cast a glance at the two performers, not entirely pleased, but since Yuanmao was footing the bill, he was content to join the revelry. He turned to Zhongyu and invited him, "Second Brother, come along! Let us share a few cups." Zhongyu demurred, "You go ahead; I still have someone to wait for." Pincai urged, "Come with us! If they have not arrived by now, they likely will not come at all." With that, he took Zhongyu by the arm and led him downstairs, entirely forgetting to pay for their theater seats. The attendant rushed forward, grabbing hold of Sier. "Hold a moment! You haven't paid for your seats." Hearing this, Pincai stopped and looked at Yuanmao. Zhongyu waved his hand dismissively, "Put it on my account. Just tell the manager." The attendant nodded in agreement.

No sooner had they exited the theater than their two young companions fell into step behind them. Pincai asked Zhongyu, "Which restaurant is best?" Zhongyu replied, "The Chunyang Restaurant just ahead is quite excellent." A few short steps brought them inside, where the manager immediately rose to his feet. "Master Zhang, a happy New Year to you! May you rise in rank and prosper!" he called out, bowing deeply. Zhongyu offered a few polite pleasantries in return. They selected an elegant private room, with Zhongyu taking the seat of honor, Yuanmao the second, and Pincai the third. Erxi and Baozhu squeezed onto a single stool together. A waiter served tea and presented the menu. Zhongyu offered the choice to Yuanmao and Pincai, who deferred back to him. Zhongyu ordered tile-shaped fish and braised duck kidneys, while Pincai requested fried gizzards and ham. Baozhu asked for white clams with tofu and stir-fried shrimp, and Erxi requested sliced fish, braised offal, and yellow-braised pork. Yuanmao announced, "I am fond of chicken, so I shall have the chicken." The waiter and Erxi both chuckled at this. Two pots of wine, several plates of fruit, and an array of small appetizers were brought in, and everyone enjoyed a few cups. The first four dishes—fried gizzards, duck kidneys, ham, and sliced fish—were served. Pincai proposed a game of finger-guessing. Zhongyu gestured to Erxi, "You set the rules." Erxi announced, "Joy within joy, bitterness within bitterness. The first loss requires a song; the second, a joke; and the third, a 'skin-cup' drink shared mouth-to-mouth." Yuanmao quickly protested, "I am useless at all three!" Pincai laughed, "That will not do. In that case, you must play the first round!" Erxi poured three brimming cups and placed them before him. "Master Li, let us begin!" Yuanmao squinted, "Keep an eye on me, my vision is poor and I might make a mistake." He extended his hand, played a round with Erxi, and instantly lost. Zhongyu laughed, "A song, if you please." Yuanmao pleaded, "I truly cannot sing. I would rather drink an extra cup." Baozhu insisted, "If you agreed to sing, you must sing." Yuanmao downed a cup of wine and begged Baozhu to sing in his stead. Erxi declared, "A substitute singer must drink a penalty of ten cups!" Intimidated, Baozhu dared not comply. Yuanmao offered him a desperate bow, "Good fellow, please sing a verse for me. I do not know a single word of these things." Seeing his genuine distress, Baozhu relented and sang a delicate rendition of 'Silver Button Silk'.

The second round was played, and this time Erxi lost. Erxi began his penalty: "There was once a man hosting a banquet, but he had no money for wine. He placed an empty cup before his guest. The host said, 'Please, drink,' but the guest did not move.

"The host repeated, 'Please!' The guest replied, 'The wine has not yet been poured, what is there to drink?' The host leaned in, inspected the cup, and said, 'Ah, it seems this wine is utterly bone-dry. You had best drink it just as it is.'" With that, Erxi raised his own cup to Yuanmao's lips. Overjoyed, Yuanmao drained it in a single gulp. Zhongyu and Pincai erupted in cheers of "Bravo!" Baozhu added, "That joke was truly delightful." He then poured a cup of wine, brought it to Pincai's lips, and called out affectionately, "Godfather, drink this cup." Pincai, highly amused, drank it down.

Baozhu poured another cup, brought it to Zhongyu, and again called out, "Godfather!" Zhongyu drank it as well.

The third round saw Yuanmao victorious. Erxi took a mouthful of wine, cradled Yuanmao's face in both hands, and delivered the wine mouth-to-mouth. Yuanmao was in raptures, though his face flushed with embarrassment. Just as he swallowed half the mouthful, he suddenly lowered his head and chuckled. The remaining wine shot forcefully out of his nostrils, looking for all the world like two streams of yellow urine, splattering messily across the table. His forehead tingling and burning, Yuanmao buried his face in Erxi's shoulder, unable to lift his head. Baozhu laughed so hard he lost his balance, slipping off his stool and onto the floor. Zhongyu laughed until he spilled wine all over himself, while Pincai clutched his aching sides.

Laughing, Erxi patted Yuanmao's chest until he finally raised his head. His eyes closed, his mouth agape, his nostrils still tickling, he let out several loud sneezes. After a long pause, he finally asked, "What is so funny?" Seeing his bewildered state, the others broke into fresh fits of laughter before settling down to enjoy the meal.

Erxi then poured wine and engaged Zhang Zhongyu in a round. Zhongyu lost, and Yuanmao eagerly demanded a song. "That is simple enough," Zhongyu replied. He downed a cup of wine and sang a verse of the 'Horse-Head Tune,' drawing cheers of approval from the group. Zhongyu lost the second round as well, requiring a joke. Glancing up, he noticed a small shrine affixed to the wall, housing an image of Zhao Gongming, the God of Wealth, riding a black tiger. He turned to Erxi and said, "When you performers encounter a wealthy patron, you rejoice, 'The God of Wealth has arrived, ready to spend!' But when a poor patron sees a dark-complexioned performer, he panics, 'A tiger is here, ready to devour anyone it meets!' Look up there—is that the God of Wealth riding a black tiger, or a destitute patron straddling a dark performer?" Pincai slammed the table in absolute delight, Yuanmao pinched his nose to stifle his laughter, while Baozhu tipped his head back to study the shrine. Erxi poured a cup of wine and presented it to Zhongyu, declaring, "A penalty for you! Your mockery is far too sharp." Zhongyu accepted the cup and drank, remarking, "Fortunately, there are no poor patrons terrified of performers here today." They played the third round, and Erxi lost, obliging him to offer Zhongyu a skin-cup. Zhongyu waved it off, "Let us skip that. Master Li did not properly enjoy his earlier cup; I ask you to pass this one to him instead." Erxi took a sip from the cup and offered it to Yuanmao, but Yuanmao shook his head vigorously, clamping his mouth shut in refusal. Undeterred, Erxi boldly straddled Yuanmao's lap. Sitting upright, he firmly grasped Yuanmao's head and tilted it back, forcing his face upward. Erxi pressed his delicate crimson lips tightly against Yuanmao's broad mouth, slowly and deliberately passing the wine. He did this three times in succession.

Yuanmao felt as though heavenly nectar was being poured over his crown; his joy was beyond words. The others listened as he audibly gulped three distinct times. Erxi poured more wine, and it was Pincai's turn. Erxi lost the first round and sang a verse of the 'Nine Linked Rings'.

Pincai lost the second round. With a sly grin, he began, "There were once two sisters-in-law. With the older brother away from home, the younger sister shared a bed with the older sister-in-law. Missing her husband terribly, the sister-in-law could not sleep. She asked the younger girl to mimic her brother's actions and lay atop her for a while. The sister-in-law was thrilled, sighing, 'It is wonderful, but you are not quite skilled at it—you are leaking water.' The younger girl replied, 'It is my first time! I will be an expert the second time around. We ought to give this a name.' The sister-in-law pondered, 'There is already a name for this—it is called grinding mirrors.' The girl argued, 'That doesn't fit. Mirrors are round. Let's just call it offering a skin-cup instead!'" This joke provoked roars of laughter. Tears streaming from his eyes, Yuanmao scolded, "You wicked man! You will surely be struck dumb by tomorrow!" Baozhu laughed until he collapsed into Pincai's lap. Erxi marched over and slapped Pincai lightly on the arm. "Is there anyone more wicked than you? Your insults cut straight to the bone!" By some twist of fate, Erxi lost the third round. Holding the wine, he asked, "How shall I sing it? You command." Feigning sternness, Pincai replied, "You listened to Master Zhang, but you ignore my words. You look down on me, and I will not stand for it today." Alarmed, Erxi protested, "I have done nothing to offend you." Pincai countered, "You may not have offended me, but you must obey my instructions." "Speak then," Erxi yielded. Pincai declared, "I decree that this skin-cup must also go to Master Li." Erxi obediently aimed the wine at Yuanmao, who protested, "Enough! You have made me your plaything all day. I will not fall for it again." Zhongyu teased, "Eldest Brother Li, if you refuse this cup, I shall invent another joke to mock you." Pincai spat in mock disgust, "Bah! A silver spearhead made of wax—so useless! One word and he loses his nerve." Yuanmao reasoned internally, "I cannot out-talk them. Whatever, there is no hardship in the world so long as one possesses a thick skin. Taking the advantage is always best." He boldly proclaimed, "I am not like the rest of you, paralyzed by bashfulness. Come, come, come! Watch me drink it again." Cupping Erxi's face in his own hands, he drank the cup. No one could mock him for that. With Erxi's penalty complete, Baozhu played a round with Yuanmao, lost, and sang a rendition of 'The River Runs Red'.

Suddenly, Pincai noticed a monk entering the room. "My Second Master!" the monk exclaimed. "Here you are! I have scoured seven or eight theaters looking for you!" Seeing the monk, Erxi and Baozhu both offered respectful greetings, while Pincai and Yuanmao rose to acknowledge him. The monk returned their bows and took a seat beside Zhongyu. Pincai appraised the newcomer: a square face the color of dark sugar, two thick mustaches, perhaps forty years of age. He wore a velvet monk's cap and a sapphire-blue silk robe lined with fox fur, tied at the waist with a yellow silk sash. On his feet were cloud-patterned black satin boots lined with fur. He bore absolutely no resemblance to an ascetic; he was undoubtedly a monk well-acquainted with wine and meat.

Yet, his demeanor was remarkably pleasant and accommodating. After inquiring after Pincai and Yuanmao's names and lodgings, he turned to Zhongyu. "Second Master, when tomorrow's affairs are concluded, we shall gather at either the Suzhou Guild Hall or the Tianqing Hall. Bring your two esteemed friends and these two young performers, and we shall enjoy a grand day of revelry. I truly cannot linger today; everyone has already assembled and is merely waiting on you to finalize the matter." Zhongyu replied evenly, "No need to rush. Have a cup, and we shall depart together." The monk stroked his mustache, grinning broadly as he downed a cup of wine and helped himself to a slice of ham.

Baozhu smiled playfully. "Master Tang, did you not bring your young son with you?" Monk Tang barked a laugh. "Preposterous! A monk doesn't even have a wife, where would I get a son?" Erxi retorted, "Your 'son' looks just like a wife anyway." Tang reached out to pinch Erxi's cheek. Erxi dodged, laughing, "I only said he looks like a wife, I wasn't wrong!" Noting Pincai and Yuanmao's presence, the monk feigned scholarly dignity, withdrawing his hand. "To mock a disciple of the Buddha is a grave sin," he chided. Zhongyu and Pincai burst into laughter. Tang again urged Zhongyu to leave. "A brief delay will do no harm," Zhongyu replied. Erxi pointed to a gourd hanging on the wall and asked Tang, "What does that resemble?" Tang chuckled, "It looks just like your mouth." Erxi shook his head. "Illogical! Why my mouth? It clearly resembles your bald pate—smooth and shiny, without a single hair." The monk laughed heartily. "It is naturally smooth. Have you not heard? Heaven has three lights, but the mortal realm has four: a monk's shining pate, a bride's smooth thighs, a patron's emptied silver pouch, and a performer's ravenous mouth. A monk's head is shaved bare, a bride's thighs are worn bare, a patron's pouch is spent bare, and a performer's mouth eats everything bare!" Roaring with laughter, he hauled Zhongyu to his feet. Offering a deep bow to Pincai, he smiled, "I speak nonsense; please do not take offense." Zhongyu said, "Let me settle the bill before we go." Pincai insisted, "Since you have business, please go ahead. I am the host today." Zhongyu replied, "Enjoy a few more cups, both of you. I will return shortly." With that, he took his leave, and the two departed.

Pincai and Yuanmao played another round of games with Baozhu, who offered two more skin-cups. Erxi ordered several more dishes, and the revelry continued for some time, well past the lighting of the candles, deep into the first watch of the night. The two performers were growing weary, and the two servants stood waiting by the wind-break door. Utterly disoriented, having consumed vast quantities of hot soup and wine, Li Yuanmao removed his hat. Steam rose thickly from his head, as if he were a bamboo steamer. "It is time we dispersed," Pincai suggested. He leaned close to Yuanmao's ear and whispered, "Take out the silver and settle the bill at the counter." Yuanmao patted his waist, a look of sudden panic crossing his face. "How strange!" he muttered. He stood up, lifted the back of his robes, and turned to Pincai. "Do you see it?" Pincai asked, "See what?" "The pouch." "It is not there." Yuanmao's face instantly went blank. "This is bizarre. Where could it have gone?" Baozhu asked, "What have you lost?" Ignoring him, Yuanmao frantically searched his inner robes, finding nothing. The blood began to drain from his face in waves. He undid his sash and shook his clothes, but the pouch was gone. Pincai grew anxious, "Do not tell me you forgot it." Yuanmao snapped, "Nonsense! You saw me wearing it!" He lifted his robe again, desperately feeling around his trouser waistband, but found nothing. Pincai pulled Yuanmao to the window; noting the servants were still present, they stepped out into the courtyard. "This is disastrous!" Pincai whispered fiercely. "Think—where could you have lost it?" The question jolted Yuanmao's memory. "Ah! Now I know. When I entered the theater, I tripped and fell. Someone helped me up and brushed the dust from my clothes. That man must have stolen it." Pincai frowned, "I didn't see you fall. When was this?" Yuanmao explained, "There was a low bench lying across the entrance. I wasn't looking and fell the moment I stepped inside." As quick-witted as Pincai was, he could think of no solution.

Erxi stepped out and asked, "What are you two discussing out here?" They returned inside and took their seats. "It is growing late," Erxi noted. He leaned intimately into Yuanmao's ear and whispered, "Come back to my place; I will take good care of you." Hearing this, Yuanmao felt a surge of joy mixed with intense panic, his face burning hot. He could only stare blankly at Pincai. Baozhu and Erxi were utterly bewildered by his behavior. Left with no alternative, Pincai finally spoke up, "Now that the silver has been lost, what are we to do?" Yuanmao added miserably, "There were other things in that pouch—it was all taken together." "What happened?" Baozhu demanded. Yuanmao explained, "The silver... at the theater entrance, a cutpurse made off with it." Erxi frowned. "I walked out with you. I saw no cutpurse." "It happened on the way in," Yuanmao clarified. Erxi's eyes narrowed. "It happened on the way in, yet you sat through the entire opera and drank with us for hours without noticing? And only now, when it is time to leave, do you mention it? Is it not more likely you simply forgot to bring it, and it remains at home?" Yuanmao bristled, "That is an outrage! Do you think I am making excuses?" Erxi offered a cold laugh. Pincai interceded hastily, "That is not what we mean. It is not as if we came here penniless, plotting to cheat you. Master Li was careless and lost his silver; naturally, it was difficult to admit at the time. Trust us—tomorrow, when we come to the theater, we will bring everything we owe, including Baozhu's share." He turned pleadingly to Baozhu, "Do you believe us?"

Baozhu replied, "I have no particular reason to doubt you. After all, this is our first meeting; you would hardly set out to deliberately cheat us. But when we return, we must present our accounts to our master. And during the New Year, returning empty-handed is especially difficult. Not only would we struggle to face our master, but it would be deeply unfair to the boys who attended us all day."

"We only ask that you find some way to spare us this hardship," Baozhu pleaded. He turned to Erxi, "Brother Xi, is that not fair?" Yuanmao insisted, "I am telling you the truth, yet you refuse to believe me! I had eight pieces of silver today, well over ten taels, loose in a pouch, and the thief took the pouch and all. To ask us to borrow money at this hour—where would we even go? You are putting us in an impossible position." Erxi scoffed, "It is not that late. Why not send your servant back to fetch more? We can wait here a while." He firmly pressed Yuanmao back into his seat. Yuanmao shook his head vehemently. "That is absolutely out of the question." Erxi's voice hardened. "If it is out of the question, then this was your plan from the start. We have spent the entire day in the company of strangers, serving wine and offering skin-cups. If you consider that a sufficient display of friendship, Master Li, then fine. But we, too, are driven by necessity—we must please our patrons, earn a few strings of cash, and maintain our standing before our master. If things always went this way, we would starve. Master Li, since you refuse to send someone home, I have one request. Please be so kind as to accompany me back and explain the situation to my master yourself. Whether you give us anything is irrelevant." Baozhu agreed, "What he says is perfectly reasonable. As long as our master hears the truth from you, that is all we ask. We do not need the money." Li Yuanmao was reduced to absolute helplessness. His face flushed a deep crimson, and he could not utter a single word. Pincai had no choice but to intervene. "We have only just made each other's acquaintance. Is this to be our first and last meeting? You ask him to face your master—your master would likely accept the explanation, but I have never heard of a performer dragging a patron before his master to settle an account." Baozhu replied coolly, "That is only because we are strangers. Once you have been our patron for years and we are well-acquainted, such measures would be unnecessary." The two sides were locked in a tense impasse. Suddenly, Sier rushed in and announced, "Young Master Sun is here! He just stepped out." Pincai saw an immediate lifeline. Knowing Sihui was familiar with these performers, he ordered, "Go quickly and invite him in!" Sier hurried out. Sihui was still at the counter, chatting with a performer who had just boarded a carriage to leave. Though he barely recognized Sier, hearing someone call for him, he returned inside, recognizing Yuanmao and Pincai. Spotting Erxi and Baozhu, he beamed, "My two gentlemen! What joyous merrymaking is this?" Yuanmao and Pincai offered respectful bows, while Erxi and Baozhu paid their respects. Once everyone was seated, Pincai quickly explained the predicament—how Yuanmao had lost his silver, had no means to pay, promised to settle the debt tomorrow, and how the performers stubbornly refused the offer. Sihui pushed back his hat, rubbed his reddened nose, pointed directly at Li Yuanmao, and launched into a grandiose, improvised recitation stringing together obscure phrases from the *Thousand Character Classic*. He proclaimed how Yuanmao's pouch of 'gold born of beautiful waters' had become like 'fallen leaves adrift in the wind.' Instead of venturing forth to 'execute thieves and robbers,' he sat here idly 'dispersing his cares in ease.' Assuming Sihui to be an 'old acquaintance,' he urgently summoned him 'to requite gladness and joy.' And seeing the two performers in their 'vigorous and resplendent' state, something deep within Sihui had suddenly 'pushed forth new shoots.' He prattled on through a bizarre parade of classical idioms, culminating in scandalous suggestions of what Yuanmao might do with the performers to ensure everyone's profound satisfaction. Wei Pincai and Li Yuanmao were already doubled over in hysterics. The two performers could scarcely follow the convoluted classical allusions, assuming he was merely reciting lines from a play, yet they, too, laughed uncontrollably. Yuanmao sputtered incoherently, unable to form a defense. Pincai, left with no alternative, finally stated he would personally assume the debt and settle with them the following day.

Hearing this, Sihui feigned profound shock and quoted dramatically, "'One whose strength cannot lift a hundred jun cannot bear a heavy burden on a long road'—I fear I am unequal to the task!" Pincai pleaded with him at length until Sihui finally, reluctantly, agreed. "What can be given tomorrow shall be given," he intoned solemnly. "'A man without integrity, who knows what becomes of him?'" He then turned to Erxi and Baozhu. "Come, let me speak plainly with you. Why not take your leave now? Tomorrow I shall personally deliver the funds to your door. Do not press people too harshly." Finding his classical rhetoric incomprehensible, Sihui was forced to repeat his guarantee in simple vernacular. Seeing that Sihui had formally assumed responsibility, Baozhu and Erxi were left with no further grounds for complaint. They made a reluctant show of gratitude and departed. Terrified they might also ask him to settle the restaurant bill, Sun Sihui practically sprinted out the door.

The waiter entered and read out the bill item by item, which, including the staff gratuity, totaled fifty-six strings and seven hundred eighty copper cash. Hearing the sum, Yuanmao stuck out his tongue in shock. "What discount can you offer?" he asked. "Full price, sir. No discounts," the waiter replied crisply. Counting on his fingers, Li Yuanmao calculated, "At a strict exchange rate, that is five thousand six hundred seventy-eight capital coins—exactly two thousand eight hundred thirty-nine hard-cash coins. Exceedingly fair! I shall certainly patronize your establishment frequently." The waiter chuckled, "We do not offer discounts. The total is fifty-six strings and seven hundred eighty copper cash." "How can it be so much?" Yuanmao protested. "We dare not overcharge," the waiter replied simply. Pincai nudged Yuanmao, "You are drunk. Stop talking nonsense. Let us go settle the bill at the counter." They approached the front desk, where the waiter recited the total once more. The manager clicked his abacus beads, then looked sharply at Pincai and Yuanmao. "You two arrived with Master Zhang, yet he has already departed. Are you his fellow provincials, or something else?" Pincai replied smoothly, "We are relatives. He had pressing business and left early." The manager pressed, "May I ask your honorable names? Where are you lodging? What business brings you to the capital?" Pincai provided brief answers, then requested a credit slip. The manager hesitated visibly. "Money is exceptionally tight during the New Year. Today is our very first day of business, and custom dictates we hope for a few cash payments in the first month. Furthermore, today is the very first time we have had the pleasure of your company."

"However, since Master Wei is a relative of Master Zhang, it would be deeply awkward for me to refuse you a credit account. But I beg of you—do not let this drag on too long." He handed a slip to Pincai. Seething with inward indignation, Pincai wrote out the full amount, added two strings for the wine service, and signed it: 'Mingke Alley, Mei Residence, Wei surname.'

The manager glanced at the slip and filed it away. A waiter brought out a lit lantern, which Sier accepted, and they exited the restaurant. The two men trudged home step by step, their heads bowed in heavy silence. It could truly be said: they arrived riding the crest of high spirits, only to depart mired in bitter deflation. But as for what unfolded next, please await the following chapter.
