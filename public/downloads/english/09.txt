Chapter 9 — At moonlit Lantern Night, flowers blaze in full bloom; amid pearl-like passion and qin-like yearning, one meeting is missed.

When Wei Pincai and Li Yuanmao arrived home, it was past the third night-watch. The Mei household had already barred and bolted its gates. Fourth Boy knocked for a long while before anyone came to open them. The two entered their rooms, and Pincai could not help voicing a few reproaches at Yuanmao for carelessly losing the silver. Yuanmao had no answer. They each went to sleep. The next day, they had no choice but to prevail upon Xu Shun to borrow a ten-string promissory note, which they divided into two drafts. Wrapping them with a letter, they had Fourth Boy deliver the money to Ye Maolin to distribute between Erxi and Baozhu. Later, when Ziyu inquired, Pincai and Yuanmao simply claimed that Zhang Zhongyu had treated them to opera and a restaurant, keeping the truth concealed.

Two days later came the Lantern Festival. Li Xingquan took Yuanmao to the year-end banquet at the guild hall, while Pincai had gone out to view the lanterns and had not yet returned. Ziyu sat alone with nothing to do when Mei Jinjin came in and announced: "Young Master Liu, Young Master Yan, and Young Master Wang invite you to go out and view the lanterns. They are all waiting at the gate." Ziyu reported to his parents, Mei Jinjin had the carriage harnessed, and Yun'er followed along. Zhongqing and the others were waiting in the carriage, stepping down when they saw Ziyu emerge. Liu Wenze said: "On such a fine night, worth a thousand gold pieces, let us take advantage of the lantern-lit moon and walk. That would be much better. We can have the carriage follow behind so we can ride back." Ziyu agreed. The four strolled slowly, chatting casually along the way, and in no time arrived at the lantern market.

The moment they entered the lantern arbors, they were engulfed in a sea of people, with carriages and horses jostling among the crowds. Ziyu and the others strolled slowly along the shop verandahs. Every shop was hung with festive lanterns and colored streamers. There were glass lanterns, painted gauze lanterns, elaborate screens displayed inside, and lantern towers erected outside the doors, while vendors set out their wares in dense, layered rows. Fortunately the streets were broad, for otherwise one could not have moved a single step. At the doorways, people set off firecrackers and rockets: shooting stars chased the moon, nine dragons played with a pearl, and fire trees bloomed with silver flowers. Mingled with gongs, drums, silk strings, and wind instruments, it was a scene of perfect peace and abundance. Everyone was in high spirits celebrating the Lantern Festival. A procession of fine carriages and ornate sedans passed by with their curtains open. Maids and servants sat on the carriage edges, burning superior incense, while the ladies inside gazed left and right through large glass windows. Wenze, Wang Xun, and the others watched attentively. Though they varied in beauty, the women's festive dress and jewels under the lamplight added radiant layers of color. As the four friends walked, they could not stay abreast, with some falling ahead and others lingering behind. After perhaps seven or eight carriages had passed, a few more pulled up to join the front of the procession, jamming the thoroughfare so it could not move.

At that moment Ziyu was in the lead and had just been blocked by a carriage axle, unable to pass. Wenze noticed a young woman inside the carriage, quite handsome and exquisitely dressed. Ziyu happened to be squeezed against the front of the carriage. Wenze saw the young woman's eyes fixed unblinkingly on Ziyu, while Ziyu had lowered his head with nowhere to retreat. He watched as the young woman placed one hand on the carriage door and leaned forward, extending one foot. It was a three-inch lotus hook, slender and delicate enough to fit in a palm. She first arranged her opposite leg, then drew the lotus foot back in and sat properly, lowering her slender hand. Turning toward Ziyu, she offered a sweet, gentle smile.

Wenze tugged at Wang Xun's sleeve and murmured softly: "Look, it seems she is taken with Yuanxiang and wishes to show off her lotus feet." Wang Xun nodded. Zhongqing, standing behind Wenze, added: "How do you know it is not you she fancies?" Wenze laughed: "I do not think so." Then he called quietly: "Yuanxiang, what is there to look at in 'The Shi Justice Cases'? You keep staring at those pairs of lanterns." Ziyu turned his face around, only to meet the young woman's gaze directly. Seeing she was still watching him from inside the glass window, he felt quite awkward.

After a moment the carriage moved on. Wenze noticed that hanging from the carriage side-panel was a small foreign-style lantern painted with two ruyi scepters. Four small characters on its face read: Qisheng Hao Pan, marking the Pan family of the Qisheng trading house. Behind it followed another carriage.

In this carriage too was a young woman, yet she was strikingly ugly. Her face was a mass of dark, fleshy features, plastered with powder that showed a purplish, greasy sheen even in the night. Her dress, however, featured every fashionable variety, and she was making affected gestures inside the carriage, fluttering her silken sleeve-cloth. Wenze noticed that a character on her lantern read 'Hua', meaning Flower. The carriage moved and passed on. The four wandered to a few more spots, where the streets began to narrow again. Wenze said to Ziyu: "Just now that young woman was so attentive in her glancing at you, yet you gave her no response and kept looking at that old gauze lantern. What is the meaning of this? Could a young woman like that not merit even one glance in return?"

Ziyu laughed: "I paid no attention to her, nor did she look at me. She was sizing you gentlemen up." The four friends bantered and laughed as they moved on to admire a few more lantern displays.

Just then a crowd of women came rushing toward them on foot in a disorderly cluster. The four men counted some ten or more of them, some in silk robes and some in plain cotton. With oiled hair and powdered faces, they giggled and chatted, their sleeves fluttering like frantic butterflies threading among blossoms, their figures darting like startled snakes from the grass. They showed no concern for whether others might give way and came barging straight through. One of them, apparently with natural feet, planted a foot squarely on Wang Xun's boot.

Wang Xun's new black boot had its side smudged, and his foot was trodden on with considerable force. He reckoned the blow carried at least thirty catties of weight, but he could say nothing and quickly stepped aside. Then he saw a woman of some thirty years, of considerable height, wearing high-platform shoes and looking up at the lanterns. Her foot caught on a brick, and she lurched to one side, nearly toppling over. She happened to grab onto Ziyu's chest, clutching it tight to steady herself. Ziyu nearly fell as well, his heart leaping in fright, utterly bewildered as to what was happening. The woman let go and giggled as the whole crowd squeezed past. He heard one of the women say: "These gentlemen are truly infuriating. They keep their eyes wide open staring at people. Can it be there are no women at home, that they block the road on purpose and will not let anyone pass?" Zhongqing and the others heard this and burst out laughing. Wang Xun said: "What rotten luck. That woman's foot hurt me badly, and she still says we blocked the road to stare at her." Ziyu had just recovered his composure and said: "Just now when she grabbed me I was genuinely frightened. I thought she had mistaken me for someone and was about to start a brawl. Is that not bad luck? Who would have imagined that among women there could be such uncouth creatures? Comparing them to the lady we saw in the carriage just now, there is truly a difference of heaven and earth." Wenze laughed loudly: "Without climbing a high mountain, you do not see flat ground. So you have been harboring private thoughts all along, silently taking the measure of things. That young woman in the carriage earned this praise from you, which is not a bad return for all her attentive glancing." Ziyu smiled faintly and said: "These lanterns have nothing much to see, and the road is hard to walk. Why not take the carriages back?" Wang Xun replied: "It is too early, and going back serves no purpose either." Wenze said: "Let us go to Yiyuan to see the lanterns there. I hear they have fine lantern riddles too, and going to guess a few would be amusing." Ziyu said: "I do not know the host, and since it is evening and we are in plain clothes, how can we go?" Zhongqing said: "That is no obstacle. Xu Duxiang is one of our sort. He places no importance on formalities or appearances. Moreover, his garden also has Xiao Jingyi, who is an even more upright and free-spirited person. Going for a stroll would be perfectly fine." All three wished to go, and Ziyu agreed to join them. So everyone got into their carriages, with the pages riding on the footboards, and headed for Yiyuan.

After about two li they passed Nanheng Street and alighted at the gate of Yiyuan garden. A long wall of tiger-stripe stone, made of rough yellow fragments cemented together, enclosed the estate. The garden gate was framed by a five-colored ceremonial arch built of bolts of silk, with only a square opening of some five feet displaying the two large characters 'Yiyuan'. Below hung four consecutive rows of eight five-colored glass lanterns painted with flowers. Through the garden gate was a set of eight oil-green gold-speckled screen doors. Beside the gate stood a table surrounded by six or seven people writing out lantern riddle strips. Beside it was a long bench with sachets, firecrackers, and the four treasures of the study arranged on it, prepared as prizes for correct answers. In the very center of the overhead canopy hung a five-colored brocade pleated cloud-pavilion, from which was suspended a gourd-shaped glass lantern.

Further inside was a square pavilion built against the wall, surrounded by railings on three sides. A flat rectangular glass lantern hung above the pond, covered with strips of paper, while below it a cluster of some twenty people had gathered. Climbing the pavilion steps, they found eight lantern riddles written out facing them. Just as Zhongqing was about to read them, a Yiyuan servant came up to greet them and said: "Why not come inside to look around?" Wenze asked about the host, and the man replied: "Our master is out at a dinner and has not returned, but Master Xiao is at home." Wang Xun said: "Let us guess a few lantern riddles first and then go in. There is no hurry." So they all looked at the first riddle: 'Roosting together, stable and undisturbed, recognizing the Lu family's tortoiseshell rafters.' Below was a notation indicating a line from the Book of Rites. Ziyu was pondering it when he heard Wang Xun ask Zhongqing: "Could this be: 'knowing that one can dwell in peace, like the swallow who is settled and orderly'?" Zhongqing replied: "I believe so." They looked at the second: 'Though ten thousand waters and a thousand mountains separate us, the wild goose's letter and the fish's message always arrive.' The notation indicated a line from the Book of Changes. Zhongqing said: "This must truly be: 'proceeding through danger yet not losing one's integrity.'" Ziyu said: "That fourth one, 'fallen blossoms, one person standing alone; fine rain, swallows flying in pairs', must be the character for 'liang', meaning both or together." Wenze said: "This seventh one: 'In the desolate village, rain and dew, sleep comes early; at the roadside inn, wind and frost, rising must be late.' These two lines on an ancient person's name must be 'Xi Fugong'." Ziyu agreed. Wang Xun said: "Let us go submit our answers." Zhongqing said: "Let us simply solve all four remaining ones too, then submit. There is no hurry. That second one, 'A crow's back lit by the setting sun', is a line from the Book of Rites; it must be: 'the sun is in the Wing constellation.'" Ziyu said: "That seven-character regulated verse on eight titles of ancient Music Bureau songs. The first couplet: 'I remember how at your home, morning and evening, the Qinhuai River wound its fragrant banks in several bends.' The answer must be 'Midnight Song' and 'Jinling Ballad.'" Zhongqing said: "The lower line of the second couplet, 'the moon's shadow particularly dislikes the dark and dusty world,' is 'Night Yellow'. But what of the upper line, 'Let not the thread of rain urge the flower-petals along'?"

Wenze said: "Perhaps it is 'Wash Not the Red'. The third couplet, 'Through the long night I hear the broken water-clock dripping; in middle age I pour out my heart and yet labor in vain', must be 'Five-Watch Bell' and 'Mourn Not, Joy'." Wang Xun said:

"The seventh line, 'Thirty-six pairs of crows fly in steady formation,' is unmistakably 'Crow with Eight or Nine Chicks'." Zhongqing said: "The last line, 'She should send the distant traveler on the road to Zhangtai,' must be 'Breaking the Willow'. As for the fifth entry, 'Born in the years of chen and si', based on a line from the Classic of Poetry, and the eighth entry, 'Not a word written, yet the style is perfect', on a Tang poetry line, I cannot solve those." Just then someone was heard asking: "'Born in the years of chen and si', could that be 'Where there are vipers and serpents'?" The man at the garden gate replied it was not. Wenze said: "Do not let others take it from us. Let us go submit." Everyone descended the pavilion steps. Ziyu said: "As for that Classic of Poetry one, I have thought of it. It must be 'not belonging to the fur-bearing creatures.'" Zhongqing said: "Precisely right. That answer was truly remarkable to have thought of."

Wang Xun said: "The one on the Tang poetry line, could it not be 'Yin Zi writing in the air'?" Wenze replied: "Let us go and try submitting it." Everyone went to the garden gate and reported their answers one by one. From inside came "Correct" for each one, until the very last, which no one had solved. Wang Xun said: "'Certainly poetry has no rival.'" From inside came "Correct" for that as well. A person came out with a new lantern and swapped it for the one already hanging. From behind the screen door someone came walking out. Ziyu saw a man of about thirty, with clear brows and fine eyes and an air of lofty elegance, dressed in understated, refined clothing. He strolled over in a leisurely, graceful fashion.

Wenze, Zhongqing, and Wang Xun all stepped forward at once to greet him, addressing him as Master Jingyi. The man exchanged courtesies with the three, then bowed to Ziyu, who hastened to return the salutation.

Wenze thereupon said to Xiao Cixian: "This gentleman is Mei Yuanxiang, the peerless scholar of this age. Has Master Jingyi not had occasion to meet him?" Cixian replied: "Meeting such a distinguished personage today is truly my great good fortune." He then invited the four men inside. Ziyu said: "Being in plain clothes this evening, I fear it is somewhat discourteous. Please allow me to pay a proper formal call another day." Cixian laughed: "Master Yuanxiang is the foremost distinguished scholar of the age, and such trifles should not matter. Moreover, the host is not at home, and we are simply gathering to chat informally. Please do not stand on ceremony." Ziyu found it hard to persist in declining, and had no choice but to follow him out of the pavilion. On either side, at ten-pace intervals, stood ground lanterns that illuminated a flat open space. Not far ahead rose a sheer cliff face. At the foot of the cliff was a five-bay veranda-room with carved windows and fine lattice-work. Beneath its eaves hung over twenty foreign glass lanterns of uniform style, all of the western fragrant-lotus type. Cixian invited them inside, and guest and host took their seats. He was acquainted with Wenze, Wang Xun, and Zhongqing, and had only an exchange of heartfelt admiration to make with Ziyu. Ziyu, seeing his speech proper and his bearing extraordinary, knew this was a man of distinction, and the two found themselves thoroughly congenial. After some conversation and tea, a page-boy came from the inner room and brought out prize gifts arranged on the table. There were fine Hu brushes, Hui ink-sticks, Duan inkstones, and elegant fans. Only the prize for Ziyu's solved riddle, "fallen blossoms, one person standing alone; fine rain, swallows flying in pairs", was the heaviest: a jade qin zither in an old brocade case. Ziyu saw the qin and felt a sudden vague sense of memory. Finding the prize too generous, he repeatedly declined alongside Zhongqing and the others. Cixian asked:

"Was it you, Master Yuanxiang, who solved the qin riddle?" Ziyu replied: "I merely guessed at random. I could not possibly presume to accept such a generous prize." Cixian replied: "This was set by the garden's master for Du Yunong, and carries a particular meaning, so pray do not decline. An inscription still needs to be engraved on the qin. I will send it once that is done." With this he had a page-boy carry the jade qin back inside, and the remaining prizes were given to each guest's attendants. The fact was that because Qin Yan loved to recite the couplet about "fallen blossoms" when Duxiang was composing the riddle, Duxiang had asked Cixian to make this a riddle with the qin as the prize, embedding within the playful game the intention of making a match. Not only did Ziyu not know who Du Yunong was, but even Zhongqing, Wenze, and the others had not been fully informed. When everyone asked, Cixian did not immediately explain, replying only that all would be known in due time.

They conversed idly for a time. Zhongqing remarked that it was a pity that the capital, despite being in the very season of lantern-viewing, had no southern-style novelty lanterns, a considerable diminishment of the festival's splendor.

Cixian replied: "You gentlemen wish to see lanterns? That can easily be arranged. Though these are not from the south, they are by no means unrefined." He had a page-boy lead the way. Skirting along the sheer cliff face, they walked the length of an arrow-shot, then mounted a succession of stone steps. Climbing over thirty tiers, they turned a corner of the cliff and found themselves on a white-stone terrace on the other side.

At the center stood a round pavilion, all of whose windows were inlaid with whole panes of glass that were concave on the inside and convex on the outside.

Entering the pavilion, they found the floor laid with a velvet carpet. In the center stood a large round table surrounded by fan-shaped stools, fitted together in a perfect circle that precisely encircled the table. Because the night air was mild, Zhongqing and the others sat on the outer gallery railing. Page-boys brought round tea-tables, one before each guest, and tea was served. Looking upward, a hazy moon shed diffuse light, and sparse stars lay scattered. Looking downward, drifting mist hung light and vast. The void and water were both clear and fresh. One felt the spirit broadened and the heart refreshed. Looking far into the distance, one could just make out overlapping peaks and towering pavilions. They were dimly visible and unclear, and not a single lantern light was yet to be seen. Suddenly, from the Lake Tai rock cave in front of the pavilion, a pair of bright lanterns lit the way for two jade-like figures. Coming closer, one proved to be Yuan Baozhu, and the other Jin Shufang. Zhongqing asked: "Where have you been hiding?" Baozhu replied: "We were playing chess in the little boat room at the front." Wenze said: "Have you performers been keeping your eyes open?" Baozhu and Shufang both smiled. Of those present, only Ziyu did not know them. Though he had seen Shufang's 'Preface to a Song' that day, that had been in full makeup and costume. Now he saw them in plain dress. Their flesh was fragrant and smooth, of jade-pure clarity and crystalline freshness. Baozhu stood slender as jade, seemingly too delicate to bear the weight of clothing. Ziyu thought to himself: "These two in beauty seem comparable to Qin Yan. What their characters are like, I do not know." Just as he was thinking this, he heard a cloud-gong strike once below the terrace. In the far tree-groves across the way, several shooting-star rockets rose. Immediately a succession of clay-cylinders followed, one after another, near and far, some two hundred in all. Orchid-flower bamboo-arrows shot across the garden. The green bamboo groves and winter trees were set aglow as if painted in firelight. The clay-cylinders burned for a while, and several large cannon shots were heard. Yellow smoke rose from the groves in every direction. Then a thousand firecrackers rang out in unison, and countless fireworks lit up all around. On one side were nine-linked lanterns, and on another, the Myriad Years of Joy. On one side was a cannon battering Xiangyang City, and on another, the burning of Red Lotus Temple. On one side was the Great Fire of Efang Palace, and on another, the burning of troops at the Red Cliff. In the distance, golden drums beat in a furious gallop, evoking the charge of ten thousand horses. Fire-birds and fire-mice flew like a hundred flashes of lightning, darting and weaving through the entire garden. Ziyu and the others gazed in dazzled astonishment.

Wenze thought to himself: "What a pity there is no wine, for we are squandering this magnificent evening of flowers and lanterns." Just then he heard Cixian say: "On such a splendid night, why do you gentlemen not drink a few cups?" He at once ordered wine to be brought. In a short while the page-boys brought four jars of wine and handed them to Baozhu and Shufang, who went before each guest, removed the teacups, and lifted a layer off the tea-tables. This revealed inlaid tiered boxes within, each containing twelve plates of fruits and dishes, with silver cups and ivory chopsticks fitted inside, all of exquisite ingenuity. Baozhu and Shufang poured the wine. Cixian said "Please!" and everyone began drinking in slow, relaxed sips. After several rounds, the cloud-gong below the terrace struck once. The fireworks in all directions died away. Then on every treetop, countless colored lanterns trembled into view. There were birds and flowers, scattered and layered, growing more and more numerous. In a short while the entire surrounding area held some several thousands, and all the lanterns in the trees had been lit. On the ground, several hundred cloud lanterns began to dance, their five colors swirling and folding, circling and spiraling. At the sound of the gong, a golden dragon emerged dancing. It was over ten zhang long, soaring and twisting like a real dragon. Presently, from within the Immortal's Grotto came a green dragon dancing, followed by a white dragon. From the groves came a black dragon, and in the light of the fireworks came a fire dragon. Each was over ten zhang long. They tumbled together while dozens of gong-beats roared like crashing waves. Their shifting smoke-clouds were truly magnificent to see. Then several dozen large and small ball-lanterns rolled out among the cloud-dragons, spinning and turning. This drew the five dragons to spread their claws and toss their horns, twisting and grasping in a frenzy. Everyone in the party was completely transfixed.

Suddenly a Yiyuan servant came forward announcing: "Young Master Shi has arrived!" Everyone rose to look. They saw two attendants supporting Shi Nanxiang, who came stumbling and staggering, step by halting step, up the stone stairs.

Just as he reached the terrace, he suddenly heaved in a great surge of vomiting.

After retching for a while, he shook his head and stood panting at the edge of the terrace, pointing at the assembled company and saying:

"You are having such a fine time, such a fine time..." He could say no more. A servant first brought a bowl of warm water for him to rinse his mouth, then he managed: "What a fine time you are all having!" Zhongqing said: "Sit down first. Rest and then tell us." They helped him up into the pavilion, and he sat on the floor. Baozhu and the others went up to greet him, and he nodded to them. Wenze asked: "Where have you been drinking to reach this state?" Nanxiang shook his head again. Baozhu whispered a few words to Cixian. Cixian instructed a page-boy to bring a small gold box, from which he took out a pill, placed it in a bowl, dissolved it in boiling water, and gave it to Baozhu. Baozhu carried it over to Nanxiang and bent down to offer it to him. Nanxiang shook his head, refusing. Baozhu said: "This is a sobering draught. Drink it and you will be well." Nanxiang's mind was clear enough; he drank the draught, closed his eyes and said: "I am drunk and wish to sleep, so my friend may go." He lay back as if to sleep. Cixian, fearing he might catch cold, had servants carry him to a kang in the small inner room, helped Nanxiang inside, and pulled the door behind him. Ziyu asked Cixian what the pill was. Cixian replied: "It is something Duxiang himself makes. No matter how dead-drunk one may be, one pill and all the stored wine is consumed, with the vital energy additionally restored. It is called the Immortal Peach Life-Prolonging Pill."

Before long, they saw Nanxiang already open the door and walk out, saying: "Delightful! Delightful! I nearly became Liu Xuanshi who slept a drunken sleep of three years. I was nearly buried alive." Zhongqing said: "Your head has cleared, so why are you still talking drunken nonsense?" Shufang had wrung out a damp cloth. Nanxiang wiped his face and asked: "Where are we?" Everyone laughed. Cixian smiled and said: "Zhujun, this is Yellow Crane Tower. How can you not recognize it?" Nanxiang looked around and burst into wild laughter, saying: "So Jingyi is here too. When exactly did all of you arrive?" Everyone laughed again. Baozhu and Shufang drew him outside the pavilion and showed him around a while. Only then did Nanxiang understand they were in Yiyuan, and thinking it through carefully, he laughed loudly again. Just as he was about to ask something, the entire garden erupted. Golden drums filled the sky, explosions burst in continuous succession, and the wind was swift and fire fast. The sounds and spectacle were overwhelming. From all eight directions, a hundred beasts converged, all fashioned from five-colored silk gauze with their fur realistically painted. On one side was a cavalcade of elephant lanterns, and on another, a cavalcade of tiger lanterns. On one side was a charge of rhinoceros, and on another, a charge of lions, plus black bears, white bulls, spotted leopards, and yellow bears. Strange and wondrous, they numbered in the hundreds. Each had four small wheels beneath and was pulled by people at a run, emitting smoke from their nostrils and belching fire from their mouths. It was as thunderous and electrifying as if the earth had collapsed and the mountains fallen. Ziyu and the others were so astonished their skin rose in gooseflesh. On this side a hundred beasts, on that side five dragons, merging together with black fog rising to the sky and firelight covering the ground. It truly evoked the ferocity of the Red Cliff battle. After a long commotion, suddenly a single crack was heard, and in mid-air a nine-burst cannon was set off. In an instant the firelight below scattered like shuttles, and in a moment the whole garden fell utterly silent, with not a single lantern to be seen. The distinguished scholars all cried out in praise together: "Truly the artistry of heaven and earth, the strategy of Sun and Wu, we have never seen the like!" Zhongqing said: "Counting this one display, I calculate there must have been at least a thousand people. Where did the garden find so many people?" Cixian replied: "If one uses people for everything, of course there are many. These five dragon lanterns use people throughout. The hundred beasts and cloud lanterns use wheels to move, so one person can operate several. The beasts leading beasts require advance and retreat, fast and slow in coordination, which too requires rehearsal. Today's actual number was probably still fewer than two hundred." All the distinguished scholars were full of admiration. Cixian invited his guests down the hill to a spacious place to rest a while, and the company, reluctant to disperse so soon, followed him down the hill.

Threading through several grottoes fashioned to resemble immortal caverns, they followed a path lined with tree-screens and bamboo until they reached a pear-blossom garden. Cixian ushered the guests inside. Passing through several more sets of gates, they entered a five-bay Western-style building, three rooms wide and two rooms deep, facing east. The interior was appointed with extraordinary elegance. There were lacquered beds, painted writing-tables, golden tripods, and bronze vases, all pleasingly beautiful. In the center hung a plaque inscribed with three characters, 'Yichun Pavilion' or Lodge of Welcoming Spring, in the calligraphy of Qu Benli. On one side hung several panels of small-script calligraphy by Sulan, and on the other side, several panels of ink-painted orchids by Yuan Baozhu. In the middle of the floor stood a lamp imitating an ancient chicken-foot design in silver, some four feet tall. It held a nine-petal lotus lamp-basin above, burning nine wicks, which filled the whole room with bright light. Everyone sat down. Cixian said: "Why not drink a few more cups?" Everyone replied: "We have already drunk too much in the pavilion. We can do without wine, so let us have a quiet, simple gathering." Nanxiang said: "I have no idea how my drunken state cleared. What happened?" Baozhu replied:

"We who were sober today watched others in their cups, and that also had its charms." Nanxiang said: "Yaoquing, I recall that you poured a great bowl of wine down my throat." Everyone laughed and said: "This fellow is still befuddled from drink. How much did you actually consume in the end?" Nanxiang said: "Today I went with Gao Zhuoran and Zhang Zhongyu, bringing Wang Jingfang and Li Peixian, and spent the whole day drinking at the winehouse. I do not know how much it was; they all got drunk and left before me. I saw Jingfang home, then dropped by Yong'an's place on the way. Learning he had gone out to see the lanterns, I went too. I do not know what the carriage-driver was thinking, but he just pulled me here. The people at the garden gate said you were inside viewing the lanterns, so they helped me in." As he spoke, he pulled from his lapel a bundle of lantern-riddle strips. Everyone looked. One read 'A spring breeze song for which the embroidered silk wrapping costs a fortune', and another read 'Little horse, run quickly along'. Both were theater-title riddles, one being 'Enjoying Autumn' and the other 'Chasing the Carriage'. Baozhu said to Shufang with a laugh: "One is yours, one is mine, and he solved them both." Nanxiang laughed: "So you two made them!" He then turned to Ziyu and said: "Yuanxiang, what do you make of these two? Judging from their looks and talents, am I telling the truth or not? Now look at this," he pointed at the calligraphy and paintings on the walls, "this is painted by Yaoquing, and that is written by Xiangwan. Compare the outside crowd of pseudo-scholars. Can any of them match these real performers?"

Ziyu laughed: "I long ago made my acknowledgment, yet my elder brother still dwells on it at every moment." Nanxiang declared: "Henceforth, among your company, when you meet us you must not bow in salutation. You may only address us by our courtesy names. Violators will be penalized." Baozhu said: "This is exactly the same disposition as Duxiang and Jingyi, so it shall be." Wang Xun said: "Yuanxiang, look at this Yaoquing. How does he compare to what you saw at the theater last year? Could the real and the fake possibly be confused?" Ziyu laughed: "Can rubble and stone presume to the name of pearl and jade? That person's name should be changed." Baozhu was puzzled and asked Wang Xun. Wang Xun told the story of having seen the fake Baozhu the previous year and of Ziyu's mishearing. Baozhu gave a sweet smile.

Thereupon Shufang drew Wang Xun into a game of chess while Wenze looked on. Ziyu and Baozhu admired the ink-painted orchid, praising it without end. Nanxiang, Zhongqing, and Cixian sat together on the Drunken-Scholar's Couch in easy conversation. Nanxiang said: "Brother Jingyi, do you still recall those fine lines: 'Only the poet-drunkard is famed among the scholars; drunk and chanting, he may ascend Yueyang Tower'?"

Cixian replied: "That cannot compare to the celebrated lines: 'I only grieve that the immortals' elixir is scarce, preventing wine from filling the whole of Dongting Lake.'" Zhongqing said: "If wine filled Dongting Lake, I fear Shi Zhujun would long since have drunk himself to death. Master Jingyi, why not paint him a 'Drunken Bamboo' picture tomorrow." Cixian nodded and smiled faintly. Taking advantage of the others' conversation, Ziyu quietly asked Baozhu: "These past two days, have you seen Qin Yan from your troupe?" Baozhu, hearing this, looked Ziyu over carefully and asked: "Are you and Qin Yan close friends?" This question rather caught Ziyu off guard. Quite embarrassed, he could only answer: "We have never met. I merely admire his name." Baozhu replied: "He is no longer called Qin Guan; his name has been changed to Qin Yan. What a pity you were just a step too late today, for Duxiang took him to a dinner engagement." Ziyu thought to himself: "It is as if fate is sparing in its dealings with us. I do not even have the fortune of a proper conversation with him." As he thought, he stared absently at Baozhu, and Baozhu too was staring absently at Ziyu. Four eyes met and both fell into a trance. Liu Wenze, turning his head and seeing the scene, gave Ziyu a light tap on the shoulder and said quietly: "Is Yaoquing fine?" Ziyu, thinking he was asking about Qin Yan, replied: "His performance of 'Startled by the Geese' is straight from heaven among immortals." Baozhu gave a languid smile. Ziyu came back to himself and realized the question and answer had been mismatched, but luckily what he had said was not wrong. He followed Wenze over to Nanxiang's side. Zhongqing asked Cixian whether any fine lantern riddles had been solved by others. Cixian replied: "Yesterday there were two love-letters that a young man solved. I happened to have a matter to attend to and stepped away, and did not get his name and address." Zhongqing asked Cixian for the rough drafts of the two love-letters, and everyone read them together. One contained herb names concealed within it, the other flower names. They read:

"Last autumn, in the eighth month, we gathered in joy at Jin Gate. Huang first smiled and beckoned, fragrant as the oil-walled carriage that came to meet me. Though my talent is but a peck and a bushel, I am privileged to follow in your perfumed train. The road ahead stretches ten thousand li, regretting this search for rank and title. A thin shadow dwelling alone, still hoping to continue my life. Asking who governs the heart of the grass, fearing that the flower-tidings come too often to urge me on. Though powdered like a fair young gentleman, the black silk not yet old, yet the fragrant attendant's jade bones have already grown cold. Only by maintaining propriety can one protect oneself. Alone through the remaining years, enduring the winter. Counting on fingers, the gourd-harvest time is nearly upon us. This heart's bitterness is entirely dissolved. This letter arrives before you, and I hope for a brief reply. The fifteenth of the fifth month, respectfully bowing before your jade presence."

Ziyu exclaimed: "What a fine puzzle in the guise of a Lantern Festival riddle!" Baozhu said: "I love most the couplet about the 'powder-faced young gentleman.'" Nanxiang said: "Among us here, only Yuanxiang qualifies as a powder-faced young gentleman. Do you admire him?" Baozhu gave a little smile, and Ziyu's cheeks turned thoroughly red in embarrassment. Then they read the reply letter:

"A foot away, reluctant to convey its fragrance, a fragrant letter suffused with crimson. Bowels twisting as if turning back upon themselves, the year cycling round, now renewed. Remembering the joyful meeting of that former night, the farewell banquet, the parting flight. The jade maiden playing pitch-pot, faintly opening fragrant cheeks in a smile. Golden lotus steps on the ground, small steps soft in the dust. Ever since the distant search for Chang'an, only grieving over shyness and timidity. Lingering by the Luo River bank, suddenly light meets light. For you, with rouge and powder cases, your beautiful form is naturally fine. For me, already with snow-thread and frost-temples, all worldly habits forgotten. Past ninety days of spring light, a few fallen petals. Praying that through all the great Dharma-realm, lotus-pairs bloom for three lifetimes. Reckoning the jade ladle at the conjunction of yin and mao, may the jeweled box divine the starry meeting. Cut into frosty silk, wishing to send but repeatedly delayed, hovering back and forth. The sixteenth of the second month, at the yin hour, another respectful salutation is enclosed."

Zhongqing said: "These two love-letters, even setting aside the riddle element, are supremely fragrant and beautiful in their own right. Moreover the concealed herb names and flower names fit each context perfectly without the slightest strain. The person clever enough to solve them must truly be a peerless wit. What a pity we do not know who it was." Wenze said: "Are both these letters written in Master Jingyi's hand?" Cixian replied: "The original letter is in Duxiang's hand." As he spoke, Wang Xun had already finished his chess game, having lost three stones to Shufang. Ziyu, the night being already deep, took his leave together with Nanxiang and the others. Ziyu also conveyed that when Duxiang returned to the garden he would first pay his respects, and that he would come again in earnest on another day. Cixian replied affirmatively and saw each guest to their carriage. So they scattered. Hear what follows in the next chapter.
