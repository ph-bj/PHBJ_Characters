Chapter 11 — Three beauties turn language into fresh brilliance; maidservants are rebuked over playful talk.

After Xu Ziyun had seen Ziyu out of the garden, he talked a while with Xiao Cixian and then returned home. Ziyun's residence was not far from the garden — directly across the way — the old ancestral mansion of his late great-grandfather, the Grand Chancellor, spacious and grand. His parents, elder brother, and sister-in-law had all left the capital, so the household now consisted only of Ziyun and his wife, with the remaining staff being servants. Ziyun told his wife, Mrs. Yuan, about the affair of Qin Yan and Ziyu, and they both marveled at the delicate tenderness of the attachment. Mrs. Yuan smiled and asked:

"These young gentleman-actors — what is it about them that you all find so appealing? What is really so wonderful about them?" Ziyun laughed: "You have seen them yourself and heard them sing — can you really say they are not fine?" Mrs. Yuan said: "When I watch them perform, it seems to me they are only imitating the manners of young ladies. And when they remove their makeup, they are indeed fair and delicate-looking. But to call them priceless treasures beyond compare — that I cannot understand. From my perspective, they seem hardly superior to my own maids." Ziyun said: "From your vantage point, naturally the female of the species seems best. But we cannot bring young women along to banquets and social gatherings — that would injure the refinement of the occasion. The merit of these young actors lies precisely in this: they have a woman's face but not a woman's body. They delight the eye and yet restrain the heart, giving one pleasure without desire. Is that not doubly perfect?" Mrs. Yuan laughed: "Nicely put, at any rate." Ziyun also laughed: "I mean every word — in all my life I have never said anything I did not truly mean." Mrs. Yuan said: "Granted you are like that — but are all your friends the same?" Ziyun said: "If they were not, they would be as incompatible with me as fire and ice. Just now, did I not tell you how Mei Yuxiang — when that Yu Ling said a few flirtatious words — flew into such a rage that he even came around to scolding me? Is that not worthy of trust? Besides, those boys themselves dislike being teased." They chatted a while longer, and then Mrs. Yuan mentioned that the following day was Lady Hua's birthday — her twentieth birthday, a major milestone — and they really ought to pay a visit. Ziyun said: "We certainly should go. Besides, she came in person for your birthday last year and brought quite a number of gifts; we ought to prepare a few fine things to send in return." And so the evening passed without further incident.

Early the next morning, Mrs. Yuan selected ten choice curios — all of considerable value — and drew up a list: a jade interlocking ring of jasper and nephrite; a seven-treasures hairpin; a fan of kingfisher feathers; a coral hair-pin; a filigree gold mountain-censer; a qin of pale jade; a rare piece of aquilaria incense; a zither bridge of precious sinking-wood; a strand of lignum aloes; an agate seal. These were dispatched ahead by servant. Then, from among her twelve red-named maids, she took Hongxue, Hongzhu, Hongxiang, Hongyu, Hongwei, and Hongwen — all six of them fifteen years old in the prime of their bloom, slender and graceful, literate, skilled in calligraphy, accomplished in poetry and embroidery. After attending their mistress through her morning toilet, Hongxue said: "The weather is cold today, and there seems to be snow in the air; we must bring several extra garments." She went to the heavy-lined clothing and selected a sky-blue satin cloak embroidered with golden figures on purple sable fur, a red satin skirt embroidered with golden celestial horses, jade pendants that clinked and chimed, and a strand of pearl tassels. In addition, a large red padded bundle was packed with two or three more garments. All the floral ornaments and precious accessories were placed in a brocaded case. The six reds finished their own preparations, and then all boarded the fragrant carriage; household men mounted horses outside and proceeded toward the Hua mansion.

Now as for the young master Hua: he was twenty-one years old. Though his appearance had briefly entered Wei Pincai's field of vision and his character had been touched upon in Fu San's words, neither account had been thorough. In truth, young Master Hua's bearing was extravagant and imposing, yet his nature was extremely pure and genuine. He indulged himself in the pleasures of daily life — good food, fine lodgings, the enjoyment of wealth and comfort. Pampered and at ease, he was disinclined toward social obligations. He was highly skilled at horsemanship and archery, and even more accomplished in verse and rhapsody; he had read a thousand volumes and could compose seven paces at a time without effort. His wife, Lady Hua, came from the Su family on her mother's side: her father, Su Chentai, was a descendant of meritorious founders, a hereditary marquis, and currently serving as President of the Board of War. He had no sons, only two daughters — the elder named Huanxiang, the younger Huanlan — both of surpassing beauty; when they walked idly beneath the flowers, a hundred butterflies would follow and dance around them. They excelled at poetry, music theory, calligraphy, painting, and the qin and chess, each accomplishment reaching a level of exquisite refinement. Beyond the household walls, a couplet circulated as a byword: "Better than ten pecks of dragon-palace pearls / Is but one glimpse of the two young ladies of the marquis's gate." At the age of eighteen, Huanxiang was wed to Hua Guangsu — truly a match of jade qin and jade zither, harmonious as fish and water. Their domestic life was all moonlit chanting and wind-watching, refined and leisurely beyond description. They also had ten beautiful maidservants, each named with the character for pearl: Baozhu, Mingzhu, Aizhu, Huazhu, Hezhu, Ruizhu, Zhangzhu, Zhenzhu, Huazhu (second), and Zengzhu. All ten were of surpassing beauty, each fifteen or sixteen years old, truly like ten varieties of fresh blossoms or a cluster of powdered butterflies — every one quick of mind and fragrant of speech, with dainty lotus-steps and willow waists, needlework that surpassed the heavenly artisans, and poetic phrases that each reached a level of marvelous refinement. Compared to Zheng Kangcheng's poetry-composing maids, they lacked the pedantic moralist's air; compared to Guo Lingong's household singers, they possessed the distinctive grace of a romantic mistress. Such refined talent and abundant happiness in the young couple was a rarity even by the measure of this world; moreover, their parents at home were still in vigorous old age, commanding prestige over all the surrounding regions, carrying on seven generations of glory, and holding a rank at the very head of the Three Excellencies.

On this day of Lady Hua's birthday, the outside world was entirely unaware. The day before, the young master and his wife had spent a family banquet together, with the Eight-Ages Troupe performing opera throughout the day. The names of all the Eight-Ages boys contained the character for "age" (ling) — simply variations of the kind: Golden-Age, Jade-Age, Orchid-Age, Cassia-Age, and so forth. Several had been trained by household tutors, several had been selected from various troupes. Though they could not compare with the celebrated female-role players celebrated in the Flower Selection, they were nevertheless superior to the ordinary run of stage performers; in their leisure hours they were normally assigned to attend in the study.

On this day, Lady Hua knew that her younger sister, Miss Huanlan, would be coming, and then word came that the Xu household had sent ten curios as gifts — confirming that Mrs. Yuan would also come. She and the young master rose early and paid their respects at the ancestral shrine and before the Buddha altar. The young master had intended to host another family banquet for his wife, but since her sister and her sworn-sister were coming, he was obliged to withdraw. Before long, Miss Su had arrived; the fragrant carriage pulled into the entrance corridor, and a soft sedan chair carried her directly into the inner courtyard. Four maids helped the young lady from the chair; Lady Hua came out to welcome her; the sisters exchanged the ritual greetings; and young Master Hua also came in to pay his respects. He inquired after his parents-in-law's health, and was just about to take his seat when a servant announced: "The mistress of the Xu household has arrived." Young Master Hua withdrew; Lady Hua and her sister came out to the main hall to receive the guest. The curtain of the palanquin was lifted, and from within, surrounded by six beautiful maids, a heavenly being emerged. She took small, delicate lotus-steps into the main hall, took Lady Hua's hand, and they exchanged deep bows with smiling warmth. Miss Su also exchanged New Year's greetings with Mrs. Yuan and said: "I had planned to come to your house tomorrow in any case — my mother and aunts all intend to come as well." Mrs. Yuan said: "I have been meaning these past two days to invite your honored mother and your sisters to come and sit a while — it would be simply delightful if you would deign to call." Lady Hua said: "On the occasion of my humble birthday, you honor me with your gracious attention and shower me with so many precious gifts — how can I but receive them with the deepest gratitude?" Mrs. Yuan laughed: "These few trifling things are merely a token of my feeling — they are far too slight to deserve mention. I had meant to come yesterday, but was unexpectedly detained by something that came up."

Miss Su said: "On the eleventh, my mother sent someone to inquire after your health. The messenger came back and reported that your sister had invited a party of ladies to her garden to admire the lanterns. He went on describing those lanterns for the better part of half the day — saying what a spectacle it was to set them up, talking of several thousand people needed, painting the most vivid picture — so that I dreamed of going to your garden to see the lanterns that very night, and then didn't see a single one."

She laughed at herself as she said it. Mrs. Yuan also laughed: "The lanterns you could certainly have seen — several thousand people would never be needed, though two or three hundred there were. We ladies hurried ahead and took a full day on the eleventh to test them out; after that, various gentlemen came one after another and the merriment continued through the eighteenth without a single day's break. And since you all live in the city proper and can only come by day rather than enjoy the lanterns in the evening, we did not send invitations." Lady Hua expressed great admiration at this account. Mrs. Yuan then turned to Miss Su: "We are grateful your honored mother thought of us and was kind enough to send gifts." Miss Su said: "When you left that day, mother said you seemed a little indisposed, and she has been thinking of you ever since." Mrs. Yuan again rose and expressed her thanks. The Ten Pearls and Miss Su's maids all came forward and paid their respects to Mrs. Yuan. The Six Reds likewise paid their respects to Lady Hua and Miss Su. After some general conversation and domestic chat, Lady Hua proposed having the opera performed. Mrs. Yuan said: "We sisters are having such a delightful conversation — there is no need to let the commotion of performers disrupt it." They took a brief stroll through several rooms and then withdrew into Lady Hua's private chambers. Lady Hua's bedchamber occupied five large bays: three as a suite of inner rooms, the outer two fitted as a study, shelves filled with books and bronzes arranged about. Mrs. Yuan admired it all briefly, and then the cluster of pearl-named maids came in to report that the banquet was ready to be laid. Lady Hua said: "Set it right here, then." The table was spread; Lady Hua offered the first cup; they settled into their seats. The jade-clear wine flowed freely; the table was laden with mountain delicacies and sea rarities beyond description.

The three ladies talked and laughed and drank for a while, until Mrs. Yuan said: "I recently came across a wine-game that is quite entertaining. It uses five set phrases linked in sequence, though I find fault with it for lacking rhyme, and the fourth and fifth phrases each add two meaningless filler words, which feels a little unnatural. In that version, the first phrase is drawn from classical prose, the second from Tang poetry, the third from dominoes names, the fourth from aria titles, and the fifth from the Almanac — all chosen at the player's discretion, which makes it too easy. Here is my version instead: with six dice, whatever pattern falls, one starts from that pattern. The first phrase must be a domino name, the second a five-character line of Tang poetry, the third a lyric from the Xixiang, the fourth an aria title, and the fifth a line from the Mao Shi. These five phrases must rhyme, so that when spoken aloud they ring out in a pleasing cadence." Miss Su was delighted to hear this, and asked her sister at once to bring the dice so they could try the game. Lady Hua said: "Clever as it is, it is quite difficult — you must be both natural and in rhyme. Don't you find that demanding?" She directed a maid to bring the dice tray, set the dice inside, and offered it to Mrs. Yuan, saying:

"Elder sister, please set us an example first." Mrs. Yuan picked up the dice, threw them several times, and produced a pattern: a flock of crows cawing in the wind. She stared at the tray, reflected for a moment, and said: "I shall venture a try, though it may not be well done — please don't laugh at me." Then she recited: "A flock of crows cawing in the wind — / Clamor of phoenixes descending through the void — / Each gone their own way, the shrike and the swallow, east and west — / At the fifth watch it turns — / I shall share with you a dream's content."

Lady Hua and Miss Su praised it lavishly. Lady Hua said: "The first three phrases are truly excellent, and phrases three through five are even more exquisite. The sentiment is warm and languorous — reading them makes one's heart melt. I doubt I could manage anything like this."

Mrs. Yuan laughed: "You are always so modest in everything. And then when it comes to your turn, you always put in great effort and refuse to let anyone surpass you by a hair." Lady Hua also laughed, picked up the dice, threw them several times, and produced a pattern: iron chains mooring a lone boat. She thought for a moment and then recited:

"Iron chains moor a lone boat — / The broad river rushes swiftly through the night — / He set our reunion for the ninth day of the ninth month. / Night Journey on the Boat — / Adrift, now sinking, now floating."

Mrs. Yuan said: "What did I tell you? I said you would have phrases to astonish the listener — these five read as one, and are far better than mine.

The third phrase from the Sequel to the Western Chamber is used with particular wit. Now I must see what Younger Sister Lan will do — surely even better, and last will surpass first." Lady Hua offered a few more modest disclaimers.

Miss Su was impatient and eager to throw; she had no time for modesty, and pulled the dice tray toward her. With a great clattering she threw several times before landing a pattern: a general hangs his seal of command. She was delighted by this. She fixed her autumn eyes in concentration, thought for a moment, assembled five phrases, and recited them with a bright smile:

"A general hangs his seal — / Three frontier regions lie in quiet solitude — / All because within you there are a million troops. / Song of Victory — / A pillar of the realm, a duke and a marquis."

Mrs. Yuan praised: "I said that last would surpass first, and so it is — Sister Lan's entry truly leaves me in absolute admiration; I can only offer a brimming cup in congratulation." Miss Su was rather pleased with herself and smiled while offering a modest disclaimer. Lady Hua also praised: "Truly fine — though it also helped that the dice fell on such an auspicious pattern and gave you your opening." She too offered a cup of congratulation, and directed the attending maids each to drink a cup as well, as a grand reward to the three armies in honor of the general's hanging his seal. The Ten Pearls, the Six Reds, and the rest all drank; Aizhu tugged at Hongxue's sleeve and whispered: "Your mistress's 'at the fifth watch it turns — I shall share with you a dream's content' is full of feeling; our mistress's 'iron chains moor a lone boat, riding the Night Journey ship' is full of good sense; and the Second Young Lady's entry is full of force and grandeur — each one has its own particular excellence." Hongxue nodded: "You have put it exactly right." Mrs. Yuan and the others, overhearing this, could not suppress smiles.

Mrs. Yuan threw again and produced a pattern: fallen red petals blanketing the ground. Determined to excel and not willing to speak hastily, she pondered carefully for a while, composed something she was quite pleased with herself, and recited: "Fallen red petals blanketing the ground — / She wipes away tears and knits her dark brows — / It is only that since last night this waist has grown more slender still.

Scolding the Jade Lad — / You shall not return until you are drunk."

Miss Su praised: "Elder Sister, this is truly superb — how can you say things so full of delicate, graceful allusion? I could never express things in quite this way; I feel my own efforts are rather blunt and coarse. We ought to congratulate you on this one." Each offered a cup of congratulation. Mrs. Yuan laughed: "You are in the grand style of Li Bai and Du Fu, while I am in the delicate, ornate manner of Wen Tingyun and Li Shangyin — how can I compare?" Lady Hua laughed: "This one is absolutely exquisite, and perfectly suited to its theme. I think Elder Sister is scolding her husband for bringing his young actor companions to the garden every day and coming home drunk, so that Elder Sister has grown thin at the waist." Mrs. Yuan felt somewhat embarrassed and said: "Coming here to tease me, are you? Take care — you are watching for color patterns, and there will be a return gesture." Lady Hua and Miss Su both laughed; the Ten Pearls, the Six Reds, and the rest, hearing all this, also smiled faintly and found it all very entertaining to listen to their mistresses banter.

Lady Hua picked up the dice and threw a pattern: two gentlemen entering the Peach Blossom Spring. She spent a moment composing her thoughts and found several fine phrases — but having just teased Mrs. Yuan, she now feared retaliation if she spoke them aloud, and felt herself involuntarily hesitating, a blush spreading across her peach-blossom cheeks. To change the phrases seemed a pity, however, and so she had no choice but to recite:

"Two gentlemen enter the Peach Blossom Spring — / The path to Peach Blossom Spring may still be found — / A heaven-ordained new-wedded pair of swallows. / Beside the dressing mirror — / Walking hand in hand."

Miss Su, hearing this, turned to Lady Hua with a knowing smile. Mrs. Yuan laughed: "What made you suddenly think of the time when you were first married? These phrases are the very height of romantic, entwining sentiment. It is as if one can see a fragrant-hearted figure leaning against the dressing mirror, two people propped side by side, murmuring softly together.

Lips still fragrant with rouge. We are here today as guests — it is somewhat awkward of you to make us feel out of place." Lady Hua laughed:

"I knew you would retaliate — that is why I hesitated and considered changing a phrase or two, but couldn't find anything better. It is my own fault for provoking this from you, Elder Sister." At this, everyone burst out laughing together, and all the maids smiled as well; cups of congratulation were offered all around.

It came round to Miss Su again; she threw a pattern: moonrise above the plum branch. She thought for a moment and recited: "Moonrise above the plum branch — / Fragrant and translucent the flowering tree — / The jade face of the beloved, deep-locked within the embroidered bed-curtains. / Cold Lock on the Casement — / The dew falls thick and heavy."

Lady Hua praised it first. Mrs. Yuan said: "Your entry achieves the ultimate in gentle warmth and fragrant allure, and has exactly the voice of a sheltered young lady of good family. Being a few years older than you, I can no longer achieve such delicate, exquisite rhyme — it truly cannot be forced." Miss Su only smiled in return, and another round of congratulatory cups was drunk. On the other side of the curtain, Hongxiang leaned over and said softly to Baozhu: "Have you noticed? Each person's entry sounds exactly like that person's way of speaking, feeling, and character — even matching their looks. Why should that be? If you were to exchange them among the players, none would suit the one who said it." Baozhu and the others smiled faintly. Mrs. Yuan picked up the dice again and threw a pattern: viewing lanterns on the fifteenth night.

Miss Su said: "This is Elder Sister's home territory — surely you can describe all those hundreds of birds and beasts, divine dragons and mangy elephants, fire-blossomed trees and silver-flowered cascades, and speak them all out together in a grand and festive lantern-festival scene." Mrs. Yuan laughed: "That is what I was thinking too — but I'm not sure I have the power to pull it off." She thought for a while and couldn't assemble anything suitable, so she set it aside and tried again, then recited: "Viewing lanterns on the fifteenth night — / What need of someone to support me when I am not yet drunk — / Voice after voice saying, better to go home. / Step by Step Graceful — / Beware the heavy dew when you walk."

Lady Hua and Miss Su lavished praise on this. Lady Hua said: "Elder Sister is carefree and graceful, and her feeling shows through her words. These phrases are as if one sees Elder Sister leaning on a maid's arm, stepping slowly along — and then again as if one sees Elder Sister viewing the lanterns in her garden. It fills one with admiring envy." Each offered a congratulatory cup. At this point Lady Hua called out to Baozhu and the others, directing them to go with the maids of both households to the back room to eat their meal.

The attendants on this side had thus thinned considerably. Mrs. Yuan could hear from the back room the intermittent clatter of dice and scattered sounds of laughter and mutual teasing and commentary. Miss Su said: "They are in there playing the wine game too — I wonder how their entries are turning out?" Lady Hua laughed:

"Even if they manage a phrase or two, there isn't likely to be anything very fine — they'll surely fall short of ours." So the dice tray was moved over again; she threw a pattern: peach-pink and willow-green, and after a moment's thought recited: "Peach-pink and willow-green — / Flowers and heart alike feel renewed — / Beyond the flowers the beloved is far away, yet the horizon feels near. / Drunk Beneath the Flowers — / Playing the zither, blowing the pipes."

Mrs. Yuan said: "This one too captures all your sentiment and grace — it is as if I see you beneath the shade of the flowers, in the midst of red and green abundance, gently dispelling your own longing. Truly a companion fit for an immortal beneath the jade-peach blossoms." Lady Hua said: "I feel my lines always come out somewhat flat — none of the electric wit of Elder Sister's. Today Elder Sister truly ranks first: a seamless richness of fragrant warmth glows through every character. I can only bow in respectful defeat." Mrs. Yuan said: "Not at all! The pure, bright elegance has all been claimed between the two of you sisters.

The scholar Xie Lingyun once said: 'Of all the talent in the world, there is but one measure of ten; Cao Zijian alone claimed eight of it.' I would say today you two together claim six tenths of it; and then there is one other small talent who would snatch away three tenths — leaving only one tenth for all the other educated ladies in the world to divide among themselves, until by the time it reaches me there isn't even a drop left." Lady Hua and Miss Su both laughed. Miss Su asked: "Who is this 'small talent' Sister speaks of?" Mrs. Yuan said: "Do you not know her? She is the second daughter of Senior Kinsman Wang Zhifu, named Wang Qionghua — we are all sworn sisters." Lady Hua said: "That would be the Senior Kinsman Wang who serves as Supervisor of the Court of Transmission? We have not had much contact with that household." Miss Su said: "What is this Wang Qionghua like?" Mrs. Yuan said: "She is seventeen this year, and her appearance is beyond compare — she and the two of you can truly stand as three equals forming a tripod. A few days ago I invited her and her sisters to come view the lanterns, and right there at the table she composed a 'Lantern-Moon Lyric' — in a matter of moments a sweeping piece of seven or eight hundred characters, brilliant and dazzling, astonishing to ear and eye, as if Li Bai himself had returned. This is something simply impossible for the women's quarters ordinarily to produce." Miss Su said:

"Does Elder Sister happen to remember this poem?" Mrs. Yuan said: "I do not recall it, but another day I will copy it out and send it to you." Each then drank a cup of wine and ate a few dishes. They could hear from the back room that the maids were having a fine time with their dice — laughter and jokes, penalties and forfeit cups. Miss Su threw a pattern: lattice-work eyes, and said with a laugh: "This one has no obvious appeal." She thought for a while.

Then she recited: "Lattice-work eyes — / A gentle breeze makes a pleasing sound — / Flutter, flutter, the paper strips are singing. / Hating the long night — / The eastern sky has not yet grown light."

Mrs. Yuan said: "You claimed the 'lattice-work eyes' pattern had no appeal, yet it has yielded you this fine entry — truly natural and unaffected." By now each of them had gone through several rounds, and a good long while had passed. Lady Hua said: "Let us rest and play another round in a moment — let us eat a little, rather than exhausting ourselves for nothing." We will set aside the outer room for now and speak of the beautiful maids in the back room, who were playing their own wine game. Some had composed good entries, some had stumbled; and some, unable to manage at all, were looking for others to compose on their behalf. Though they dared not be overly raucous, they were nonetheless leaning in to whisper in each other's ears, shoulders brushing and hair strands touching as they crowded together. The Twelve Reds of the Xu household and the Ten Pearls of the Hua household were well matched in age, appearance, and ability — as they should have been on the best of terms, each respecting and fond of the other.

But this was not the case. All harbored a competitive spirit, each unwilling to yield to the other. So long as they avoided these matters of literary and artistic skill, they got along well enough. The moment poetry, lyrics, or such accomplishments came up, however, it would inevitably become a matter of you belittling me and me belittling you, each trying to gain an edge. It would end in a war of words, faces reddening on both sides. In this round of the wine game, several had already composed poorly and suffered no small amount of cutting remarks. When Hongwei's throw produced a pattern, she was unable to think of anything for a long while, her cheeks flushing crimson with frustration. Aizhu thought of something and said with a laugh: "I'll compose for you, but you'll have to thank me, the matchmaker." Still smiling sweetly, she pointed a finger at Hongwei and recited: "Intoxicated Xi Shi — / The flush of wine and beauty come on slow — / Last night in the clear moon and gentle breeze, deep in the night. / Good Elder Sister — / The gentleman of quality tempts her with his wooing."

Everyone praised it as fine. Hongwei said: "You truly are a 'good elder sister' — no wonder someone wants to tempt you." Aizhu said: "I was describing you, with your good looks. Don't you think you look just like an intoxicated Xi Shi?" Everyone laughed again.

Ruizhu threw a pattern called "a loach slipping into a water-chestnut nest," and objected that the name was distasteful and wanted it not to count. Everyone refused to accept this.

Ruizhu had no choice but to think hard, but try as she might she could not string the phrases together in a connected way. Hongxue laughed:

"I'll compose for you too, but you'll have to thank me, the matchmaker." Ruizhu said: "If it's something decent, then say it. If it's an insult, spare me the favor." Hongxue said: "I won't insult you — you'll actually be grateful to me." Everyone said: "Go on, recite it." Hongxue laughed and said: "A loach slipping into a water-chestnut nest — / A green-feathered bird sporting on the orchid sedge — / A marquis's gate — the old monk is not permitted to knock. / That bald little fellow — / Shall grow old together with you."

Ruizhu reached out and pinched Hongxue's mouth shut. Hongxue struggled to pry the fingers free; everyone doubled over with laughter. Mingzhu laughed so hard her sleeve knocked over a wine cup. The ladies outside could hear all of this clearly; Mrs. Yuan laughed: "They are even more entertaining than we are." On this side, Hongyu threw a pattern called "eight refusals," and said: "This pattern is also difficult — it can't be assembled; let me throw again." Baozhu said: "Of course it can be assembled — I'll assemble it for you, and I guarantee it won't leave you with 'eight refusals.'" Hongyu said: "Are you saying this in fun, or are you serious? If you are going to be unkind to me, I'll tear your mouth open." Baozhu said: "I don't like being unkind to people." Then she pointed at Hongyu and recited: "Eight refusals — / Startled from her dreams, the mandarin-ducks rise — / What sustains her through this long, long night? / Taking Off the Cloth Shirt — / The heart's longing, center-deep." She added: "The character for 'longing' here must be read as 'itching.'" Hongyu cursed: "There seems to be a certain itch in your mouth too — let me scratch it for you." She picked up a piece of sea cucumber with her chopsticks and pushed it toward Baozhu's lips. Baozhu knocked her chopsticks away with one hand; they clattered to the ground under the table, and a white cat dashed out and ate up the sea cucumber. The maids dissolved into helpless laughter once more.

Zhangzhu threw a pattern called "climbing the ladder to gaze at the moon," said something about it that was merely passable and showed no particular brilliance. Hongwen said: "The name of this pattern is itself so fine — presenting it so poorly is an injustice to the subject. Let me say it for you." She then recited: "Climbing the ladder to gaze at the moon — / Song Yu is in the western neighborhood — / Over the wall they exchanged poems and compositions until the sky grew light. / Stirring of the Flower's Heart — / A young woman is moved by longing." Zhangzhu laughed and scolded Hongwen: "What a way for a girl to behave — climbing a ladder to peep at someone, and then saying your own heart is stirred! Aren't you ashamed?" Hongwen laughed: "I am saying it of you — you are suppressing it all inside. Better to say it plainly rather than make yourself sick holding it in." Zhangzhu gave Hongwen a shove; Hongwen, caught off guard, stumbled backward and fell against Baozhu, landing on her bound feet. Baozhu frowned, placed one hand on Hongwen's shoulder for balance, and used the other to rub her own shoe-tip; after rubbing it for a moment, she gave Hongwen two light smacks on the back. Everyone laughed again.

Hongxiang threw a pattern called "a pair of matched butterflies, precisely." She too could not assemble anything. She thought of a few phrases, but they wouldn't rhyme together. Hezhu said: "I'll compose a good one for you — one that'll make sure you never ignore me again." Hongxiang, assuming this was a kindly offer, said: "Good elder sister, please compose it for me then." Hezhu laughed: "Although I am composing it for you, the entry still counts as yours." Then she recited: "A pair of matched butterflies, precisely — / When will the wish be granted — / Every moment endured stretches like a summer's day. / Parallel Lotus Blossoms — / May it be so, morning and night." Hongxiang's face went red; she tried to tear Hezhu's mouth open, but the others intervened and held her back. Hezhu threw a pattern called "a single flower." She was just beginning to think of some fine phrases when Hongzhu turned toward her with a bright smile and said: "Let me compose for you." Hezhu, suspecting nothing good would come of it, shook her head: "No thank you." Hongzhu said: "Even if you don't want it, I'm going to say it anyway." Everyone was eager to hear something amusing, so they all pressed for her to speak. Hongzhu recited: "A single flower — / We still cherish the time of the embrace — / This is what is called 'the talented youth and the beautiful maiden, indeed.' / A Single Point of Red — / Lightly soiling my private heart." Everyone burst into irrepressible laughter. Hezhu was furious; she walked over, grabbed Hongzhu around the waist, and with great force pressed her down onto the heated bed-platform, pinning her and saying: "Now let me ask you in earnest about this 'single point of red.'" Hongzhu, being weaker, could not turn over; her skirt had already parted to both sides. Everyone saw her two little bound feet flailing and kicking in all directions; everyone's sides shook with laughter until they could barely hold themselves upright.

Hongxue and Hongxiang went over and pulled Hezhu off. Hongzhu found that the flowers in her hair had fallen out and her tresses had been thoroughly disheveled; she smoothed them back with her hand for a while and then scolded Hezhu: "What rough and reckless play — saying a thing is all very well, but you didn't have to take it so seriously." The commotion had now grown to a pitch where Lady Hua and Mrs. Yuan could no longer contain themselves; they sent a married maidservant to go in and inquire and forbid any further play. The cluster of maids immediately fell silent and composed themselves; they hurried through their bowls of rice and came out to resume attendance. The ladies looked at this troupe of unruly maids — some with flowers askew, some with hair disheveled, some with their backs turned, still trying to suppress laughter, and some with brows knit in aggrieved silent reproach — and could not help but find them amusing. They turned to Aizhu and the others and said: "At your ages, how can you still be this mischievous? Were it not for our guest being present today, I would certainly punish you." Mrs. Yuan also spoke a few words of mild censure to the Six Reds. The maids all bowed their heads and stood in attendance with looks of contrition. Miss Su asked: "What kind of entries were you playing in there? What was so terribly funny?" At this, several of the maids could not hold back their smiles again, which in turn nearly set both ladies laughing as well.

Lady Hua laughed: "These foolish girls — they are at once maddening and amusing." Miss Su said further: "If any of your entries were really good, you may as well tell us — we would like to hear and appreciate them. If they are truly fine, I shall even give you a reward. And your mistresses certainly won't scold you for them."

Aizhu's expression suggested she was about to speak, but Hongxiang tugged at her sleeve, warning her not to. Aizhu said: "There were a great many — I can't recall them all clearly." Miss Su was eager to hear and so addressed Lady Hua and Mrs. Yuan: "They are afraid of their mistresses and don't dare speak — if you call on them to say it, they will." Lady Hua also knew that the maids had their own sharp little wits and that several of them had produced good entries; she exchanged a smile with Mrs. Yuan. Mrs. Yuan, being by nature lively and unconventional, also wished to display her maids' talent, and so said: "If what you say is coherent, you may go ahead and say it. If you say something incoherent, each one will kneel and drink a large penalty cup." Among them, Hongwei and Mingzhu had the best memories, and moreover none of their own entries were among the questionable ones, so they said: "They are coherent enough — but I fear that having said them, instead of earning a reward, we will receive a punishment." Lady Hua laughed: "Go on and say them one by one." So Mingzhu reported all three of the entries by Aizhu, Baozhu, and Hezhu that had offended the others; and Hongwei also reported all three of the entries by Hongxue, Hongwen, and Hongzhu that had caused trouble — to such effect that both ladies laughed until the pearl ornaments on their hair quivered and swayed. Though they tried to compose their faces into stern expressions to reprimand the girls, they simply could not manage it. Miss Su, though she felt the entries were somewhat too free and familiar, nonetheless admired the sharpness of mind behind them, and refrained from laughing outright, contenting herself with a slight nod of appreciation.

The two ladies laughed for a good while, and then together called forward the six culprits — the three Reds and the three Pearls who had given offense — and, suppressing their laughter, said: "You are so impertinent — how could you? If word got out, what face would you have to show anyone? Now kneel, all of you!" The six maids, blushing with shame, had no choice but to kneel before the assembled company. Miss Su interceded on their behalf: "Two dear sisters, for my sake — forgive them this first offense. Though their words were improper and free, we must give them credit for their quick wits and nimble tongues. Let the merit cancel the fault." Mrs. Yuan said: "Since Second Sister has spoken, I dare not refuse — but we must still admonish them. If we do not, they will get into the habit of it and become completely unbridled." She and Lady Hua then judged the six entries: for the most egregious, the penalty was a large cup of wine drunk at a single draft and three slaps of the palm, as a token chastisement; for the lesser offenses, a forfeit cup was sufficient. Thus Hongxue, Hongzhu, Hezhu, and Baozhu received both wine and punishment; Aizhu and Hongwen received the wine-forfeit only. The maids in receiving their punishments felt utterly ashamed, and having drunk those hasty cups of wine found themselves a little unsteady on their feet; they struggled to hold themselves upright, and privately regretted their moment of high spirits.

Mrs. Yuan saw that the day was growing late and that it was time to break up the banquet; she laughed and said to Lady Hua: "Throw once more and say a few well-turned phrases to close the game — something to cleanse the air after all that." She directed that the rice be brought at the same time. Lady Hua, seeing that the hour was indeed right and it would not do to stay too late, ordered the dishes to be served and the rice brought. She picked up the dice, threw, and produced a pattern: golden chrysanthemums facing lotus blossoms. She was privately delighted — such a fine pattern — and after quiet thought composed her entry and recited it: "Golden chrysanthemums face the lotus blossoms — / Patterned candles curl as the plates gleam red — / And yet you ask me, blue-sleeved, to hold the jade cup with tender care. / Perfectly at Peace and Happy — / May ten thousand blessings converge upon you."

Mrs. Yuan and Miss Su praised it without end. Lady Hua urged both of them to drink two more cups each, and then the rice was eaten and the meal concluded. After washing and rinsing, Mrs. Yuan saw the evening sun beginning its descent and knew she must not delay further; she offered her thanks and bid farewell. Lady Hua and Miss Su, attended by the Ten Pearls and the cluster of maids, escorted her to the palanquin. The Six Reds supported the sides of the chair; with small, careful steps they made their way all the way to the outer corridor before mounting the carriage, which then flowed smoothly away.

On the other side, Miss Su did not return home until the second watch of the night. As for what happened next — hear it told in the following chapter.
