Chapter 11 — Three beauties turn language into fresh brilliance; maidservants are rebuked over playful talk.

After escorting Ziyu out of the garden, Xu Ziyun lingered a moment to converse with Xiao Cixian before wending his way homeward. His own residence, the vast and imposing ancestral mansion built by his late great-grandfather the Grand Chancellor, lay only a short distance across the road. With his parents, elder brother, and sister-in-law departed from the capital, the sprawling estate housed only Ziyun, his wife, and a retinue of servants. When he recounted the tender devotion between Qin Yan and Ziyu to his wife, Mrs. Yuan, they both marveled at such delicate affection. Smiling, she inquired:

"These young gentlemen of the stage, what enchanting spell do they cast to so thoroughly captivate you all?" Ziyun answered with a laugh: "You have laid eyes on them and heard their songs. Can you truly claim they lack a certain exquisite charm?" "To my eye," Mrs. Yuan replied, "they merely mimic the delicate graces of sheltered maidens when they perform. Even unadorned, they are certainly fair and fragile. Yet to elevate them as matchless, priceless treasures escapes my understanding. They seem scarcely more refined than my own maidservants." Ziyun reasoned: "From your station, naturally the feminine grace appears supreme. But we cannot bring young ladies of good standing to our banquets and revelries, lest we tarnish the purity of the occasion. The true brilliance of these young actors is this: they possess a woman's visage without a woman's form. They delight the eye while restraining the heart, offering joy untouched by base desire. Is this not a twofold perfection?" Mrs. Yuan laughed: "A well-tailored defense, indeed." "I speak only truth," Ziyun chuckled. "Never in my life have I uttered words I did not sincerely mean." "Perhaps you are so pure of heart," Mrs. Yuan teased, "but can you say the same of your companions?" Ziyun replied earnestly: "If they were not of a similar disposition, we would be as incompatible as fire and ice. Did I not just tell you how Mei Yuxiang erupted in fury, even turning his wrath upon me, merely because Yu Ling uttered a few flirtatious jests? Does that not prove their honor? Moreover, the boys themselves disdain such frivolous teasing." They conversed a while longer until Mrs. Yuan observed that the morrow marked Lady Hua's twentieth birthday, a milestone of great significance, demanding their attendance. "Indeed, we must go," Ziyun agreed. "She honored your birthday last year with her presence and numerous gifts. We are obliged to prepare a selection of fine treasures in return." Thus the peaceful evening drew to a close.

At the break of dawn, Mrs. Yuan meticulously selected ten exquisite curios of immense value and drafted an inventory. It listed a jade interlocking ring crafted of jasper and nephrite, a hairpin inlaid with seven treasures, a fan formed of vibrant kingfisher feathers, a coral hair ornament, a filigree gold censer shaped like a mountain, a qin carved from pale jade, a rare block of aquilaria incense, a zither bridge of precious sinking-wood, a rosary of lignum aloes, and an agate seal. These treasures were sent ahead by a servant. From her retinue of twelve maidservants, each named with the character 'Hong' (Red), she chose Hongxue, Hongzhu, Hongxiang, Hongyu, Hongwei, and Hongwen. These six, all fifteen years of age and in the very springtime of their youth, were slender and graceful. They were literate, skilled in calligraphy, and deeply accomplished in both poetry and embroidery. After assisting her mistress with her morning toilet, Hongxue observed: "The air bites bitterly today, and snow seems to linger in the clouds. We must prepare additional garments." From the heavy winter wardrobe she selected a sky-blue satin cloak, richly embroidered with golden motifs upon a lining of purple sable fur. To this she added a red satin skirt adorned with golden celestial steeds, jade pendants that chimed with every step, and a cascade of pearl tassels. A large, crimson padded bundle was also packed with several extra layers, while all floral hairpins and precious ornaments were carefully nestled within a brocaded case. Once the six 'Reds' completed their own preparations, the entire party boarded the fragrant carriage, while the household men mounted their steeds and led the procession forward.

The young master Hua was then twenty-one years of age. Though his striking figure had fleetingly caught the eye of Wei Pincai and his temperament had been hinted at by Fu San, his true nature remained untold. Master Hua possessed an extravagant, commanding bearing, yet his heart was uncommonly pure and genuine. He immersed himself freely in the worldly delights of fine delicacies, luxurious dwellings, and the boundless comforts of vast wealth. Pampered and unburdened, he held little affection for tedious social obligations. He proved an exceptional horseman and archer, yet his mastery of poetry and rhapsody was even more profound. Having devoured a thousand volumes, he could effortlessly compose verse within seven paces. His wife, Lady Hua, was born of the maternal Su lineage. Her father, Su Chentai, descended from the glorious founders of the dynasty, held the hereditary title of marquis, and currently reigned as President of the Board of War. Lacking sons, he cherished his two daughters: the elder, Huanxiang, and the younger, Huanlan. Both were endowed with surpassing, celestial beauty. When they strolled languidly amidst the gardens, a hundred butterflies would flutter and dance in their wake. They were masters of poetry, musical theory, calligraphy, and painting, equally adept at the qin and chess. Every talent they touched reached the pinnacle of exquisite refinement. Beyond the high walls of their estate, a popular couplet echoed their fame:

"Better than ten pecks of dragon-palace pearls,
Is but a single glimpse of the Marquis's twin jades."

At eighteen, Huanxiang was wed to Hua Guangsu, forging a union as harmonious as a jade qin paired with a jade zither, as natural as fish swimming in water. Their days were spent in quiet refinement, chanting beneath the moon and gazing at the wind in unutterable leisure. Serving them were ten beautiful maids, each bearing the character 'Zhu' (Pearl) in her name: Baozhu, Mingzhu, Aizhu, Huazhu, Hezhu, Ruizhu, Zhangzhu, Zhenzhu, a second Huazhu, and Zengzhu. All fifteen or sixteen years of age, they bloomed like ten distinct varieties of fresh spring flowers, fluttering like a cluster of powdered butterflies. Each possessed a nimble mind and a fragrant tongue, walking with dainty lotus-steps upon willow waists. Their needlework rivaled the artisans of heaven, and their poetic verses attained marvelous elegance. Compared to the poetry-composing maids of Zheng Kangcheng, they lacked any pedantic stiffness; compared to the household singers of Guo Lingong, they radiated the enchanting grace of a romantic muse. Such exquisite talent and boundless fortune in a young couple was a rarity in the mortal realm. Furthermore, their parents remained vigorous in their twilight years, commanding reverence across the lands, sustaining seven generations of untarnished glory, and standing foremost among the Three Excellencies.

The outside world remained entirely ignorant of Lady Hua's birthday festivities. The day prior, the young master and his wife had shared a private family banquet, entertained from dawn till dusk by the Eight-Ages Troupe. Every boy in the troupe bore the character 'ling' (Age) in his name, known as Golden-Age, Jade-Age, Orchid-Age, Cassia-Age, and the like. Some were cultivated by the household's private tutors, while others had been handpicked from renowned traveling companies. Though they could not rival the legendary female-role performers celebrated in the Flower Selection, their artistry easily eclipsed the ordinary stage performers. In quieter hours, they were customarily appointed to serve in the master's study.

Knowing her younger sister, Miss Huanlan, would attend, Lady Hua was further delighted by the arrival of the Xu household's ten precious curios, signaling Mrs. Yuan's impending visit. Rising early, she and the young master offered reverent respects at the ancestral shrine and the Buddhist altar. The young master had intended to honor his wife with another private banquet, but the arrival of her sister and sworn-sister necessitated his polite withdrawal. Before long, Miss Su's fragrant carriage rolled into the entrance corridor, where a soft sedan chair ferried her directly into the sanctuary of the inner courtyard. Four maids assisted the young lady as Lady Hua emerged to offer a joyous welcome. The sisters exchanged warm, ritual greetings before young Master Hua entered to pay his respects. He had scarcely inquired after the health of his parents-in-law and moved to take his seat when a servant announced: "The mistress of the Xu household has arrived." Master Hua discreetly withdrew as Lady Hua and her sister advanced to the grand hall to receive their honored guest. The palanquin curtain lifted, revealing a heavenly figure surrounded by six beautiful maids. With delicate, tiny lotus-steps, she entered the main hall, clasping Lady Hua's hand as they exchanged deep, smiling bows of profound warmth. Miss Su offered New Year's greetings to Mrs. Yuan, adding, "I had intended to call upon your home tomorrow, as my mother and aunts are planning a visit." Mrs. Yuan replied gracefully, "For the past two days I have longed to invite your honored mother and sisters to sit with us. It would be an absolute delight if you would deign to honor us." Lady Hua interjected, "On the occasion of my humble birthday, you honor me deeply with your gracious presence and shower me with such precious gifts. I receive them with the most profound gratitude." Mrs. Yuan laughed softly, "These trifling tokens merely convey my affection. They are far too slight to warrant mention. I had hoped to visit yesterday, but was unexpectedly detained by sudden matters."

Miss Su recounted, "On the eleventh, my mother dispatched a servant to inquire after your health. The messenger returned to report that your sister had hosted a gathering of ladies in her garden to admire the lanterns. He spent half the day spinning tales of the spectacle, speaking of the thousands of men required to arrange them, painting so vivid a scene that I dreamed of wandering your lantern-lit garden that very night, though I beheld not a single one awake."

She laughed at her own fanciful tale. Mrs. Yuan smiled in return: "You certainly could have viewed the lanterns. We hardly needed thousands of men, though two or three hundred were indeed employed. We ladies hurried ahead on the eleventh to privately test their glow. Thereafter, various gentlemen arrived in constant succession, carrying the merriment unbroken through the eighteenth. Since you reside in the city proper and can only call by day, missing the true nocturnal beauty of the lanterns, we refrained from sending invitations." Lady Hua expressed profound admiration at the description. Turning back to Miss Su, Mrs. Yuan added, "We are deeply grateful that your honored mother kept us in her thoughts and so kindly sent gifts." "When you departed that day," Miss Su replied, "mother noted you seemed somewhat indisposed, and you have lingered in her thoughts ever since." Mrs. Yuan rose once more to express her gratitude. The Ten Pearls and Miss Su's maids then stepped forward to pay their respects to Mrs. Yuan, while the Six Reds mirrored the courtesy to Lady Hua and Miss Su. Following a gentle exchange of domestic pleasantries, Lady Hua proposed summoning the opera troupe. "We sisters are enjoying such a delightful, intimate conversation," Mrs. Yuan demurred gently. "There is no need to let the clamor of performers shatter the peace." They took a leisurely stroll through the elegant chambers before withdrawing into Lady Hua's private sanctuary. Her bedchamber spanned five grand bays: three formed an inner suite, while the outer two served as a refined study, adorned with rows of books and ancient bronzes. Mrs. Yuan admired the exquisite arrangement briefly before the Pearl-named maids entered to announce the banquet was prepared. "Lay it here," Lady Hua directed. The grand table was set, Lady Hua poured the first cup of honor, and they took their seats. Jade-clear wine flowed in abundance alongside a feast of rare mountain delicacies and ocean treasures beyond description.

The three ladies shared wine, laughter, and elegant conversation until Mrs. Yuan spoke: "I recently learned a highly entertaining wine-game. It strings together five set phrases, though I find fault with its lack of rhyme, and the final two phrases rely on unnatural filler words. That version draws arbitrarily from classical prose, Tang poetry, domino tiles, aria titles, and the Almanac, making it far too simplistic. I propose a more refined version: using six dice, the player begins based on the pattern thrown. The first phrase must be a domino name, the second a five-character line of Tang poetry, the third a lyric from the Western Chamber, the fourth an aria title, and the fifth a line from the Mao Shi. These five phrases must weave into a single rhyme, ringing out with a pleasing, poetic cadence when spoken." Delighted by the challenge, Miss Su eagerly urged her sister to summon the dice. "It is a clever game, yet quite demanding," Lady Hua observed. "One must remain perfectly natural while holding to the rhyme. Is it not overly taxing?" She signaled a maid to fetch the dice tray, placed the ivory cubes within, and offered it to Mrs. Yuan, saying:

"Elder sister, pray set the example for us." Gathering the dice, Mrs. Yuan cast them into the tray, landing upon the pattern 'A Flock of Crows Cawing in the Wind.' She gazed at the ivory faces, lost in quiet reflection for a moment, before murmuring, "I shall venture a modest attempt. Pray do not laugh if it falls short." She then recited:

"A flock of crows cawing in the wind,
Clamor of phoenixes descending through the void,
Each gone their own way, the shrike and the swallow, east and west,
At the fifth watch it turns,
I shall share with you a dream's content."

Lady Hua and Miss Su offered lavish praise. "The opening phrases are truly excellent, and the final three are utterly exquisite," Lady Hua marveled. "The sentiment breathes such a warm, languorous beauty that it melts the heart to hear it. I fear I could never weave anything quite so lovely."

Mrs. Yuan laughed gently: "You are forever modest in your claims. Yet when your turn arrives, you summon immense effort, refusing to let anyone surpass you by even a hair's breadth." Lady Hua smiled, took up the dice, and threw the pattern 'Iron Chains Mooring a Lone Boat.' Pausing briefly in thought, she softly recited:

"Iron chains moor a lone boat,
The broad river rushes swiftly through the night,
He set our reunion for the ninth day of the ninth month.
Night Journey on the Boat,
Adrift, now sinking, now floating."

Mrs. Yuan exclaimed, "What did I tell you? I knew you possessed verses to astonish the ear. These five lines flow as a single breath, far surpassing my own meager attempt. Using a phrase from the Sequel to the Western Chamber shows particular wit. Now we must see what Younger Sister Lan will compose. Surely it will be finer still, proving that the last to speak surpasses the first." Lady Hua merely offered a few graceful, self-effacing disclaimers.

Impatient and eager, Miss Su abandoned all pretense of modesty, pulling the dice tray eagerly toward herself. With a sharp clatter, she threw the pattern 'A General Hangs His Seal.' Delighted, she narrowed her autumn-water eyes in deep concentration, arranged her thoughts, and recited with a brilliant smile:

"A general hangs his seal,
Three frontier regions lie in quiet solitude,
All because within you there are a million troops.
Song of Victory,
A pillar of the realm, a duke and a marquis."

"I proclaimed the last would surpass the first, and so it has," Mrs. Yuan praised warmly. "Sister Lan's verse leaves me in absolute admiration; I can offer nothing less than a brimming cup in congratulation." Immensely pleased, Miss Su smiled while offering a gentle disclaimer. "It is truly fine," Lady Hua agreed, "though fortune aided you in throwing so auspicious a pattern for your opening." Offering her own cup of congratulation, Lady Hua commanded the attending maids to drink as well, serving as a grand reward to the three armies in honor of the victorious general. As the Ten Pearls and Six Reds partook of the wine, Aizhu tugged Hongxue's sleeve, whispering: "Your mistress's verse of sharing a dream's content overflows with tender emotion. My mistress's vision of the lone boat adrift is imbued with profound philosophical depth. But the Second Young Lady's verse radiates immense martial grandeur. Each possesses its own unique, flawless excellence." Hongxue nodded softly. "You perceive it perfectly." Overhearing this whispered critique, Mrs. Yuan and the ladies could not suppress their knowing smiles.

Taking the dice once more, Mrs. Yuan rolled the pattern 'Fallen Red Petals Blanketing the Ground.' Determined to maintain her supremacy and refusing to speak in haste, she pondered deeply before composing a verse that brought her quiet satisfaction. She recited:

"Fallen red petals blanketing the ground,
She wipes away tears and knits her dark brows,
It is only that since last night this waist has grown more slender still.

Scolding the Jade Lad,
You shall not return until you are drunk."

Miss Su sighed in admiration: "Elder Sister, this is truly magnificent. How do you summon words steeped in such delicate, graceful allusion? My own efforts feel impossibly blunt and coarse by comparison. We must surely congratulate you for this." They each raised a celebratory cup. Mrs. Yuan laughed, "Your verses echo the grand, sweeping style of Li Bai and Du Fu, whilst I linger in the delicate, ornate shadows of Wen Tingyun and Li Shangyin. How can they be compared?" Lady Hua smiled knowingly. "It is absolutely exquisite and perfectly suited to the theme. I suspect Elder Sister is subtly scolding her husband for bringing his young actor companions to the garden each day, returning home so deep in his cups that she pines away, her waist growing ever more slender." A faint blush touched Mrs. Yuan's cheeks as she replied, "Have you come merely to tease me? Tread carefully, for you are waiting on the dice, and I shall surely return the favor." As Lady Hua and Miss Su shared a laugh, the Ten Pearls and Six Reds smiled faintly, thoroughly enchanted by the elegant banter of their mistresses.

Lady Hua cast the dice, revealing the pattern 'Two Gentlemen Entering the Peach Blossom Spring.' She spent a quiet moment gathering her thoughts, quickly weaving together several exquisite phrases. Yet, having just teased Mrs. Yuan, she feared a swift retaliation if she voiced them aloud. A sudden hesitation seized her, a rosy blush spreading across her peach-blossom cheeks. Finding it a pity to discard the lovely lines, she reluctantly recited:

"Two gentlemen enter the Peach Blossom Spring,
The path to Peach Blossom Spring may still be found,
A heaven-ordained new-wedded pair of swallows.
Beside the dressing mirror,
Walking hand in hand."

Hearing this, Miss Su turned a knowing, luminous smile toward Lady Hua. Mrs. Yuan laughed softly: "What possessed you to suddenly dream of the days when you were newly wed? These phrases represent the very pinnacle of romantic, entwined devotion. One can almost envision a fragrant-hearted beauty leaning against her dressing mirror, two lovers resting side by side, murmuring softly to one another,

their lips still sweet with the fragrance of rouge. We sit here today as mere guests, making it rather awkward for you to render us so entirely out of place." Lady Hua laughed in surrender:

"I knew you would exact your revenge. That is precisely why I hesitated, hoping to alter a phrase or two, yet I could summon nothing finer. I have only myself to blame for provoking this from you, Elder Sister." The room erupted in harmonious laughter, the maids sharing in the mirth as celebratory cups were poured all around.

The turn fell once more to Miss Su, who rolled 'Moonrise Above the Plum Branch.' After a brief contemplation, she recited:

"Moonrise above the plum branch,
Fragrant and translucent the flowering tree,
The jade face of the beloved, deep-locked within the embroidered bed-curtains.
Cold Lock on the Casement,
The dew falls thick and heavy."

Lady Hua was the first to offer her praise. Mrs. Yuan observed softly, "Your verse achieves the very ultimate in gentle warmth and fragrant allure, capturing precisely the pure voice of a sheltered maiden from a noble house. Being a few years your senior, I find I can no longer summon such delicate, exquisite innocence. It is a grace that truly cannot be forced." Miss Su responded with a serene smile as another round of wine was shared. Beyond the curtain, Hongxiang leaned close to Baozhu, whispering, "Have you noticed? Each lady's verse perfectly mirrors her own voice, her deepest feelings, even her very appearance. If you were to exchange the verses among them, the words would utterly fail to suit the speaker." Baozhu and the other maids smiled faintly in silent agreement. Mrs. Yuan took up the dice again, casting the pattern 'Viewing Lanterns on the Fifteenth Night.'

"This is Elder Sister's true domain," Miss Su declared. "Surely you can paint for us the hundreds of birds and beasts, the divine dragons and mottled elephants, the fire-blossomed trees and cascading silver flowers, conjuring the grand, festive spectacle of your lantern festival." Mrs. Yuan laughed: "That was indeed my intention, yet I fear my pen lacks the power to capture it." After struggling fruitlessly to weave the grand imagery together, she set the thought aside and recited instead:

"Viewing lanterns on the fifteenth night,
What need of someone to support me when I am not yet drunk,
Voice after voice saying, better to go home.
Step by Step Graceful,
Beware the heavy dew when you walk."

Lady Hua and Miss Su showered the verse with praise. "Elder Sister is so carefree and elegant, her true spirit glowing through her words," Lady Hua marveled. "These lines conjure the very image of you leaning gracefully upon a maid's arm, stepping slowly through the night, viewing the brilliant lanterns of your garden. It fills the heart with an admiring envy." As they shared celebratory cups, Lady Hua instructed Baozhu and the other attendants to retire to the back rooms with the visiting maids to partake of their own feast.

With the attendants withdrawn, the room grew intimately quiet. Yet from the back chambers, Mrs. Yuan could discern the intermittent, sharp clatter of dice, mingled with bursts of laughter and the playful barbs of mutual teasing. "They are playing the wine game as well," Miss Su noted. "I wonder what manner of verses they are managing to compose?" Lady Hua laughed softly:

"Even if they string a phrase or two together, they are unlikely to produce anything of true elegance. They shall surely fall far short of our own efforts." Drawing the dice tray near, she threw the pattern 'Peach-Pink and Willow-Green,' and after a moment's reflection, recited:

"Peach-pink and willow-green,
Flowers and heart alike feel renewed,
Beyond the flowers the beloved is far away, yet the horizon feels near.
Drunk Beneath the Flowers,
Playing the zither, blowing the pipes."

Mrs. Yuan smiled warmly. "This, too, captures the entirety of your sentiment and grace. It is as if I behold you resting in the cool shade of the blossoms, surrounded by a lush abundance of red and green, gently dispelling the quiet longings of your heart. You are truly a companion fit for immortals beneath the jade-peach trees." Lady Hua demurred gently: "I feel my lines inevitably fall flat, utterly lacking the electric, dazzling wit of Elder Sister's verse. Today, Elder Sister undeniably stands foremost. A seamless richness of fragrant warmth illuminates your every word, and I can only bow in respectful defeat." "Not at all!" Mrs. Yuan protested. "The true, bright elegance has been entirely claimed by the two of you sisters.

"The ancient scholar Xie Lingyun once famously declared: 'Of all the talent in the world, there is but one measure of ten; Cao Zijian alone claimed eight of it.' Today, I would say you two together claim six tenths. Then there is another small talent who would snatch away three tenths, leaving merely one fleeting drop for all the other educated ladies of the world to divide amongst themselves. By the time the cup reaches me, nothing remains." As Lady Hua and Miss Su laughed at her charming exaggeration, Miss Su inquired, "And who is this 'small talent' you speak of?" "Do you truly not know her?" Mrs. Yuan asked in mild surprise. "She is the second daughter of our Senior Kinsman Wang Zhifu, named Wang Qionghua. We are all sworn sisters." "Would that be the Senior Kinsman Wang currently serving as Supervisor of the Court of Transmission?" Lady Hua mused. "We have rarely interacted with that household." "What manner of girl is this Wang Qionghua?" Miss Su pressed. Mrs. Yuan's eyes brightened. "She is seventeen this year, and her beauty is absolute and beyond compare. She and the two of you could truly stand as three peerless equals, forming a perfect tripod of grace. A few days ago, I invited her and her sisters to view the lanterns. Right there at the banquet table, she composed a 'Lantern and Moon Lyrics.' Within moments, she produced a sweeping masterpiece of seven or eight hundred characters, so brilliant and dazzling it astonished both ear and eye. It was as if the immortal Li Bai himself had returned to the mortal realm. It is a work simply impossible for the secluded women's quarters to ordinarily yield."

"Does Elder Sister happen to remember the verses of this poem?" Miss Su asked eagerly. "I cannot recall it fully," Mrs. Yuan admitted, "but on another day, I shall transcribe it carefully and send it to you." They drank another cup of wine and sampled the delicate dishes before them. From the back room, the lively commotion of the maids grew louder: a chorus of laughter, teasing jokes, and the clinking of penalty cups. Throwing the dice, Miss Su landed the pattern 'Lattice-Work Eyes' and laughed, "This pattern holds no obvious poetic appeal." After pondering for a moment,

she softly recited:

"Lattice-work eyes,
A gentle breeze makes a pleasing sound,
Flutter, flutter, the paper strips are singing.
Hating the long night,
The eastern sky has not yet grown light."

Mrs. Yuan smiled in admiration: "You claimed the 'Lattice-Work Eyes' pattern lacked inspiration, yet it has yielded you this delicate, beautiful entry. It is wonderfully natural and unaffected." Having progressed through several rounds, a pleasant stretch of the afternoon had passed. "Let us rest and allow the game to pause for a moment," Lady Hua suggested. "We should partake of the food rather than exhaust our minds for nothing." We shall leave the ladies in the outer room for now and speak of the beautiful maids gathered in the back chambers, deeply engrossed in their own wine game. While some had composed clever entries, others had stumbled awkwardly, and a few, unable to form a rhyme at all, sought desperately for others to compose on their behalf. Though they dared not become overly raucous, they leaned close, whispering urgently into one another's ears, their shoulders brushing and dark tresses tangling as they crowded tightly together. The Twelve Reds of the Xu household and the Ten Pearls of the Hua household were perfectly matched in age, striking beauty, and sharp intellect. In truth, they should have been the dearest of friends, bound by mutual respect and fondness.

Yet this was far from reality. Every girl harbored a fierce, competitive spirit, entirely unwilling to yield even an inch of superiority to another. As long as they avoided matters of literary skill or artistic accomplishment, a fragile peace reigned. But the moment poetry, lyrics, or cleverness entered the fray, it inevitably spiraled into a contest of mutual belittlement, each desperately striving to seize the upper hand. Such skirmishes inevitably ended in a bitter war of words, leaving faces flushed crimson on both sides. In this particular round of the wine game, several had already faltered, suffering a relentless barrage of cutting remarks. When Hongwei threw her pattern, her mind went entirely blank. For a long, agonizing moment, her cheeks burned a furious crimson with frustration. Suddenly inspired, Aizhu laughed and said, "I shall compose a verse for you, but you must thank me properly as your matchmaker!" Still smiling her sweet, cutting smile, she pointed a delicate finger at Hongwei and recited:

"Intoxicated Xi Shi,
The flush of wine and beauty come on slow,
Last night in the clear moon and gentle breeze, deep in the night.
Good Elder Sister,
The gentleman of quality tempts her with his wooing."

The room erupted in praise for the clever verse. Hongwei retorted sharply, "You are indeed a 'good elder sister', small wonder there are those longing to tempt you!" Aizhu replied smoothly, "I was merely describing you and your striking looks. Do you not agree you perfectly resemble an intoxicated Xi Shi?" The maids dissolved into laughter once more.

Ruizhu threw the dice, landing the pattern 'A Loach Slipping into a Water-Chestnut Nest.' Disgusted by the unrefined name, she declared it invalid and demanded a redraw, but the others vehemently refused to allow it. Forced to comply, Ruizhu thought desperately, yet try as she might, she could not string the awkward phrases into any connected, poetic sense. Seeing her struggle, Hongxue laughed aloud:

"I shall compose for you as well, but you too must thank me as the matchmaker." Ruizhu eyed her suspiciously. "If the verse is decent, speak it. But if it is merely an insult, spare me the favor." Hongxue smiled mischievously. "I would not insult you. In truth, you shall be deeply grateful to me." Urged on by the eager crowd, Hongxue laughed and recited:

"A loach slipping into a water-chestnut nest,
A green-feathered bird sporting on the orchid sedge,
A marquis's gate, the old monk is not permitted to knock.
That bald little fellow,
Shall grow old together with you."

With a shriek, Ruizhu lunged forward and pinched Hongxue's mouth tightly shut. As Hongxue struggled wildly to pry the fingers from her lips, the entire room doubled over in helpless laughter. Mingzhu laughed so violently that her flailing sleeve knocked a wine cup entirely off the table. The raucous merriment drifted clearly to the ladies in the outer room, prompting Mrs. Yuan to laugh, "They are proving vastly more entertaining than we are." Meanwhile, Hongyu threw the pattern 'Eight Refusals' and immediately groaned, "This pattern is far too difficult. It cannot be assembled; allow me to throw again." Baozhu interjected, "Of course it can be assembled. I shall weave it for you, and I guarantee it will not leave you suffering 'eight refusals.'" Hongyu warned her, "Are you speaking in jest or earnestness? If you intend to be cruel, I swear I shall tear your mouth open." "I take no pleasure in cruelty," Baozhu replied innocently. Pointing directly at Hongyu, she recited:

"Eight refusals,
Startled from her dreams, the mandarin-ducks rise,
What sustains her through this long, long night?
Taking Off the Cloth Shirt,
The heart's longing, center-deep."

She paused, adding with a wicked glint, "The character for 'longing' here must, of course, be read as 'itching.'" Hongyu cursed her roundly. "It seems there is a terrible itch in your mouth as well. Allow me to scratch it for you!" Snatching up a slippery piece of sea cucumber with her chopsticks, she thrust it forcefully toward Baozhu's lips. Baozhu struck the chopsticks away with a swift backhand; they clattered loudly to the floor beneath the table, where a white cat instantly darted from the shadows to devour the fallen delicacy. The maids once again dissolved into tears of irrepressible laughter.

Zhangzhu rolled the pattern 'Climbing the Ladder to Gaze at the Moon,' offering a verse that was merely passable, devoid of any true brilliance. Hongwen immediately criticized her: "The name of this pattern is exquisitely poetic, yet presenting it so poorly is a grave injustice to the subject. Let me speak it properly for you." She then recited:

"Climbing the ladder to gaze at the moon,
Song Yu is in the western neighborhood,
Over the wall they exchanged poems and compositions until the sky grew light.
Stirring of the Flower's Heart,
A young woman is moved by longing."

Zhangzhu laughed bitterly, scolding Hongwen: "What a disgraceful way for a proper girl to behave! Climbing a ladder to peep at a man, and then boldly declaring your own heart is stirred! Have you no shame?" Hongwen laughed brightly in return: "I am speaking of your heart, for you suppress it all so deeply inside. It is far better to speak your desires plainly than to sicken yourself holding them back." Furious, Zhangzhu shoved Hongwen hard. Caught entirely off guard, Hongwen stumbled backward, crashing heavily into Baozhu and landing squarely upon her tightly bound feet. Baozhu winced in pain, placing one hand on Hongwen's shoulder for balance while frantically rubbing the tip of her embroidered shoe with the other. After soothing the ache for a moment, she delivered two sharp, punitive smacks to Hongwen's back, sending the room into another fit of laughter.

Hongxiang threw the pattern 'A Pair of Matched Butterflies, Precisely,' but found herself utterly incapable of assembling the verse. The few phrases she conjured stubbornly refused to rhyme. Hezhu offered sweetly, "I shall compose a beautiful verse for you, one that will ensure you never ignore me again." Assuming the offer was born of genuine kindness, Hongxiang replied, "Good elder sister, please compose it for me then." Hezhu laughed, "Though I compose the words, the entry remains entirely yours." She then recited:

"A pair of matched butterflies, precisely,
When will the wish be granted,
Every moment endured stretches like a summer's day.
Parallel Lotus Blossoms,
May it be so, morning and night."

Realizing the mockery, Hongxiang's face flooded with crimson fury. She lunged to tear Hezhu's mouth open, but the others swiftly intervened, hauling her back. It was then Hezhu's turn; she threw the pattern 'A Single Flower.' As she began to quietly arrange her poetic phrases, Hongzhu turned toward her with a brilliant, predatory smile. "Let me compose for you." Suspecting nothing but malice, Hezhu shook her head fiercely. "No, thank you." "Even if you do not want it, I am going to say it regardless," Hongzhu insisted. Hungry for more amusement, the entire room clamored for her to speak. Hongzhu recited loudly:

"A single flower,
We still cherish the time of the embrace,
This is what is called 'the talented youth and the beautiful maiden, indeed.'
A Single Point of Red,
Lightly soiling my private heart."

The room exploded into utterly irrepressible laughter. Beside herself with rage, Hezhu marched over, seized Hongzhu fiercely around the waist, and with surprising strength, slammed her down onto the heated bed-platform. Pinning the struggling girl, Hezhu demanded furiously, "Now, let me ask you in absolute earnest about this 'single point of red!'" Being the weaker of the two, Hongzhu could not twist free, her silken skirt parting wildly to both sides. The room watched in hysterics as her two tiny, bound feet flailed and kicked desperately in all directions, the maids laughing until they clutched their sides, barely able to remain upright.

Hongxue and Hongxiang finally rushed over, prying the furious Hezhu away. Sitting up, Hongzhu discovered the delicate flowers had been torn from her hair, her elegant tresses left a disheveled ruin. She spent a long moment smoothing her hair back into place before fiercely scolding Hezhu: "What brutish, reckless play! Speaking a jest is one thing, but you had no right to take it to such violent extremes." The chaotic commotion had now reached a pitch that Lady Hua and Mrs. Yuan could no longer ignore. They dispatched a senior married maidservant to investigate and strictly forbid any further rough play. The cluster of maids fell instantly into a terrified silence, hurriedly composing themselves and finishing their bowls of rice before marching back out to resume their attendance. The ladies observed this troupe of unruly girls: some with floral hairpins knocked askew, some with hopelessly disheveled hair, others turning their backs in a desperate bid to suppress lingering giggles, and a few with brows knit in silent, aggrieved reproach. Despite themselves, the ladies found the entire spectacle deeply amusing. Turning to Aizhu and the others, they scolded gently, "At your ages, how can you still behave with such unbridled mischief? Were it not for our honored guest being present today, I would certainly see you punished." Mrs. Yuan added her own words of mild censure to the Six Reds. The maids bowed their heads deeply, standing in contrite silence. Unable to contain her curiosity, Miss Su asked, "What manner of verses were you playing at in there? What on earth was so terribly funny?" At this, several of the maids failed to hold back their smiles, which in turn brought both ladies to the very brink of laughter themselves.

Lady Hua laughed softly: "These foolish girls, they are at once maddening and entirely amusing." Miss Su pressed further: "If any of your entries were truly clever, you may as well share them with us, for we would love to hear and appreciate them. If they are genuinely fine, I shall even bestow a reward upon you. And I promise your mistresses will not scold you for them."

Aizhu's bright eyes suggested she was eager to speak, but Hongxiang sharply tugged her sleeve, warning her to remain silent. Aizhu demurred, "There were a great many verses, I cannot recall them all clearly." Eager to hear the tale, Miss Su turned to Lady Hua and Mrs. Yuan: "They are terrified of their mistresses and dare not speak. But if you command them to recite, they surely will." Knowing full well her maids possessed razor-sharp wits and had likely composed highly entertaining verses, Lady Hua exchanged an amused glance with Mrs. Yuan. Being of a lively, unconventional spirit, Mrs. Yuan was secretly eager to display her own maids' cleverness. "If what you composed is coherent, you may go ahead and speak it," she commanded. "But if you recite something incoherent, each of you shall kneel and drink a massive penalty cup." Knowing their own memories were flawless and that their personal verses were free of offense, Hongwei and Mingzhu stepped forward. "They are coherent enough," they admitted cautiously, "but we fear that by speaking them, instead of earning a reward, we shall only invite punishment." Lady Hua laughed, "Go on, recite them one by one." Thus, Mingzhu recounted the three offensive entries composed by Aizhu, Baozhu, and Hezhu, while Hongwei recited the three scandalous verses authored by Hongxue, Hongwen, and Hongzhu. The effect was absolute: both ladies laughed so helplessly that the heavy pearl ornaments in their hair quivered and swayed wildly. Though they desperately attempted to compose their features into masks of stern reprimand, they completely failed. While Miss Su felt the verses were undeniably too free and scandalously familiar, she deeply admired the nimble sharpness of mind required to compose them. Refraining from laughing outright, she offered a subtle, appreciative nod.

After laughing together for a good while, the two ladies summoned the six culprits forward: the three Reds and the three Pearls who had authored the scandalous verses. Suppressing their lingering amusement, they scolded: "You are impossibly impertinent! How could you utter such things? If word of this escaped these walls, what face would you have left to show the world? Now kneel, every one of you!" Blushing furiously with deep shame, the six maids had no choice but to kneel before the gathered company. Miss Su quickly interceded on their behalf: "Two dear sisters, for my sake, I beg you to forgive them this first offense. Though their words were highly improper and excessively free, we must grant them credit for their lightning-quick wits and nimble tongues. Let their poetic merit cancel their fault." Mrs. Yuan sighed in concession, "Since Second Sister has spoken for them, I dare not refuse her request. Yet we must still admonish them; if we do not, they will grow accustomed to such license and become completely unbridled." She and Lady Hua then passed judgment upon the six entries: for the most egregious offenses, the penalty was a massive cup of wine to be drained in a single draft, accompanied by three sharp slaps of the palm as token chastisement. For the lesser offenses, a simple forfeit cup of wine was deemed sufficient. Thus, Hongxue, Hongzhu, Hezhu, and Baozhu suffered both the wine and the stinging slaps, while Aizhu and Hongwen escaped with only the wine forfeit. Receiving their punishments in utter humiliation, and having thrown back the harsh wine in such haste, the maids found themselves swaying unsteadily on their feet. They fought to hold themselves upright, privately, bitterly regretting their fleeting moment of high spirits.

Noticing the shadows lengthening as the day grew late, Mrs. Yuan realized the banquet must draw to a close. She smiled and turned to Lady Hua: "Throw the dice one final time and weave a few well-turned phrases to conclude our game. Let it be something pure and beautiful to cleanse the air after all this foolishness." She softly instructed the maids to serve the rice. Seeing the hour was indeed ripe and wishing not to overstay, Lady Hua ordered the final dishes brought forth. Picking up the ivory dice, she cast them, producing the highly auspicious pattern 'Golden Chrysanthemums Facing Lotus Blossoms.' Privately thrilled by the beautiful omen, she retreated into quiet thought before reciting her final verse:

"Golden chrysanthemums face the lotus blossoms,
Patterned candles curl as the plates gleam red,
And yet you ask me, blue-sleeved, to hold the jade cup with tender care.
Perfectly at Peace and Happy,
May ten thousand blessings converge upon you."

Mrs. Yuan and Miss Su praised the verse endlessly. Lady Hua warmly urged them both to drink two final cups, after which the rice was served and the magnificent meal gracefully concluded. Following the washing and rinsing of hands, Mrs. Yuan noted the evening sun slipping low on the horizon and knew she could delay no longer. She offered her deepest thanks and bid a fond farewell. Accompanied by the Ten Pearls and the entire cluster of maids, Lady Hua and Miss Su escorted her to the palanquin. The Six Reds carefully supported the sides of the chair, taking small, measured steps all the way to the outer corridor before Mrs. Yuan mounted the carriage, which then flowed smoothly away into the dusk.

As for Miss Su, she did not return to her own estate until the second watch of the deep night. To learn what transpired next, you must hear the tale told in the following chapter.
