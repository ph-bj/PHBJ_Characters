Chapter 18 — In a pleasure house, tricks are taught for exploiting performers; at a courtesan's door, willow songs are sung.

Qinyan's illness lingered and he remained listless, keeping his door closed to visitors; only a few close friends from the same troupe came by from time to time to offer comfort. Baozhu and Sulan also passed on word from Ziyu, recounting his recent days and explaining his inability to visit often, yet urging Qinyan to take the utmost care of his health. Hearing these messages, Qinyan only grew more heavy-hearted. Since falling ill, he had gone nowhere at all; even Yiyuan he had long since ceased to visit. But his teacher Changqing depended on him as a money tree, and with Qinyan ill for so long and unable to receive clients, Changqing had lost a good deal of his enthusiasm.

Now, those who made their career as teachers of theatrical troupes were originally dan role performers themselves; over thirty years their lives went through four transformations.

What were the four transformations? In their youth, they possessed a lovely bearing and were cherished by all. When the undifferentiated was breached and the two positives met, people called them "rabbits." After the age of twenty, the body would grow larger, the looks would coarsen, yet they still flirted and primped, wearing splendid headgear and fine clothes. When performing on stage, they cast aside all shame to display the utmost seductive wantonness. They twisted and minced, constantly striving to captivate souls and draw men's essence, earning them the name of "foxes." After thirty, the voice had gone hoarse, the beard had appeared, and acting was no longer possible; with no other recourse, they would put on the look of a market-town roughneck, buy some boys, train them for a year or so, and then send them out to earn money. Those who were well-favored earned more money and were treated almost like a father. Those of ordinary looks who could not charm people and could not earn were nagged morning and evening, scolded for a day's absence from entertaining clients and beaten for two days' absence. When the boy's apprenticeship was up, the teacher would open his mouth and demand three or five thousand cash; once the money was in hand, he'd send the boy out the door as bare and empty as he'd come in, without even an old garment to his name. If the teacher had no wife, he would require the disciple to share his bed at night. Such ferocious ruffians, more dreadful than a wild tiger by several degrees, were at this stage appropriately compared to "tigers."

Finally, when their luck ran out, they could only linger in the troupe carrying flags and playing bit-parts, reducing them to the status of "dogs." Such was the standard life-portrait of a troupe teacher. Four months had passed since Qinyan arrived in the capital the previous twelfth month. In that time, Xu Ziyun had already spent several thousand taels of silver. This was no small sum, and it explained why Changqing treated Qinyan exceptionally well.

Should Qinyan remain ill for a year and a half, one might fear Changqing's heart would change. However, this remained mere speculation by others, and we shall let it rest for now.

Now let us speak of Wei Pincai, who had entered the Hua mansion full of expectation, imagining he would immediately make his mark. Who could have known that after a month inside, the host and guest had still not come face to face? The several times he had asked for an audience, he was put off with the excuse that the young master was busy. Those he did meet and mingle with were all dubious characters. There was also that class of arrogant household servants who put on great airs: they would meet him with merely a perfunctory bow, openly take the superior seat, and speak in nothing but muddled nonsense. The young ones were full of bluster and pretense of understanding; the old ones traded on their seniority and were thoroughly devious. Pincai tried his utmost to get along with everyone and attended to all social obligations. He dared not offend even a dog in the Hua household, all in the hope of securing some favorable outcome. But unknown to him, the losses were mounting steadily. The household had several hundred friends, private secretaries, and servants; hardly a day passed without some occasion arising. Once accustomed to this social round, one could not be selective about whom to flatter. Whenever there was a celebration of any kind, one had to contribute a share. Occasionally, three or four friends would gather together and generate some matter, whether it involved splitting a meal bill or gambling away a stake. Then there were the young lads, the coachmen, the grooms, the cooks, and the like, who came regularly to extract small gratuities; fail to comply and gossip would spread at once. Though only a month had passed, he had already become mixed up with all the idle hangers-on of the household. Some proved congenial, while others did not. The congenial ones included Gu Yueqing, Zhang Xiaomei, and their sort, whereas the uncongenial ones were men like Yan Jian'an and Wang Qingyun. Pincai could only attend to his own station, keeping close to the congenial ones while distancing himself from the uncongenial. But what truly vexed him was that he had still not had a proper meeting with the young master, despite having been there a month. Meanwhile, he had nothing useful to do, merely mixing with the crowd day after day. Truly: two meals of old rice, one pillow of dark and sweet sleep.

On this particular day, he went out for a stroll, came out of the city gate, and found the scene much livelier than inside the city, with carriages flowing like water, horses like dragons. Following the road, he came to Mei's residence on Mingke Alley, went in and found Ziyu still ill in bed, listless and low-spirited. Ziyu asked how Pincai was doing; Pincai could only speak positively, rattling off a few easy lies. He then went to pay his respects to Lady Yan and express his thanks. Coming out to find Li Yuanmao, he discovered his door locked. He took his leave of Ziyu again and went out, feeling lonely and desolate with nowhere to go.

Wandering aimlessly to the edge of the Tiger-Subduing Bridge (Fuhu Qiao), he recalled that Zhang Zhongyu lived at the Wu residence nearby. He inquired at the gatehouse and, as luck would have it, Zhongyu was at home and invited him in to sit. After a few pleasantries and a serving of tea, Zhongyu soon spoke: "Now that my little brother is in the city, it must be hard to get out often. Why not find a place to sit and watch some opera to dispel the tedium?" Pincai replied: "Very well. I have actually been quite worn out these past two days, so if we are going, let us go." So the two of them went together to the theater, found seats, and watched two or three plays. Some young protégés came by to chat with them. Across the way, on the lower level of the opposite gallery, Pincai spotted Li Yuanmao together with Sun Sihui. Pincai went over, exchanged a few words, and came back.

Zhongyu remarked: "Those two brothers-in-law are a pair of born good-for-nothings if ever there was one. What is the point of looking after them?" The plays that day felt stale and old-fashioned, making Zhongyu restless enough to say: "Let us go." He paid for the seats, and together with Pincai went up to a wine-house for a small drink and a talk. Observing that Pincai appeared in poor spirits and lacked his usual demeanor, Zhongyu asked: "I heard you have entered the Hua family mansion. Given the grandeur of that establishment, and that the young master Hua loves social connections, things must naturally have taken a great turn for the better lately." Pincai said: "If elder brother hadn't asked, I would not have brought it up myself. At first, Third Master Fu told me that the young master Hua held the reputation of Lord Mengchang, keeping hundreds of retainers at his table. I went in to find plenty of retainers indeed, yet they are all thoroughly mercenary people, constantly plotting to squeeze one out or cut another down. I have been inside for over a month, and the young master continues to be cool and distant. If this goes on much longer, I fear I have misread the situation entirely." Zhongyu said: "How many times have you met the young master?" Pincai said: "I've met him a few times, but only for a few casual words before he moves away. He has many old familiars, leaving a newcomer naturally unable to push through." Zhongyu was silent for a good while, then sighed: "In the world today, if you want to maintain your self-respect and dignity, you can only stay home behind closed doors. To move within the circles of wealth and influence requires freshly drilling your speech and comportment, which is itself no easy matter. At the highest level stood two men whom we could never emulate: the former worthy Chen Meigong, and Li Liweng, the author of the 'Ten Kinds of Songs'. Both possessed learning second to none. When unfavorable fate prevented them from obtaining official positions to render great service to the state, they assumed the roles of lofty recluses. In doing so, they deployed all their lifetime's learning to navigate the gates of the powerful. They relied on a few brushstrokes of painting and calligraphy, a few poems and essays, to make princes and lords change their expression and the courtly world run toward them; even those great officials had to flatter them in return. Had they offended someone, they could have been cut down anywhere, so naturally they relied on an unshakeable skill. Isn't that formidable? Those at the highest level, nowadays, are gone. As for the second tier, it would be hard to find one even when selecting from a wide pool. Their ten essential qualities are:

Pincai said: "What are the ten qualities?" Zhongyu said: "A full measure of amiability; a second-tier degree of talent; three jin's capacity for wine; four-seasons appropriate dress; mastery of the five musical scales; an official rank of the sixth grade; skill at the seven-syllable verse; ability to cast the net eight ways; comprehension of the nine streams of learning; and perfect ease in all social occasions." Pincai shook his head: "So many requirements?" Zhongyu replied: "After each, one must append an additional word: the word 'not'! One must maintain amiability without ever changing, possess talent without ever showing it off, and hold a capacity for wine without ever getting drunk. One needs seasonal dress without ever pawning it, a mastery of scales without ever making an error, and the rank of the sixth grade without ever actually serving. One must command the seven-syllable verse without ever going rusty, cast the net eight ways without ever letting a thread drop, comprehend all nine streams without ever running short, and achieve perfect social ease without ever appearing common." Pincai said: "On that account, being a person becomes very difficult indeed. I don't have a single one of these. How could I learn all of them?" Zhongyu said: "Actually, one needn't have all of them; a few will do for social management. Moreover, each person has their own timing and fortune, but one does need to have some genuine skill of one's own, so that people look up to you." Pincai said: "And the third tier?" Zhongyu said: "The third tier also has its seven-character formula. The first is 'tong'." Pincai asked the meaning of this, and Zhongyu laughed: "If you have passed the first examination level, you have naturally read some books. You know a little about the refinements, which already elevates you above market-town roughnecks. The second quality is 'half-passable'. It means knowing how to be obsequious, buttering up those inside and below. It involves the servility of a slave while addressing others as brother, pulling on the family façade and leaning on the ancestors, boring through cracks to fill holes, and drumming up the draughty take. That constitutes the third tier." Pincai remarked: "Do not look down on the third tier. I suspect nowadays everyone belongs to the third tier." Zhongyu agreed: "Is that not so! In my view, it follows no fixed pattern. Even someone with all ten qualities may fail to get anywhere, while someone lacking a single one of the seven characters might stumble onto an opportunity. In the end it's all each person's own fate. As the saying goes: 'When luck arrives, the wind carries you to the Prince of Teng's Pavilion; when luck retreats, thunder smashes the Jianfu stele.' I know that this young master Hua is an easy person to deal with. Just look at how many people pass through his household and manage to extract all sorts of benefits. Let me give you a method: to get on his good side is not so difficult. I also know this person, as he once entrusted me with some matters.

"I know there is someone in his household called Lin Shanzhi, his personal attendant." He put up a thumb as he spoke: "He is a person of this caliber. His word is followed, his plan is adopted, and he never leaves the young master's side. You must first work through this connection; once that gate is opened, everything else becomes easy. There is also that group of eight young pages always at the young master's elbow. Children lack great discernment, so offering them a small advantage is enough. Slowly, word by word, blow it into his ears. Today he hears that Master Wei is fine, and tomorrow he hears it again, until the sentiment works its way into the young master's heart. This strategy is known as 'releasing the leashed sparrow.' You let it out on fifty or sixty feet of string, knowing that in the end, it will always be reeled back in. Just don't begrudge the small initial outlay." Pincai nodded and said: "I am much obliged and truly grateful!" Zhongyu continued: "For instance, once you've established a connection with the young master, observe what his temperament is, what pleases him and what he dislikes, and naturally follow his preferences.

"Follow them to nine parts in ten, but always hold back one part for later rather than spending it all too quickly. Consider the handling of a wayward horse. If you wish to manage it, going head-on against its nature proves absolutely impossible. Instead, let it run until it is completely exhausted, and then a single pull of the reins will turn it around. Or take this case: if someone can think through a matter to nine parts, you must think it through to ten. That one extra part constitutes the art of turning the wayward horse, which is known as 'reining in the wayward horse.'

"Then there are those who have all sorts of faults and do not mix with people at all. They just stay in their rooms with their wives and concubines and enjoy themselves in isolation. With those, too, you must discover one or two things they enjoy; then meet those preferences, offer small tokens of loyalty and faithfulness, and never ask them for anything. Naturally they will set their minds at ease, saying: 'That man has a bit of genuine sincerity, and he's not just trying to exploit me.' Once they've swallowed that bait, you can manage them however you like. This is what's called 'fishing for the golden cicada.' As for one's general manner, though one must be amiable, one also cannot be spineless through and through. Those who have no relevance and serve no purpose, such as Yan Jian'an and Wang Qingyun, don't bother with them; mingling indiscriminately with them serves no purpose at all. Among the household retinue, there are those who cannot say a single useful word inside, yet are masters at bamboozling outsiders. You must see clearly for yourself: those worth attending to, attend to; those not worth attending to, don't attend to. If you attend to those who are useless, the ones who matter will look down on you." Pincai burst out laughing when he heard all this: "Elder brother is truly the foremost great talent of our time. Chen Ping's stratagems and Zhuge Liang's plans are no more than this. You can describe the temperament and character of every sort of person in the world as if you had written them on the palm of your hand. Your little brother ought to come to you with a student's card and pay formal respects as a disciple, so that the method of the heart may be properly transmitted." Zhongyu laughed: "I've already told you everything. What is there left to bow to a teacher for? Follow what I have said, and it is guaranteed not to go wrong. In the future, if good things come to you, do not forget your teacher. That is all the good conscience a student needs." With that, both laughed, and before they knew it, half the day had passed. Zhongyu settled the bill and they came out together. Zhongyu said: "Little brother, go back into the city now. I have some business to attend to and cannot keep you company." With that, he bowed and departed. It was still early. As Pincai walked along the way, he spotted Sihui and Yuanmao in the distance, just rounding a corner ahead. Pincai thought: "Where are they going?" and quietly followed. He entered a small alleyway packed with idle onlookers, all crowded at someone's doorway peering in. Pincai had heard people say that the East Garden was where prostitutes gathered, so he joined the crowd and stood watching. In one household, a thatched-wall mud enclosure containing two thatched rooms, he saw Sihui and Yuanmao standing just ahead of him. They were looking at two women seated on a long bench. Both were around twenty years old, fat-faced and full-bellied, their hair greased and their faces powdered, wearing rather showy clothing. He saw one woman say to Sihui: "Come in and sit." She laughed and simpered, making Sihui and Yuanmao itch with desire. They hovered on the edge of entering but not quite entering, staring in a daze. He also saw an awkward-looking man over forty squatting on the ground, wearing a short jacket belted at the waist. A large pouch hung from it, big enough to hold two strings of cash. Then from behind the curtain walked out a woman of around twenty or so, rather good-looking. She had an oval face with a charming scattering of freckles, her hair done in a T-style knot with her temples soft and loose, a flower tucked in her hair. She wore plain clothes, and on her feet she had a pair of pointed-toe "four-happiness bats" high-soled shoes with pile ornaments. She sat down on the bench and chatted with the other two. Her speech sounded different from northern speakers, more like a southerner. From head to toe she possessed an appealing prettiness noticeably different from the others. He heard one of the plainer-looking women begin to sing:

"Fine young lord,
Every day at the gate you stare and stare,
Your staring sets my heart astir,
Your longing has blurred your very eyes.
Wait a while.
Cloud and rain on Mount Wu, done in an instant,
All for a mere two hundred copper cash."

Pincai found this amusing, and then thought: though it is a crude and lewd ditty, the feeling expressed is truthful. He listened again: the homely one had turned and was singing directly at Sihui and Yuanmao:

"One of them has a pockmarked face, and the other has bad eyes,
A blind-eyed hen paired with a mangy toad.
You like me, and I like him. Do not make such a fuss.
In the tender quarter, you are clearly not an old hand."

While the bystanders could not make out the meaning, Pincai understood perfectly that she was insulting those two. He nearly burst out laughing and had to hold it in. He looked again at the good-looking one, who appeared to be a newcomer. In the capital, a prostitute wanting to enter a proper establishment would first spend a few days in the East Garden or the West Depot to get a sense of the market. Naturally, she would soon lose all sense of shame and become practiced and worldly. Likewise, those no longer of use in the better houses were demoted to these two places. This was none other than the White Chrysanthemum (Bai Juhua) that Gao Pin had spoken of, newly arrived from Guangxi. Watching her behavior, Pincai could see she still had a measure of bashfulness about her. A small boy came up beside her and offered a pipa lute. She took it and played a suite of "Wang Zhaojun's Lament" (昭君怨), causing the crowd at the doorway to grow even more numerous.

Yuanmao, being shortsighted, pushed straight through into the doorway and stood there staring. Pincai watched the woman play and sing:

"The willow branch, the willow branch,
In years past, within the palace, we vied in slender waists.
Now cast aside to grow at the roadside,
Kingfisher brows knotted, lamenting the crossroads.
Where is the painted boat moored?
The fine horse neighs into the wind.
I wait in vain for the harbinger of spring to come down the path in the second month,
And have become only the withered wood of autumn in the west wind."

Tightening the strings a notch, tuning again, one key higher, she sang on:

"To think that once we were mandarin ducks and paired;
Today we are the stars Shen and Shang, never meeting.
And so it is that a dew-and-morning marriage does not last long.
Through ten thousand mountains and rivers I came to this place;
Two separated phoenixes gaze at each other in vain from afar.
I lament that for a beauty with ill-starred fate, there is seldom a good ending;
Even to hold the pipa again would weep the bowels dry.
I think of my love,
Seven feet tall, the bearing of a heavenly god.
A captain of a thousand, in command of a hundred,
From south to north of Dongting Lake, his fame was great.
A kindly father and loving mother,
One tender while on the Li River.
And now he has abandoned me, this slight, solitary, friendless girl.
Truly I have become a single spray of late chrysanthemum braving the autumn frost.
At Shi Gong Dam, the sorrow pierces the heart;
At Huamei Pond, the wasted body nearly perished.
Through all of Xiang and Yuan, the three rivers and nine rivers,
I only hoped to catch up, to welcome the twin oars on Tao-gen and Tao-ye.
Who knew that from Chu's end to Wu's beginning the sky stretched endlessly long?
Then passing Jinling where the royal air had not fully yielded,
Looking toward Yangzhou from the lamplight at Guazhou,
Crossing the Yellow River,
Dreading to see the three-dam current raging day and night,
Through Huai, Xu, Ji, and Yan, I had no heart to admire the scenery.
Mercifully, all the way safe and sound I came to the imperial capital.
I only wonder where that fickle young man has hidden.
I am Zhao Wuniang who cut her hair to search for her husband;
Do not mistake me for a common street-walker on the road to Handan."

As she played and sang, her voice filled with grief, moving Pincai to tears. He thought: "This person truly is a woman of deep feeling. As she recounts her whole life's suffering, I wonder where that heartless man has gone." Then he heard Sun Sihui say: "Alas, alas. The things on my body are now completely empty. How vexing! How vexing!" He stamped his foot and sighed: "Ha! A gentleman has no cause to remove his jade from his person, yet it has been rolled up and carried away in his bosom. From now on I shall be as in mourning without my belt-ornament and see what he can do about that!" Yuanmao said: "These pickpockets in the capital are truly hateful. Last year I took ten taels of silver from my father for Wei senior Pincai to take me to the theater. At the theater gate I stumbled, and someone helped me up and dusted me off. I still thought him a good man, but when I looked later, the silver was gone. When my father found out afterward, he scolded me roundly for a full day. Now you see what these dog-things do to people." The listeners could not help smirking inwardly. Sun Sihui said: "'That lovely person, a true lord's desire to match.' Why do you not hasten your steps and enter?" Yuanmao laughed: "Not me. With ten pairs of eyes watching, how could I go in?" Pincai heard this and gave an involuntary laugh. Yuanmao recognized the voice as very familiar, and looked around in all directions; spotting Pincai, he flushed crimson and pushed his way out with Sihui to greet him. Sihui said: "Elder Brother Wei, I know that you are now a crafty three-burrow rabbit, fully installed in the public gate with a bow and formal step. You do not even bother to look in on old friends any more. Today, having unexpectedly encountered you, my heart is glad." Pincai said: "Occupied with all manner of affairs, I had been meaning to come and pay my respects. The day has gotten late and I must return to the city." Yuanmao said: "How are you getting on these days in the Hua household? Are you going back into the city now?" Pincai said: "I'm heading back now." Yuanmao said: "We are also heading back, so let us walk together." As they chatted along the way, Pincai asked: "Just now, when you heard them singing, did you understand what they were singing?" Yuanmao said: "Not a word. I was rather taken by that plump woman. She kept smiling and beckoning at me, but I did not dare go in and sit down."

Sihui said: "Splendid, splendid! Cheap in price and little work required. Tomorrow I shall take you along and give it a try, cousin. If we delay our journey, the nimble-footed may get there first, and though we repent it will be too late. As long as one finds true pleasure, why mind ten pairs of watching eyes?" Pincai, hearing him still mouthing his pedantic bookish phrases and talking absolute nonsense, suppressed his laughter and let it pass. At the crossroads they went their separate ways. Behind Pincai came the rumbling of a carriage passing at speed; Pincai quickly stepped aside, and saw that seated in the carriage was the woman who had just been playing and singing, with an old woman sitting on the carriage's running board. It went rushing past like the wind.

But let us set aside Pincai's side of the story.

As for this White Chrysanthemum, she hailed from Wuzhou Prefecture in Guangxi and was extraordinarily good-looking. She had been married to a man named Song, a shiftless fellow. Chrysanthemum was by nature good at making connections, and she had come to know a military officer surnamed Zhang, a native of Huguang. The two became very intimate in Guangxi and vowed to grow old together, a relationship that endured for several years. Last year this Officer Zhang was sent on a duty assignment to the capital. White Chrysanthemum, being a person of feeling and loyalty, set off after him as soon as he left, taking some travel funds and a small maidservant.

Somehow she took the wrong road and ended up going all the way out of Guangxi Province and into Hunan, and still had not caught up with him; she did not know how far he had gone ahead; moreover her travel money was exhausted, she knew no one in this strange place, and she was trapped with no way forward or back, so she settled in Hunan. Then she received false information that this Officer Zhang had been stationed in the capital as a company commander and could not leave the capital.

So she sold some of her clothing for travel funds, boarded a convenient boat, and headed for the capital. When she arrived, Officer Zhang had long since completed his assignment and returned; as a result, Chrysanthemum found herself stranded in the capital, and had no choice but to lean on her doorway and smile for a living.

The woman who had come to fetch her today was from the Tao household, an establishment keeper. This Madam Tao had three girls in her house; the best of them, known as Jade Celestial Fairy (Yu Tianxian), was a native of Yangzhou, born vivacious and coquettishly attractive. These past two days she had received a big-spending patron, none other than Xi Eleven from Guangdong. Madam Tao had investigated his background and learned that he was from a wealthy family in Hainan with assets in the millions. His ten brothers all held posts as circuit intendants and prefects, their father was a currently serving brigade commander, and the family ran a foreign trading firm. She had also learned that he was a thoroughly naive big spender and plotted to make a large profit from him. Unfortunately, none of her girls could understand much of his speech. Xi Eleven was moreover addicted to opium, smoking one or two liang a day. Although the three girls could take a few puffs of white-earth tobacco, they were laid low by a few fractions of the black-earth variety. Furthermore, neither side's speech was very intelligible to the other.

As a result, Xi Eleven was not greatly pleased. Madam Tao knew that Chrysanthemum hailed from Guangxi and was also very good-looking. Confident that Chrysanthemum could hold his interest, Madam Tao brought her over and formally adopted her as a foster daughter. At once new clothing was found for her, and she was introduced to all her foster sisters. She and Jade Celestial Fairy became especially fond of each other. After suffering such terrible hardships, Chrysanthemum felt as if she had escaped hell, leaving her with nothing to complain about. With one heart she awaited the coming of Xi Eleven.

Now, this Xi Eleven had been in the capital for less than half a year, yet he had already spent tens of thousands of taels of silver, dumping it all into a cesspool. Someone advised him to take a concubine. Being a habitual wanderer, he found respectable women of good families most disagreeable; he hunted for someone among the prostitutes, but found no one to his taste. One day Madam Tao came to invite him, saying she had a new arrival from Guangxi at her establishment. Xi Eleven, hearing she was from Guangxi, was filled with joy; he called a small page boy and had him bring the opium equipment, then, making no use of a carriage, strode off on foot in a grand manner. When he arrived at the Tao household, Madam Tao came out first to greet him and spent some time fawning and flattering with the most eager of shoulder-hunched smiles, then said: "Come quickly and bring out Fourth Miss." Not long afterward, White Chrysanthemum came out, graceful and swaying, fragrant in her attire, a spring-blossom expression on her face. She came forward to meet him, and they exchanged a few words, finding their speech perfectly matched to each other's. Xi Eleven looked at her appearance: she was beautiful as a flower, pliant as water, sweet as honey, sticky as candy. He was enormously pleased; after a few brief questions they went into the room together. He had his page arrange the opium equipment properly, lit the lamp, and smoked and chatted. Xi Eleven was a man who took big pulls of opium. Chrysanthemum prepared his pipe, beginning from half a fen per pull and gradually building up to three fen per pull before he was satisfied. Chrysanthemum's skill in preparing opium was excellent, as she could produce it neither too raw nor too well-cooked. Xi Eleven also liked to smoke the "noodle-style" opium. She would take the opium and twist it into a narrow rod on the needle, holding it over the flame and turning it on all four sides until it hung in a string about five inches long. She would place it at the pipe-bowl opening, and Xi Eleven would take a great shuā-shuā-shuā pull to draw it all in at once. He would then hold his mouth closed so that not a single wisp of smoke escaped, a practice that remained his lifelong supreme technique. Chrysanthemum also took a few puffs, then nestled into Xi Eleven's arms and continued preparing the pipe for him. Xi Eleven smoked seven or eight qian in all, which proved enough. His ardor surged up, and the two of them put away the lamp, closed the door, and enacted a secret drama. Their encounter resembled the butterfly's love of the flower or mandarin ducks turned upside down, displaying manifold subtleties beyond description. One was as fierce as a tiger bounding down from the mountain, while the other was as ripe as a melon dropping from the vine. They carried on for two full hours before each was fully satisfied and content; then tidied themselves up, relit the lamp and smoked more opium, followed by drinking wine and eating food.

Xi Eleven stayed there seven or eight days running. Each day he spent several dozen strings of cash, and the madam was already sharpening her ax. He paid for clothing on the person, jewelry to make, and furnishing for the room, all of which went without saying. Then all the girls started wanting this and wanting that. Even the madam herself, the hangers-on, and the general crowd put on their act, every single one of them. But Xi Eleven was by nature free and generous, never stingy.

Chrysanthemum saw that Xi Eleven's powerful and vigorous manner surpassed her previous love by twice over. Knowing he was a great gentleman waiting in the capital for an official appointment, she conceived the desire to become a proper wife. Xi Eleven had originally come searching for a concubine, and seeing Chrysanthemum's great beauty and her expert skills, he too wanted to buy her as a concubine. But the madam was not entirely willing. Chrysanthemum had only been there a few days, was not actually her person, and there was no purchase price to extract. The madam wanted to keep her in the house to earn more money. If the girl went off to make a proper marriage, would all that effort not be wasted? So she worked to stir up trouble between them. To Chrysanthemum she said Xi Eleven was a heartless man who had dozens of concubines at home. She claimed to have heard from his second brother that once a woman was brought home, she was immediately forgotten as he went off chasing new ones, marking him as a man of the new-is-exciting, old-is-discarded type. She warned that a woman absolutely must not marry such a man and told Chrysanthemum not to make the wrong decision. To Xi Eleven she said that Chrysanthemum still had a husband living and was unwilling to be sold. She also said that Chrysanthemum's nature was difficult, and having grown accustomed to this way of life, she could not settle to propriety. She promised that if the lord wanted to take a concubine, she could guarantee to find him a perfect one, so he need not take this one. But the two of them had already formed so warm a bond that despite the madam's interference, neither believed a word. Chrysanthemum told Xi Eleven her whole history in full detail, explaining that the madam had brought her over expressly to serve him. Her desire to make a proper marriage had nothing whatsoever to do with these people and only required gifting them a few taels of silver. Xi Eleven made up his mind at once, summoned an official matchmaker, and bestowed fifty taels of gold on Old Madam Tao. He took Chrysanthemum away, bought a maidservant, and hired an elderly female attendant. And so Chrysanthemum married Xi Eleven as a concubine, entering the proper path of life from that point on.
