Chapter 19 — Lewd plots and treachery hide within the wooden barrel; clever speech and quick wit deceive for gain.

Following Zhongyu's instructions, Pincai put them into practice: he first lavished every effort on winning Lin Shanzhi's favor, and then painstakingly cultivated the good will of the eight young pages. Shanzhi had originally been a celebrated young dan actor in the Lianjin troupe; he later joined the Dengchun troupe, and Young Master Hua, having taken a liking to him, purchased him at a high price. The eight pages who came afterward had all been trained by Shanzhi. Needless to say, this Lin Shanzhi was thoroughly schooled in music and song. Wei Pincai was by nature a quick-witted person, and his kun-opera singing was superb; even playing and strumming, he could hold his own. Moreover, on the journey up the previous year he had traveled along with a troupe, and on the boat had managed to pick up a great many operatic texts; after arriving in the capital, with theater-going every two or three days, he had naturally added a good deal more to his repertoire.

One day, Lin Shanzhi was teaching Yuling to sing an aria when Pincai happened to wander in. Shanzhi invited him to sit while continuing the lesson. The aria in question happened to be one of Pincai's greatest accomplishments; he began to join in from the sidelines, his five tones pure and his lips and teeth articulate, singing with such inspired mastery that Lin Shanzhi was quite astonished. From that point they became close; Shanzhi thereupon praised Pincai to Young Master Hua morning and evening, without stint.

Young Master Hua placed the most trust in Shanzhi; since Shanzhi did not praise people lightly, if he was willing to praise someone, that person must truly be fine — and the young master formed a favorable impression. The eight young pages were all natives of Suzhou and Yangzhou, so their dispositions were naturally congenial with Pincai's. Pincai knew how to please them: today he gave one a fan, tomorrow he gave another a fragrant sachet — always just the kind of things that delighted small children, so that they all felt this master secretary truly knew what people liked. As for the senior stewards and the office staff — he cultivated those even more thoroughly. Within a month, everyone was speaking well of him. As for Yan and Wang — needless to say. Even Zhang and Gu, though also sociable, could not match Pincai in warm-hearted thoroughness and devoted attentiveness.

One day, having learned that the young master had gone out, Pincai invited Zhang Xiaomei to come out of the city with him. Xiaomei wanted to find Feng Zipei; the two rode together in a carriage directly to Feng Zipei's residence. This Zipei was on the closest terms with Young Master Hua, had already met Pincai, and the two got along naturally. Feng Zipei too was the son of an official family, who had lost his father early and had accumulated some official savings; his mother doted on him and let him drift freely among performance halls and theaters, mingling with the powerful and wealthy. In time, the family's affairs gradually declined; but some good friends helped prop things up, so that he always seemed to have money at hand, his clothing was fine and fresh, and the way he combined naive charm with gentle appeal inspired a certain fondness and sympathy.

That day, Zhang and Wei came out, brought a small page boy, and arrived at Zipei's gate; they had the page boy go in to enquire. He happened to be at home; they were invited inside and sat down in the study. Pincai was calling for the first time; he observed the room — two bays facing north, the furnishings rather splendid, but the entire tabletop covered with half-read books, brushes, inkstones, curios, and all manner of things piled in helter-skelter disarray. On the walls hung xiao flutes, pipa lutes, and bows and arrows and the like. Pincai said to Xiaomei: "Little Feng lives like this, so why doesn't he at least tidy things up a bit?" Xiaomei laughed: "He probably doesn't have time to tidy things — there's no one in the world busier than he is." As they spoke, Zipei came out; it being the end of the fourth month, he was all in silk and gauze, looking all the more graceful and charming. Before he had cleared the entrance screen he was already preceded by laughter, calling out: "What are you two coming for? Have you come to pay your respects to Lord Number Two?" Pincai laughed and was about to reply when Zhang Xiaomei stepped forward and wrapped him in a tight embrace; Zipei also embraced Xiaomei back, and they gave each other a good hug around the waist. Xiaomei said with a grin:

"Indeed, we have come to pay our respects to Lord Number Two." He then pressed his face close to smell Zipei's cheek, and added: "How fragrant! Not a Lord Number Two at all — positively a little darling." Zipei said: "There you go being wanton again — stirring up Lord Number Two so his heart can't bear it." Pincai laughed loudly from the sidelines; the three of them romped together for a while, then sat down — and found they had nothing particular to say to each other.

From outside came the sound of someone shouting: "Is Feng Number Two at home?" Zipei answered back: "He's not at home." Pincai recognized the voice as very familiar; then a man barged straight in, saying: "Look at this! You're right here in your burrow and you still answer 'not at home.'" Everyone looked up — it was Yang Meichuang, whom all knew; the group grew livelier still, and the talk ran entirely to amusements and merriment. The four deliberated: "Are we just going to sit around chatting and call it a day? Would that not be a waste of this fine time?" Xiaomei said: "I was planning to watch some opera." Feng Zipei said: "Pah! You're like a country bumpkin coming to the city who mistakes a bright lantern for a pig's bladder. Today is a memorial day — and you're still thinking of watching opera?" Yang Meichuang said: "It really is a memorial day today — let's go to a restaurant." The other three were all happy with that. Zipei went back inside to change his clothes, then they all walked out together and went to a wine-house.

The waiters, seeing four young men and recognizing Yang and Feng, perked up immediately and knew today would be a lively table. Old Yang the Eighth said: "What shall we eat?" Feng Zipei said to the waiter: "Recite the menu." The waiter ran through dozens of items; the four ordered five or six dishes and settled in to eat while they talked. The waiter first brought four pots of yellow wine and a table of fruit and snacks, then gradually brought out the dishes one by one. The four drank for a while, told some jokes, and then Meichuang said: "Are we just going to do this and call it done? The waiter would look down on us — let's summon someone." Pincai was most enthusiastic and said: "Certainly — but who?" Meichuang laughed: "I have a lot of people in mind, and I have a taste for novelty — unlike some people who always call the same person every time. The other day I saw someone in the Lianzhu troupe called Yulin who was very good-looking; as soon as he came offstage someone was already taking him out — I imagine he's quite popular." Pincai said: "He can't be as good as Qinguan." Meichuang said: "Which Qinguan?" Pincai recounted what he had seen of the theater visit at New Year, then added: "This Qinguan, other than with Mei Gengxiang, gives everyone else the cold shoulder; I fear he may not come if summoned." Meichuang said: "What young protégé cannot be summoned? You go and summon him today." Pincai thought inwardly: "Now that I'm at the Hua household, they must know about it — naturally they'll regard me differently from before. I'll go and summon him; if he doesn't come, I'll summon someone else." Meichuang then asked Xiaomei: "And you — who will you call?" Xiaomei said:

"I'll call Rong-guan." He then asked Zipei; Zipei said: "With three already summoned, that's quite festive enough. I won't summon anyone — I'll just be a guest who drinks the sideshow wine." Meichuang laughed: "You might as well count yourself as the young protégé." Zipei, hearing this, took a mouthful of wine and spat it straight at Meichuang's face; Meichuang dodged, but quite a bit still landed on his robe. Meichuang said: "What need to be so agitated at a single remark — it must have hit your real sore spot." Everyone laughed. He took off the robe, asked for some spirits to spray it with, spread it on the railing in the eaves to dry, then laughed again: "What a waste of that mouthful of wine — why didn't you spit it into my mouth?" Zipei threw a handful of melon-seed shells at him; Meichuang received them without retaliating.

Then the waiter came in and said: "Qinguan and Yulin both say they're ill and can't come; Rong-guan will be here shortly." Pincai had expected Qinguan not to come and took it philosophically. But Yang Meichuang was irritated: "Odd — how can two out of three summoned fail to come? I wonder if they're really ill or just making excuses?" Xiaomei said: "Of course they're really ill — what excuses would they make?" Pincai said: "There's also that Qiguan — also very fine; I called him several times in the first month and he always came without fail." Pincai wrote a note to summon Qiguan, and Meichuang summoned Er Xi (Second Happiness). The waiter reported: "Someone has been sent to summon Qiguan. Er Xi is over there at another table keeping a guest company; they've already eaten and are about to break up."

The waiter informed Er Xi; before long, Er Xi came over, greeted everyone in turn, and sat down beside Meichuang. He poured a round of drinks and sent around some dishes, then asked: "Who else is summoned for today's table?" Meichuang said: "Those we summoned are all sick — they couldn't come." Pincai, upon seeing Er Xi, was not greatly pleased, since at the New Year banquet Er Xi had said quite a few cutting things to him. Er Xi, however, bore no grudge; and since the payment from that day had been sent over by Pincai the very next morning without any trouble, Er Xi still thought well of him. So Er Xi asked Pincai: "Where is that one from before — whose surname I can't remember — the one who was looking at me sidelong? The one who had the silver stolen by Xiao Li? I don't see him around anymore." Pincai said: "I'm now living in the city; I don't have much contact with those friends anymore." Er Xi said: "Where in the city?" Pincai said: "The Hua household." Er Xi exclaimed: "Oh! The Hua household!" He then asked where Zhang Xiaomei lived; Xiaomei said: "I'm in the same residence as he is." Er Xi said: "I hear that at the Hua household they have opera every single day — do they have their own troupe?" Pincai said: "Several troupes, in fact." Er Xi then went around to each person urging wine, with finger-guessing games and drinking forfeits — no end of amusements.

They had been making merry for a while when still neither Rong-guan nor Qiguan had arrived. Xiaomei said: "Strange — today being a memorial day, we can't even summon a young protégé." Er Xi said: "Who else?" Xiaomei said: "Your troupe's Qiguan, and Rong-guan from the Lianzhu troupe." Er Xi said: "Rong-guan — when I was going out I saw him heading to Sanhe Tower; he's probably not done yet." Meichuang said: "That Yulin in your troupe — is he really sick?" Er Xi said: "Yulin! Don't even bring it up — he and Qiguan both had a terrible incident the day before yesterday; it nearly cost someone their life. Their teacher is still insisting on pressing charges. Qiguan can't come today either." Everyone asked what had happened. Er Xi said: "It's quite a story — let me have a couple more cups first." Everyone drained a few more cups. Pincai, hearing that Qiguan had been involved in an incident, asked Er Xi again: "Just tell us — let everyone hear." Er Xi said: "Are you acquainted with a certain Cantonese gentleman called Xi Eleven?" The three said they were not; Feng Zipei said: "I have met this man, but we are not on familiar terms — just say what you have to say." Er Xi said: "This Lord Xi is in the capital as a candidate for office; I heard he brought several tens of thousands of taels of silver with him and wanted to purchase a large post. Somehow it all got used up without reaching the right amount, so he only managed to purchase a district magistracy. This man is truly a lavish spender: when he first came to the capital he got to know Chunlan of the Dengchun troupe, and immediately installed Chunlan in his rooms every day — clothing, gold bracelets, fine carriages, and so on goes without saying. Chunlan has the most stage roles of anyone; for each of Chunlan's performances Xi Eleven had a dozen or so sets of stage costumes made, with gold hairpieces — the total value must be around ten thousand taels. Chunlan's teacher would deliberately call Chunlan back to vex and squeeze Xi Eleven; in drips and drabs, still more was spent. Later, to buy out Chunlan's apprenticeship contract, another five thousand strings of cash were spent; and then Chunlan went to live with him — smoking opium together on the same kang, eating at the same table. For instance, if a dish tasted too salty or too bland to Chunlan, he would smash the whole bowl. A lined fur coat costing several hundred strings of cash — if Chunlan said 'the fur trim is poorly done, I don't want it,' Xi Eleven would look and agree it was not good, and with one hand tear it to shreds and order a new one made. This kind of eccentric spite might be excusable as a personal flaw, but having been so intimate with Chunlan, he should not have gone after others. Yet he paid no heed — anyone he fancied, regardless of whether the other person was willing, he simply forced the issue." Everyone was listening in perfect silence. Pincai said: "I asked you about Yulin and Qiguan's business, and you just keep going on about this naive spendthrift." Er Xi laughed: "Let me tell it in order — in any case it's better than an opera. Now, this man had many ways of luring people. He is from Jiayingzhou, so he had those Western-style methods. His way of enticing people was first to move their hearts with money — though there are also those who are not attracted by money but by personal quality. This Lord Xi's appearance was rough and coarse — very tall and broad, like a military officer — and his speech was also dull. He also smoked opium — an ounce a day — and his face was a greenish-black." Meichuang said: "Get to it — what Western-style methods?" Er Xi said: "He had a wooden barrel, with a narrow mouth and a wide bottom, lacquered in Western style with gilt designs. Inside it went ding-ding-dang-dang, rather like the sound of a clock.

"On top was a lid; inside was a layer of board, and under the board a crossbar; on the outside there was a brass-lock door — you couldn't see anything from outside. Whenever he had his eye on someone and they were being uncooperative, he would lure them into his inner room to look at the barrel. The person, hearing the sound inside, would naturally lean over the barrel to peer in; then Xi Eleven would take some objects — perhaps gold or silver ingots, or jade and kingfisher-feather trinkets, all valuable things — and throw them into the barrel, saying, 'If you can fish them out, they're yours.' The person, of course, not knowing the trick, would reach a hand down. Now, the board in the middle had two holes — a hole just large enough for one hand, no more; so not finding anything, one would reach the other hand in too. Then he would take the key and turn it in the lock door, and both hands could no longer be withdrawn. The barrel was also too heavy to lift; there you were, bent double over it. He would then do as he pleased whether you were willing or not — only when his satisfaction was complete would he release you. Calling out was useless — it had already happened. Once released, there was nothing to be done about it. The savvy ones knew to demand more things from him; there were also those who didn't know better and made a scene — he would turn on them and accuse them of extortion, beat and scold them, and even have them sent to the ward office for processing. Most of the ward officials were in league with him; once delivered, the person was locked up and subjected to all manner of humiliation, and the teacher would have to pay cash to get them out — at minimum a hundred or so strings. Such was Xi Eleven's manner of operating. Now, you ask about Yulin and Qiguan — this was the same Xi Eleven. The first time was at Yulin's place for a meal and drinks. Yulin is an honest, gentle sort — not skilled at flattery. But Xi Eleven took a fancy to him and gave him a set of clothing, a pair of bracelets, and forty strings of cash for his teacher — which worked him into a fever. The second time he invited Yulin alone, without many other guests, and gave Yulin another hundred strings of cash. Yulin couldn't stand the way Xi Eleven looked and always acted cool toward him, which made Xi Eleven annoyed. For the third occasion, his teacher organized many young protégés and invited Yulin again — but this time Yulin simply didn't come. His teacher, still seeing Xi Eleven as a big catch, forced Yulin to go and pay his respects. Xi Eleven then went even further: he apparently had it all planned — he kept Yulin for dinner, poured several cups of wine into him, and then tried to trick him into looking at the barrel. But somehow Yulin had heard about the barrel trick beforehand and refused to look. Having no recourse, Xi Eleven tried to assault him by brute force. Having the advantage of strength, he pinned Yulin down; Yulin refused and wept and screamed. His attendant heard it, and tried to come in to look; but Xi Eleven's people wouldn't let him in. He forced his way in regardless and saw Yulin pinned down with his trousers already pulled off; only the sight of someone entering made Xi Eleven release him, and he had to claim it was only playful teasing — the boy didn't know how to take a joke. Yulin rushed out, straightening his clothes as he went, weeping and cursing; the attendant also made a scene in the gatehouse. Xi Eleven wanted to beat Yulin but couldn't catch up, so in a fury he sent him to the ward office — and that might have been the end of it. But as true ill-luck would have it, more trouble was yet to come. That evening he also summoned Qiguan. You all know Qiguan's temperament — how could he possibly comply? Xi Eleven tried to lure him into looking at the barrel; Qiguan, not knowing about it, fell for the trick and put both hands in — he could not withdraw them; and Xi Eleven tried to use the same method again and started pulling at Qiguan's undergarments. Qiguan understood what was happening and let fly with his leg, landing squarely on that particular organ — Xi Eleven nearly died of pain and sank down in a crouch." At this, Zhang and Wei burst into laughter and said: "Served him right! Served him right! Such a thing must have its heaven-sent retribution. More wine — let's all raise a cup to celebrate." Feng Zipei said nothing; Yang Meichuang said:

"Get on with it." Er Xi drank some wine and continued: "Qiguan was also in a terrible state — his hands couldn't be withdrawn, and he became furious; ignoring the pain, he wrenched and twisted with all his strength until the mechanism was broken. Qiguan's two hands were scraped raw, dripping blood; he too came out weeping and cursing. Xi Eleven, his own vital part in agony, retreated to hide. Qiguan went back and told his teacher; and since he is close to Yuan Baozhu, he also told Baozhu. Baozhu was furious and went into Yiyuan to tell Lord Xu. Lord Xu was outraged and said: 'How can the world contain such a creature — that anyone would tolerate him behaving like this? This is beyond endurance!' He then learned of Yulin's situation as well, and immediately sent someone to the ward office to have Yulin released overnight. At the same time he considered reporting the matter to the Metropolitan Circuit Censor, intending to have Xi Eleven's barrel confiscated and Xi Eleven prosecuted. Lord Xu is a straightforward and upright person; his words were spoken without any attempt at concealment, and somehow word leaked out. Xi Eleven, afraid of a public scandal, and moreover having run out of money, gave up his debts on the western account, rushed to the Board of Personnel and paid to have himself listed as unable to continue due to insufficient travel funds — and left the city. I hear he went to Tianjin; he'll probably hide there for a while and then come back. So Yulin is furious and unwell, and Qiguan's hands are not yet healed — how could he possibly come out? That's the whole story — now you all drink a big cup. My tongue is quite dry."

This tale set everyone laughing and marveling at the strangeness of it. Feng Zipei said: "What a vile, despicable creature — he doesn't care who anyone is; if he sees a young good-looking person he thinks they're fair game. And he doesn't even look at himself." Meichuang laughed: "You're so indignant — can it be that you've already had a look at his precious barrel?" Zipei spat at Meichuang twice. Meichuang said: "Can they make that kind of barrel here in the capital, or not?" Xiaomei laughed: "Are you thinking of copying the design?" Meichuang gave a little laugh. Pincai laughed and said to Er Xi: "You've described it so clearly — you must have had a look at the barrel yourself." Er Xi's face reddened, and with a sideways glance he reached over to pinch Pincai's mouth. Meichuang said: "He probably doesn't need to use the barrel." Er Xi then gave Meichuang two pinches and said: "Those of us who work in this trade — if you have money, everything is fine; why go to all that bother with such theatrics? It's a pity they can't read feng shui the way you can — that's why they had to suffer like that."

Having said this, Er Xi laughed himself. Pincai thought privately: "Who would have expected that Qinguan and Qiguan, even as performers, should be so proud and dignified — so steadfast in their principles. That is something to marvel at." He then asked Er Xi who exactly this Xi Eleven was — how he could behave so lawlessly and still have so much money. Er Xi said: "I heard from Chunlan that he too is the son of a well-born family — his grandfather served as a Provincial Administration Commissioner, and his father is currently serving as a Brigade Commander; his family runs a foreign trading firm." Pincai said: "What about Chunlan now?" Er Xi said: "Went out with him." The group then talked and joked some more, drank another round. Seeing that the day was growing late, Xiaomei and Pincai both needed to return to the city and settled the bill. Meichuang arranged with Er Xi to settle the payment the next day. Meichuang declined to accompany Pincai and the others into the city — since he lived outside the city, he went back to Zipei's place, and the two of them had a smoke of opium together; then Meichuang dragged Zipei off to Jade Celestial Fairy's establishment in Vermilion Cosmetics Lane.

Now let us speak of Pan Qiguan: since being tricked and humiliated by Huifang, he had been genuinely vexed, wishing to settle scores with Huifang but fearing his wide connections and numerous supporters. At the same time his lascivious urge had not been extinguished and he still hoped to actually get what he wanted. He was also smarting over the two hundred strings of cash, and had begun to suspect that Zhang Zhongyu had colluded with Huifang to make a fool of him, so he made veiled, insinuating remarks to Zhongyu. Zhongyu, having been unjustly blamed, truly had nowhere to air his grievance, and came to resent Pan Qiguan. "Since he suspects me anyway, I might as well dig a proper pit for him — I'll scheme with Huifang to deal with him." Pan Qiguan then had to go to Baoding to inspect some fabric shops he owned there and was away for two months before returning. Idle with nothing to do, he had a series of quarrels with his wife and was suffering from pent-up frustration with no way to relieve it. He thought again of going to Huifang's place for amusement, but this time without Zhang Zhongyu — he came alone.

It was already evening that day; as chance would have it, he arrived at Huifang's gate at the very moment Huifang was returning from Chunhang's place. Huifang, seeing it was Pan Qiguan, was inwardly startled but had no choice — he jumped down from his carriage and invited the Third Master Pan inside. Pan Qiguan seized Huifang's hand and puffed along into the inner room, where they sat down in the reception room. Huifang said: "Third Master Pan — I haven't seen you for days. Where have you been making your fortune? You rarely honor us with a visit. I recall that the other day, because the young master Hua was staying outside the city, I was summoned there and was truly remiss in attending to you — please don't hold it against me. We have plenty of time ahead of us to be friends." Pan Qiguan, seeing Huifang so warm and gracious, felt half his former indignation dissolve, and said slowly: "What have I come here for — I know perfectly well you look down on me. Two hundred strings of cash, and all I got was old Zhang Number Two spitting a mouthful of wine in my face. The rabbit hides in its burrow while the wildcat can only drool and dream." Huifang heard these words and was quite annoyed, but had to put on a smile and say: "What interesting things you say. So what are we going to do tonight? Let us find somewhere to sit together." Pan Qiguan said: "What other place is there to look for? Right here is very comfortable. But I have sworn an oath and given up drinking — today I won't have a single drop." Huifang heard this and grew more anxious still, thinking: today is truly bad — here he is alone, not even willing to drink, and he certainly won't be willing to leave. If I make some excuse to go, he probably won't let me. She turned it over and over in her mind, and a flush gradually spread across her face. After a long silent pause she made up her mind defiantly: "I might as well let him stay — if I were afraid of him, I wouldn't be called Su Huifang." She said: "Third Master, if you won't drink wine, at least you'll eat dinner." Pan Qiguan nodded; Huifang personally went to the kitchen for a while, then brought out the meal: three meat dishes, three vegetable dishes, a bowl of Shaoxing soup, and a pot of yellow wine.

Huifang said: "Even if you've given up wine, since you've come to my place you must keep up appearances." With a smiling face, she took up a large jade cup, filled it to the brim, and presented it with both hands. Pan Qiguan had not actually given up drinking — he simply feared wine would impair his plans. Seeing Huifang's manner now, he could not help grinning and saying: "All right — finish this whole pot, no refills." Huifang paid no attention to his conditions; the two drank together, also eating some boiled dumplings and similar fare. Pan Qiguan was becoming tipsy; as the cups went back and forth and another pot was added, he gazed at Huifang's peach-blossom cheeks and autumn-water eyes — glances full of allure, a hundred seductive expressions — which revived all Pan Qiguan's old nature. He sighed: "You little creature, you'll truly be the death of me — what are two hundred strings of cash? You have no need to do me such harm! Son — as long as you show me just a little heart, there's nothing I won't agree to." Huifang forced a smile and said: "Third Master — I don't understand. What do you mean, agree or not agree?" Pan Qiguan said: "As long as you have heart for me, whatever you want I'll agree to." Huifang said with a laugh: "You may not really be willing — what I want, what I want is a silver exchange house (yin hao). You yourself said so before." Pan Qiguan said: "I have three silver houses; and I'm already forty-eight years old, still with no sons — to give you one is no great matter. And what will you give me?" Huifang said nothing. Pan Qiguan said: "Why have you gone quiet again? It's just the two of us — no outsiders here. Is there anything that can't be said?" Huifang continued to give that not-quite-smiling, not-quite-unsmiling expression and said nothing. Pan Qiguan moved closer and pulled Huifang into his embrace; with his sticky, syrup-soaked face, he tried to press it against those fragrant, powder-warm cheeks. Huifang interposed a hand lightly and softly said: "You're being too impossible — let go, and then I'll talk." Pan Qiguan rubbed his face back and forth on the back of Huifang's hand, panting: "Good boy, good darling — just say it." Huifang put on an expression of feigned annoyance: "Third Master, with you being so impatient, I simply won't say it now." Pan Qiguan had no choice but to release his hold. Huifang's hand was wet with saliva; she wiped it with a handkerchief and stood up, saying with composure: "Third Master Pan, I am no fool — do you think I can't see through what's on your mind? But since I am a performer, I cannot pretend to speak of purity and cleanliness. Still, I am only eighteen years old this year and I have just completed my apprenticeship; out there in the world, I ask you to leave me some dignity and let people regard me as a person — please don't harass me this way. If I have heart for you, that should be enough — don't let people see through it. Don't treat me like the disciples of a barber's shed. Third Master, you think I spent two hundred strings of your cash and you begrudge it — well, if you want it back, I can repay it." Pan Qiguan said: "Good boy — who wants you to repay money? Don't blame me: I've been thinking about this for a full six months, and you won't let me feel just a little ease. If you truly have heart for me, that's fine — but I still fear you're just taking me for a fool again. If you try to trick me again, I won't stand for it. But whatever you say, I'll go along with it."

Huifang laughed again: "I just said, Third Master is used to frequenting the barber's sheds — you're treating my place as just the same kind of thing. I heard from Zhang Zhongyu that Third Master Pan is most generous: if he sets his heart on someone, not only three or five hundred, even one or two thousand strings he'll spend freely. How is it that Third Master also takes a liking to me, yet after spending a mere two hundred strings here you're already trying to recoup your investment? As for two hundred strings, not only would Third Master not look twice at it, even I, surnamed Su, don't consider it anything at all. Could it be that Third Master, for a cup of wine and a song, wouldn't even tip a hundred or so strings? Like those small-time operators who call a young protégé for a whole day and give four or five strings of Beijing cash? Let me tell you this: as long as you can truly show heart, I guarantee I won't let you down. But don't forget me — don't treat me as a lowly person, so that once you've had your way you drop me and go chasing someone else. Then don't blame me." Pan Qiguan, having been worked over by Huifang's speech, was left with nothing to say. Hearing line after line of apparent agreement, yet with the implied sense of wanting more money and that two hundred strings was too little. And then he thought again: "Such a celebrated young protégé — naturally doesn't come easily to hand." He said to Huifang: "If you truly won't let me down, I can put my mind at ease. But words are no guarantee — later I fear you'll change your tune again." Huifang laughed coldly: "You mistrust me a thousand times and ten thousand times — what, do you want me to write you a contract?"

At this moment Pan Qiguan's lascivious heart was blazing; and yet he also wanted to appear generous, so he couldn't resort to brute force. Thinking it over, the only recourse was to win Huifang over with more money. He said: "I know you're a free-spending young protégé with a big style, always needing cash. Starting today, whenever you're short of money, just come to my shop and take what you need." Huifang said: "How could I go there? We can't let Third Mistress find out — she'd give you such a scolding that you'd suffer all over again." Pan Qiguan laughed: "Nonsense — tell me honestly: are you actually short of money or not?" Huifang thought: "This creature has been cut short by me and still doesn't understand — he still imagines he can buy me with money. I might as well milk this muddled fool for some — it can serve as lamp-oil money for Tian darling." With a smile she said: "Money? Of course I want money — it's just awkward to say so. I fear Third Master would suspect that I'm always taking money and giving nothing in return — spending a great deal and getting nothing." As she spoke, she looked down at the kingfisher-jade bracelet on her own wrist, then took it off and showed it to Pan Qiguan: "Tell me — is this jade any good?" Pan Qiguan looked: it was clear as water and entirely green in color. He praised it: "Fine kingfisher-jade — rarely seen in the city. It must be Yunnan-origin jade." Huifang said: "It was a gift from Lord Xu at Yiyuan — one of four identical pieces he gave to four people; I received one. I heard it was bought in Guangdong; one piece was priced at one foreign silver dollar." Pan Qiguan clicked his tongue: "More expensive than gold — ten liang of gold weighs as much but is worth only two hundred silver." Huifang said: "Fine as it is, it's a pity there's no gold piece to match it." As she spoke, her eyes kept glancing at a heavy gold bangle on Pan Qiguan's wrist. Pan Qiguan understood; he wanted to give it to her, but it weighed ten liang and was worth two hundred silver, and his heart ached to part with it; yet if he didn't give it, he feared she would look down on him — so he said nothing.

He raised his arm and studied the bracelet for a while, then said to Huifang: "Pair it with this one and it would look perfect — if you want it, take it." He kept raising his arm but made no move to remove it. Huifang moved close beside him, thanked him, and began to take the bracelet off his wrist; the moment it was slipped onto Huifang's own hand, Pan Qiguan grabbed her around the waist, murmuring endless endearments — "my heart, my darling" — nuzzling and sniffing at her face incessantly, and then began groping and pawing. This time Huifang was truly in a difficult position; saying anything more would be useless, as Pan Qiguan was past listening. He had clearly resolved not to leave empty-handed today; and having parted with the gold bracelet, he felt his face was quite bright and shining with it. Huifang could only put on a smiling expression; noticing a small ivory comb hanging from his lapel, she took it and began combing his beard. At this critical moment, they suddenly heard from outside someone calling out: "Is Huifang at home?" Then another voice: "The old father has come!" There seemed to be the sound of many footsteps. Huifang hurriedly broke free and said: "Something's wrong — the ward official is here to do his nightly inspection!" Pan Qiguan was a wealthy merchant; at the words "ward official's nightly inspection" he was instantly agitated and looked for somewhere to hide. Huifang said: "There's nowhere to hide — you'd better just go now, rather than running into them and being questioned up and down, which would look quite bad." Pan Qiguan had no choice; just at the moment when he had gotten his hands on something, he was scattered away again. He could only slink out through the dark and make a dash for the main gate. As for who the actual caller was — let the following chapter tell the tale.
