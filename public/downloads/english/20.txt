Chapter 20 — Dragon boats race for the championship; paired lovers pass cups through a playful wine game.

The previous chapter told how Pan Qiguan had Huifang cornered at the crucial moment when someone suddenly came shouting, and Huifang feigned alarm and cried: "Dreadful — it's the ward officials come to do their inspection!" Pan Qiguan was a wealthy man of timid nature, naturally afraid of causing trouble, and had no choice but to slink away.

In fact, when Huifang had gone to the kitchen earlier, he had calculated that Pan Qiguan would certainly not let him off tonight, and so had instructed the household servants to pretend to be ward officials conducting the nightly inspection, and had also brought in two actual ward constables; at the moment when Pan Qiguan's harassment became intolerable, they were to come in shouting. Knowing that Pan Qiguan's lust was great but his nerve small, the scheme worked perfectly and he left tricked once more. Though she had obtained his gold bracelet, Huifang was still genuinely troubled in her heart, fearing he would come back the next day. The only recourse was to move to Chunhang's lodgings and hide for a few days, then see. Pan Qiguan went home in low spirits; fortunately he was afraid of his wife and dared not cause scenes openly outside the home — otherwise, even clever as Huifang was, it might have been hard to hold him off. Today had once more come to nothing; the only plan was to gradually win her over with more money and buy her heart around.

This chapter now turns to speak of several men of elegant tastes engaged in an affair of elegant taste. The company of celebrated men had by now gradually thinned. Since Ziyu's one meeting with Qinyan at Yiyuan, they had never managed to see each other again.

Qinyan's illness came and went; he was not appearing in the troupe's productions, and only occasionally, forcing himself despite illness, would he go to Yiyuan for a visit.

Meanwhile Ziyu's own illness had also arisen from melancholy and worry; sometimes when he went to Yiyuan, Qinyan happened not to have come; sometimes when he went to Qinyan's lodgings, there was always some inconvenience — either his teacher Changqing was entertaining guests, or someone was sitting there. He also could not easily go to Sulan's again; Ziyu felt utterly helpless and could only mourn the grudging lot of fate. Qinyan, at home, did not know how many times Ziyu had come by; and hearing that Ziyu was ill, he felt all the more heartsick — yet since he had never once called at the Mei residence, it was awkward for him to go on his own initiative. Thus a deep and tender feeling of admiration and longing had nowhere to be eased; all he could do was sigh short sighs and long sighs, and let it haunt his dreams. You may wonder, reader, why when Ziyu went to find Qinyan, his teacher never tried to bring them together. First, Ziyu was only an occasional visitor — not one of those who regularly kept company outside — and so Changqing did not know him well, and not knowing what position Ziyu held, took him for no more than a young student with no great usefulness. Second, Ziyu had not spent a single cash on Qinyan. The spiritual sympathy between Ziyu and Qinyan being of a purely ideal and heartfelt kind, neither of them had thought about this dimension. Changqing, on the other hand, thought only about money and not at all about quality of person. That is simply how those of the inferior dan-actor class are — such was Changqing's nature, unchanging from beginning to end.

Let us speak of Ziyu: he was at home recuperating from his illness and not going out at all. Gao Pin had been invited by the prefect of Hejian, Lord Hu, to assist in compiling a local gazetteer. Liu Wenze's mother-in-law had been missing him and had come to fetch him along with his wife née Wu to the office of the Viceroy of Zhili. So three of the group were gone; only Ziyun, Cixian, Nanxiang, Zhongqing, Chunhang, and Wang Xun — six people — continued to meet from time to time.

One day, Ziyun and Cixian invited the celebrated gentlemen to the garden to view the dragon boat and enjoy the pomegranate blossoms. This day was the first of the fifth month, which happened also to be the Intendant-General Wang's birthday; though no formal celebration was held, a number of close friends, relatives, and colleagues came to offer their congratulations, and indoors there were also ladies — such as Lady Yan from the Mei household and Madam Lu from the Sun household — so it was quite festive. Wang Xun and Zhongqing were thus unable to attend the Yiyuan appointment. On learning of this, Ziyun and Cixian also went to offer birthday congratulations; there they happened to find Nanxiang and Chunhang both present, so they invited them to come back with them. Zhongqing and Wang Xun said that if the guests dispersed early, they would also come to keep the appointment.

"But don't wait for us — we may be early or late." Cixian and the others agreed and returned to Yiyuan. Coming together to the foot of the steep rock face at the garden's entrance, Ziyun invited everyone to change out of their formal attire. He also said: "The day is very hot and the red sun beats down on people; moreover the dragon boat is at the Chanting-Autumn Water Pavilion, and the pomegranate blossoms are at Little Red Citadel — both are quite far from here. Better to ride over." The servants had already prepared horses in advance and brought them forward; the four mounted. From below the steep rock face they turned left, going up and down over a winding and twisting path along a small sheep-track of blue stone, with old vines brushing their heads and fragrant grasses catching their clothing. When the mountain path was done, they followed the enclosure wall; on the other side was the rippling surface of the pond, red and green reflected in the water, with a short red railing running along the embankment, slender bamboo and drooping willows, and assorted flowers covering the trees in abundance, orioles flying and calling here and there — enough to wash away all worldly care. Less than half a li further, another mass of towering stones had been piled into a high hill over ten zhang in height — like a single peak of Luofu overlooking the curve of the sea — blocking the way ahead.

Ziyun invited the guests to dismount; they walked up stone steps from the foot of the hill, over thirty tiers, to a small pavilion with a stone table and stone benches inside, called the "Misty Void Pavilion" (缥缈亭). Looking across, several dozen old pines, their dark color rising to the sky, cut off the view; at their tips one could just make out a glimmer of green tiles and a corner of a vermilion tower. Here one felt a fine breeze drifting and the water and stones cool and clean — a floating sensation as of rising above the void. Chunhang said: "How wondrous and profound! Even if a literary imagination were to come here, even the Incense Burner Peak of Mount Lu would offer nothing superior." Nanxiang said: "The earlier view resembled Wang Lutai's style; this reminds one of Xiao Chizhu — secluded and deep without being narrow or precipitous." Ziyun said: "Both were designed by Jingyi's hand; when laying out the arrangements, the plan went through several revisions." Cixian said: "Those several old pines are really what makes it — without them, one glance would exhaust the entire view." Chunhang said: "One can only wonder how much landscape lies in Master Jingyi's imagination — truly a divine hand that commands mountains and rivers. Ni Yunlin and Xu Qingteng would certainly want to take him by the arm into the woods." Cixian could only respond with a few modest disclaimers. The four rested briefly, then descended the stone steps. On the side was a two-story loft of five bays, arranged at varying heights — neither quite a tower nor quite a pavilion — with painted beams soaring in the clouds and pearl curtains rolled up against the rain; a different kind of spectacle altogether. Beyond the railing outside the windows lay a square pond of about ten mu, its surface covered in overlapping lotus leaves, half of them already grown into canopies. In the center was a six-curve red bridge, angled and slanted, connecting a row of a dozen or so rooms and pavilions on the opposite side. To the right were moored several small painted boats, all with brocade cables, ivory-white sides, and orchid oars of cassia-wood. Cixian said: "That over there is the Chanting-Autumn Water Pavilion (吟秋水榭)." Looking again at the water pavilion, it was three stories high; on the left ran an unbroken line of willows drooping to the water's surface, connecting to the bamboo groves and long woods of the opposite bank, the two shores seeming to want to meet.

Ziyun asked the guests to hold back and not cross the bridge yet, but first to enter this loft. It was a neat set of three central bays, with curtains strung from fine copper wire, water-polished nanmu carved railings, and the furnishings inside of uncommon refinement — precious tripods and jade-stone zithers, jeweled tables and marble desks beyond enumeration. By the railing was placed an antique bronze water-clock, with several bamboo arrows inserted in it; suspended from the center was a plaque reading: "A Studio of Gathering Clouds and Exchanging Rain" (停云叙雨之斋). Beside it was a couplet reading: "Prostrating before the rock, there are times one dons formal robes and hat; / Looking at the clouds, everywhere one becomes a celestial immortal." The signature read: Huaguang Suxiu. Nanxiang exclaimed in surprise: "This is Young Master Hua's calligraphy — I had no idea his literary arts were like this." Ziyun said: "Young Master Hua has exceptionally high natural ability — his effort has only been somewhat shallow, which is also due to being misled by his position and circumstances. In terms of calligraphy, painting, poetry, and lyrics — he runs rather counter to what one might expect from his station." Chunhang said: "If one judged only by what circulates in common gossip, one would nearly miss this person entirely. From this couplet alone one sees the refinement of his mind; from this calligraphy alone one sees the gallantry of his spirit." As they were speaking, two flower-decked boats appeared in the distance, gliding out from the water pavilion — white hulls and green curtains still separated by the red bridge and green willows, the lazy creak of soft oars, and the graceful tune of a lotus-picking song. The water bright as a mirror, pavilions reflected in the water, swallows skimming low, fish blowing at the surface — and in an instant the boats had passed the red bridge and drawn slowly, slowly in to moor. One could now see Wang Lanbao with his silk robe hem lifted, his pigtail wound up, a pomegranate blossom tucked sideways at his temple, holding a small purple-bamboo pole, punting and at the same time driving away the domesticated ducks and wild ducks, which startled the mandarin ducks and water birds into a scatter of flight. Then a white egret came swooping straight at the railing, and reaching the eaves with one tilt of its wings, had already flown up to the rocky ridge.

Cixian laughed: "The proverb about 'catching the duck and startling the mandarin ducks' — today one sees it in action." While everyone was watching with interest, they saw figures stepping out of the boats like blossoming flowers: one boat held Baozhu and Shufang, the other Huifang and Sulan — five people in all. The boatmen moored the craft against the railing; Nanxiang said: "The hibiscus has not yet opened, and the water pavilion lacks a certain brilliance. With all this company of flowers making the crossing, it will no longer be desolate. Xiang Consorts and Luo Goddesses sporting on the river's edge — I imagine Duxiang-xiong would receive them with his jade pendant offering." Everyone laughed. All the young dan actors came up and made their greetings all around. Cixian said: "Look at the way Jingfang's narrow-sleeve movement stops and starts with an ease and flow — it really brings out an air of free and graceful charm. Zhujun's praising words — 'Gliding like a startled wild goose, swaying like a wandering dragon' — one feels truly captures the essence." Nanxiang said: "Jingfang's free-spirited ease, Meixiang's lively wit, Yaoqing's soft grace, Shouxiang's prettily quiet refinement, Xiangwan's rich elegance — all are celestial immortals become human. As for their artistry — Zhao Feiyan dancing on the palm, Zhang Jingwan singing within the curtains — these could perhaps give some idea of it." Ziyun invited the guests onto the boats; Nanxiang and the others stepped aboard. The prow was carved with a pair of mandarin ducks neck to neck; the boat's body was of crabapple-pear wood, with short red railings on both sides, glass windows running the length of the interior, and on the canopy a great rolled pavilion of green clay-dyed cloth with scattered flowers, with painted-flower silk in white hanging down from both sides. Inside were two cabins; the floor was laid with fine white velvet carpet; the aft section also had long windows; in the center was a bed-platform with Hu-style rattan chairs on each side interspersed with tea tables; a round table in the center could seat five or six people. The second, slightly smaller boat was for the attendants. By now Wang Lanbao had already changed his clothes and was sitting there quietly and decorously. Baozhu said to Nanxiang: "Did you call at Lord Wang's this morning?" Before Nanxiang could answer, Ziyun said: "I invited them from Wang's residence directly."

So everyone chatted and talked, looking at the garden scenery along the way. In several places soaring lofts rose to the clouds, carved ridgepoles overlooking the ground below; in several places towering crags jutted out starkly, with ancient trees and gnarled branches. They could also see, to the northwest, a long gallery that was the Peach Grove (Taohu); next to it was the Apricot Village (Xinglun); to the due north, looking through the bamboo grove, was the Pear Courtyard (Liyuan), behind which was the Peony Fragrant Kingdom (Mudan Xiangguo); to the northeast was a stretch of delicate and ingenious rock-work, below which were thousands of trees in green shade, with golden globes — the plum fruits hanging thick, half green and half yellow — covering the Plum Vale (Mei Yan) so it could not be clearly seen. The opposite shore was thickly overgrown with trees and rocks; how many more pavilions and courts it concealed one could not know. Chunhang asked Nanxiang: "How many parts of this garden have you toured so far?" Nanxiang said: "I've been here many times, but I've never actually toured it properly. A drinking session takes the whole day — when would one find time to stroll about? I've been to perhaps seven or eight places." Baozhu said: "I have toured it completely with Shouxiang." Huifang said: "I haven't been to Osmanthus Ridge, Chrysanthemum Patch, or Orchid Path; all the rest I've seen."

Sulan said: "Osmanthus Ridge is in front of the front hill; Orchid Path and Chrysanthemum Patch are behind the back hill. You cross the ravine into a large open space where there is a village-style compound — that's the Chrysanthemum Patch. Orchid Path is on the hillside, going from the foot of the hill up to the halfway point, with high and low galleries and winding paths — a most delightful place to explore. Beyond Chrysanthemum Patch there is also a Rice Farm, with shadoofs drawing water, looking like a genuine village — fishing nets, crab weirs, everything. There are also two craneenclosures and a deer pen nearby."

As they finished speaking, the boat had traveled over half a li to a bend, and the pond opened out into a spacious expanse. The Chanting-Autumn Water Pavilion was built out in the water, surrounded on all four sides by the pond, standing three stories tall. The first story had twelve bays arranged in a hexagonal pattern, with windows open on every face — purely glass-inlaid carved windows — partitioned into six sections. Each section was further divided into yin-yang, bright-and-dim arrangements, making twelve subdivisions in all, each varying in shape — square, round, flat, and angled — each with its own setting. The furnishings inside were beyond description. Looking from one room through to the next, only a pane of glass separated them, yet to actually get from one to the other required winding through several turns. The company roamed about this first story for quite a long while. Ziyun said: "The guests must be hungry by now; it is nearly noon — shall we be seated?" Four small pages were seen carrying in four gold-lacquered trays with several bowls of apricot-kernel porridge, which were distributed to each person. Chunhang said: "Why not go up to the upper two stories and then come back to sit?" So they turned up the stairway to the second story, which was somewhat smaller — four-sided in design, with an open corridor running around it with railings on all sides, and carved windows partitioning it into eight sections, with antique vessels and utensils of equal refinement. Looking northeast at the willow shade, one could see the dragon boat moored there — over three zhang in height; the hull was carved and painted in the form of a green dragon, with five or six tiers of framework built in the center, made entirely of five-colored silk, brocade, velvet, and clay, fashioned into parasols, canopies, and pennants — embroidered with scattered-thread, flat-gold, and knotted-stitch flowers of every variety, set off with peacock-gilded parasols, pearl parasols, and parasols threaded from silver needles. In the center were constructed paper-cut towers and pavilions, royal palaces and Buddhist temples, adorned with scenes from famous historical sites. Every figure inside moved by pulleys — the mechanical ingenuity was extraordinary. Layer upon layer of construction, a blaze of brocade and cloud, glorious as bright flowers and blazing flames. Atop it all stood a constructed golden peacock; the boat's bottom was weighted with stones; twenty-four rowers on both sides propelled it, with a band of eight musical tones inside — drums, gongs, strings, and winds, both the coarse ten-beat and the refined varieties. This design had been seen by Cixian in Jiangsu and drawn in a sketch from which artisans had been engaged to build it. Chunhang had come from the south and had also seen something like it before; he said: "Truly magnificently made — even the dragon boats of Changzhou Prefecture, the finest in the entire province, are no better than this."

They then all went up to the third story, which was three-sided in plan, with an outer corridor on three sides as well, the interior partitioned into six sections. Here all the windows, storage units, and doors were a uniform fragrant nanmu wood, exceedingly old and simple in style — even more refined and tranquil than below. The elevation being higher, the atmosphere was cooler and more refreshing; leaning on the railing and looking out, eight or nine tenths of the entire garden of Yiyuan could be taken in at a glance, only the shaded side of the hills remaining out of view. One felt only layer upon layer of pavilions and towers, trees and flowers spread in gentle profusion, fragrant grass like a green carpet laid flat, clear springs flowing like quicksilver, water winding like ribbons, hills arranged in the relations of host and guest — where much, not overcrowded; where little, not sparse. A natural painting — the Wangchuan scroll was no finer than this. The product of human ingenuity — the Pingquan Villa need not even be mentioned. The company each leaned at the railing to gaze in all directions; only the sound of xiao flutes and drums from the dragon boat came drifting, as its green-wave rowers rowed it out in stately procession.

At the stern of the dragon boat hung a swing frame, with two children swinging up and down. Cixian said: "Please, let's go back down to look — looking from above down is not as fine as looking from below up." Everyone descended the goose-step stairway back to the first story, where they found a round table already set out in the front corridor of the central bays. This gathering was four host-and-guest gentlemen and five beauties. Ziyun was about to change into formal dress, but Cixian, Tian, and the three others stopped him; so they sent round the wine in informal attire, seating by seniority. To the east sat Nanxiang; Ziyun asked Lanbao to sit at his shoulder. To the west sat Chunhang, with Huifang at his shoulder. At the head sat Cixian, with Shufang at his shoulder.

Ziyun took the host's seat, with Sulan and Baozhu on either side. The table talk was the usual sort; let us not linger over it.

The dragon boat had now drawn up before the pavilion and was circling round and round, coming and going without end.

Servants at a distance set off a string of five-thousand-cracker "all-red hundred-seeds" — the sound unceasing. Everyone was watching and cheering when suddenly four men appeared at the railing outside, wearing short green-lacquered silk jackets and red-lacquered silk trousers, bare-chested with sashes at the waist, red scarves bound at their foreheads, barefoot in straw sandals, their legs wrapped tightly in blue cloth. They lined up at the railing and performed a full bow toward those above. Nanxiang did not understand the meaning; he was about to ask when a drumbeat sounded from the dragon boat, and all four men simultaneously turned somersaults and plunged into the water. Ziyun said: "These foolish fellows — they want to show off some skill too." He ordered servants to catch several pairs of ducks, and also told them to bring several red-lacquered gourds to throw into the water; then everyone understood it was a competition to seize the prize. The servants obeyed, first throwing a white duck into the water; the duck, hitting the water, gave a flip of its head, turned a somersault itself, then stretched its neck, flapped its wings, and quacked qua-qua-qua several times.

One of the men pressed himself flat on the water's surface, kicked both legs, and shot across like a shuttle. The duck, seeing someone coming to catch it, fanned its wings and, half-submerged and half-floating, sped away. Just as it was going, a head suddenly emerged from the water, and one hand seized the duck. Ziyun said: "Well done! Make a note to reward him." Three more ducks and two gourds were thrown in together. The four men competed with each other to excel, displaying every skill: now face-down, now face-up, now sinking, now surfacing, now turning sideways, now lifting a leg — swimming and playing without end. Some caught their prizes, some could not; some caught and then lost hold; some caught halfway and had their prize seized by another — drawing laughter and delighted shouts from everyone at the table, and each person drank several cups. The young dan actors were even more excited and all came out from the table to lean at the railing and watch the performance.

Ziyun called them back inside and poured fresh cups. Cixian said: "Shall we use this as the basis of a drinking game today?" Everyone asked: "How would the game work?" Cixian asked the servants: "Those large gourds that were cultivated in the garden last year — I imagine you still have some." The servants replied: "There are a dozen or so lacquered ones; the rest are unlacquered." Cixian told them to bring the lacquered ones. Before long, the servants brought a large string of them; the ropes were undone and they were placed on an empty side table. Cixian then asked for the set of wine-forfeit tallies; the servants brought up a set of ivory tallies. Cixian drew out several at random, placed them face-down on the table with the blank side up, and said to everyone: "Each person take one tally at random — don't look at the written side — and inscribe your own name on it." Everyone did as he said.

Cixian then lifted the lid of a gourd, placed one tally inside each, screwed the lid back on tight, and instructed the pages to throw them into the water. "Whoever's gourd is retrieved — that is the person who must drink. This is a perfectly fair amusement." Everyone agreed: "Excellent — but what does it say on the tallies?" Cixian said: "This set of tallies takes its figures from the Water Margin — each character has a drinking episode associated with them. I drew them at random; I don't know exactly which names have come out." Ziyun laughed: "These tallies would also be good fun — the drinking would be hearty. Only, among them there are a few characters with large capacity; drawing one of those would really put one to the test." Everyone said: "There is nothing to do but submit to fate." One gourd was seen retrieved from the water; a page brought it to the table, dried it with a towel, opened the lid, and poured out the tally — it was Xiao Cixian's. Everyone looked at the other side, which had seven large characters and two lines of small text as annotation. The large characters read: "Li Kui Creates a Great Disturbance at Xunyang River." The annotation read: "The first two seats are Song Jiang and Dai Zong; the last seat is Zhang Shun. Li Kui drinks one large cup by himself; Song and Dai accompany with one small cup; then Li Kui finger-guesses ten rounds with Zhang Shun. If Li Kui wins, Zhang Shun drinks; if Zhang Shun wins, Li Kui drinks plain water." Everyone laughed. Cixian first drained the opening cup; Nanxiang and Chunhang accompanied with one cup each. Then he finger-guessed with Ziyun; Ziyun drank six cups of wine, Cixian drank four cups of plain water. Everyone said: "Quite entertaining." Then another was retrieved; the tally was Nanxiang's. The inscription read: "Wu Song Drunkenly Seizes the Happy Forest." The annotation read: "Without three cups you cannot cross the ridge — first drain three full cups. The person opposite is Jiang Menshen; win three rounds in a row to pass, then play a general round." Nanxiang said: "This one is too much — I'll drink the three cups, but let the general round be waived." Ziyun said: "Waiving is not allowed; and moreover you have a great capacity." Lanbao said: "For the general round, perhaps use half a cup, or split one cup into three portions." Everyone agreed. Nanxiang drank three cups, then began finger-guessing with Chunhang, and actually won three rounds in a row; then played out the general round; twelve cups of wine in all.

Then two more were retrieved from the water. Opening the first: it was the character for Xu Ziyun. The inscription read: "Song Jiang Kills Yan Poxixi in a Rage." The annotation: "Drink two cups; the person seated beside is Yan Poxixi. Song Jiang first drinks one cup himself, then urges one cup on Yan Poxixi; if Yan Poxixi refuses, Song Jiang drinks it himself.

"" Ziyun laughed: "Who is Yan Poxixi among us?" Everyone laughed. Cixian said: "No need to guess — it's the two sitting right beside you; and moreover, you still drink it yourself. There's no need for them, but you still have to invite them." Ziyun laughed and drank one cup, then offered a cup to Sulan: "Xiangwan — you are a good person; you mustn't imitate that Yan Poxixi, who only had Zhang Sanlang in his heart and paid attention to nothing else. Just drink this cup." This set everyone laughing. Sulan was originally going to drink, but the laughter made a flush rise on his face, and he pursed his lips toward Baozhu and said: "Yan Poxixi is over there — ask him to drink." Baozhu also gave a titter. Ziyun then took a cup and said to Baozhu: "What do you say — will you drink?" Baozhu took the cup, turned to Sulan, and said: "You fell into the trap — look at the tally: Yan Poxixi is the one who doesn't drink; if you drank, you wouldn't be Yan Poxixi." And he drained the cup. Sulan thought about it and realized that Baozhu had outwitted him. Now the second tally was looked at — it was Huifang's. When everyone saw it they burst out laughing; only Tian Chunhang managed to suppress his laughter, though his face showed some color. As it happened, this tally fell to Huifang — which was rather a case of karmic retribution for the Pan Qiguan business. The inscription read: "Pan Jinlian Flirts with Her Brother-in-Law on a Snowy Day." The annotation: "Three cups; the person seated to the left is Wu Song. For the first cup, one must reveal the chest, place one hand on Wu Song's shoulder, and say: 'Brother-in-law, drink this cup.' For the second cup, one must first drink half oneself, then say: 'Brother-in-law, if you have feeling for me, drink the other half.' For the third cup, one must stand up, feign an expression of anger, and drink it oneself; the whole table accompanies with three cups." At this Huifang flatly refused, saying: "Let's finger-guess away these three cups." Ziyun said: "The tally makes it perfectly clear — it was retrieved from the water; how can it be changed?" Cixian said: "And moreover you yourself wrote it on the tally — how can you go back on it now?" Nanxiang said: "If you're going to change the game, why did we follow ours as prescribed?" Huifang had no choice and hesitated for a long time. Lanbao laughed: "Retribution comes swiftly — now you're truly going to play Pan Jinlian's role for real." The company did not entirely understand, assuming Lanbao meant only the Pan Jinlian on the tally — not knowing that Lanbao had heard about the Pan Qiguan business. Chunhang understood perfectly and only lowered his head without speaking. Huifang, hearing this, blushed all the more and paid no attention; he had no choice but to take a cup of wine, stand up, lean toward Baozhu, and say: "Brother-in-law, do drink this cup." Baozhu was in the middle of eating some vegetable when Huifang's unexpected address caught him off guard, and he spat his mouthful all over the table with laughter, leaned against Ziyun, wiped his mouth with a handkerchief, and still could not stop laughing. Everyone broke into uproarious laughter. Huifang was at a complete loss; he put down the cup and started laughing himself. Cixian said: "Meixiang — another mistake. The annotation says the person seated to the left is Wu Song — not the person on the right. How is it you've offered this cup to Yaoqing?" Huifang said: "After all, who do you want me to offer it to? Isn't he seated beside me?" Baozhu said: "I don't quite count as beside you. Though it's a round table, I'm facing north and you're facing east — I certainly didn't expect you to address me as 'brother-in-law.'" And he laughed again. Huifang still refused to comply. Ziyun laughed: "Meixiang — can it be that you've never offered Xiangfan a cup of wine? Quickly, quickly! Look — two more have been retrieved. If you break the game rules, what will happen after this? In any case it's a one-time thing — it won't be your turn two or three times in a row." Cixian said: "Be quick and offer the wine!" Nanxiang said: "When Pan Jinlian was flirting with her brother-in-law in the old story, she had to show some seductive manner to make it convincing — none of this stiff, by-the-book stuff." Each one saying a word, everyone pressing together — Huifang truly had no recourse. The sharp-tongued too can sometimes come to grief. Huifang had no choice but to lean slightly toward Chunhang, raise the cup, and say: "Brother-in-law — drink this cup." Chunhang also had no recourse; he could only brazen it out and drink. For the second cup, Huifang also had to first take a sip, then bring it to Chunhang's lips; Chunhang, without waiting to be asked, drank it. Everyone said: "That cup doesn't count — start over. The tally says the correct words must be spoken for it to count." Chunhang pleaded again and again; finally it was let go. For the third cup, things were much easier. Huifang poured a cup for himself, stood up, and Cixian said: "This cup must be done with an expression of anger." Sulan said: "He has genuine grievance in his heart." Huifang gave a laugh, then quickly composed the beautiful face into an expression of anger and drained the cup in one go. Ziyun, seeing all this, felt great admiration in his heart; he drank three cups himself and urged everyone else to drink three cups as well.

They then looked at the next tally — it was Lanbao's. The inscription read: "Lu Zhishen Drunken at the Mountain Gate." The annotation: "First drain one large cup; the first two seats are the Guardian Kings; finger-guess three rounds with each." Huifang said: "He gets off so easily, while I had to go through all that trouble." Lanbao followed the game rules and finger-guessed three rounds each with Nanxiang and Chunhang.

The next tally was Shufang's; the inscription read: "Jin Cuilian Selling Her Singing in the Wine-House. She must play the pipa and offer a respectful cup to Lu Da, Li Zhong, and Shi Jin each." Everyone said: "This one is still manageable — on the edge between closeness and distance. And since she really does have the surname Jin — what an extraordinary coincidence!" Shufang had no choice but to take up the pipa, and offered cups to Nanxiang, Chunhang, and Cixian.

The next gourd retrieved contained Tian Chunhang's tally. Chunhang quickly looked at the inscription, thought for a moment, and said: "Terrible!" Everyone burst out clapping and laughing again: "Today Meixiang and Xiangfan are clearly bound together by fate." Huifang's face reddened and she said: "You've arranged this on purpose — otherwise why would only these two tallies come out like this?" Cixian said: "An absolutely unjust accusation! Say I purposely chose them — did you then purposely choose to draw them?" The inscription read: "Yi Zhangqing Captures Wang Duanhu." The annotation: "After, they became husband and wife; hold a red handkerchief between yourself and the person beside you, exchange three cups in bridal-goblet fashion; the whole table raises a congratulatory cup." Chunhang wanted to change the game rule, but everyone refused to allow it. He could only hold a handkerchief between himself and Huifang, and they each drank half a cup; the third cup set the whole table talking and laughing together — talking and laughing, laughing and talking — saying: "To witness this bridal goblet is rare indeed — let us each raise a great cup." So the whole table drank a congratulatory cup, which so embarrassed Huifang that she said: "From now on my unlucky stars have all passed. Now it will be my turn to laugh at people." The next tally was Baozhu's; the inscription read: "Wang Po Discusses Romantic Intrigue Upstairs." Seeing the annotation, Huifang laughed: "Now this is karmic retribution — who expected that someone else would also put on a fine show for people to watch." Baozhu's face had already flushed bright red. The game required three cups: for the first, the person to the right is Ximen Qing; adopt the manner of lifting the window curtain with a bamboo pole, strike Ximen Qing once with a fan, and offer the first cup. For the second, Ximen Qing must kneel, one hand clasping the tip of Pan Jinlian's tiny foot, and offer this cup to Pan Jinlian. For the third, the person to the left is Wang Po; Pan Jinlian curtsies and says: "Godmother, please drink this cup." Ziyun laughed: "As luck would have it, it has now come around to me." Chunhang said: "Having fragrant dust cling to one's stockings is the finest of affairs — and moreover with a lotus hook in the hand; why not drink ten cups?" Nanxiang laughed loudly: "'Fragrant dust cling to the knee' is still manageable — only don't kneel in the mud; then the lotus hook would be out of reach, and what you'd grasp would be a donkey's leg." This set everyone into wild uncontrollable laughter; Jin Shufang laughed so hard she twisted at the waist and tumbled straight into Cixian's lap. Wang Lanbao and Lu Sulan laughed and moved away. Baozhu said: "Another case of heavenly retribution — perfectly clear and precise." Everyone kept laughing, which made Chunhang quite uncomfortable; yet unable to take it seriously, he had to hold it in and say: "Zhujun's mockery deserves to be punished — he should draw an unpleasant tally." Nanxiang laughed: "I was speaking from the facts — what mockery is there?" Huifang said: "That is enough — don't be so boastful. You too will have your moment of losing the wind someday." Cixian laughed: "Yaoqing — what to do about this tally? It seems it cannot be changed — one can only accept some discomfort. And it is asking a lot of Duxiang's golden dignity on his knees." Everyone burst into laughter again. Huifang urged Baozhu to offer the wine quickly. Baozhu, being of a gentle and compliant nature, and unable to withstand the general pressure, had no choice but to brazen it out, and with the handle of the fan gave Ziyun a light tap, offering the first cup. Ziyun received it with a smile; everyone said: "Splendid! Let us each drink a cup." Ziyun said: "The drinking-game law is stricter than military law — no choice in the matter; honored guests, please don't laugh at the short man trying to see the show." He poured a cup, bent one knee, and offered it to Baozhu; Baozhu promptly took it and drank. Everyone said: "Splendid!" and each drank another cup. Baozhu then offered the third cup to Huifang, curtsied, and said: "Godmother, please drink this cup." Huifang accepted it and drank, laughing: "Good daughter — you've gone to such trouble." Everyone praised: "What a fine godmother and god-daughter! Let us drink another congratulatory cup." And each drank again. The last remaining tally turned out to be Sulan's; it read: "The Heroes of Liangshan Marsh Assemble in Righteous Unity." The whole table each drinks three cups. Everyone said: "That closes things out most entertainingly — this drinking game today really seemed as if it had been arranged on purpose." Baozhu said: "Only, it's too easy on him, and too easy on Jingfang — and Shouxiang only had to play the pipa. First of all, it's Yaoqing and I who had the worst of it."

Huifang said: "Someone knelt to offer you wine — and you're still not satisfied? What more do you want?" Baozhu said: "If you wanted someone to kneel to you, why didn't you take my tally and perform it for me?" This round of drinking lasted until the red sun was setting in the west, and then they had their meal.

After rinsing their mouths and washing their hands, they drank another round of fragrant tea. Nanxiang said: "We still haven't appreciated the pomegranate blossoms at Little Red Citadel — shall we take advantage of the evening glow to go look at those blazing pomegranate fires?" Ziyun then led everyone back to the boats, which crossed the red bridge back to the west side, where they disembarked at the foot of the artificial hill, passed through the Immortal's Cave, wended through the Apricot Tower (Xinglou) and Peach Grove, and arrived at Little Red Citadel. They saw pomegranate blossoms ringing in all directions like a rampart — some hundred or two hundred trees — crimson gleams flickering, a flowing fire about to blaze; interspersed were several varieties in yellow, white, and variegated agate colors, all blooming in succession. Truly as the "Rhapsody on the Tiantai Mountains" says: "The red citadel glows and rises to mark the summit" — which is why the place was called Little Red Citadel.

The day was now late. Nanxiang and Chunhang wished to leave; small attendants brought their outer garments and hats, they dressed and put them on, thanked the host and Cixian, and took the roundabout path out of the garden. Ziyun said: "Today there was actually one matter I had meant to trouble you two gentlemen with. The paired inscriptions for various spots throughout the garden still need several more sets added; today we were sidetracked by the dragon boat. Perhaps another day I can invite another outing and also include Gengxiang, Jiantan, and the other gentlemen — what do you say?" Shi and Tian both agreed, then mounted their carriages and departed. The five young dan actors also gradually dispersed. This second grand entertainment at Yiyuan — it was a pity it was not fully attended. As for what the second volume will relate — one must await a careful reading.
