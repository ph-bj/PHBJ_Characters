Chapter 21 — Rumor-making wins only cold contempt; true feelings are confessed in hidden tears.

This installment turns again to Wei Pincai. After dwelling in the Hua mansion for more than a month he had cultivated everyone above and below with such thorough success that Young Master Hua granted him some dignity and his purse grew easier; thereupon he began to show the petty man's triumph and would no longer keep his place. Within were Gu Yueqing and Zhang Xiaomei; outside Yang Meichuang, Feng Zipai, and such company — nightly revelry — and whatever he saw and heard ran to schemes of influence and trimming to power; his heart grew fat and his daring swelled. Formerly at the Mei residence Academician Mei had been at home: though he did not lecture Pincai, fear naturally restrained him. Moreover Ziyu kept company only with gentlemen of name — none of those idle roamers. Like a horse stalled in its manger, however much it wished to bolt the traces it could not. Now he might indulge every whim without scruple.

One day Zhang and Gu both had business; Pincai therefore went out of the city alone, hired a fashionable thirteen-panel glass-windowed heated carriage, dressed up Fourth Boy as well, and with an air of triumph came expressly to inquire after Ziyu's illness.

Arriving at the Mei house he paid his respects to Lady Yan and went straight to Ziyu's chamber. Ziyu had now been ill over a month; though not gravely sick, he felt leaden and weary, ate far less than before, and his spirits flagged utterly. Sometimes he diverted himself with books, but his vitality was too weak for any pursuit; he sat alone without speaking, wearing the vacant brooding air of one who reads into empty air. Even Lady Yan could not guess what ailed her son — she assumed only that study had worn his blood and prescribed ginseng and tuckahoe tonics; useless as medicine ill-matched to the ailment. As the proverb runs, the heart's sickness needs a heart's cure; passions stirred from the seven emotions cannot be soothed by roots and bark. Only Ziyu himself understood — unless Qinyan came in person and talked freely through a whole day and night he could not suddenly recover. Pincai guessed part of this; entering, he asked many questions. With no visitors these few days Ziyu felt stifled — Pincai's presence eased him somewhat. Asked how things stood at the Hua mansion, Pincai praised Young Master Hua as unequalled under heaven, boasted how fine were his attendant Lin Shanzhi and the Eight Ling troupe — and declared even Qinyan could not match them.

At mention of Qinyan Ziyu's old sorrow stirred; he said to Pincai: "You were the first to speak of Qinyan — when I met him he seemed peerless in the world; how can there now be so many said to surpass him?" Pincai replied: "Qinyan's looks are indeed fine, but his temper is cold — like a blossom whose color is superb yet hangs on the highest branch where you cannot climb to reach it; whoever loves flowers naturally loves what lies near the hand. Or like winter's moon: brilliantly clear, yet when you lift your eyes that cold radiance pierces flesh and bone — beside the moons of spring's third month or autumn's eighth it seems less kindly though brighter." "Such are the shallow metaphors of the mundane world," Ziyu countered. "A flower unplucked preserves its own pristine fragrance; a moon unadmired shines absolute in its solitary grace. If a high blossom eludes the grasping hand, it is meant for the rare plumage of exotic birds, for the caress of immortal dew and celestial winds—its very unattainability is its crowning glory! And though the winter moon may seem too austere for casual admiration, when it illuminates the blooming winter plum and the pristine snow, when it dances on clear waves and silvers the drifting clouds, its cold radiance attains an ethereal purity. How can mere frost diminish its splendor? Roughly speaking, Qinyan's living fragrance and vivid color anyone may note; Qinyan's deep earnest feeling few can grasp. Taking him only by ordinary likeness misses him — hence vulgar and refined part ways. That Qinyan should meet fortune such as this is Heaven shaping the man — not turning him into roadside peach and plum." In these words Ziyu spoke from true insight into Qinyan; without noticing he treated Pincai as a vulgar sort of different taste. Pincai inwardly smarted but answered dryly: "Quite so, quite so — Qinyan's merits I said long ago — few in this world beside Your Excellency and myself appreciate him so far. I mean to visit him — what truly is his illness?" Ziyu said: "Will you go today?" Pincai said: "If errands permit — I'll pass by." Ziyu said: "If you see him, do not say I am ill. If he asks, say you do not know." Pincai said: "I understand — what message shall I carry?" Ziyu said: "Nothing much." After a pause: "Only tell him not to be ill." Pincai laughed: "If you can bid him not to be ill, he can bid you the same." Ziyu knew he had slipped and smiled, then amended: "Since he is already ill, nothing can be done — only urge him not to heap illness on illness. If he hears I am ill, do not hide it — say mine is slight and will clear in days. Say Xiangwan is the kindest soul — he may often seek him out and talk away gloom — then recovery comes faster." Pincai did not fully grasp this but agreed. Ziyu added: "I cannot visit him myself — seeing Xiangwan will do." As he spoke his look grew bleak. Pincai saw clearly this sickness rose from Qinyan and thought: "Yuxiang is useless — if he loves Qinyan why not summon him often and pour out both hearts — then neither would ail — why bottle it — he is no boudoir maiden barred from sight." Taking leave he did not seek Yuanmao but exchanged brief civilities at accounts and gate offices, then went straight to Cherry Lane and Qinyan's lodging.

As it happened Changqing had gone out; Pincai walked straight into Qinyan's bedchamber. Green windows were tightly shut; the small yard stood empty; a plum tree bore clusters of yellow fruit, half flushed red on one side, several fallen on the ground. Suddenly a voice cried: "A guest — do not enter!" Looking up he saw a white parrot hung under the eaves — at sight of Pincai it began to speak. Someone stepped from the opposite wing and barred the way: "The young gentleman is ill and cannot receive visitors — sir, please sit in the outer guest room." Pincai said: "I am no stranger — we are closest of friends. Go in and say my surname is Wei; I come from Academician Mei's house to see him about his illness — I have something to say." The man relayed this; Qinyan's cough sounded twice within; then he heard: "If he is from Academician Mei's house, show him in." The fellow came out smiling: "Sir, if you please." The suite ran to three bays — outer room held a table and stools. A page lifted the curtain; stepping through the curtain, a delicate mingling of floral fragrance and herbal medicine instantly refreshed the senses. This was still the antechamber. Pincai took a seat, noting how the emerald glass of the windows cast a soft, verdant luminescence over the white plush carpet. Above the heated platform hung a scroll, "Princess Shouyang's Plum Blossom Makeup." Beside it hung a couplet: "A heart cradling the autumn moon in a vase of ice / A soul resting by the plum blossoms against a paper canopy." On the kang table a vase held a spray of plum; to one side four panels of red plum by Xiao Cixian; to the other four seal-script scrolls by Xu Ziyun. Before the window stood an antique brick-and-pearwood qin stand bearing a plum-patterned duan qin. The inner chamber hung a moonlight autumn-gauze embroidered curtain patterned with every variety of plum — this was the sleeping room.

Pincai was about to step further when Qinyan lifted the curtain and came out. At first sight he wore a pale lake-green lined silk jacket and blue gauze short-sleeved surcoat — somewhat thinner than before, like plum in snow that prefers cold — only the more pitiable and lovely. He seated Pincai in the place of honor but himself sat far off on a plum-blossom stool by the qin table; they brought tea and a lad offered a white brass water-pipe and filled several bowls for Pincai. Pincai said: "I heard you were unwell — I came out of the city especially to see you — are you better lately?" At the words "out of the city" Qinyan pondered — so Yuxiang had not visited because he had moved inside the walls — and asked: "When did Yuxiang move into the city? Which ward? How far from here?"

Pincai realized Qinyan had misunderstood: "Yuxiang has not moved — I now lodge in the city; I no longer stay at his house." Hearing this Qinyan fell silent; his spirit seemed to slacken and vexation crossed his face. Pincai thought: "Ask after Yuxiang and he brightens — speak straight to me and he turns ice." To probe he asked: "Have you seen Yuxiang this month?" Qinyan said: "Since New Year's meeting at Yiyuan we have not met." Pincai smiled faintly: "I gather Yuxiang no longer remembers you." Qinyan started: "What do you mean — remembers?" Pincai feigned hesitation: "Nothing — only Yuxiang has been busy lately — naturally he forgets." Qinyan pressed: "Busy how?" Pincai said: "Nothing special — friends, opera, wine." Qinyan reflected — something felt wrong — "I heard Yuxiang was ill — that he went to Yiyuan several times — I never crossed him." Pincai gave a cold laugh and said nothing. Qinyan's doubts sharpened: "Could Yuxiang truly have forgotten me — taken up with someone else?" Yet he recalled how Yuling had flirted that day and Yuxiang flared — hardly likely. Pincai's manner was evasive — half swallowed — as if hiding something. "He said he lives in the city — not at Yuxiang's — yet how does he know Yuxiang's affairs? If Yuxiang had nothing against me why would he lie? Still Pincai has never been trustworthy." Resolving that Yuxiang could not mean ill, he asked aloud: "Yuxiang is an upright man — not one for idle romps." Pincai calculated: "If I name someone he knows he will verify — I'll name someone distant." Slowly he said: "Desire is never fixed — without companions to lure one, one never thinks it; without tasting sweetness one never seeks it. They say 'near vermilion you turn red — near ink you turn black.' Bad company hooks you onto that road; a taste of honey draws you in." He sighed: "Pity Yuxiang — he began so principled and tasteful — would not run wild. At first when people gossiped I doubted — today I visited him — I enter without announcement as always — reached his study outer room — heard laughter inside. Yun'er cried loudly: 'Guest!' When I stepped in Yuxiang lay on the couch face flushed — flustered — I saw no one — the laughter was not his — I thought nothing of it."

"When we talked he fenced with me — answers missed questions. Behind the bed-curtain came rustling — someone hiding — I could not ask openly — anyone who might meet me would not hide. I sat on his bed awhile; the curtain stirred again — my fan dropped; I stooped as if to retrieve it and lifted the hem — beneath I glimpsed boots and the hem of white patterned crepe — I guessed an actor — thought perhaps you — yet you were sick — unlikely out — nor would you hide from me — Yuxiang's face burned; he feigned sleep — spare him embarrassment — I withdrew. At the gatehouse pages gossiped with Lianzhu troupe Rongguan's boy Dezhi — then I knew it was Rongguan. Yuyong — why keep company with such — even an actor need not be hidden — I cannot fathom our brother's temper. I know you bore deep feeling for him — at first he missed you too — they say he sought you several times — you refused — his heart cooled. You did not ask — I kept silent — now that you ask I cannot hide — frolicking with actors is common — I hate only that he cast you aside for Rongguan — wasting true feeling and staining his own clean name." Qinyan listened blankly, half believing; hearing this his heart twisted — tears slipped — he forced them back — finger slowly traced the gold studs on the qin — "When you saw Yuxiang — nothing else was said?" Pincai said: "Leaving he only asked what troupe I would hear." Qinyan said: "All this is true?" Pincai sneered: "When do I lie — believe what you like." Qinyan said: "You sat so long — only that?" Pincai said: "I did not stay long — seeing him troubled I did not press." Qinyan said: "Truly only one sentence?" Pincai said: "Not two — else may I perish." Qinyan's chest tightened — he could ask no more. Pincai added: "They say at Yiyuan you were close — you gave him a qin — why drift apart — I hear the qin passed to another." Qinyan bent his head — could not answer. Pincai added: "Perhaps because you often visited Duxiang — he grew jealous — if you truly cared you should not always haunt Duxiang — some distinction of near and far is owed — no wonder he turned sour." Qinyan's bitterness welled — near tears — he burst out: "What words are these — what 'close' — what 'jealous' — I understand none of it." With that he wept outright. Pincai secretly rejoiced and soothed: "I misspoke — you cannot bear jest — forgive me." He stepped up to wipe the tears — Qinyan flushed with indignation, rose, and withdrew to the inner room — called the boy: "See Master Wei out — I am exhausted and cannot keep company." With that he lay down on the bed.

Pincai heard this and rage flared — almost to explode — then thought: "He is Yuxiang's darling — ruin him and Yuxiang may blame me — endure for now — settle slowly — time and again his cold eyes treat me as worthless — hateful. If I cannot master him what am I as Young Master Hua's man?" Swallowing fury he swept out, climbed his heated carriage, and flew off.

Qinyan lay on the bed — wave on wave of acid grief — tears without end — could not puzzle it out. He recalled Sulan's words that day — he and Yuxiang had come but the master was feasting guests and could not enter — then someone cut the tale short; these days he could not find Sulan — sent for him; Sulan changed to plain dress and came; Qinyan received him and they sat in the room. Sulan opened: "You wanted me — not again to play Yuxiang's stand-in?" Qinyan forced a smile: "A puzzle I cannot fathom." Sulan said: "Speak." Qinyan asked: "You mentioned Yuxiang — have you seen him lately?" Sulan smiled: "As expected — aside from Yuxiang you never send for me. The other day at Yiyuan watching dragon boats Duxiang invited him — illness kept him away. Duxiang spoke of his illness — more than a month — face grown thin — ten days ago he did visit Duxiang. There's a joke — hilarious — I'm afraid you'll weep — maybe I should not tell." Qinyan's heart already shifted — he pressed:

"What joke — say it now." Sulan said: "On Meixiang's birthday Tian Xiangfan wrote a short preface — everyone praised it — Duxiang copied it. The day Yuxiang came Jingyi showed him the piece; Yuxiang admired it. Standing by, Duxiang said: 'Xiangfan's style — the thicker the richer — the richer the better.' Jingyi and Duxiang at first did not catch Yuxiang's drift — then realized: he misheard 'thick and rich' as asking which of you or Qiguan ranks higher — he answered: 'Yuyong naturally above Yuyan — Yuyan though fine still falls short of Yaoqing and Meixiang — while Yuyong stands like trees of jade and blossoms of pearl — no place on human flower-lists.' Jingyi and Duxiang laughed until they could not stand — Yuxiang saw his error — deeply embarrassed — covered with a few mumbled words — so one moment he has not forgotten you — this illness may be hard to cure — will you cry?" At this Qinyan could not hold back — silent sobs — tears strung like thread — his blue gauze half-sleeve dark with a spreading wet patch across the chest.

Sulan soothed: "Why weep — you are barely mending — grief only heaps frost on snow — that is why I would not tell." Qinyan stamped: "Wei Pincai is less than human — slandering and staining people for nothing." Sulan asked who Wei Pincai was; Qinyan hid his face in the sleeve and wept without stopping; Sulan coaxed until he ceased, then told the drinking game the other day with Baozhu and Huifang — Yaoqing was middling — Meixiang sharp and unwilling to lose yet had swallowed that dull loss; also how Pan San plagued him — Meixiang would not tell — gossip said Meixiang feared him and hid at Xiangfan's for days — now he instructed servants: if Pan San called, always answer not at home — rumor added he had opened a door behind his bed through to the kitchen to dodge Pan San.

Hearing this Qinyan smiled slightly. Sulan asked who Wei Pincai was; Qinyan sketched last year's boat north and lodging at Mei's — then said: "Listening close, that Wei is a petty man — why ask — better not speak of him." Sulan said: "Nothing special — yesterday at Chunyang Tower I heard the boss tangled with a secretary of the Hua mansion — dragged to the constabulary — twenty blows — dozens of strings fine — two tables of wine besides."

"People said the man was also surnamed Wei — something Cai — from the Hua mansion." Qinyan said: "I heard him say he lives in the city now — not at Yuxiang's — I did not ask where." Sulan said: "The waiters told the tale end to end. New Year's week this Wei and others brought Baozhu and Er Xi — fifty-odd strings — the boss did not know him and must have spoken out of turn when tallying credit."

"Later the Wei man repaid in scraps — account still unsettled. Another day he brought five actors from si hour to shen — seventy-odd strings — the boss, not knowing he was from the Hua house, stalled on the chit and mentioned old debt — the Wei drunk smashed brush and shoved the inkstone off — shattered a table of crockery below — boss raised uproar — crowd parted them — ticket named Hua mansion secretary — next morning constables yoked the boss away — Zhang Zhongyu spent dozens of strings to settle — twenty blows only — two feasts for Master Zhang — they said the name was Wei something — hearing you curse him I guess it is Pincai." Qinyan said: "Whether or no — anyone called Wei Pincai is no good." He asked again what Sulan and Yuxiang had begun to say the day the master feasted guests. Sulan recounted that day in detail — "I've talked myself dry — you offered no tea." Qinyan laughed: "Talk ran urgent — forgot — brew tea." Sulan drank and smiled: "Yuxiang matches your heart — cast from one mold." Qinyan asked how. Sulan said: "Your rooms and dress — everywhere plum blossoms — because his surname is Mei — you cherish the token. Yuxiang lately wears nothing but qin motifs — brocade pouches, fan tassels — fans painted front and back with two qins — isn't that the same bent?" Qinyan laughed through tears: "Don't tease — how carry heavy instruments on the body?" Sulan laughed: "Fool — did you plant plum blossoms on your skin?" Qinyan smiled; Sulan added: "Duxiang says Yuxiang's every trinket is qin-shaped — fans show two instruments, one face, one reverse." Qinyan wept again; Sulan said: "You cry again and I'll hush." Qinyan swallowed: "Go on — I won't cry." Sulan asked why Pincai earned his hate; Qinyan repeated Pincai's lies about Yuxiang. Sulan reflected: "Yuxiang would never — you must not believe him." Qinyan said: "At first his tale seemed true — hearing you I know it cannot be — why must that mongrel invent slander — is there no decency?" Sulan said: "Perhaps Yuxiang crossed him — perhaps he seeks to drive you apart for some scheme." Qinyan sneered: "His schemes — do I flatter him because he entered the Hua mansion?" Sulan warned: "Now that knave rides Young Master's favor he may throw weight — wrong no petty villain — evil men make mischief — guard yourself as against thieves." Qinyan said: "I ignore him — what can he do?" They talked on until past second drum; Sulan rose — parting she said: "In a few days I'll devise a way for Yuxiang to meet you." Whether Pincai could swallow such cold treatment without further plots — hear the next installment.
