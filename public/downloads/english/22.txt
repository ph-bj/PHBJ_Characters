Chapter 22 — Under an unlucky star, plain hearts weep in double grief; touring the canal, Mei and Du renew their bond.

The last chapter told how Lu Sulan promised Qinyan that he would arrange for Ziyu to come out for a meeting. But no sooner was the promise spoken than the next day proved to be the Dragon Boat Festival, when there was no leisure to be had; and then came three days running of private engagement performances, which left Sulan so worn that he had to rest quietly for several days more. Qinyan meanwhile passed each day as if it were a year, sending someone daily to press him, until Sulan was quite at his wits' end. Moreover his own lodgings saw too many comers and goers to be convenient; and to borrow another man's rooms, or a wine-house or restaurant, would be more awkward still for intimate talk — nor could he well invite companion guests, leaving the two with full hearts and no way to pour them out. He turned it over and over, and could only borrow the pretext of an outing on the canal: a quiet day's excursion, and secluded enough. His mind made up, he sent a man outside the Great East Gate to hire an elegant boat, and had some of his own curios and ornaments, brushes, inkstones, and flowering plants carried down and arranged aboard. He then sent word to Qinyan to come down to the boat early next morning, that they might enjoy the day to the full, inviting no one else. Then he considered: how was Ziyu to be invited? Only by borrowing Duxiang's name. So he wrote out in detail the reasons for the invitation, sealed the letter, and sent a man to deliver it, instructing him to tell the gatekeepers simply that Master Xu of Yiyuan was inviting the young master to an outing on the canal.

The messenger, as instructed, went straight to the Mei residence and presented the letter. Ziyu was sitting in listless gloom with the jade qin that Ziyun had given him, turning over the notation and picking out the easier pieces to practice; then he turned the qin over again and recited its inscription several times through. He felt only that green shade filled the courtyard and the long day stretched like a year, and he could think of nothing to dispel his tedium. Just as he sat fretting, Yun'er came in with a letter and laid it on the table, saying that Master Xu of Yiyuan had sent it, with an invitation for an outing on the canal tomorrow, and that a reply was wanted. Ziyu took up the letter and saw the envelope inscribed "For the hand of Young Master Mei" in neat, upright characters, quite unlike the hand of Ziyun or Cixian, and thought: "Perhaps he set a page-boy to write it." He opened it, and the first line read: "Lu Sulan respectfully addresses the poetic altar of Master Yuxiang" — at which his heart gave a leap. Why should Xiangwan be writing to him? Could something be afoot concerning Yunong? He read it word by word, and having finished, read it again, two and three times over, until a smile rose unbidden to his face. He said to Yun'er: "Go tell the messenger to wait a little; I will write a reply at once." When Yun'er had gone out, Ziyu read the letter once more and felt the flowers of his heart open wide — his illness was nine-tenths gone. He hastened to grind ink and spread paper. The first half of his reply was words of gratitude; the second half, words of promise, engaging himself to keep the appointment at the chen hour next morning. When he had finished he read it over, sealed it, and was about to write the direction when he suddenly paused: how should it be addressed? After a moment's hesitation the answer came to him: naturally, to "Master Xu" as well. He finished it, stamped his seal, and bade Yun'er hand it to the messenger with word that he would come without fail on the morrow. The messenger, reply in hand, returned at once and presented it to Sulan, who, seeing with what earnest warmth it was written and how inexhaustible its gratitude, was delighted in his turn, and taking the letter walked over in high spirits to Qinyan's.

He was just inside the second gate when he heard an uproar of shouting. Startled, Sulan lightened his step, slipped into the guest room on the east side, and peered through a crack in the window. There were two men: one seated, the other standing in the middle of the room, pounding the table and cursing. The sight frightened Sulan badly. The seated one wore a dark silk gown; he was about thirty, his black, oily face heavy with brutal flesh, a pair of iron balls turning in his hand as he dropped cold remarks that half stirred the quarrel and half feigned to soothe it. The other, also past thirty, was short-necked and barrel-chested, thick of waist and broad of shoulder, a great queue coiled on his head; he wore a dark silk short jacket over dark silk trouser-leggings, and dragged black satin embroidered slippers. Swinging his arms, he bellowed: "What sort of thing is he? A xiaodan, nothing more — and who isn't a patron of his? Whoever has money may summon him. Yet this little whelp must play the spook and sham sickness and see no one! Xianggong far redder than he wait on the gentlemen whenever they're called: do well, and there's a few strings of cash for reward; do ill, and they can roll their mother's egg and be off. Little turtle's spawn! If you don't roll yourself out here, Third Grandpa will wreck this little bastard's dog-kennel and thrash that old turtle of a master of yours into the bargain!" The seated one said: "Lao San, don't lose your temper yet — bide a while. Mark me: now that we've come today, he won't dare stay in."

The several members of Qinyan's household did their best to placate them, pleading: "Qinguan is truly ill — it's still touch and go with him. If he mends within these few days, his master will surely bring him to both your lordships' gates to kowtow. Today we beg you to hold your hand high: his sickness lies very heavy on him; curse him as you may, he simply cannot come out. And his master has gone into the city. We beg you to show a little grace. Let today pass, and tomorrow we may speak again; we will make his apologies for him — pray cool your anger." With that he made a low bow, and patting the man on the back, begged him to be seated. The man, still fuming, would not sit. The one in the dark gown spoke again: "Lao San, listen — what he says is fair enough. Let us spare him this once and come again tomorrow or the day after. If he still won't come out then, we'll take a horsewhip to him — and what can he do about it?" At this, Qinyan's man turned to the dark-gowned one: "We beg you to persuade this gentleman to wait until the illness has passed. He cannot possibly be well by tomorrow — you must grant us a few days' grace. Tomorrow his master shall first wait upon the mansion to apologize, and when Qinguan is recovered they will come together to make amends." He bowed again, presented two cups of tea, filled the man's water-pipe with tobacco, and offered it up. The fellow could only take in his sails, saying: "It's not that I have a bad temper — but this passes all bearing. I've seen sixty-two catties and a half in my day, but never so grand a xianggong as this. Go ask about: Chunlin, Fenglin — boys as red as they are come running the moment your Third Grandpa nods his head. Never once have I stood at a door and been denied, blocked by servants and fobbed off with lies about sickness. The monkey's cub! What is he skulking for? He's not missing an eye, nor short a leg, that he can't be looked at." The dark-clothed one rose: "Lao San, enough — we ought to go get our dinner." The other said: "Go where for dinner? Let them lay a meal for us here; we'll eat first and talk after." And the two sat down again. Qinyan's people, seeing the look of this, suspected extortion was the game, and reflected that once you have run into a plague-god, he will not be exorcised without burning paper-money. There was nothing for it but to go in and consult Qinyan, then bring out two strings of cash, saying with a placating smile: "We ought by rights to keep your lordships to dinner, but the cook is away today, and poor fare would only slight you. Qinguan offers this small treat — pray take yourselves to a restaurant with it."

And he presented the money with both hands. The dark-gowned one was on the point of taking it when the short-jacketed one saw there were only two strings, and fell to cursing again: "His mother's! Two strings of cash — what are the masters to eat on that? I'll have you know the masters don't eat at boiled-pork shops and dumpling lofts. When did a dinner ever cost us less than eight or ten strings — and this is two?" And he glared with bulging eyeballs. Qinyan's man, quick of wit, smiled again: "No haste — that was meant to honor one of your lordships only; there are two strings more, we'll fetch them straight out." He turned and brought out two more strings of cash, made a bow, and begged them again and again to accept. The short-jacketed one still wore his mask of fury, but the dark-gowned one picked up the money, slung it over his shoulder, and with his free hand hauled his companion out.

Sulan, still spying at the window-crack, had been struck quite dumb, and did not think of their coming out; as he hurried away the short-jacketed one caught sight of him and shouted: "And who are you, you little bastard? Where are you running? Come here — your grandpa has been wanting you!" Sulan fled for his very life, and the man chased him out the main gate; luckily Sulan was quick and had already got home. The lane was a short one — the two houses stood diagonally opposite, both at its mouth — and the man, supposing he had run clean out of the lane, gave up the chase and asked Qinyan's people: "That little rabbit just now — what troupe is he in, and where does he live? He runs at the sight of your Third Grandpa; then your Third Grandpa will make a point of finding him."

Qinyan's man answered: "He's from the Dengchun troupe — I can't recall his name. He lives a long way off, in Shitou Hutong." The two men, still spouting their wild talk, went reeling and swaying away. Within, Qinyan, who had heard himself so abused, was already faint with rage.

And who do you suppose these two were, coming to raise a row out of a clear sky? One was a carriage-driver of the Hua mansion; the one in the dark gown was the third boy of the resident cook — bought for the work by Wei Pincai at a cost of eight strings of cash.

Lu Sulan, meanwhile, had run home so frightened that his heart pounded wildly and both temples flamed scarlet; he had very nearly burst into tears. He hurried to his room, sat down, and steadied his spirit. But after a while his heart went back to Qinguan: after such a storm of insult, who knew to what state rage had brought him? He wished to go over to see him, but feared to meet those two again; and after wavering a long while, still unable to set his mind at rest, he sent someone ahead to look. The coast being clear, he hastened across in a few quick steps. In Qinyan's room the blue gauze bed-curtains hung lowered, and from behind them came a sound of sobbing. Sulan lifted the curtain and patted Qinyan: "Up with you! Good news has come. Put away your anger for now — here is a letter for you to read." Qinyan turned over, and at the sight of Sulan only grieved the more. He heaved a sigh and said: "I am going to die in any case. To live on and suffer like this — death were cleaner. Brother Lan, you are my great benefactor. Since we have loved each other so well, be my friend to the very end. When I am dead, beg Duxiang for me to bury my bones beneath the plum trees of Meiyan in Yiyuan; then even as a ghost I shall be happy. Or else burn me to ashes, and where the mountains are high and the waters deep scatter them down the wind, and spare the world one wretched relic of an ill-fated life — so that none who think of me need hate, and none need ache. Brother Lan, Brother Lan! You love me — then let me die, and do not dissuade me. I am but sixteen, and already weary of living. Born from childhood into a poor man's house, and made a singer of plays, I have drunk every shame and insult. I cannot tell what Heaven means to do with me — if it wants my life, let it be quick about it. Why must it trample a man so?"

So saying, he burst into loud weeping, and his words set Sulan weeping too. He had meant to console him; but hearing such talk, with those two men fresh before his eyes, he felt sorrier for him the more he thought, and so wept on with him and could not stop.

The two of them wept face to face for a full hour. Qinyan, seeing Sulan grieve so on his account, was filled with gratitude; he took Sulan's hand and wept afresh. Sulan, feeling Qinyan clasp him as he cried, knew it was meant in thankfulness, and thought: "With such talent and such beauty, Qinyan meets with such torment — then Heaven and Earth are jealous indeed of all who have talent and beauty. I, Sulan, count first or second among the flowers myself; if Heaven and Earth should grow jealous of me too, and grind me with torments like these, I should be even as Yunong — and at such an hour, perhaps, without one intimate friend to comfort me."

And he thought further: "Those two chasing me out with curses just now — that was the first time in all my life. Who knows what mischief may yet spring from it?" At this his heart wound through its nine turns, and he gave way and wept aloud facing Qinyan, till the whole household took alarm and came crowding in to stand about, dazed, not daring to offer comfort, knowing well it was over the day's ugly business. Two of them at last came forward to soothe them, and when each had somewhat quieted, went out and fetched two basins of water for their faces and steeped two bowls of tea, then withdrew. The two, though their weeping had ceased, could bring out no words, but still sat swallowing sobs, their eyes brimming. After another long pause, Lu Sulan spoke: "What happened today I saw with my own eyes, and I am grieved to death for you. That fellow looks like some country clod — only how did it all begin? Do you know where he is from?" Qinyan waited a moment, and answered, still weeping: "No one here knows them. They say the pair looked utterly savage — who can tell where they came from, storming in for no reason at all? It is only my bitter fate: such fiends and demons are written in my destiny." Sulan, whose mind was quick, pondered a while and said: "To my eye those two look like carriage-drivers from some great house — or some Third Master's men. What if it were that Wei fellow who set them on? It is not impossible." Qinyan said: "Who can say? But what quarrel has Wei Pincai with me, that he should send men to raise a row?" Then, thinking again, he saw it all at once: "Yes — yes! It must be Wei Pincai's doing. Otherwise there is no sense in men walking in at the gate and cursing without cause or reason."

"But this dog Wei — what hatred does he bear me, that he must trample me so and hound me to my death?" Sulan said: "I told you the other day to be on your guard and not to offend him — and see, he has struck the first blow." Then he added: "Though after all this is only our guessing, and can't be taken for certain." Qinyan said nothing, staring blankly; then: "I shall be dead soon in any case. Let anything more come — I am not afraid of it." Sulan said: "Now you are talking folly. Life and death are written in fate — would you go seeking death yourself? Besides, if you truly died, you would drag another down with you, who would die too." Qinyan said: "I have no father or mother, no brothers, no sisters — whom should I drag down? I go clean, and I go alone." Sulan said: "No one else would be dragged down, true; many love you, but not to the point of dying for you. How is it that today of all days you cannot call Yuxiang to mind — would he not die for you? Look, then — whose hand wrote this?" And he handed Ziyu's reply to Qinyan, who took the letter, glanced at it, and laid it down, saying: "This is a letter from someone to Master Xu — why show it to me?" Sulan laughed: "Not so fast — that is the envelope. Look what is written inside." Qinyan could only draw the letter out. He read it from beginning to end, then began at the top again, reciting it sentence by sentence; read it through once more, and then smiled faintly: "Is this not Yuxiang's reply to you? He is to see the canal tomorrow — and by this letter he is certain to come." Sulan said: "Would you rather he came, or rather he did not?" Qinyan smiled again and answered: "It is you who invited him. Only who knows whether the weather will hold tomorrow. In the fifth month rain and shine are never settled; if it should start pouring at first light, then he cannot come." Sulan laughed: "Heaven bends to man's wishes. The tears we two have shed today may fairly pass for a day's rain — tomorrow is sure to be clear. Sleep soundly tonight, rise early, and come to my place to go together; to your master say only that you are going to Yiyuan. Your health is frail and the weather uncertain — bring an extra garment or two, for the wind may be strong on the water." So they talked on, and little by little the two faces brightened. Sulan took his evening meal with Qinyan and sat talking a while longer; not till past the second watch did he go home, and Qinyan too retired to rest.

By overnight the illness had retreated by eight-tenths, but a new ailment appeared: the more he wanted to sleep, the less he could.

He heard the fourth watch strike; then all at once several great gusts of wind came roaring up, and with them a downpour of rain, thunder and lightning interlocked. Qinyan sat up in bed and sighed long and deep. When the cloudburst had passed there lingered still a thin, soughing drizzle, and the thunder went rolling on without pause; only toward daybreak did it cease.

Qinyan, utterly spent, sank upon his pillow and fell fast asleep after all. He dreamed that Sulan and Ziyu were already in the boat, and he himself just stepping aboard, when suddenly two men came running out upon the bank: one in dark clothes, bare to the waist, queue coiled on his head, rushed up, seized him, and hauled him back, cursing: "You little bastard! Shamming sick by day and seeing no one — and how is it you've run out here now?" Qinyan screamed for help and wrenched his body free with all his strength — and there he was, still on his own bed, startled into a cold sweat, the red sun already filling the window.

Then he heard the parrot outside the window begin to talk: "Yesterday's men have come again!" — and it gave Qinyan another great start, thinking those two had indeed come back for him. In fact it was Sulan who, having waited a while without sign of Qinyan, had sent a man to fetch him, with word to his master that they were going together to Yiyuan. Changqing gave his consent and hurried Qinyan up. He washed his face, ate a bowl of iced bird's-nest, had his attendant pick out a few garments and wrap them in a bundle to take on the cart, took his leave of Changqing, and went straight to Sulan's.

On seeing Sulan he asked: "Did you settle with Yuxiang yesterday that he should come here?" Sulan said: "I arranged for him to go straight to the boat. And lest he fail to find it, I sent a man besides, passing as one of the Yiyuan people, to guide him there; by this time he is surely aboard before us. I am only waiting until the wine and all the gear are complete, so that nothing runs short at the last moment — then we shall be off too." Observe what a man this Sulan was: precise and clever both, very nearly the match of Huifang, wanting only Huifang's ruthless touch that gulled Pan San; set against the general run of men, he might well be called deep in wit and full of stratagem.

As for Ziyu, from the moment he lay down at the second watch he could scarcely wait for morning; and when he heard that storm of rain he sighed short and long and cursed his luck, fearing that if the morning brought more of the same, his mother would never let him leave the house. He rose and opened the window to look at the sky, just as the south wind rose in force and drove the rain straight in. Looking up he saw black clouds like ink, and where the lightning split them, golden serpents flickering. Then came a thunderclap that shook the very corners of the house. He shut the window in haste, trimmed the lamp, and sat alone. By good fortune the rain stopped at daybreak, though the eaves went on dripping, now broken, now resumed, for a good while after. It was too late by then to sleep again, so he woke Yun'er. Day had fully come, and the red sun was on the point of rising. He washed his face, drank his tea, took a few refreshments, and went to the upper rooms, where Lady Yan was not yet risen. Ziyu had a maid dress his hair in the outer room and came out again; everywhere all was still and quiet. Returning to his study, he thought: "Sulan has shown me such feeling, and I have put him to trouble again and again. He may set no store by such things, but I cannot rest easy on it. If I gave him silver he might well take offense, as though I held him cheap; best send him a few things instead."

So he opened his chests and picked out several of the odd trifles that kinsmen and friends had given him over the years, adding two bolts of Jiangnan silk and two bolts of Huzhou silk, and took along a dozen-odd taels of loose silver. He had just put all in order and was about to go to the upper rooms to take his leave when Li Yuanmao, a short jacket thrown over his shoulders and his feet bare, came bustling in all in a flurry: "I made a point of rising early today — who'd have thought you would be up earlier still!" Ziyu said: "I have business abroad today, so I am somewhat early." Yuanmao said: "There is a matter I want to talk over with you." Ziyu was about to ask what, when Yun'er came in: "Master Xu has sent a man to say the guests are all assembled, and requests the young master to come over now." Ziyu, with no more time for Yuanmao, hurried in to the upper rooms, where Lady Yan was at her toilet, and announced his outing. Lady Yan said: "You have been better these last days, and an airing will do you good. Only come home early; don't go courting the cool or sitting in a draft. Take a few more people with you, and pack an extra garment or two." Ziyu replied: "The clothes are packed already; and I don't need many people — Yun'er alone will do." Lady Yan said: "As you please; but mind you come back early, and be careful what you eat and drink." Ziyu answered "Yes!", went out, dressed, and had the gifts and clothing-bundles carried out to the cart beforehand.

He was on the very point of leaving when Li Yuanmao once more planted himself in the way: "Not so fast! I have a pressing matter that must be talked over." Ziyu said impatiently: "What is it then? Out with it!" Yuanmao rubbed his eyes, gave a yawn, and hemmed and hawed without bringing anything out. Ziyu said: "Well? If you have something to say, say it short and quick — someone is waiting for me at the gate. Speak!" Yuanmao said: "Who's waiting for you? Such a hurry! It's still early yet — listen, the seller of sweet congee hasn't even cried his wares past the door, and here you are in a fluster. What for?" Ziyu was growing truly weary of it: "You said you had a matter to discuss; asked, you will not speak, but run on instead with idle chatter without end. What is it, once and for all?" Yuanmao said: "These days I am poor past bearing. I want to borrow a few strings of cash of you — there, that's the whole of it." Ziyu said: "And was that worth so much ceremony? Speak to the accounts room — it comes to the same thing." And he made to go; but Yuanmao caught hold of him: "Good fellow, good fellow — you must send Yun'er to say a word for me. I have borrowed of the accounts room before, and am ashamed to go again for fear of a snub." There was no help for it: Ziyu called Yun'er in once more and sent him to speak to the accounts room; and when word came back that it was agreed, Yuanmao at last let Ziyu go.

What with all this entanglement, his watch showed the first quarter of the si hour. Ziyu hurried onto the cart and made for the Great East Gate. The way was long, and by the time he was out of the city it was the beginning of the wu hour. Sulan had long since arrived ahead of him, and had besides posted a man at the head of the road to watch. Before long, catching sight of Ziyu's cart approaching, he came up the bank in his neat and elegant dress to welcome him as he alighted, paying his respects and asking after his health. They boarded the boat together, where Sulan relieved Ziyu of his hat and outer robe, and himself changed into informal dress. Ziyu thanked him: "I am deeply touched by your elegant kindness, so complete in every point; my lingering ailment lifts from me at a stroke, and my breast is opened wide. Receiving such generous feeling, how am I ever to repay it?"

Sulan smiled: "My poor services have fallen short. As luck would have it, Yunong's illness has taken a turn for the worse today, and he cannot come out. Yesterday, besides, two ruffians gave him a savage tongue-lashing, and rage has made him iller still. Having invited you yesterday, I could hardly send round today to cry off; so we two must simply sit together a while, and then part. With Yunong so ill, my own spirits are none of the best. In short, 'good things are ever crossed' — there is not a hair's error in the saying." These few words fell upon Ziyu like ice-water poured over his body. He was silent, staring blankly at Sulan a long while; then he sighed and said: "So today's affair has indeed turned out thus — not a jot beyond what I foresaw. Xiangwan, the pity is only that you have spent your pains for nothing: a luckless man like me is not permitted to enjoy them. You do not know how last night, because of that storm of rain, I lay amid a thousand griefs and ten thousand cares, knowing already that today I should never see Yunong. That I forced myself to come notwithstanding was, first, for this beautiful kindness of yours, which must not be betrayed; and second, because Heaven sometimes deals in winds and clouds past all reckoning, and something unlooked-for might yet fall out — who could say? But no: in this world the thing one longs for is not to be had for any dreaming, while the thing one dreads comes at the first thought. That Yunong could not come I had long foreseen; what I cannot tell is whether Yunong at this moment guesses that I came out, or guesses that I did not. If he guesses I did not come, well and good; but if he guesses I did, then I fear the heaviness on him at this hour outweighs mine by no small measure."

As he spoke his eyes had already reddened, and he shook his head, saying: "It is strange — it is strange indeed." Sulan could scarcely keep from laughing, and said to him: "Come, let us go together now and find Yunong, and see how his illness does." Ziyu considered a moment and said: "Better not. Since I cannot meet him even here, some fresh hindrance would be sure to spring up there, and I should not see him even so." Sulan said: "He lies sick in his bed — how should you fail to see him?" Ziyu said: "The other day when you and I went together — was not Yunong sick in his bed then? Afterward I went twice more, and never saw him. If I go again today, I most surely shall not see him." At this point his tears fell, and he went on: "Yunong! Yunong! Between you and me, it seems, there was fate for that one meeting only." Then, turning to Sulan: "I, for my part, can see through it and think it through; only you must persuade him to see through it too, and think it through too — it will spare him many a sorrow." Sulan laughed: "So now you are grown enlightened! But suppose Yunong had come out today, sick as he is, to see you — would you still see through it, or would you not? If you truly saw through it, you would naturally speak your mind plainly to him, and thenceforth neither side need pine for the other. If you cannot see through it, then each will go on longing as before. At this moment, not having met him, you think with perfect clearness; I fear that once you meet, your thinking will cloud again." Ziyu had no word to answer. Sulan laughed once more: "Since Yunong could not come himself, he has found a substitute to meet you — will you see him or no?" Ziyu asked: "What sort of person is he? Does he know me?" Sulan said: "One of our own troupe, whose face is much like Yunong's. Yunong means no more by it than that you should gaze at plums to quench your thirst. What do you say — shall I have him out?"

Ziyu pondered a while, then said: "If he is unlike Yunong, I might well meet him; but if he is like Yunong — I committed affront enough that day at Yiyuan; why set the maid to ape her mistress a second time? Not only am I unwilling to see this person: for Yunong's own sake I could not bear it. Xiangwan, you are a man of understanding and will surely see my meaning — this is no affectation of nicety in me." Sulan said: "What you say is all very well; but though you will not see him, I mean to call him out all the same." Ziyu was still trying to prevent him when Sulan called out from the rear cabin a figure lovely as flower and jade. At first sight Ziyu was in some confusion: for one thing, he had conversed with Qinyan but a single time; for another, these past months had worn Qinyan's beauty sadly thin. Once he had been the very glory of the nation and fragrance of heaven, lucent, full, and splendid; now he was like fallen petals that say nothing, a figure grown pale as the chrysanthemum. But by the time Ziyu saw him clearly, Qinyan had already hidden his face in delicate weeping, his ice-silk sleeve soaked through, and sat turned away, doing nothing but cry. Ziyu said: "This is strange — is this not Yunong himself? Xiangwan, why did you spin me that tale?" Sulan said: "Be sure you are not mistaken — is it he, or is it not?" Ziyu said: "How should it not be? Only somewhat wasted. That immortal maid of Miaogushe — could any common mortal counterfeit her?" Then he said: "Yunong, you need grieve no longer; all that is in your heart I know." Before he had finished, Qinyan checked his weeping and said: "Is your illness mended? I know you came to me several times; but I never once saw you, and so could hardly go to you. Only yesterday, when I read your letter to Xiangwan, did I understand it all at last — it is I who have done you harm." And with that he fell to weeping again. Ziyu said: "I have no great illness — only a certain unease of body. And I know how to take care of myself. If you too will only see things a little more lightly, mending will come easily." And his own tears ran down as he spoke. Qinyan said: "Had Xiangwan not come over yesterday, I should be dead by now, and you would not have seen me today." And he wept anew. Ziyu could not fathom what he meant, and only watched Qinyan — like a pear-blossom laden with rain, so soft and tender he seemed ready to fall.

And seeing how with every phrase he spoke came a fresh sob, Ziyu felt a rush of bitterness drive straight up through his heart, and could hold back no longer, but wept too. It ended with Sulan quite at a loss: watching the two of them so desolate — for all the world like parting at death or separation in life — before he knew it he was weeping with them.

The three wept in one knot, till at last it was Sulan who first came to himself and urged them: "I asked you here today for a day of pleasure — why all this sobbing and wailing? Half the day is gone already, and before evening we must race for the city. We have but a few hours left for gladness; better we should all laugh a little." Ziyu forced himself to assent: "Xiangwan is quite right; Yunong, grieve no more." Qinyan said: "And what is there to laugh over? From our one meeting at Yiyuan to this day is five months. For a second glad meeting we may well wait a year; and within that year, who knows whether I can last to see it? Most likely this hour ends it all." So saying, he wept again. Ziyu comforted him: "No matter — only let your health mend, and we may meet every day; why speak of a year?" Qinyan wept on: "Mend I might; only I fear this Wei Pincai will not suffer me to mend — nothing short of my death will content him." Ziyu was startled: "You must not wrong Wei Pincai there — behind your back he praises you to the skies." Qinyan stamped his foot: "You do not know yet! If he spoke well of me, he would not have spread slanders about you, nor sent men to raise a riot at my door." Ziyu, quite in the dark, asked again: "Of all this I understand nothing. How has Pincai made slanders? And how came he to raise a riot?" Qinyan said: "Ask him, and you will know." Then Sulan recounted in detail all that Pincai had said that day, till Ziyu's face turned ashen and rage left him speechless. After a long pause he said: "Strange! Strange! He lived half a year under my roof, and I never once offended him — why must he trample me to such a pass? And how comes it that entering Duke Hua's mansion has turned him wicked? It passes all dreaming. Henceforth I have done with him, that is all. But this sending of men to raise a riot — what was that?" Sulan and Qinyan, hearing that Pincai had entered Duke Hua's mansion, understood at last that the row at the Chunyang Restaurant had been his doing too — and then yesterday's affair no longer left room for doubt. Sulan then recounted also the two men's cursing of the day before, and how he himself had been chased. Ziyu, hearing it, cursed and raged by turns, and could not help weeping once more.

By this time the boat was under way, and Sulan's servants set out the wine and dishes. Sulan poured the wine, urging and coaxing; Ziyu and Qinyan could only take their seats, but with sorrow flooding up in them they sat wordless, face to face — what heart had they left for drinking? Under Sulan's patient pressing they forced down a few cups, but it was gaiety worn as a mask, and they scarcely knew why they kept up the pretense. In Qinyan's heart the feeling would not leave him that this was a parting of the living from the dying — this one meeting, and after it, no more. Ziyu likewise felt that fate had set itself against them, and made certain they could not often meet. So both hearts, thinking their way to the very end of things, bred sorrow of themselves — in this all human hearts are alike; only in feeling do some go to the uttermost and some fall short.

By now the boat had traveled three or four li; the three sat quietly, drinking clear wine for a time. As Ziyu held his cup, he gazed at Qinyan, who appeared like a rose washed in dew, or a peony veiled in mist—truly a figure of the same immortal stripe as Prince Qiao or Master Shi. Merely to sit beside him felt like a profound karmic blessing, far beyond vulgar fortune. He then looked at Sulan, who possessed a different kind of loveliness—graceful in bearing, refined and elegant in every gesture—truly a perfect match of pearl and jade. This gathering today felt like a once-in-a-lifetime chance; sudden joy welled up, and their sorrows gradually dissolved. Qinyan, in turn, saw Ziyu as a tree of jade and coral, an autumn moon in an ice-pure vessel; his deep and tender affection was unlike anyone else's. He was indeed the powdered He Yan, the incense-scented Xun Yu; let alone the elegant Wang and Xie clans, even a whole flock of Black-Clad youths could hardly match him. If he could only form a bond of incense with him, to be confidants of flowers and moonlight, it would be something unachievable even in several lifetimes of cultivation. Even if they only had this one or two fleeting encounters, his heart was deeply satisfied. Gradually, their gazes flowed and mingled, warming their icy hearts.

Sulan watched the two of them facing each other, words forgotten but feelings encompassing and complete. Their brows were silent yet wishing to speak; their eyes just met then pulled away. They were truly a perfect pair, their hearts tied in a single knot, as if putting all the gifted scholars and beautiful ladies in the world to shame. No wonder this one pined morning and night, while that one forgot food and sleep. Since both were born with such qualities, heaven was naturally jealous, decreeing they must part often and meet rarely. If they were allowed to be together every day, it would not show heaven's intent to cherish and hide them away. Feeling deep admiration, Sulan walked over and sat at Ziyu's side, warmly and gently offering him three cups of wine, then offering one to Qinyan. At this moment, the deep affection among the three was perfectly complete; it was as if they had become the Boundless Celestials of the Pure Land. They only wished Duke Yang of Lu would swing his halberd in fierce battle once more, forcing the red sun to roll backward and refusing to let it set.

Just at the height of this fullness of happiness, a boat was seen approaching from ahead; at a distance came the sound of silk-string and wind instruments.

Listening again, they heard hurrying pipes and thronging strings, and wanton, garish songs. In a little while the boat came rowing past, and Ziyu, peering through the cabin blind, saw three men in it, blowing and drumming away at full blast, every one of them stripped to the skin; one held a xiaodan in his arms, and the pair were drinking mouth-about from one cup, while two more xiaodan sat at the side, one plucking the strings, the other singing. The din of their merriment was like quick thunder bursting from the earth, their wild laughter like a torrent racing down the rapids — so that Qinyan in alarm made to hide in the rear cabin, and Ziyu let down the boat window, wondering who they might be. Sulan, looking through the crack of the window, said to Qinyan: "Come and see." Qinyan came and peeped in his turn, then said: "Those vulgar louts — what is there to look at?" Sulan pointed to the one seated in the lower place: "That is the Pan San who kept pestering Meixiang." Qinyan cried: "Aiyo! Such a creature — a wonder Meixiang could endure his acquaintance; and however did he contrive to gull him?" Sulan said: "You did not see them — yesterday's two were ten times fiercer than he!" Qinyan sighed, walked back, and sat down. Ziyu asked: "What manner of man is this Pan San?" Sulan told over the whole story of them, and Ziyu exclaimed again and again: "Detestable! Detestable! That this Pan San should dare nurse such wild designs! It was lucky he met Su Meixiang; had it been another, the fellow would likely have ruined him." Then he asked Qinyan: "Do you know those xianggong?" Qinyan said: "I know not one of them — what troupe are they?" Sulan said: "I know them all. The one held in his arms is Yumei of the Dengchun troupe; the one at the strings is called Chunlin, and the singer Fenglin — both of the Fengtai troupe." Ziyu said: "Look at them at their pleasures — what pleasure is really in it? And were they to see this scene of ours, no doubt they would call it dreary and flavorless."

Sulan laughed: "Each person finds pleasure in their own way — if they did not behave like this, they would not consider it pleasure." Looking on, the red sun was nearing the western horizon. Ziyu's heart at this moment was full of joy — he almost felt the pleasure of having forgotten to return.

Qinyan knew in his heart that the day was growing late, yet could not bear to hurry the parting. Sulan, fearing that if it grew later they could not enter the city, called to the boatmen to ply their oars faster, for the hour pressed; and meanwhile they began to pack up. Ziyu then divided the things he had brought between the two of them, and they, unable to refuse, accepted. Ziyu also shared out the loose silver from his bundle in rewards to Sulan's and Qinyan's servants, saying, "You have all had a weary day"; and the company kowtowed their thanks for the bounty.

The boat came to the Great East Gate, and they went each to his own cart. Ziyu held Qinyan's hand and said: "We shall meet again another day; in all things you must learn to console yourself" — and his tears fell once more. Qinyan answered, choking: "Go, and set your heart at rest; the gates will soon be closing. When we meet, it will be either at Xiangwan's or at Yiyuan — one place or the other." Ziyu nodded, and could only harden his heart as each mounted his cart. The carters, fearing the hour, laid on a lash apiece, and away they rattled at a gallop.
