Chapter 22 — Under an unlucky star, plain hearts weep in double grief; touring the canal, Mei and Du renew their bond.

In the previous chapter, Sulan promised to arrange a meeting between Ziyu and Qinyan, but Dragon Boat Festival duties and several days of performances delayed everything. Qinyan waited in torment, while Sulan finally decided to rent a quiet boat outside the East Gate, furnish it tastefully, and invite only Ziyu for a day on the canal under Duxiang's name.

The letter reached Mei Ziyu while he sat in melancholy practicing easy qin passages. Seeing Sulan's message, he read it again and again with rising joy, then wrote an earnest reply promising to come at the appointed hour and sent it back immediately.

When Sulan entered Qinyan's compound he heard shouting and, peering through a window crack, saw two rough men cursing and threatening the household, demanding Qinyan come out.

Qinyan's attendants pleaded that he was truly ill and begged for a few days' grace, offering tea, tobacco, and apologies while trying to calm the men.

The men began extorting money; two strings were refused as too little, so the household added more and finally got them to leave.

As Sulan fled after being spotted, one thug chased and insulted him, then questioned the servants about who he was and where he lived.

The servants lied that Sulan belonged to another troupe and lived far away; the two thugs staggered off while Qinyan inside burned with rage.

They were later identified as hirelings connected to the Hua mansion, bought by Wei Pincai to create trouble.

Sulan returned shaken and nearly in tears, then, unable to rest, went to Qinyan's room, where he found him sobbing behind a curtain and speaking despairingly of death and burial under Yiyuan's plum trees.

Qinyan's grief drew Sulan into tears as well; both wept uncontrollably.

They cried together for a long while, and Sulan reflected bitterly that heaven seemed jealous of beautiful and gifted people.

Their crying alarmed the household. After attendants briefly soothed them, Sulan asked about the attackers, and the two concluded Wei Pincai was the likely instigator.

Qinyan asked what grudge Wei could hold. Sulan warned him not to give up on life, then showed him Ziyu's reply. Qinyan first misunderstood the address, then read the contents, smiled through tears, and agreed to come next morning with warm clothes in case of canal wind.

By overnight the illness had retreated by eight-tenths, but a new ailment appeared: the more he wanted to sleep, the less he could.

At fourth watch a violent storm broke with thunder and lightning, and only at dawn did the rain finally stop.

Exhausted, Qinyan slept and dreamed thugs seized him as he tried to board the boat; he woke in cold sweat to full daylight.

A parrot's words frightened him again, but it was only Sulan's messenger coming to fetch him. He washed, took nourishment, packed clothes, and set out.

At Sulan's, he asked about Ziyu; Sulan said Ziyu was already guided to the boat and preparations were being finished with his usual careful attention.

On Ziyu's side, he too had endured a sleepless stormy night, then at dawn prepared gifts instead of money so as not to insult Sulan.

He selected silk, small valuables, and silver, received his mother's permission to go out, and was warned to return early and avoid chill.

Li Yuanmao delayed him at the door, first stalling and then asking to borrow money; Ziyu had the accounts room handle it before finally escaping.

By the time Ziyu reached the East Gate area it was late morning. Sulan greeted him formally, brought him aboard, and both changed into lighter clothes.

Sulan smiled: "I have not served you well — Yunong is worse today and cannot come out. Moreover, yesterday two ruffians gave him a thorough verbal thrashing, which has left him quite ill from rage. Having invited you today and not wishing to send word to cancel, I thought we two could sit a while together and then I will find another time to bring you two together."

With reddened eyes Ziyu declined going to visit Qinyan, saying fate would only frustrate them again. Sulan teased him about detachment and hinted at a "substitute" companion.

Ziyu objected, but Sulan called someone from the rear cabin. It was truly Qinyan, thinner and weeping. They spoke in sorrow, each blaming himself, and soon both were crying again.

Seeing each other cry made both men cry harder, and Sulan was moved to tears as well.

Sulan finally urged them to stop weeping and use the remaining daylight. Qinyan lamented Wei Pincai's persecution; Sulan recounted Wei's slanders and yesterday's assault, leaving Ziyu shocked and furious.

The boat moved on as wine and food were served, but Ziyu and Qinyan drank only under persuasion, each feeling this meeting resembled a painful farewell.

After several li the mood softened; Ziyu admired Qinyan's delicate beauty and Sulan's grace, while Qinyan gazed at Ziyu's refined tenderness. Their glances slowly warmed their hearts.

Sulan watched them and thought them a perfect pair envied by heaven itself, toasting them gently while all three wished time could stop.

Just at the height of this fullness of happiness, a boat was seen approaching from ahead; at a distance came the sound of silk-string and wind instruments.

As it passed, they saw obscene drunken revelry. Sulan identified one man as Pan Third and named the actors aboard; Ziyu found the spectacle coarse and joyless.

Sulan laughed: "Each person finds pleasure in their own way — if they did not behave like this, they would not consider it pleasure." Looking on, the red sun was nearing the western horizon. Ziyu's heart at this moment was full of joy — he almost felt the pleasure of having forgotten to return.

Fearing gate closure, Sulan ordered faster rowing and packed up. Ziyu distributed gifts to Sulan and Qinyan and rewarded their attendants with silver.

At the East Gate they parted by carriage. Ziyu held Qinyan's hand with tears, and Qinyan, choking with sobs, promised they would find another way to meet.

Ziyu returned home; the lamps were already lit. Lady Yan asked where he had been; he could only give a vague answer to put her off. As for what happened next, listen to the following chapter to find out.
