Chapter 33 — Inspector Mei sends a family letter instructing his son; Young Master Hua bids farewell and dismisses his guests.

Shiji-shi took up his post, and Nanxiang went with him, planning to return the next year for the metropolitan exam. Famous scholars and celebrated actors saw them off, and after several more days of gatherings, time flew to mid–twelfth month. Ziyu, with Nanxiang and Gao Pin gone from the capital, had lost two close companions. The previous month, Academician Wang had told Lady Yan that Ziyu’s marriage would be completed either at winter’s end or in early spring.

Lady Yan replied she could not decide alone and must write to Jiangxi first; only after receiving a reply could they settle the matter. Because of this, Ziyu also stopped visiting the Wang household as often. Xu Ziyun had recently filled an official vacancy and had more office duties, so he could no longer stay in the garden every day.

One day, with clear weather, melting snow, and calm wind, Ziyu planned to visit Pincai and inquire about Qinyan.

After breakfast he informed his mother and rode toward the city, but before going half a li he began to hesitate, thinking Pincai might not even be home. The more he thought, the gloomier he became, so he said, “Forget it—turn back out of the city.” Yun’er turned the horse, and as they came out they met a traffic jam of carriages and camels. Amid the shouting, Ziyu’s curtained carriage drew abreast of another. Looking through the glass window, he saw the other passenger turn as well—it was Baozhu. Smiling, Baozhu asked where he was coming from and where he was headed. Ziyu said he was returning from town and going nowhere now. Baozhu invited him to his lodging for a chat, saying they had not met in two months. Since it was still early, Ziyu agreed. With Ziyu’s carriage in front and Baozhu’s behind, they reached Baozhu’s place, got down, and Baozhu ushered him inside.

It was Ziyu’s first visit. In the inner court he saw three main rooms; the western room was the study, with a plaque reading “Little Lang… Studio.” Inside, fragrance filled the air and there was not a speck of dust. Two pots of narcissus were in full bloom. On the table stood an antique bronze vase with nandina and wintersweet; nearby were more potted blooms. The calligraphy and paintings on the walls were all genuine works of earlier masters, nothing vulgar. In a niche hung Baozhu’s own portrait “Leaning on Bamboo,” and beside it four flower panels painted by Jin Su, his painting instructor. Ziyu sighed: “How strange fortune is. A man of such talent suffers such disaster—does Heaven truly envy talent this way?” He told Baozhu he had heard of Baozhu’s loyalty and support, especially after that gentleman’s misfortune, and praised his rare sincerity. Baozhu stayed silent, eyes reddening; he sighed, quoted, “We are both fallen people at the ends of the earth—why must prior acquaintance matter?” and began to cry. Realizing he had touched a sore point, Ziyu quickly changed the subject, pointing to bamboo outside and joking that only in winter could one see bamboo’s noble character beside decorative flowers. Baozhu laughed through tears. Ziyu then remarked how quickly the year had passed. Baozhu said he had heard from Jiantan that Ziyu would likely be congratulated after New Year and asked whether they would be invited to wedding wine. Ziyu said nothing was fixed until a family letter arrived. Baozhu then invited him to stay for dinner and said he would call Xiangwan (Sulan) to join them.

Ziyu hesitated: if he stayed for dinner he would return too late. He suggested instead that, after the recent heavy snow, they should boil snow-water for tea and invite Sulan for a refined talk. Baozhu laughed and agreed, saying Ziyu was always different from others. He sent for Sulan and had a brazier, tea-stove, and full tea set brought out. A large bronze tray was laid down first, then an antique bronze stove was set on it, live coals kindled, and snow-water poured into a blue porcelain bowl and a carved Yixing kettle. Ziyu admired it and said the tea was not yet brewed, yet the sight already cleared one’s mind. Sulan arrived. He joked to Baozhu that such elegance must be for elegant guests, and adding a vulgar man like himself might spoil it. Baozhu said Sulan was elegant enough. Sulan asked Ziyu why he had stayed home lately and whether he had met Yunong (Qinyan). Ziyu said no; at present Yunong could hardly come out, and he had not expected Yunong to end up settled in that household. Baozhu replied that perhaps it was not truly Yunong’s settled place, since someone with Yunong’s aspirations could not bear long humiliation. Ziyu said he had heard Yunong was treated well. Baozhu said “well” was relative: Young Master Hua was not truly considerate, and if even Duxiang’s devotion could not fully satisfy Yunong, how could that side do as much?

“The situation there is different in every way,” Baozhu said. “Everything is rule-bound, hard for someone used to freedom. On the fourteenth of the eighth month I saw Yunong come out to attend people—it was clearly forced.” Sulan added that now even when Yunong saw them, he still seemed depressed and closed-hearted. Ziyu sighed. When the water boiled, Baozhu personally mixed four famous teas in a jade tea vessel, brewed them, and poured three pale porcelain cups. Ziyu thanked him for the trouble. After tasting, both Ziyu and Sulan praised the fragrance and complexity. Baozhu explained he had blended several teas together. He then served fruit as accompaniment. They chatted, and Sulan asked whether Ziyu had seen his old family friend Wei Pincai recently. Ziyu said not for two months; he had in fact intended to visit him today but turned back. Baozhu teased that he should have gone—if Pincai were absent, “the one in your heart” would surely come out upon hearing Ziyu had arrived. Ziyu said nothing. Sulan then asked if he knew Pincai’s recent scandal. Ziyu asked what happened. Sulan said that although he had hated Pincai for sending men to harass Yunong, he had thought Pincai merely petty, not deeply evil—yet now Pincai had himself been disgraced and could hardly face people. Ziyu asked in shock what could be so shameful. Baozhu asked too. Sulan said friendship nowadays was dangerous because many people looked human but were beast-hearted: did they not know Wei Pincai had been seized in a prostitution case and sent to the Ministry of Punishments? Ziyu was stunned and asked why it had gone that far. Sulan said Zhang Zhongyu, now a command official, had told him about it four or five days ago.

That night Pincai had invited Lord Fu Third to drink at Rongguan’s residence. Fu remembered other business and returned to the city first. Pincai stayed, had Rongguan summon a woman called Yu Tianxian, and planned to pass the night there. Near second watch they were still drinking and singing when clerk Yu Taisun came on night inspection. He knew Pincai and had dined and watched opera with him before. Pincai relaxed, invited him to sit, and joked with him when he refused. Yu suddenly turned stern: “Old Wei, no joking today. Do you understand official business must be done officially?” Pincai thought he was still joking and replied that even high officials drank with courtesans; why put on the inspector’s airs? He even tugged him to sit. Yu snorted: “Not just you—even if our chief censor were sitting here tonight, I’d arrest him.” At once constables chained Pincai and dragged him out; others shackled Rongguan and Yu Tianxian. Pincai, in a brand-new outfit, was tied behind a cart and made to run. At the clerk’s office he was interrogated harshly. Fortunately Pincai’s servants found a legal copyist, negotiated at 1,600 strings, wrote bonds, and found guarantors before the shackles were removed. They fabricated testimony: “Wei Pincai” became “Li Sancai,” and the gathering was recast as an ordinary meal where Rongguan’s married sister happened to visit, causing mixed company but no illicit banquet.

Ziyu said, “If the facts were clarified that way, why was it still sent up?” Sulan answered that a city patrol superior discovered the clerk had extorted money, altered testimony, and privately released suspects, so the case was reopened and sent to the Ministry. Baozhu asked whether Pincai was now jailed; “Serves him right—but why didn’t Young Master Hua intervene?”

Sulan said Zhongyu reported the matter had been concealed from Young Master Hua, and the false name made it harder to trace at first. Pincai would lose face regardless; perhaps the criminal penalty would not be severe, but he had recently purchased a rank and might lose that title if convicted. Ziyu, worried for him, said this was terrible; even their acquaintance Li had recently made a fool of himself in similar debauchery, and now Pincai too had caused scandal. Sulan replied it was deserved retribution—he once used coachmen to torment others, and now it returned doubled. Ziyu laughed in spite of himself.

After chatting for a long while, Ziyu saw it was getting late, took leave of the two, and returned home.

At home, Lady Yan looked displeased. Ziyu dared not ask and stood silently. She said a family letter from his father had arrived and his father now knew all of Ziyu’s conduct, blaming her for failing to discipline him; she handed Ziyu the letter. Before reading it he was already terrified. After reading, his face flushed bright red; he stood speechless, only looking at his mother. Pitying him but forcing sternness, she said if he knew fear he should not have done such things in the first place; whether he corrected himself was up to him, but when his father returned he might not be so lenient. Ziyu could only answer “Yes, yes” repeatedly, neither daring to sit nor withdraw. Unable to comfort him directly, Lady Yan asked whether he had seen Wei Pincai today.

Ziyu hesitated; when pressed, he said he had not seen Pincai and had heard Pincai had been accused and jailed at the Ministry. Lady Yan was alarmed and demanded who said this and for what reason. Ziyu said Zhang Zhongyu, a relative of Pincai, had said so—apparently a case involving drinking with prostitutes and arrest by ward officers. Lady Yan cursed him as shameless and wondered whether the Hua household did nothing. Ziyu said Pincai often stayed outside the city, had used a false surname Li, and likely kept the matter hidden from Hua; people also said he had recently purchased a minor rank. Though he called himself Li Sancai, everyone knew he was Wei Pincai. Lady Yan flushed with anger and complained all these young men were worthless; even Li was rarely home and likely up to no good. Still, though Pincai was not worth much, he was the son of her husband’s old associate, so they should verify facts and try to help. She summoned servant Mei Jin and told him to visit Elder Wang by marriage and ask for intervention at the Ministry. Mei Jin said he would go, but noted the detention was unusually strict—family could not even deliver food—so Wang might refuse to intercede; perhaps the Hua side would handle it. Asked what he knew, Mei Jin recounted the whole story in detail. Lady Yan said regardless, they must do their part, and sent him anyway. After he left, she coldly remarked this was the model outcome of frequenting actor households. Ziyu blushed with shame, sat quietly, attended his mother through dinner, and returned to his study. Frightened at heart, he dared not indulge wild thoughts for several days.

Mei Jin went to the Wang residence. Wenhui received him, heard the request, and sneered: he had known at first sight Wei Pincai was no good; they had insisted on keeping him, and now at least he was gone. With no personal channels at the Ministry, he could only ask others and let fate decide. Mei Jin reported this honestly on return; Lady Yan had no choice but to let events run their course. Meanwhile, in jail, Pincai promised Rongguan and Yu Tianxian large sums to follow his testimony: that clerk Yu had invited them to drink and then demanded a loan of one thousand taels from Pincai; when refused, a quarrel followed.

Their statement continued: Yu had brought constables in advance, shackled them, and took them away, then recast it as a lawful night arrest while extorting money and forcing debt notes. Yu Tianxian further testified that Yu often visited her home to smoke and drink; half a month earlier he had sent invitations for a cash contribution, but when money did not arrive he held a grudge and arranged this arrest in retaliation.

They said that after the arrest Yu demanded another five hundred strings to alter testimony and release them. Rongguan testified the same. The Ministry held two hearings and cross-checked statements. Young Master Hua had by then learned of the affair; though inclined to ignore it, he felt uneasy and sent people to intercede, resulting in lighter penalties. Clerk Yu was dismissed; Pincai received twenty strokes; Yu Tianxian was expelled beyond jurisdiction; Rongguan was released home. The case was closed.

Pincai returned to town in high spirits, boasting that he had “won the lawsuit,” and went straight back to the Hua residence.

Gate attendants offered consoling words. Pincai swaggered that he had suffered no real grievance because all the officials were his friends, even the jail warden his close associate, and everything had been smoothed over through connections. He asked whether the young master knew of it; the attendants could only answer they did not know.

Back in his room, people came in groups to inquire after him, but no message came from Young Master Hua. Pincai took this as proof the master knew nothing and relaxed. On the third day Lin Shanzhi entered carrying a large wrapped package, set it down, and said only, “This is from the young master,” then left before Pincai could even thank him. The unusual manner disturbed him. He checked the wrapping: “Traveling gift, two hundred taels.” His heart jolted. After reflection he understood the meaning, but did not know how to respond and waited for a chance to clarify.

After waiting two days with no visitor—not even usual acquaintances Gu Yueqing and Zhang Xiaomei—he spent half the night deciding what to do. Next morning he wrote a thanks note: first gratitude, then an excuse that family matters at the Mei house required his temporary return; he would come back in spring. He delivered it through the gate office. About half an hour later the gatekeeper returned: the young master had read it and agreed returning to Mei house was appropriate; he was too busy to see him off personally. Pincai had still hoped for reconciliation; hearing this, he felt ashes in his heart. He thanked everyone for half a year’s care and said he would move out that day without formal farewell. With no choice, he packed, hired a large cart, and left the city with one horse, two pages, a cook, and a driver, lodging temporarily at an inn before finding new quarters.

Pincai had stayed at the Hua house only ten months, yet through bluff and petty swindling he had amassed considerable money. Beyond regular stipend, Young Master Hua had given many gifts. Pincai spent over four hundred taels buying a minor rank, and roughly two thousand more on clothes and extravagance. Even so, after receiving that two-hundred-tael farewell gift he still had about three hundred taels and various valuables. Since his purchased rank stood high on the list and might be assigned within half a year, his confidence swelled. After two noisy days at the inn, he rented Chunhang’s old rooms at Hongji Temple, put on grand style with carriage and horse, and did not even visit the Mei house. Rongguan and Yu Tianxian came and went often; he gave each money and trinkets, feasting and drinking daily, naturally drawing a circle of similarly dissolute companions.

When Lord Fu Third heard of Pincai’s trouble, he had inquired at the Ministry but found no channel; only after hearing Young Master Hua had interceded did he relax. Later hearing Pincai had left Hua’s residence, he worried again and wanted to bring him home. One morning he went out to the temple and found commotion at the gate: a man in shabby sheepskin coat and old cap was cursing loudly, complaining he had served diligently yet was promised one hundred strings and paid only thirty, threatening revenge if called again. Fu recognized him as a prison guard and entered frowning. Pincai’s people announced him. Inside he heard clappers and noisy merriment. Rongguan came out with Pincai; Rongguan flirtatiously reached for Fu’s hand, and they joked roughly. Fu then congratulated Pincai and said he had worried much, and asked his plans: seek another post, stay outside the city, or come live with him through New Year? Pincai thanked him but said staying outside was more convenient for now. Fu asked if he had enough travel funds and advised economy since he had grown used to Hua-house luxury. Pincai agreed and offered to dine in-house. Fu suggested a restaurant, but Rongguan laughed that it was already the twenty-fifth of the twelfth month—restaurants had closed for year-end collections. Fu admitted his troubled mind had made him forget dates. Asked what troubled him, Fu said not New Year itself but his pending assignment: two vacancies had opened, Hubei and Guizhou. Hubei was desirable, Guizhou harsh. By order he should get Hubei, but a senior candidate had inserted himself. Guizhou’s revenues were said to be miserable. Could he lobby the Selection Office? Pincai said the temple monk had a brother who was a clerk in the Personnel Ministry selection bureau; perhaps they could ask him to reverse it. Fu agreed and asked Pincai to speak to the monk on his behalf, promising heavy thanks if successful. Pincai said the monk was easy to talk with—hardly monk-like, adept in drink, women, gambling, singing, and even venereal treatment, lively and useful.

People called him Master Tang, though he preferred “Brother Tang.” As Pincai spoke, the wind-door creaked and a head in a brown felt cap with thick mustache peered in, then withdrew. Pincai called him in. He first declined, then entered when told Lord Fu was present and wished to meet him. Smiling broadly, the monk greeted Fu as a great man; Fu returned the courtesy. Tang sat and looked Fu over. Fu in turn noted Tang’s odd appearance: short and stocky, blue monk robe, yellow sash, gray fur-lined shoes, crafty lines on his face, lustful eyes, and a white-jade snuff bottle. They exchanged snuff. Tang claimed old ties: his late master had been close to Fu’s father, who used to visit the temple and play go; a calligraphic couplet by Fu’s father still hung there. He asked where Fu currently served. Fu said he had worked in Revenue and now had purchased a prefectural appointment awaiting assignment. Tang asked province; Fu said undecided between Hubei and Guizhou. Tang immediately declared Fu would surely get Hubei—his own native place—and laughed. Pincai said Tang should stay for the meal and discuss a matter. They spoke privately; Fu could hear enough to know Tang was promising full assistance. Tang then told Fu he would call his younger brother to handle things and guaranteed success without Fu’s worry. Just then a young novice of sixteen or seventeen entered, strikingly pretty, in fine warm monk attire with bright red-edged shoes, carrying a water pipe to pack tobacco for his master. Tang began smoking without yielding to guests. Fu stared at the youth with obvious desire. Rongguan, behind Pincai, made gestures teasing Fu, who smiled and asked Tang if this was his disciple, saying he seemed familiar.

Tang proudly said the novice was called Deyue, fifteen years old, passable at chanting and singing, but mischievous, so he was usually not allowed out. Fu, squinting with a smile, pretended to think and suddenly laughed, saying he had mistaken the boy for a nun from Great Compassion Convent because he looked so similar. Pincai roared with laughter; the novice blushed crimson. Tang laughed it off. Pincai joked that if dressed as a nun no one could tell. He praised Tang as a man of perfect pleasures in food, dress, use, and play. Tang replied with mock piety that monks were no saints now. His elder monk-brother had once restricted him, but after his death life became freer. The elder had hoarded rent and lived austerely only to be cheated by his nephew and die empty-handed, while Tang handled funeral matters. Better, Tang said, to enjoy life while alive; modern monks were meat-and-wine monks anyway, and he himself did not hide that he tasted drink, women, and gambling. Fu laughed and called him refreshingly frank. As they talked, Fu kept eyeing the lovely novice with growing lust, especially seeing him whispering closely with Rongguan on the same bench.

By noon, Pincai’s servants finally laid the table. Fu said not to make too much fuss; Pincai answered there was little to eat anyway. They took seats as host and guests; Fu had Deyue seated too. Tang told Deyue and Rongguan to pour wine. Seeing the table piled with snacks, Fu told them to remove most and keep only four dishes. Rongguan protested and snatched a dish of oranges and kumquats, saying others liked them. Nine hot dishes followed and were quite tasty. Rongguan fed Tang a meatball, then another; Tang swallowed both whole. Rongguan made a lewd joke, prompting laughter from Fu and Pincai. Tang returned with even grosser teasing and grabbed Rongguan. Rongguan pulled off Tang’s cap, rubbed his bald head, and asked what it looked like; Tang answered with obscene banter. Rongguan called him a “thieving bald monk.” After a few cups Deyue’s face reddened and he looked even more charming. Fu praised Deyue’s refined look; Rongguan teased whether Fu wanted to “take him as apprentice” (innuendo). They carried on boisterously. Eventually Tang’s opium craving hit, so he lit a pipe at Pincai’s place and smoked; the party dispersed only near late afternoon.

Fu returned to the city after again entrusting matters to Monk Tang; Rongguan also left. What happened afterward is told in the next chapter.
