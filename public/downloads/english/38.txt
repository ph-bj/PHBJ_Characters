Chapter 38 — On authenticity and forgery, annotations debate the Divine Yu stele; on omen counting, Taiyi numerology is refuted.

To return to our story: Xu Ziyun had invited Master Qu to attend, with Nanxiang, Zhongqing, Wenze, Chunhang, Wang Xun, and Ziyu as companions, again in the Plum-Blossom Hermitage. Wang Xun on this particular day had been called away by Sun Lianggong on an important matter: the wedding date had been fixed for Li Yuanmao, who was to be adopted into the Sun household as a son-in-law. Since Sun's two sons were not yet capable of managing such affairs, everything had been entrusted to Wang Xun, and so Wang Xun was unable to come.

Knowing that Qu Daosheng was a man of refined tastes who preferred quieter company, Ziyun had invited only four performers: Baozhu, Shufang, Huifang, and Sulan. Shufang was indisposed and unable to attend; as an added guest, Qinyan was also notified. That day Master Qu arrived first and spent a good while in conversation with Ziyun and Cixian, catching up on old times.

Let us briefly describe Master Qu's background. Master Qu was a native of Wuchang Prefecture in Hubei, a descendant of the Grand Officer of the Three Lü Clans [i.e., the poet Qu Yuan]. His learning spanned heaven and earth, and he had mastery of all six classical arts; yet his entire life had been beset by ill fortune and difficult circumstances, and outside of his books he had nothing at all.

His father had been appointed a compiler of the Hanlin Academy through the examination for broad learning in belles-lettres, but had died before reaching the age of thirty.

At that time Daosheng was only four years old. His paternal grandparents were still alive, and his grandmother bore many years of widowhood in fidelity, single-handedly both rearing and educating him. Daosheng was admitted to the prefectural school at the age of sixteen, only to observe mourning for his grandfather. When the three years of mourning were complete and he was about to sit for the higher examinations, his grandmother died, and he entered mourning once more for another three years. After completing that mourning period, his mother too passed away in quick succession. Daosheng had observed nine consecutive years of mourning and by the time it was over he was twenty-five. He then married a woman of the Min family, a paragon of virtue and wisdom. Making his living by his brush, Daosheng roamed as a private secretary and advisor to various officials, travelling through Yan, Zhao, Wu, Yue, and the provinces of Yunnan and Guizhou — tens of thousands of li in all, wandering for twenty years. He acquired a name in his time, and those who admired his character and talent compared him to Du Shaoling [Du Fu] and Meng Dongye [Meng Jiao]. Yet by nature he was high-minded and impractical, with no gift for managing household affairs, and he was constantly burdened by poverty. He later came to the capital to sit for the examinations but failed twice, took a post as a tutor in the household of Secretary Liu, where he taught Wenze for two years. He was then engaged by the young master of the Hua family to teach, remaining there another three years before finally returning to his native place. The local magistrates, admiring his virtuous reputation, recommended him through the "filial and upright" examination channel. He was selected as a county magistrate in Jiangxi — a hard and poorly rewarded post — and upon completing his term was promoted by memorial to assistant prefect of Nanchang. The year before, his wife had died, leaving him quite alone in the world, without a single close relation.

He had a few servants, all of them engaged through outside recommendations. Only one, his personal attendant Liu Xi, had been with him for five or six years and was a man of some conscience; the rest were of no particular consequence. Though Master Qu travelled light in the way of baggage, a lifetime of collecting had filled several trunks with bronze and stone antiques, precious books, and old paintings. When he arrived in the capital, Secretary Liu, mindful of their old connection, saw that Qu's official purse was thin and presented him with two hundred taels of silver.

Young Master Hua, learning of his arrival, came out of the city to pay a formal call and gave him three hundred taels. With five hundred taels in hand, Master Qu went round the antique shops and bought a good many books and famous rubbings. He also distributed a fair amount among impoverished friends of former times, so that only a little remained by now.

In former times, when Grand Secretary Xu had been in the capital, he too had been on friendly terms with Master Qu, had consulted him on various matters, and had engaged him from time to time to compose poems and prose pieces on his behalf — which is why Ziyun treated him with the deference due to an elder. Shi Nanxiang was a younger native of the same region, and it goes without saying that he was already acquainted with him.

Tian Chunhang had met him the day before, but Zhongqing and Ziyu were making his acquaintance for the first time. Seeing that otherworldly, Taoist-sage bearing, and mindful moreover that he was a friend of his father's generation, Ziyu naturally showed the deepest respect. Master Qu saw in Zhongqing a refined and spiritually luminous quality and knew him immediately for a man of exceptional character. Then he looked at Ziyu — warm and composed as a piece of jade, bright as the glow of a pearl, fine without and wise within, serene of spirit and grave of manner — and recognised that he was no mere person of outward graces alone. His heart was greatly pleased, and he thought to himself: "Old Mei Tie'an may indeed be said to have a worthy son." He spoke to Ziyu about matters in Jiangxi, saying, "Your honoured father strictly refuses to be swayed by personal connections and absolutely bars the door to bribes and gifts — the entire province compares him to Sima Guang and Wen Yanbo. The gratitude of the scholars and people goes without saying." He also asked about Ziyu's experience at the provincial examinations the previous year, and Ziyu answered each question in full. Qu saw that his speech was clear and gentle, his manner unassuming, and that he was evidently already a man of deep learning; he thought to himself that he would like to put him to the test, and would do so gradually over the course of the meal.

Just then the four performers arrived together. Huifang he already knew; all four came forward to pay their respects. Qu clasped his hands in return, bade them be seated, and looked them over carefully, asking the names of three of them. He said to Ziyun, 'The young performers in the capital today are even finer than in former times.' Ziyun said, 'Strictly speaking I ought not to have invited them to wait upon us today; but they are not entirely without refinement and can handle an ink-stone and unfurl paper. Besides, when they heard beforehand that you were coming, they wished to have a glimpse of a great man of letters. A few words of praise from your lips would be worth more to them than a handful of gold, and I trust you will not think me frivolous for the invitation.' Qu smiled and said, 'You speak as though I were the sort of stiff old "filial and upright" man who would object. Drinking wine and admiring beautiful people — what harm is there to one's character? Not to flatter you, but I think these four look nothing like theatrical entertainers. You of course know them intimately; I am meeting them for the first time. Let me venture a brief description of each, and you shall tell me whether I am right or wrong.' Everyone settled in to listen carefully to what he would say. Cixian said, 'I know you have a gift for physiognomy, Elder Brother. But spare us the predictions — tell us about the past instead. Talk of great careers and fine outcomes is something any of us can manage. If you can describe each person's actual temperament and nature, then I shall believe in your art.' The performers all smiled at this. Ziyun said, 'That may be beyond even his ability.' Qu said, 'It is not so difficult — let me tell you.' At this point the table had been laid. Ziyun offered wine, and the company was divided into two tables, east and west. Master Qu, needless to say, took the head of the east table.

The head of the west table was to be taken by Nanxiang, who said, 'This is a senior from my own region — how could I presume to take such a position?' And so Zhongqing was given that seat instead. Second on the east table was Nanxiang; second on the west was Chunhang. Third on the east was Ziyu; third on the west was Wenze. Ziyun presided at the east table while Cixian acted as second host at the west. Baozhu and Qinyan sat on the east side; Huifang and Sulan on the west. Everyone took their places. The host circulated wine and the guests each drank a few cups. Qu said, 'I shall begin with Su Meixiang, whom I met the other day.' Everyone at the west table listened attentively. Qu said, 'In my physiognomy I do not rely on complexion or the formal positions of features — those are secondary matters. What I observe is the spirit and bone-quality of the whole person, the bearing and movement, the manner of sitting and standing, and the voice and manner of speech, through which I distinguish refinement from coarseness and discern the heart, in order to judge a person's fortune. But my very first observation may be somewhat surprising: in my view Meixiang comes from a good family background — he is not an ordinary household's son. You will all be aware of this, no doubt — am I right?' Everyone felt a quiet surprise at this, suspecting that he had already inquired into Huifang's origins, and that this was why he had started with him, to display his skill. Cixian said, 'Let us not imagine you have been making inquiries about his background beforehand.'

Qu said, 'There is no need for inquiries. I can see that he is exceptionally perceptive, courageous, and spirited — a person who dares to act. Yet despite his good background, he clearly suffered greatly in his early years; what one might call an escape from the jaws of death. From what I can observe, within the next year or two he will undergo a significant change and leave his current profession. How he will fare in the end is still far off, and I shall not venture to say. If I did, Jingyi would dispute me again.' He then looked at Sulan and Baozhu — their general qualities were much the same — and at Huifang as well, finding no great difference; for these three he made no comment about their origins. He said, 'Emerging from the mud unsullied — that is the general impression these three give.' When his gaze reached Qinyan, Qu said, 'This one is rather different. Are you still in the troupe at present?' Cixian said, 'Still in the troupe — and a fifth-month pomegranate blazing before the eye at that: admired by sophisticated and common alike, at the very height of his fame.' Qinyan smiled at this. Qu said, 'Admired by the sophisticated, perhaps — but by the common, I rather doubt it. I see in him a proud and unyielding spirit that absolutely cannot bend itself to the times; and moreover a heart full of concerns for which nothing in his present life is truly suited. Were this person to have studied, he would turn out very like me — absolutely incapable of passing any examination.'  Everyone laughed, hearing how apt this was.

He then asked to see Qinyan's hand and examined it. 'A pity,' he said. 'The lines show talent — change your profession as soon as you can. You will not be wealthy, but yours is the path of the pure and lofty. Your heart is made of different stuff from other people's. What is not your own will is something you cannot endure even a moment's constraint of; what is your own will, you would embrace unto death without complaint. In matters of loyalty, fidelity, and duty, you would be capable of doing what others cannot. But one word of caution: if you direct your heart toward magnanimity, you can shape your own destiny; the only danger is that your allotted span of years may be insufficient. Yet through self-cultivation and attentiveness you can turn even that around.' Everyone who heard him speak felt the precision of what he had said and knew that his art was genuine and not mere talk. The words he had spoken struck Qinyan to the very core of his being, and he felt a great warmth towards the old man. Seeing too that Master Qu had about him an air of one who might at any moment ascend to the immortal realm, Qinyan felt an intimacy and affection toward him that grew by the moment.

Qu turned to Nanxiang, who was seated beside him, and asked, 'Has your honoured father made any notable achievements since taking up his post? Tell me of any good governance he has carried out.' Nanxiang said, 'My father has only recently taken a provincial post, and it has been just three months — he has not yet accomplished much. He did discover one powerful local magnate and two corrupt runners, which has greatly pleased the local people, and the Governor-General wrote to commend him with a few words of praise. Beyond that there is not much to report.' Qu said, 'I know your father is an upright and resolute man who will certainly achieve things. Powerful magnates and corrupt underlings — where are there none? In Jiangxi, when I first took my post, the local magnates and corrupt runners were particularly severe, and the people who had suffered at their hands were beyond counting. Seven successive county magistrates had all turned a deaf ear and blind eye, not daring to act against them — which only made the culprits bolder still. There was a local saying: "In the east village there is a tiger; in the west village there is a wolf. The tiger eats the people's flesh; the wolf eats the people's entrails. When wolf and tiger have devoured all but the last blood, the greedy serpents and starving scorpions are still not sated. The courthouse gates stand wide open, and serpents and scorpions come streaming in. The county magistrate sits on his dais like a clay idol, the scorpion climbing his back and the serpent coiled about his head." The wolf and tiger were the local magnates; the serpents and scorpions were the corrupt runners. The magnate of the east village had purchased a title as a garrison commander, and the one to the west was his own brother — one a successful military candidate, one a military student, with a band of thieves and ruffians at their command. They served as the ring-leaders of the criminal world, conspiring with the east-village tiger, manipulating lawsuits, and controlling the courts, with the serpents and scorpions acting as their go-betweens. By the time I arrived in my post I found that within the past three years seven county magistrates had been replaced, with more than two hundred cases of banditry and homicide outstanding. After half a year of painstaking effort I had dealt with these five individuals, after which the district became peaceful — not a single case of homicide or banditry has occurred since.' Ziyun said, 'That was no small achievement — the people who owe you gratitude extend far beyond a single county.' Qu said, 'I dare not claim credit. Whatever the district requires to be dealt with, I deal with; I put in the effort regardless of personal risk, and see what comes of it.' He then went on to discuss many other matters with the assembled scholars.

Ziyun noticed that one of the house servants who was bringing dishes had a louse crawling on the shoulder of his new jacket. He waited until the man had set down the dishes, then called him over and told him to pick it off. Qu immediately turned to Ziyu and asked, 'Since the young gentleman has read widely in the classics and histories, I wonder — in which text does this creature make its earliest and most venerable appearance? I need not say anything of it in poetry and miscellaneous writing.' Taken off guard by this question, Ziyu paused a moment in thought. "The character is indeed unusual," he reflected. "He asks which text has the earliest occurrence — so passing references like flicking lice or threading lice on a string need not be considered." He answered with graceful modesty: 'My learning is shallow and my reading limited, I fear. Of what I have encountered in histories and records, the earliest instance is probably the passage in Ruan Ji's "Discourse on the Great Man" — "How does the gentleman dwelling within the four seas differ from a louse dwelling within a [trouser-seam]?"' Nanxiang said, 'There is also the line from the Records of the Grand Historian: "The horsefly attacking an ox cannot pierce a nit."' Qu said, 'Both of these come after a passage in the Book of Lord Shang. In antiquity there was an office called the "lice official," which is recorded in the Book of Lord Shang. The Bibliographical Treatise of the Han Shu records that the Book of Lord Shang was transmitted in twenty-nine chapters, three of which were later lost, leaving only twenty-six in circulation. Among them, benevolence, righteousness, ritual, and music are referred to as the offices of lice. Du Mu of the Tang dynasty inscribed a version of this passage on the reverse of the stone stele at the Confucian temple in Chuzhou, writing: "That Shang Yang was able to cultivate and fight, was able to enforce his laws, and thereby laid the foundation of Qin's greatness, this he called the office of the benevolence-and-righteousness lice." The idea is that benevolence and righteousness arise naturally from the human heart, just as lice arise naturally from human grime. The meaning of the character for louse seems to connote something easily bred and densely proliferating — though I am not certain this is correct.' Nanxiang and Ziyu bowed in admiration. Cixian said, 'Master Qu is opening his trunk of books today — fortunately the guests at this table are all capable of receiving instruction. If it were just me alone, I would not give you permission to lecture.' Qu laughed and said, 'You are all accomplished men of letters, and there is no book you have not read. I am an old fogey — how would I dare "play the craftsman before the master carpenter"? Besides, in my younger days my learning was all from hearsay, and whatever I heard I immediately forgot — by now none of it remains.' Ziyun said, 'The other day Cixian mentioned having seen a collection in your collected works called the Waking and Sleeping collection — do you have it with you?'

Qu said, 'The woodblocks for that were long ago split up for firewood. It is a worthless thing I wrote in my youth, without the least sense or reason.' Ziyun said, 'I also heard there was a section of couplets matching opera titles.' Qu said, 'There were several dozen — I cannot remember them now.' Cixian said, 'We ourselves produced a good many the other day,' and, gesturing toward Qinyan, Huifang, and Baozhu, he added, 'These three, along with one Wang Guibao, matched a great number of pairs — and their results were somewhat better than ours.' He sent a servant to his study to fetch a sheet of paper on which the results of that session had been written out, with the participants' names noted against each entry — including also the word-game from the earlier occasion. Qu read it through and praised it repeatedly. 'I would not have expected these four to be capable of this — they are truly of our own circle. I am genuinely fortunate to meet them today. In the theatrical world I have encountered many over the years — but they generally amount to dashing off a few orchid-and-bamboo sketches, smearing out a few seven-character quatrains, and even those half-baked at best. To find something like this — I have never seen it in my life. No wonder you gentlemen hold them in such affection. What a pity this old man was born forty years too early — otherwise I too might have joined the company of admirers.' All present, seeing his pleasure and appreciation, were thoroughly delighted.

On the other side of the table, Zhongqing asked, 'Your collection of bronze and stone inscriptions is extensive, and you are known for the precision of your assessments. Of seal script, clerical script, and carved steles, which examples do you consider the finest?' Qu said, 'Seal script is not much studied in recent times. Take the Heng Yue Stele, traditionally said to contain seventy-seven characters, located on the Miyun Peak of Mount Heng. As late as the Song, in the Jiading reign, a certain He Zhi visited the Southern Peak, made a rubbing of the text, and had it re-engraved at Yuelu; Yang Yongxiu had it re-engraved again in Yunnan; Yang Shiqiao re-engraved it yet again at Qixia — a chain of re-engravings about which I shall not say more. I once attempted a rendering of the text as follows:

"The Thearch spoke: Alas — with loyal support you serve my ministers." "Islets and shoals do you mount — the gateway of birds and beasts." "Your person entered the great flood, and in brightness Yu arose." "Long wandering, forgetting home, you lodged at the Yuelu courtyard." "Your wisdom shaped and your form yielded; your heart left nothing unattended." "You went forth to seek stability — Hua Yue and Tai Heng." "Ancestral succession the tasks assembled; your toil and your spirit [rested]." "Dense the blockages, confused the migrations — southward the floods abated and came to fruition." "An enduring provision of food was established; the ten thousand kingdoms found their peace; the wanderers danced and fled forever." [These are the seventy-seven characters in total.] Wang Yuanmei remarked: 'The inscription's phrasing does not accord with the holy classics — it resembles the language of Zhou dynasty bronze inscriptions or the Travels of King Mu.' This is a perceptive observation.

Next in order comes King Wu of Zhou's Copper Basin Inscription, which reads:

"To the left a forest, to the right a spring; behind a ridge, before a road." "For the peace of ten thousand generations, this place shall be treasured." [Can this truly be the language of the Three Dynasties? That it is a forgery admits of no doubt.] As for the Stone Drum inscriptions: Zheng Qiao's contention that they date from after the time of Duke Hui of Qin, and the three grounds for doubt advanced by Ouyang, are both insufficient. Wei Yingwu's attribution to the time of King Wen, with the poems engraved at King Xuan's command, and Ma Ziqing's claim that they were made during the Northern Zhou of the Yuwen clan, are even more baseless. Only the evidence cited by Dong and Cheng, which draws on the account in Zuo Zhuan of King Cheng's royal hunt at Qiyang, is solidly documented and reliable. After these, the Yishan Inscription of Qin was re-engraved in the Chunhua reign of Song by Zheng Wenbao — it still preserves something of the ancient seal style. Among Han clerical script, the finest is the Ritual Vessels Stele of the Confucian Temple, which stands first; next is the Han stele to Cao Jingwan — one being miraculous and wholesome in its primitiveness, the other rich and lofty in magnificence. As for the Memorials of Accession and Received Abdication stelae of the Wei, and the stele honouring Confucius, together with the Zhang Jun Panegyric by the Northern Wei Prefect of Luye, Li Zhongxuan's Stele for the Restoration of the Confucian Temple, and others of that kind — they show varying degrees of excellence and inferiority. Once Han clerical script had been lost, how much more so everything that came after.' Zhongqing expressed his admiration.

Chunhang said, 'The Preface to the Orchid Pavilion has been debated endlessly, and even the Dingwu edition is said to exist in two versions. Now that the question of authentic and spurious has been settled, how does one tell them apart?' Qu said, 'The Orchid Pavilion text was engraved in the Zhenguan reign of Tang Taizong. Before Taizong became emperor, while still the Prince of Qin, he obtained it from the monk Biancai. In the tenth year of Zhenguan he commissioned Tang Pu, Feng Chengsu, Zhuge Zhen, and Zhao Mo each to make traced copies to be presented to favoured ministers. At that time both Chu Suiliang and Ouyang Xun made their own hand-copies, which were universally admired. What is called the Dingwu edition is Ouyang's version. The Tang silk version is Chu's copy. In those days Ouyang's stone-engraving was kept in the imperial archives, but during the upheaval caused by Later Jin and the Khitan incursion, the stone was carried off and discarded at Shahukou. It was subsequently recovered by Prefect Li Jingwen of Dingwu, who placed it in his treasury. During the Xining period, when Xue Shizheng went out as governor, he had a second version cut to satisfy demand. This is why Dingwu has both a genuine and a spurious engraving. Xue Shizheng's son Xue Daozhu then made yet another copy on a different stone, secretly substituting this for the ancient original, and further chiselled away the characters 湍, 流, 带, 左, 右 in the ancient stele to create distinguishing marks. In the Daguan period an imperial order was issued to take it from his descendant Xiangneng for installation in the Xuanhe Hall; after the Jingkang catastrophe it was lost. Then in the Hongzhi reign of the Ming dynasty it was recovered from a Heavenly Master's hermitage and placed in the Imperial Academy, whereupon Ouyang's version came back into view. Chu's silk copy was in those early times widely distributed to prefectural academies throughout the land — the Yingshang stone and the Changzhi County stone both derived from it. Later, in the Ming period, a well in Yingshang gave off light at night like a rainbow, and the county magistrate, a man named Xun, thought it strange. He had the ground excavated and found the Orchid Pavilion, together with a bronze lock and several śarīra relics, which Magistrate Xun then took home. Where it has ended up since, nobody knows. As for the various hand-copied versions by different masters — they are beyond counting, and you gentlemen have your own discerning eyes; there is no need for me to say anything further.' 

Chunhang also said, 'People say that Han stelae and Song model calligraphy books each stand supreme for their era. The four great model books — Chunhua, Daguan, Jiang tie, and Tan tie — how do they compare?' Qu said, 'In my view, Chunhua ranks first, Daguan second, Jiang tie third, and Tan tie fourth. Yet Song connoisseurs often held Tan tie to be superior to the palace model books, and also maintained that Chunhua, being the first of its kind, was limited by the inadequate skill of Wang Zhu as the copyist and could not match the refinement of Daguan. Nevertheless, Chunhua has a quality of simple and unaffected vigour, while Daguan is brilliant but somewhat showy — compared in terms of poetry, Chunhua is the High Tang and Daguan the gradual decline toward the Late Tang.'

Everyone present expressed their admiration and assent.

Ziyu asked, 'A moment ago you drew the distinction between the High and Late Tang in poetry. In my own view, Li Bai, Du Fu, and Han Yu are the natural summit of Tang poetry. Yet Wang Anshi, in ranking poets, placed Du above Li, and in his selection of four poets — Du, Li [Bo], Ouyang [Xiu], and Li [Bai] — put Li Bai fourth. Yuan Zhen also held Du Fu superior to Li Bai, as his views make clear in the Epitaph for the Ministry of Works. Even given Li Bai's towering genius, it seems there are those who find him wanting in certain respects. Han Changli, on the other hand, wrote: "The writings of Li and Du endure, a blaze ten thousand zhang long. How can those foolish children know to what use their slanderous words are put? Trying to shake a great tree with the strength of an ant, a laughable lack of self-knowledge." This was clearly an expression of genuine and heartfelt admiration. How then should one arrive at a balanced judgement?'

Qu said, 'Poetry speaks to us through the natural affinity of our own spirits. Those whose hearts resonate with Li Bai will love Li Bai; those who share a kinship with Du Fu will love Du Fu; those fortunate enough to encompass both will naturally admire both. Yuan Zhen, with his careless, unpolished prose and rather drooping, slack-spirited verses, was hardly a kindred spirit to Li Bai; it was only natural for him to elevate Du Fu while diminishing Li. Wang Anshi argued that Li Bai possessed only a single style, while Du was capable of encompassing all styles. This criticism misses the fundamental truth that Li Bai was equally capable of encompassing all forms; he simply disdained to trouble himself with trivial stylistic constraints, which led people to suppose he could not manage them. When most people discuss Li Bai's poetry, they invariably praise his unbridled, headlong genius and the startling power of lines that seem to rend the heavens, much like his celebrated pieces "The Road to Shu" and the "Sky-Mother Mountain" poems, which have been widely imitated. For my part, I hold a special fondness for quieter masterpieces, such as "The Crow Perches at Dusk" and "The Crow Cries at Night." The "Crow Perches at Dusk" goes:

"When crows settle at dusk on the Gusu Terrace,
The King of Wu's palace holds the drunken Xi Shi."

"The songs of Wu and dances of Chu, the revelry not yet done,
And the western hills already clutching at half the setting sun."

"Silver arrow, golden vessel, the water-clock drips on and on;
Rising, I watch the autumn moon sink into the river's waves.
The east grows slowly bright: what can be done with so much joy?"

And the "Crow Cries at Night" goes:

"Yellow clouds by the city wall, the crow about to roost,
Flying home with harsh cries, perching to call on the branch."

"The woman of Qin, weaving brocade at her loom,
Speaks through the gauze blue as smoke that veils her window."

"She drops the shuttle, lost in longing for her man so far away,
Sleeping alone in an empty room, tears falling like rain."

Both of these poems demonstrate a lofty talent and a transcendent spirit that are in perfect harmony with Chen Zi'ang [Chen Zhuyi]. Moreover, Li Bai's own remarks on poetry are revealing: "Since the Liang and Chen dynasties, the poetic manner has grown effete and thin to the extreme; Shen Yue further imposed his tone-pattern rules. If the ancient way is to be restored, who but myself?" Consequently, he wrote very little regulated verse, and he often maintained that when it comes to conveying profound and subtle feelings, the five-character line is inferior to the four-character line, and the seven-character line the most dissipated of all. In my estimation, Li Bai's poetry reaches back to carry forward the ancient traditions, while Du Fu's poetry opens up the pathways for all that was to come. This is a subtle distinction, but it places both masters far beyond the grasp of those who merely tinker with tonal patterns and mechanical metrics. As for Han Changli's ancient-style verse, it connects directly to the timeless Odes and Hymns, echoing the enduring style of the Western Capital [Han dynasty]. His five- and seven-character ancient verse, in particular, delights in straining after the unusual, producing a hundred variations of wild eccentricity, transmitting what Li Bai and Du Fu never transmitted. Reading works like his "Southern Mountain" reveals a poetic force that challenges the supremacy of classical rhapsodies like the "Two Capitals" and the "Three Capitals". Those readers who lack both his vast breadth of learning and his sheer creative power see only pages of dense and jagged difficulty. For this reason, his poetry is sometimes dismissed merely as "literary prose," with some critics going so far as to claim that his rhymed writings are practically unreadable. Such a view is no different from hearing the divine music of heaven and complaining that the notes are poorly arranged. It must be noted, however, that his five- and seven-character quatrains and his regulated verse were not his primary concern; they represent just one facet of poetry's vast possibilities, and he never intended for future generations to learn the art of poetry solely from those shorter, simpler pieces.' This discourse was so cogent that everyone present nodded in agreement, and even Baozhu, Huifang, and the others were quite capable of following it.

Ziyu said, 'I have now received your instruction on the art of poetry. On the question of rhyme and the distinctions between standard, transferred, and merged rhymes — this system began to take shape in the Wei and Jin period. Thus Li Deng compiled his Rhymes for Poetry, Lü Jing his Collected Rhymes, and in the Qi and Zhou dynasties a work on the Four Tones and切韵 was compiled; Shen Yue of the Liang composed his Four Tones in one chapter, and so the rhyme tables were established. In the Sui, Lu Fayan, Liu Zhen, and others, drawing on Shen Yue's principles, compiled a further Expanded Rhymes; in the Tang, Guo Zhixuan produced his Rhyme-Cuts; Sun [Mian] compiled the Tang Rhymes; Ding Du and Song Qi compiled the Collected Rhymes. After the Jingyun era, a further Ministry of Rites Rhyme Table was produced; there were also Wang Zongdao's Rhyme-Cuts, Wu Yu's Supplemented Rhymes, and in the Yuan, Yin Shifu's Forest of Rhymes in Groups. With all these systems for combining or separating rhyme categories, which arrangement of rhymes is ultimately to be considered correct?'

Qu said, 'The debates in rhyme scholarship have each school drawing on its own authorities for the merged and transferred categories. Shen Yue established a standard for the eight regional dialects using the Yue dialect — how could that possibly cover all of them satisfactorily? And the same character may be pronounced one way by a dental and palatal speaker, yet another way when palatal and dental are reversed. Even the native dialects of the five regions are difficult to reconcile precisely — which is why the zhi and yuan rhyme groups are the most chaotic, and one genuinely does not know what regional speaker could pronounce them all as a single rhyme. In former times the rhymes were divided into two hundred and six groups; it was only from the Chunyou era onward that Liu Yuan of Pingshui consolidated them into one hundred and seven groups. The Expanded Rhymes counts twenty-six thousand one hundred and ninety-four characters; the Collected Rhymes counts fifty-three thousand five hundred and twenty-five characters; the Ministry of Rites Rhyme Table includes only nine thousand five hundred and ninety characters; Mao Huang's Augmented Rhymes added two thousand six hundred and fifty-five characters to the Ministry of Rites table; Liu Pingshui's Concise Ministry of Rites Rhymes added a further four hundred and sixty-three characters — and so the ancient books were thoroughly transformed. Critics have said that the failure of rhyme scholarship lay not in the division into two hundred and six groups, but in their consolidation into one hundred and seven groups. Yin Shifu, in turn, comparing his work against the Ministry of Rites table, Mao Huang, and Liu Pingshui, deleted more than three thousand one hundred characters, including some that were ancient and elegant but had fallen into corrupt popular usage. Huang Gongshao's Rhyme Treasury divided and combined according to Mao and Liu's systems and provided quite rich annotations and notes, adding twelve thousand six hundred and fifty-two characters — a not negligible contribution. But its arrangement, being bound to the system of seven sounds and thirty-six initial consonant groups, has in turn attracted criticism from later scholars. The rhymes in use today are essentially those of Shen Yue; but ancient rhyme transferences seem more reliable than modern ones. Zhang Fu's Collected Essentials of Rhyme Learning calibrated and fixed the four tones, and from this the transferences and mergers of ancient rhyme can be extrapolated by category. If one examines the ancient songs and poems of the Odes, Hymns, and Li Sao in the light of this, the differences between ancient and modern transferences become readily apparent.' Ziyu rose from his seat and bowed in thanks.

Nanxiang said, 'The ancients who discoursed on the Book of Changes spoke of principle and not of number; scholars today speak of number and not of principle. Can number really fully encompass principle? Moreover, since Shao Kangjie [Shao Yong] no true transmission of the system has survived. As for what is now called the Great Yi numerology, it is used to verify the fate of dynasties, the incidence of calamity and fortune, of war, flood, and fire, and to determine a person's rank and station. The calculation of the Yangjiu and Baili cycles yields uncannily accurate correspondences to historical events — might I be permitted to hear something of this teaching?'

Qu said, 'After the Southern Song, a certain Wang Di composed the Three Scrolls of the Taiyi Elbow-Sleeve Handbook, covering the yin and yang excursions and drawing one hundred and forty-four diagrams. The work uses the Great Yi system to illuminate the virtuous and wicked acts of sovereigns, with the specific calculation of the Yangjiu and Baili cycles proceeding as follows: four hundred and fifty-six years for one Yangjiu cycle, two hundred and eighty-eight years for one Baili cycle. Yangjiu is an odd number — the exhaustion of yang; Baili is an even number — the exhaustion of yin. According to Wang Di: the chaos of Hou Yi and Han Zhuo corresponds to the seventh Yangjiu count; the decline and weakness of King Nan corresponds to the eighth; the feebleness of Emperors Huan and Ling to the ninth; the destruction of Emperor Yang of Sui to the tenth. Verified against the historical chronology, each is precisely on the mark. He further states: the reign of King Li and then the five-time fallen King You of Zhou corresponds to the twelfth Baili count; during King Jing's time, when Wu and Yue fought each other and the realm was in turmoil, this corresponds to the thirteenth; the destruction of the Six States by Qin corresponds to the fourteenth; the migration of Eastern Jin and the splitting of the realm into sixteen kingdoms corresponds to the extreme of the Baili count, and then its return to unity; the chaos of the Five Dynasties corresponds to the third Baili count. These Baili correspondences have verifiable evidence. Yet there are also cases that do not correspond: the perfect governance of Shun and Yu, the model for ten thousand generations, falls at the seventh Baili count; the period of Kings Cheng and Kang, during which punishments went unused for forty-odd years, falls at the eleventh; the reigns of Xiao Jia and Yong Yi fall at the fifth Yangjiu and ninth Baili counts; the reigns of Geng Ding and Wu Yi fall at the sixth Yangjiu; Bujian's reign of fifty-nine years falls at the eighth Baili count; the period of Pan Geng and Xiao Xin falls at the tenth Baili count; Emperors Ming and Zhang of Han, following Guangwu in bringing about great peace, fall at the fifteenth Baili count; the twenty-third year of the Tang Zhenguan period falls at the second Baili count. All of these fail to correspond — why is that? And more remarkably: the exile of Jie of Xia to Nanchao, the fall of Zhou of Shang at Muye, Wang Mang's usurpation of Han, and An Lushan's rebellion against Tang — none of these coincide with any Yangjiu or Baili count either. What then explains that? This is why I say number cannot overcome principle. Principle arises from nature itself; number seems to be predetermined. For this reason the sages spoke of principle and not of number — number is but one thread within the whole of principle.'

Nanxiang said, 'A truly bold and penetrating argument — it dissolves doubts that have accumulated through the ages.' Cixian said, 'Let us set aside the great risings and fallings of the world, and attend to what stands before us in this cup. I have an extremely trivial and vulgar point I wish to put to you. I have read in the Yuejue shu [Records of the Vanished State of Yue] the saying that "the sagely spring forth from the sage-stock, the mad from the mad-stock; the osmanthus fruit produces osmanthus, the tung fruit produces tung." And yet I have repeatedly observed dull-witted and stupid parents producing children of the most brilliant intelligence and striking beauty. Looking at the fathers and mothers, there seems to be no such cause — no prior condition, whether innate or acquired — that could account for such a marvellous result. How does this come to be?'

Qu laughed and said, 'That is a difficult principle to explain fully. Yet the Qimin Yaoshu [Essential Techniques for the Common People] contains the following on the method of growing pear trees: one pear yields ten seeds, but only two of them produce pear trees, while the rest become wild pears. Duan Chengshi wrote: a goshawk produces three chicks, one of which becomes a kite. The Classic of Birds states: the stork produces three chicks, one of which becomes a crane. The Huashu [Book of Transformations] says: the summer sparrow produces quail chicks; the Chu dove produces a certain bird; the Nanhai ji [Southern Sea Records] states: the crocodile produces hundreds of offspring, of which only twelve become crocodiles — the rest become soft-shelled turtles or giant turtles, each transforming according to its pneumatic endowment. Extending this principle further: sages do not necessarily produce sages; worthy men do not necessarily produce worthy men. Earlier scholars said that Yang Xiong ought to have descendants but Zhang Tang ought not — yet can human shortsighted wisdom really determine the patterns of heaven? Principle has its constants, but also its variations. Is it not possible that some are moved by surrounding pneumata to transform their fundamental constitution? Or perhaps a foolish husband and a stupid wife, though dull of outward appearance, have some aspect of their seven emotions and six desires that is not quite so dull, and from this one spark of brightness a child is formed — who knows?' Everyone burst out laughing.

Ziyun said, 'Among the beautiful people of antiquity, their physical loveliness has been preserved only in the words and brushes of those who wrote about them. Among all the poems, prose-poems, and literary descriptions, those who described beautiful women are beyond counting on one's fingers — but which piece of writing has rendered the subject most perfectly? Will you indicate one or two for us?'

Qu said, 'All the writers of antiquity are admirable — how can one single any out? But the piece of writing that depicts a beautiful woman most fittingly, and that every reader would unanimously acclaim as superb, is none other than the description of Lady Zhuang Jiang of Wei in the ode "The Stately Lady" [Shuo ren, Ode 57 of the Book of Songs]. It opens: "Stately and tall is the lady, robed in embroidered silk under plain gauze" — two lines that already make radiance shoot from the page. Then from "Her neck is like the larva of the tree-borer" to "her beautiful eyes glisten" — every character conveys perfect beauty, without a single filler phrase and not a single borrowed image. As the saying goes, to write of the moon is to write the full moon; to write of a flower is to write the flower in bloom — all misty clouds and propping mountains have been swept away. That is the most difficult achievement of all. If one describes the richness of dress and adornment, or the elegance of bearing, one has still not shown how the eyebrows, eyes, nose, and mouth are arranged in relation to each other. Song Yu's "Rhapsody on the Divine Woman" makes its attempt, saying: "When she first appeared, she blazed forth like the white sun newly risen, illuminating a roof-beam; as she drew a little nearer, she shone pure and bright like the full moon releasing its light" — all this is doing is saying how brilliantly she shone. "Pure and bright" may still pass, but comparing a person to "the white sun" and "a roof-beam" is incongruous. Then Cao Zijian's "Rhapsody on the Goddess of the Luo River" borrows the same idea: "seen from afar, she was bright as the sun rising through morning clouds." And "The Divine Woman" also writes: "Suddenly she changed expression, graceful as a wandering dragon riding the clouds" — and then the Luo River rhapsody again borrows the line verbatim: "Like a startled wild goose in flight, graceful as a wandering dragon." That is truly a failure to comprehend the original: to compare a beautiful woman to a wandering dragon — I do not know what the author could have been thinking. Then there is Song Yu's "Rhapsody on the Beautiful Woman," which says: "Add a tenth of an inch and she is too tall; take away a tenth of an inch and she is too short." As a general statement, all well and good — but if one must examine the matter precisely, there is a logical flaw. If adding a tenth makes her too tall, she is truly tall, and subtracting a tenth should not make her too short. If subtracting a tenth makes her too short, she is truly short, and adding a tenth should not make her too tall. That is a case of literary exaggeration. In prose fiction there are some where the delineation is so thorough, saying what no one else has dared to say, that readers' eyes swim and their minds are transported, with the character vivid on the page — and none surpasses the description of the young woman Ying's body in the Zashi Mixin [Secret Fascinations in Miscellaneous Affairs]. Consider this passage — let me read it to you carefully: "The female official came with an imperial warrant to spend time with Ying in her sleeping quarters. Dismissing the attendants and barring the inner door, at a moment when the sundial showed the hours passing, sunlight came through the frosted-glass window and shone upon Ying's face, his radiance shining like morning clouds mixed with snow so brilliant one could not look straight at her. Her eyes shone clear and fresh; her brows were delicate and curved; her mouth was red and her teeth white; her ears were long and her nose well-proportioned; cheeks, dimples, chin, and jaw were all perfectly arranged. The female official then removed Ying's hairpin and unloosed her hair, combing it out length by length — black as lacquer-ware, lustrous enough to see one's reflection in, wound in the hand eight coils, and when let down, extending a half-handful beyond the floor. Then she asked that Ying relax her undergarments. Ying's face went crimson and she tried to block the request. The female official told Ying: "The imperial household is observing the proprieties; this wretched person is merely here to look — once these garments are relaxed, they will be replaced by official robes and adornments." Ying wept several tears, closed her eyes, and turned her face inward. The female official then gently loosened the garments with her hands, supporting them as the sunlight came through; a fragrant warmth rose up, and the skin was fine and smooth, the hand gliding over it without catching. Rounded in front and square behind, she was as though hewn from fat and carved from jade. The breasts swelled like budding beans; the navel could hold half an inch of a pearl. The private area was raised and full; drawing apart both thighs, the inner channel was moist and crimson, as if a fire-gem were about to emerge. Truly a woman who had strictly observed decorum, a virgin in the fullest sense. To estimate Ying's body overall: her blood was sufficient to nourish her skin; her skin was sufficient to adorn her flesh; her flesh was sufficient to clothe her bones. Proportion between long and short was perfectly maintained. From crown to heel she measured seven feet one inch in height; her shoulders were one foot six inches wide; her hips were three inches narrower than her shoulders; from each shoulder to the tips of the fingers, two feet seven inches; the fingers four inches from the palm — resembling ten bamboo shoots newly peeled. From the thigh to the foot, two feet two inches; the foot eight inches long; calves and instep shapely and fine, the sole flat and the toes neatly gathered. About them thin silk was wrapped close as a band of gauze, binding them minutely as if they were confined within the palace; and for a long while there was no sound. The female official told her to thank the Emperor for ten thousand years; Ying then slowly bowed and said: "Ten thousand years to the Emperor" — like a gentle wind stirring a flute, a soft and pleasing murmur to the ear." Though the passage is improper in content, for precision of delineation nothing surpasses it.'

Everyone said, 'Quite so — from ancient times to the present, no one has ever taken someone's measurements in such detail.' Ziyu said, 'The binding of feet is said to have originated with Consort Pan of the Later Chen dynasty. Yet the passage in the Mixin — "thin silk wrapped close as a band of gauze, binding them minutely as if they were confined within the palace" — is that not an even earlier origin of foot-binding?'

Qu said, 'That is merely a loose binding to prevent them from spreading freely. If one reads the words "calves and instep shapely and fine, the sole flat and the toes neatly gathered," it seems not at all like the tight and severe binding of today that forces feet into the pointed shape of a lotus petal.' Huifang said, 'And how do those measurements work out? Body height seven feet one inch; shoulders one foot six inches wide — how is this calculated? By today's measurements, no one could be as tall as that.' Qu said, 'These are Han dynasty measurements. Against the Board of Works construction inch of the present day, each Han inch is worth only seven and a half fen. And the Board of Works construction inch is only nine point three fen against the common tailor's inch used in the marketplace. Converted to the construction inch: seven times seven makes four feet nine, five times seven makes three point five inches, adding seven and a half fen gives five feet three inches and two and a half fen in height. If reckoned further in today's tailor's inch: five times nine makes four feet five, three times nine makes two point seven, add two point two fen, for a total of roughly four feet eight inches. That is quite a tall body, about the same as you, perhaps a little taller. Shoulders one foot six inches: converted to construction inch gives one foot one and a half inches; in tailor's inch, one foot one inch and a fraction. Hips three inches narrower than shoulders: in today's tailor's inch, the lower body is only eight inches and a fraction wide — indicating a slender and fine build. Arm length from shoulder to fingertip, two feet seven inches: in construction inch, about two feet and two and a half fen; in tailor's inch, about one foot eight inches. Thigh to foot, three feet two inches: in construction inch two feet four inches; in tailor's inch two feet one inch and six fen, showing perfectly balanced proportions between the upper and lower body. Foot length eight inches: in construction inch six inches; in tailor's inch five inches four fen. This is still different from a bound foot, and not excessively small. Taken all together, the figure is somewhat on the tall side. Then again, ancient beauties were generally described as tall and stately.' Cixian said, 'You have made a remarkably detailed calculation. It is possible that when the female official was measuring, she made a small error and recorded an inch too many — one never knows.' This made everyone laugh.

Master Qu then said, 'There is a supremely cultivated and erudite person currently in the capital — young in years but an old acquaintance of mine, and also from a great Nanjing family. In terms of family standing, his lineage is on a par with Ziyun's and Xingbei's, and I am sure you all know him well.' Ziyun said, 'Who might that be?' Master Qu said, 'His family name is Jin, given name Su, courtesy name Jifu — are you acquainted with him?' Everyone said together, 'We have long heard his name but have never yet had the honour of meeting him.' Master Qu said, 'In scholarship, learning, and personal character he is among the very first or second in the world today. He also has a prose work — a historical novel about the suppression of the Japanese pirates. I have forgotten the title. I read through it once, and the prose style is magnificently forceful. The two rebel leaders — King of Settled Rivers and Chancellor of Stilled Seas — are excoriated with such biting precision that it is truly the brush of a man of genius.' Cixian said, 'Such traitors and rebels have inflicted misery on countless lives; every person's blood boils to speak of them. Such a denunciation is a cause for rejoicing; I must find a copy to read one day.' Master Qu said, 'Yet his luck in life has been very poor, and his great talents have never been fully employed. It is a true shame.' Baozhu quickly said, 'How fortunate this gentleman is — to find, today, a true friend who understands him.' Master Qu said, 'Are you on close terms with this gentleman, Yaoqing?' Sulan, who was beside Baozhu, said, 'He taught Yaoqing painting, calligraphy, and the qin. Only last month they spent two days together at Cuiwei Mountain. His regard for this gentleman is no less than Meixiang's regard for Xiangfan.' Baozhu smiled and said, 'Surely not quite to that degree.' Ziyu said, 'I once saw at Yaoqing's some of his brushwork — supremely elegant and refined, with much of the quality of Tang Liuru [Tang Yin].' Master Qu said, 'Not only is his brushwork like Tang Liuru's — his horoscope is also like Tang Liuru's, which is extraordinary. How can the ancient and modern coincide so exactly? This is another case where principle alone is insufficient and number must be invoked. I am still planning to call on him tomorrow.' Ziyun said quickly, 'Will you not introduce me first? To acquire such a fine friend would itself be a great delight.' Master Qu said, 'An excellent idea — excellent!'

Baozhu said, 'This gentleman is rather reclusive and not fond of society.' Master Qu said, 'With these gentlemen I think he would find himself perfectly at ease.' That day Master Qu had talked at length from beginning to end — everyone was weary. After finishing the meal they sat about in a relaxed manner for a while; it was already about the second watch. Liu Wenze, being a former student of his, did not venture to ask difficult questions. Baozhu asked Ziyun for a folding fan so that he could request a poem from Master Qu; Ziyun went further and had four fans brought out, one for each of the four performers. Thereupon Baozhu wiped the small table clean, Huifang shifted the ink-stone into position, Sulan ground the ink, and Qinyan moistened the brush — all four together asked Master Qu to write a tribute. Master Qu was greatly pleased; he captured the general character of each person and composed a regulated poem of seven-character lines for each, written in a semi-formal semi-cursive hand modelled on Yu Shinan, with a double inscription. The four performers gave thanks, they chatted a while longer, and then the company dispersed. As to what happened next, listen to the following chapter for the explanation.
