Chapter 41 — Cherishing spring, all butterflies turn to dreams; beating erotic rhythms, paired mandarin ducks envy no immortals.

To return to our story: ever since Qinyan had taken his leave of absence, the appointed time had long since passed with no sign of his return, and Young Master Hua found himself missing the boy more with each passing day.

One afternoon he was leaning on the railing at Guihong Islet in the garden, rod in hand, fishing at the water's edge. Shanzhi and the two pages Jin Ling and Yu Ling attended him, along with a little maidservant named Xiang'er. Jin Ling had fetched a large porcelain basin and filled it with water at the pond's edge to hold any catch. The young master had been fishing for a while and caught a small fish barely three inches long, yet felt quite pleased with himself. He observed how the water ran cold and clear, and how at certain eddying bends the builders had set copper strips into the stone bases of the banks, so that the flowing current produced a clear, ringing sound, like the music of a qin or a zhu. He also noticed countless flower petals drifting on the surface, and a red carp swimming to and fro, feeding on the fallen blossoms; it had spotted the bait on his line and moved in to swallow it.

The young master gave a sharp tug on his rod, but the line snapped—the fish had swallowed the hook whole up to the midpoint, and the broken end of the line floated on the water. Delighted by the spectacle, the young master called out to Jin Ling and Yu Ling to bring the little boat around.

The two pages needed no second telling; they flew down off the stone terrace and ran at full speed. Crossing the bridge, they reached the far shore where the hillside of the Tanshui Pavilion stood. In his haste, Jin Ling failed to notice an old tree root underfoot, tripped over it, and tumbled down, badly scraping his knee, and for a moment could not get up from the ground. Yu Ling helped him to his feet and rubbed the knee until the pain eased, then together they climbed into the little craft, cast off the mooring lines, and sculled it across. The boat was over thirty feet long, its paintwork gleaming bright; railings ran along both sides, there was a small pavilion at the prow, and a little huali-wood round table occupied the midship cabin. The canopy was covered with green oilcloth and fringed with a flying border of white silk. With Jin Ling and Yu Ling plying the oars fore and aft, they brought the craft over to the islet. Seeing the spring day so radiant—peach and plum blossoms vying in loveliness—the young master at once sent the little maidservant to invite his mistress to come out and enjoy the garden.

After about half an hour, the sound of tinkling ornaments announced the arrival of Madam Hua, attended by four maidservants—Mingzhu, Huazhu, Hezhu, and Zengzhu. The young master stepped forward with a smile to welcome her. Madam Hua said: "The weather has been so fine these past two days—I had been meaning to take a stroll. A moment ago Xiang'er told me you were here fishing, and I came by way of the passage alongside the west study; it was not far at all. I have also had the servants prepare some refreshments to bring along." The young master replied: "That was precisely what I had in mind, and yet you have already arranged everything in advance." The two of them rested their arms on the railing and gazed about for a while, after which the young master said: "Why do we not go out on the boat and take a turn around the pond?" The four Zhus helped the mistress slowly descend the steps; Mingzhu and Zengzhu boarded the prow first and steadied Madam Hua as she stepped aboard. The young master followed, and the two of them settled in the midship cabin. Mingzhu and Zengzhu moved to the stern, while Huazhu and Hezhu took the prow—but Huazhu's oar stroke went one way while Mingzhu's went the other, and the little boat began to spin in circles on the water. Huazhu then lost her grip altogether, and her oar sent a great splash of water straight into her own face. Hezhu laughed without stopping. The young master called out:

"What is this? You have all handled oars before. How is it you have forgotten today?" Huazhu laughed, saying, "It is Mingzhu who cannot scull. I face forward, but she faces backward." Mingzhu replied, "Never mind your own failings. Must you blame it all on me? Hezhu, you take over; let her keep at it and this boat will capsize." Hezhu replaced Huazhu, and at once all went smoothly. A light breeze drifted past; the ripples shimmered deep green as the craft glided slowly beneath a small bridge. The young master and his lady admired the ancient wisteria that twined over the bridge railings and the garden rocks, hanging in dense, feathery masses down to the water's surface, while beneath them the stream gave off a clear, jade-like chime. They passed the little bridge, and along a willow-lined causeway peach and plum trees interlaced their red and green in the warm spring breeze, accompanied by the chorus of many birds. Past several pavilions and terraces, rounding the hillside of the Tanshui Pavilion, they arrived at the Liuxian Courtyard and found, amid a stand of slender bamboo, a compound blazing with the bloom of countless blue-peach trees. The young master declared, "This is the finest spot of all; let us go to the Liuxian Courtyard." Hezhu made the boat fast and laid out a gangplank. The young master stepped ashore. The four Zhus helped the mistress along a winding path beneath the peach-blossom canopy, past several heaps of ornamental rockery. Beyond a small stone slab bridge stood a stone gateway. Passing through it, they came to a pavilion called the Xifang Ting, the Bower of Cherished Fragrance, and beyond it the covered walkway of the Liuxian Courtyard.

The Liuxian Courtyard comprised three successive courtyards, its winding galleries and graceful kiosks, its stacked lofts and lofty terraces all richly appointed. The red, white, and blue-tinged peach trees had already burst into fine bloom. The young master said to his lady, "No flowers should be admired without wine. You mentioned just now that the servants had made preparations. I wonder whether they are ready yet?" Madam Hua sent Huazhu to go and look, and Huazhu tried to drag Mingzhu along to pole the boat with her. Mingzhu said, "There you go again with your nonsense. You simply want an excuse to play on the water. Do you not know the way? Go through the old-wisteria study at the back, then past the Yixiang Pavilion, and you will reach the path we came by. What do you need a boat for?" Huazhu, who did indeed want an excuse to play rather than truly not knowing the way, had no choice but to go alone. When she was nearly at the wisteria-bower study, she saw Lin Shanzhi coming toward her, calling out, "Huazhu is here; they must all be at the Liuxian Courtyard." Before Huazhu could ask anything, a group emerged from beside the wisteria trellis. It was the Six Zhus, two older maidservants, and several young girls. Aizhu said to Huazhu, "What have you been doing all this time? Not a word to let us know, and here we have been wandering all over the garden searching." Huazhu replied, "We came by boat, and we have not been there long. Anyone on the bank should have been able to see us. I was actually coming to find you just now." The two senior maidservants carried the food hampers, the Six Zhus bore the smaller items, and Er Ling and Shanzhi lent a hand. Everything was conveyed to the Liuxian Courtyard and arranged in proper order. The Zhus stepped forward to serve tea; one side of the table was set with fruit boxes, the other with food boxes. Tea braziers and wine vessels were laid out in readiness, and the Zhus arranged themselves in two rows to attend.

Butterflies drifted back and forth in swarms, while others lay still and sleeping inside the blossoms. The Ten Zhus managed to catch quite a few of them; they plucked a single hair from one of the little maids' heads, tied two large butterflies to it, and let them flutter and dance together in pairs.

The young master watched with great delight and said to his lady: "With spring so glorious around us, we must not fail to appreciate it. These butterflies make merry more thoroughly than we do ourselves. The garden is most celebrated for its peach blossoms—we ought to pay them a proper libation. Why not fetch that Bamboo-Leaf Spring wine brewed from the dew of a hundred flowers, and pour it as an offering at their roots?"

Madam Hua said: "I know how much you love that wine—I have already had some brought, though there are no particularly fine fruits to accompany it. If we are to make an offering to the flowers, ordinary food will hardly do. What, then, do you propose to offer?" The young master laughed: "Now you have me stumped. Year after year we have offered flowers nothing but vegetables and fruits. This time we make a sincere and special libation—it must be something worthy of the blossoms."

After a moment's thought, he called Aizhu to go and ask Shanzhi to fetch the key from the study-boy in charge of the storeroom. Presently Aizhu returned with it, and the young master had her unlock two antique cabinets, then led his lady over to examine them at leisure. They were filled with ancient bronzes, old jades, and similar treasures. Opening a drawer, he found a rosewood box; lifting the lid, he saw it contained a hand scroll, its label inscribed: "Portrait of Lady Huirui, painted by Madam Guan." Madam Hua smiled: "This is just the thing." The young master unrolled it. It was an exquisite, meticulously detailed painting on silk, of surpassing delicacy and beauty. Appended in small regular script by Zhao Mengfu was Lady Huirui's own Palace Lyrics, forming a double masterpiece. The young master said, "What a pity we have only this one piece. What else shall we find to accompany it?" Madam Hua asked, "What about Ma Shouniang's orchid painting?" The young master shook his head: "It does not measure up. Better to use the album leaf of Li Xiangjun's peach-blossom fan, and then add your embroidered copy of the 'New Songs from the Jade Terrace Preface' to make an even finer pairing." Madam Hua laughed: "How could that possibly stand alongside those two pieces? How could it compare with either of them?" The young master replied, "Each has its own particular excellence. And besides, your needlework is itself a supreme achievement." Madam Hua thereupon sent Baozhu and Aizhu to fetch these two items. The two maids were gone for quite a while before they returned. A Han-dynasty jade wine-cup was also found, and it was filled with wine. They moved a table out to the covered veranda and arranged all three treasures on it, then set a hill-shaped censer burning hundred-petals incense. Madam Hua asked, "Well then, shall we bow, or not?" The young master replied, "No need for bows. Let us rather select the finest blooms and pour the wine at their roots." The two of them walked into the grove; the young master chose a red blue-peach tree, and the mistress chose a white blue-peach tree. He poured out half a cup first; she followed suit. The two of them stood beneath the blossoms, smiling as they admired the scene.

Madam Hua called one of the older maidservants to go and fetch another large jug of wine, and not to dawdle. The young master asked: "What do you need so much wine for?" His lady laughed: "I noticed that when we poured our offering on the flowers, all the maids were looking on with what seemed a very thirsty expression. So I want some wine for them to enjoy as well."

The young master laughed: "That is what one calls sharing one's pleasure with others. But they must bow when they make their offering—they cannot do it the same way as we did." All ten Zhus broke into quiet smiles. Zhangzhu said in a low voice to Hezhu: "If we are to bow, let all ten of us bow at the same moment—no one first, no one last, so that those who bow first won't be laughed at by those who bow after." Aizhu said: "Would it not be better for us to bow in pairs?" Huazhu leaned close to Aizhu's ear and whispered: "This is not a wedding ceremony—why on earth do you want to bow in pairs?" Aizhu gave her a light swat. By this time the older maidservant had come tottering in with a large jug of wine, which she set down at the veranda's edge. The Ten Zhus each filled a small cup and scattered through the trees, seeking out the most brilliantly blooming branches. One by one they poured their cups, weaving in and out among the peach trees like a swarm of butterflies threading through the flowers—and then, all in unison, they solemnly bowed four times.

The young master and his lady watched with great amusement. The young master called for two brocade cushions to be brought out and spread beneath the flowers; the two of them sat facing each other there. A lacquered partitioned box was laid out before them, and they drank several cups of the Hundred-Flowers Spring wine. Madam Hua said: "Why not have them sing and play a round, to complete our elegant pleasure?" The young master replied: "Excellent—you may assign their parts." His lady divided the Ten Zhus into five pairs and gave her instructions: "Each pair is to select one aria, but it must contain a line about peach blossoms somewhere within it. I shall assign the partnerships: one sings while the other plays, or one plays while the other sings. If anyone makes a mistake in singing or playing, she is to kneel beneath the flowers and drink a full large cup of wine as a forfeit." Aizhu laughed: "Mistress, this game of yours is rather too cruel. We have only a limited repertoire, after all—it may well happen that one person knows how to sing a particular aria but her partner cannot play it, and the partner who can play it has no one who can sing along. If you assign our parts ahead of time like this, all ten of us will end up kneeling beneath the flowers and drinking down half this great jug of cold wine—and that will be that." This set both the young master and his lady laughing.

His lady said: "Very well—the theme I chose was indeed on the difficult side, and arias with peach-blossom lines are scarce. The ten of you shall take turns singing the two acts 'Visiting Cui' and 'Sleeping amid Fragrance' from The Peach Blossom Fan."

The young master brightened at this and said: "Perfect—I know those lyrics well myself. The two suites together have eleven arias; the shorter ones can be combined so that each person sings one. Have some cushions laid out in front of the Xifang Pavilion for them to sit on while they sing."

The Ten Zhus were greatly pleased; they fetched their strings, flutes, and rhythm boards, each one pushing the other to go first. After much good-natured wrangling, they settled on Baozhu to lead. Baozhu sang:

The gold and powder of glory have not yet faded away;
I catch the perfume of the Six Dynasties drifting near.
Across the wide heavens, smoke and grass cut the heart with longing.
I fear the flower-tidings quicken all too fast;
Wind and rain, wind and rain...
So the springtime slips away. [Moon over Mount Gou]

I look toward Pingkang Ward,
East of Phoenix City, among a thousand gates of green willow.
All along the way, purple-silk bridles
Lead the pleasure-seekers forth.
Whose house is that, with swallows flying, always two by two?

Across the spring waters,
Bluish smoke tints the window.
In the bright clear sky,
A red apricot peers over a wall.
A long wooden bridge stretches ahead,
And I lazily point out the teahouse boats and wine barges beyond. [Brocade-Wrapped Road]

Hark... hark... the flower-sellers' cries run busy in the lanes,
Threading through one narrow alley after another.
I pin in my hair a dewy branch of pale willow-gold.

The young master said, "These lyrics are truly fine. One might compare them favorably with the four dream-plays of the Four Dreams of the Jade Tea Hall at Yumingtan Studio; they are the work of a genuine literary talent." His lady agreed: "After them, only the Nine Works from Red Snow Tower can stand as a rival; all else falls short."

At this point Mingzhu took up the next aria and sang:

Silk kerchiefs are knotted, courtesans fly in formation like wild geese;
At festival time all vying in fresh array.
Sea delicacies, river clams, jade-clear spirits—
Each has her place; they come flying to offer the cup,
By secret tryst in lotus-embroidered tents. [Red Slave Trimming the Silver Lamp]

The young master cried: "A penalty! You left out the line: 'The ruan-lute is plucked; flutes and pipes ring out clear.'" Zhangzhu took up the next aria and sang:

So refined—the windows bright, the courtyard open wide;
Come early to this warm and languorous realm.
A phoenix flute, a luan pipe, sound from the clouds above;
The strings sway and soar,
Jade tones ring—each note, each note unravels my tender heart.
Two phoenixes soar together in flight.
Rare southern treasures drift on the breeze,
Arching straight to the aching heart of a beauty. [Sound of Geese Passing]

All the while she sang, Zhangzhu tied a knot in her handkerchief and flung it at Hezhu's face.

Hezhu burst into laughter; the young master called out his approval, and his lady smiled serenely. Both drank a cup each, then listened as Hezhu sang:

I have wandered by mistake to the peak of Wu Mountain
And added to my thoughts of floating clouds.

So suddenly I forgot what a fairy beauty looks like.
Let not the flowers and moonlight of this spring night prove an empty dream;
A good match, once in one's hands, must not be let go—
Make ready to travel up to the high palace of clouds. [Little Peach Red]

"Visiting Cui" being thus concluded, Aizhu took up the next suite, "Sleeping amid Fragrance," and sang:

Short spring jacket, both sleeves double-rolled;
Tuning the zither amid the flowers in Milou Tower.
Today all the embroidered curtains are fully drawn up—
Let not the golden-threaded willow
Block the painted pleasure-boat from view. [Immortal by the River]

The young master laughed: "Verses so exquisite as these would call for Fan Su, the beloved of Bai Juyi himself, to render them justice." His lady smiled: "How could we ever hope for Fan Su? Even as they are, these girls more than hold their own—compared with the leading actors and actresses of commercial troupes, they are far superior." The young master nodded in agreement. 

Zengzhu now sang:

Garden peaches blush red as embroidery,
Dazzling above the wine of Wenjun;
A screen unfolds with golden peacocks,
Wrapping the spring afternoon.
The golden cup rinsed clean,
The fragrant animal-shaped incense lit—
This crimson-sleeved girl behind the wine-counter
Is far too tender-hearted;
She ought to let Xiangru enjoy her company. [A Spray of Flowers]

Huazhu kept the drum and rhythm-boards going as she took up the next aria and sang:

Qi-Liang lyrics, Chen-Sui blossoms—
Day after day the tender feelings linger.
A young scholar leans close in a blue gown;
Today a little Du Mu visits Yangzhou.
He thinks to trace the painted brows,
To point the way for the flute's breath;
From this day spring is held within his hands.
A scholar's thirst must urgently be slaked—
But the slanting sun is slow to sink behind the tower,
Only one cup of wine has yet been drunk. [Liangzhou Xu]

The young master turned to his lady and said: "For lines so beautiful as these, we cannot but drink a full great cup." A large cup was poured, and he called Baozhu to present it to his lady with a bow. Baozhu raised the cup with both hands and sank to her knees. His lady said: "My capacity is too small for such a large cup—pray drink it yourself." She poured from the large cup into a small one and told Baozhu to carry it to the young master. Baozhu knelt before him; the young master drained it in one draught.

Mingzhu broke two sprigs of red-and-white peach blossom, arranged them in a Ru-ware vase, and placed it before the young master and his lady. Zhenzhu then sang:

The towers and terraces shake with the flowers' trembling;
Curtain and lattice quiver in the breeze;
Leaning against his imposing, graceful figure—
Spring longing knows no bounds;
For her, gold hairpins are combed and set anew.

Idle blossoms lend their splendor,
Wild grasses breathe their fragrance—
All this was made for my mistress to enjoy.
Tonight the lamplight glows red through the gauze;
Even one long used to wonders ought to be ashamed;
To set the first rhyme—that is the hardest thing of all. [Opening Aria and Closing Aria]

The young master said: "'Even one long used to wonders ought to be ashamed'—could any ordinary poet have found such a line?" His lady said: "Together with 'a little Du Mu visits Yangzhou,' these are truly from the same inspired pen." 

Then Ruizhu began, with Baozhu joining in harmony:

Gold goblets assist the tallying of drinks,
Urging and urging without cease;
In a deep stupor, jade falls at dusk.

Hands clasped in secret;
Sorrowful painted brows;
The perfumed body grown thin.
A single night of spring—an hour is worth a thousand gold;
Before others, how dare one loosen the lotus-flower clasp?
Waiting until the lamps dim and the banquet ends,
Until the palace censer has dripped out its last lotus-flower drop. [Section by Section Higher]

Huazhu joined in with the next aria, while Mingzhu sang the harmony:

Pipes and flutes descend from the painted tower,
Carrying a pure, melodious air.
The lamplight, dreamy, glows like a spring noon.
The Tiantai peaks,
Where Ruan and Liu met their fair companions...
A truly perfect match.

Layer upon layer, brocade curtains fragrant with incense;
The onlookers grow jealous, their brows furrowed with envy.
She who leans against you in her wine is all stormwind and waves:
The happiness of loving flowers, bestowed at birth. [Opening Aria and Closing Aria]

On Qinhuai, moonlit mist knows neither old nor new.
Scent of rouge and powder fills the waters flowing east.
Night upon night, the springtime longing is never spent.

When the coda was done, the young master and his lady were thoroughly delighted, and the Ten Zhus rose together. The young master declared, "Today they have all done their best; we must give them something as a reward." His lady suggested, "We ought to rank them in order of merit, so the rewards and forfeits will be seen to be just." She called for writing brush and ink-stone. Aizhu was the first to seize them and carried the brush, ink, and flower-patterned paper to the young master. He deferred to his lady for the assessment; she in turn deferred to him, until he said, "In matters of musical judgment I am truly no match for you, and I fear I would not be impartial. Please, you must be the one to decide." She smiled, took up the brush, and wrote down ten names, entering each one by the character before the Zhu of her name. Then she turned to the young master and said, "By my assessment, Baozhu stands first. Her singing had a spirit that was free and flowing, yet refined and warm; among the ten she was the peerless performer. Second is Aizhu, who conveyed both emotion and charm. Then Zengzhu, then Zhangzhu, then Ruizhu, then Zhenzhu, then Huazhu, then Hezhu, then Huazhu the younger, and then Mingzhu last. I hope this is not unjust?" The young master affirmed, "The ranking is entirely right." His lady also invited the Ten Zhus: "If anyone feels hard done by, please speak freely." Huazhu said with a conciliatory smile, "I would venture to think that my singing was above both Ruizhu's and Zhenzhu's." Madam Hua replied, "Just because you are unhappy with the ranking does not mean you know your own weaknesses. You were trying to show off your strengths and outshine the others, which led you to add embellishments not in the original. That is what is meant by theatrical singing, not pure singing. Pure singing must be refined; only when all ornamentation is stripped away does the true, natural character reveal itself. You have been so accustomed to the decorated style of stage performance that you cannot wash it clean all at once. If someone without a discerning ear had been judging, would they not have ranked you first?" Only then did Huazhu accept the verdict, and she asked, "Mistress, what did you think of Shanzhi's singing?" Madam Hua replied, "Shanzhi also has the theatrical style. As for Qinyan, though still raw in some ways, he still achieves that quality of purity and meaning."

At the mention of Qinyan, the young master turned to his lady and said: "That boy Qinyan is truly something of a puzzle. We have treated him well by any measure, and yet one always senses that he carries some private grievance in his heart. He has been on leave for over a month now, with no sign of coming back. In truth, looking at him, he does not seem like the common sort—yet I cannot fathom why he has never seemed content. I simply cannot understand it." Madam Hua said: "In my observation, the boy is by nature proud—he does not appear to be the kind of person who willingly takes a place beneath others. But not knowing how he came to be in his present situation, there is nothing to be done. As the saying goes: 'Holding this office, one must perform its rites.' To be vainly arrogant in such a position would be truly foolish." The young master smiled without speaking. His lady then recorded the rewards for the Ten Zhus: first rank would receive hair-ornaments and jewelry; second rank, perfumes and cosmetics.

The two older maidservants who had come along, standing at a distance, had quietly consumed nearly half the remaining wine while no one was looking. One of them, already tipsy, was slumped against a garden rock and had all but fallen asleep on the ground, while the other had her ear cocked trying to overhear the conversation—without much success.

When Aizhu walked past, this maidservant called out, "Miss, what has the mistress been saying to you all? And I see she's written something on a list." Aizhu laughed, "She is giving us presents." The old woman remarked, "You young ladies truly have the blessed life, always receiving gifts. Miss, if you get something you do not want, pass it to me, don't let those little girls make waste of it." Aizhu smiled and walked away. The little maidservant Xiang'er laughed and said, "They haven't even received their gifts yet, and you're already hoping for hand-me-downs. Tomorrow I'll buy you a clay wine-flask as a present, so you can store your spirits in it and not have to hold that handle-less jug bottom-up to drink from it. As for anything better, do you really think you're worthy?" The old woman was teased but dared not scold Xiang'er, so she could only open her eyes wide and stare. The other old maidservant sighed under her breath, "Alas, as they say, a pearl grown old and yellowed is worth nothing. Why bother arguing with them?" At this point the young master suddenly recited two lines from The Peony Pavilion from Hundred Yuan Plays: "A fine day and a beautiful scene, what good are they? Joy in the heart and delight in the eye, whose garden holds them?" His lady laughed, "The 'A Stroll in the Garden and Waking from a Dream' scene of The Peony Pavilion from Hundred Yuan Plays is all languorous beauty, warm fragrance, and soft grace. Yet when I read that play, Liu Mengmei always seems somehow unsuited to Du Liniang." The young master agreed, "I have always thought so too. There is a certain coarseness about Liu Mengmei that naturally falls short of Liniang's quality. As for those hundred varieties of Yuan drama, they can only be performed on stage and are utterly unfit to be read. If one is to speak of literary artistry and lyrical brilliance, those scripts deserve nothing but to be thrown into the fire.

Yet there are those who praise them endlessly, moved by nothing but the sound of the music, though for the lyrics themselves, not a single fine sentence could be pointed out." Madam Hua said, "I suspect that before they were set to music, the texts may have been somewhat better. It is the singers who must make them conform to the musical notation, and so at every point the gold is turned to iron. I do not mean to be harsh, but those librettos serve as nothing more than syllable-charts for the notation system. The correctness of phrasing, the elegance or vulgarity of style, none of that is considered at all. When a text does have some literary merit, there is no one who can sing it. Ever since the Nine-Palace Score notation was fixed, everyone knows only how to alter the words to fit the notes, but no one knows how to adapt the notation to fit a fine text, and that is one of the great deficiencies of our age." The young master praised her, "A truly brilliant observation! I think, here we sit facing these famous blossoms, having just listened to beautiful singing. I feel an impulse to compose a lyric, and have them sing it. It cannot compare with the magnificent writing of The Peach Blossom Fan, of course, but it would be something to amuse ourselves. What do you say?" Madam Hua suggested, "Splendid! Why not compose it to the tune of 'Liangzhou Xu'? Using the same notation but singing new words of our own, would that not be convenient?" The young master agreed, "Marvelous! You go first." His lady laughed, "How could I? You must lead the way, and I will compose the answering verse." The young master agreed, drank several cups of wine, thought for a while, and produced a lyric to 'Liangzhou Xu', which he passed to his lady. She read it aloud:

Bright clouds weave brocade.
Icy silk is cut like scissors.
A thousand tender feelings, lightly trusting.
A fine day and a beautiful scene.
Black gauze cap, red sleeves... how we cherish each other.

Ah, the immortal maiden.
Idly she leads the wandering traveler in.
In secret she dispels all mortal longing.
A moment of spring is cheaply worth a thousand gold.
Bead curtains and silver screens become a paradise.
Do not let the gold in our cups lie shallow.

When his lady had finished reading it, she praised it without end. She drank a small cup herself and laughed, "This is me obeying your teaching: 'do not let the gold in our cups lie shallow.' But when the original is so fine, how am I to answer it? Even among the songs in The Peach Blossom Fan this would rank among the very finest, delicate in its imagery, lofty in its conception, assured in its tone. I shall not attempt a reply." The young master laughed, "Do not be modest. You must certainly have some fine idea I could never arrive at myself. Write it out quickly, so they can sing it." His lady read his lyric through once more, praised it again several times, and then wrote out a stanza in response, passing it to the young master to read aloud:

The curtains and lattices half-sway in the breeze.
The towers and terraces spread out in full view.
Crimson snow and flying jade vie in loveliness.
A pure voice, a light beat of time.
Bright eyes, white teeth... beauty blooms.

Youth runs on like water.
Green leaves have grown to shade the boughs.
Could one squander such spring days?
In Shi Chong's Golden Valley, flowers open on every side.
I envy only the mandarin ducks, not the immortals.
Do not let the gold in our cups lie shallow.

The young master read it again and again, intoning it aloud several times, and then struck the table in rapturous admiration, declaring, "Comparing the two, my own pales beside yours. These fifty-seven characters drift like fragrant clouds and scatter like a rain of flowers; not even The Peach Blossom Fan contains lines as exquisite as these." His lady laughed, "You flatter me far too much. I think mine is plainly inferior to yours. Your praise only makes me uneasy." The young master said, "'I envy only the mandarin ducks, not the immortals.' Though the phrase has been used before, the way you have employed it surpasses the original; even Cui Yingying and Zheng Zhagu will not be able to claim sole possession of that sentiment now." He at once called Baozhu and Aizhu to come over and memorize the words so they could sing them.

After reading through several times, the two maids thought they knew the words, and began to sing—but fell out of meter almost immediately. His lady said: "You are not yet secure. Write the notation marks in the margins, and you had better read from the score as you sing." Baozhu and Aizhu wrote in the notation and then sang through it again, note by note, and this time fell perfectly into the melodic mode, to the great delight of both the mistress and the young master. The young master laughed: "With these two arias, the two of us might as well play our own lead roles. Why do you not sing as well? Here we are singing—no outsider could possibly hear us." His lady laughed: "Have you ever seen me sing?" The young master said: "If you truly do not know how to sing, then how do you know all the subtle nuances, and how is it that whenever someone makes even a small rhythmic error you always catch it?" His lady laughed: "When you listen day in and day out, do you not end up knowing it by heart?" The young master said: "In truth I know it well too, and yet I often miss an error when I am not paying close attention—that must always be because of inattentiveness."

His lady asked, "Why do you not sing?" The young master replied, "Singing alone has no pleasure in it." His lady suggested, "Have Baozhu sing with you. After all, the line 'do not let the gold in our cups lie shallow' is meant to be sung in chorus." The young master demurred, "Let us not sing today; tomorrow I shall compose a few more stanzas and we can make a complete suite called 'Viewing Flowers'. Then have them dress as ourselves and perform a little play, to be called 'Sacrificing to the Flowers'. What do you think?" His lady mused, "That would be rather dull, and in performance it would come out no different from 'Appreciating the Lotuses'. Though I suppose those ten maids of ours would do nicely for the comic and villainous roles, but then again, that would be vulgar." The young master laughed, "If anyone is to play the clown, only Huazhu could carry it off." Huazhu, overhearing this, flushed red, turned her head away, and addressed Aizhu, "And Aizhu could too." Before Aizhu could reply, the young master said, "Aizhu would play the secondary female lead, Huazhu the older woman, Baozhu the principal female, and Ruizhu the ingenue. The rest could take on the male lead, painted-face, senior, and supporting roles, better than eight of our pages put together." His lady said, "That could indeed work. I only fear they would be too shy to carry it off convincingly." As she spoke, she gazed at the peach blossoms, which in the slanting afternoon sun had turned an even deeper crimson, the white ones now showing faintly pink, while others had taken on a golden hue, all extraordinarily beautiful. The sky was growing toward evening, and she said to the young master, "Today one may truly say we have had our fill of pleasure. I am growing tired; let us go in." She rose, and the young master also stood. Madam Hua, with her ten maids in attendance, gathered up the portrait of Lady Huirui, The Peach Blossom Fan album leaf, and her embroidered "New Songs from the Jade Terrace Preface", and they all set off slowly together. Reaching the old-wisteria study, they went in and rested briefly. When they came to the Yixiang Pavilion, the garden rock path was steep and narrow, and the maids carefully supported their mistress step by step. Ahead lay a stretch of smooth bluestone paving, very level, and beyond three or four more garden buildings they entered the inner apartments. The two older maidservants were left behind to collect the things; though two young girls helped them, it was still too much to carry in a single trip. On the way in, the Six Zhus had helped carry some items; now they had no recourse but to beg Shanzhi, Jin Ling, and Yu Ling to carry several loads for them.

The two old maidservants, stumbling and swaying, walked for quite a long stretch before finally making it back inside.

Meanwhile, the young master had personally escorted his lady to her rooms and seen her seated, then looked over the lyrics they had composed that afternoon a while longer, and the two of them dined together. Afterward, in high spirits, he went to the Xihong Study, and as he sat there the thought of Qinyan came to him—how the boy still had not come back, though his leave seemed already to have run over. He called a little maidservant to go and summon Shanzhi. The maid went and presently came back with Shanzhi. The young master asked: "What day did Qinyan take his leave?" Shanzhi replied: "The twenty-fourth day of the first month." The young master said: "The twenty-fourth of the first month—and today is already the second day of the third month. He asked for a month's leave. Why has he not returned these seven or eight days past?"

Shanzhi said nothing for a moment, then offered: "He must have some matter to attend to; no doubt he will come back once it is settled." The young master said: "I cannot think what matter he could have. Tomorrow I will send someone out of the city to find him and ask when he plans to return." Shanzhi acknowledged the order.
