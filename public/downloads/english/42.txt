Chapter 42 — A guardian extorts support fees at inflated rates; in tea-house rounds, a parasite loses his money.

To return to our story: Qinyan had spent several days at the Yiyuan garden in the company of Ziyu, and felt thoroughly content and at ease during that time. After Changqing's funeral was seen through, the bustle of two or three days left Qinyan rather worn out and a little unwell; he thought to rest quietly for a few more days before returning to the Hua household. One morning after breakfast he was lying in his room when his shimu—his master's wife—came in. Qinyan quickly rose to his feet. His shimu told him to sit down, and then said: "When you first entered the Hua household, I never knew what terms your master had agreed to with Young Master Hua, and your master never told me. While he was alive I stayed out of these matters entirely, but now I am the one who must keep the household going. As I reckon it, we need at least three thousand cash a month to cover our expenses. Can Tianfu and Tianshou earn that much? Before you entered the Hua household, you used to bring in at least two or three hundred cash a month at your busiest. Now that you are no longer in the troupe, that money must naturally come from the Hua household—and I should think they would not use a person for nothing. You must settle it with me: how much will they give each month? The rest you may keep for yourself, to set yourself up in life one day. Even if we economize now that your master is gone, we still need about two hundred cash a month. Your master had no family in Suzhou, and I have nowhere to go back to—what else should I do but hold onto this old establishment? I am a woman in my thirties—what other road is there? Managing a household is not easy, I tell you.

Then there are the social obligations and incidental expenses that cannot be avoided. I know you are a person of conscience—think about it for my sake: if not you, who else can I count on?" Qinyan listened, and sat in silence for a while. He thought to himself: "This is indeed a difficult problem.

I myself never knew the details of the arrangement—I never learned how much money master received. I only heard it said that he came to collect from the household once a month, but I never knew how much. Now that master is dead, they may well not continue the payments as before. And if not through the Hua household, whom else would one go to for money? It is hardly possible to negotiate with Duxiang—he is not someone one can meet with easily. Now shimu is asking me to fix a monthly sum—that is a difficult thing to do. And besides, the young master has not been as warm toward me as he used to be; how could I possibly bring up money with him?" He turned the problem over and over without arriving at any answer, and could not reply. His shimu, for her part, imagined that Young Master Hua treated Qinyan with the greatest favor, and assumed that whatever she asked for—a thousand cash, two thousand—would simply be given. For days she had been scheming about how to make the most of Qinyan's situation, until her head was quite muddled with calculations. She also feared that now that his master was dead, Qinyan might think himself free and cut loose from her. So—Changqing's widow having much the same appetite for money as Changqing himself had, a pair well-matched in that respect—she pressed Qinyan hard, determined to use him as her meal ticket. Seeing him silent, she grew suspicious and said again: "Why do you not speak? Whatever the amount, there must be a settled figure." Qinyan said: "When master first placed me in the Hua household, it was to keep me out of trouble—I truly do not know what terms he arranged. Whether the household paid him or not, and how much, I was never told. And after I moved in, I never once set eyes on master.

I only heard that master came to the household once a month, and that he waited in the gatehouse—I do not know how much he received. As for giving shimu a monthly sum, that is of course right and proper; but since I do not know for certain whether I have any money or not, how can I rashly make a promise? And what if I promised and then no money appeared—how could I face shimu?" His shimu made a sound in her throat and said: "I don't believe it—I don't know the full details either. Your master had no idea he was going to die; if he had known, he would have told me everything long ago. I've heard that last year, before you went in, Young Master Hua sent people out to say he wanted to buy out your contract—is he the sort of man who would be stingy with money? A person should act in good conscience—how is it that the moment your master dies, you change your tune?" Qinyan, stung nearly to tears by these words, could only suppress his feelings and say: "The only way is to wait until I go back and then discuss it. I have not kept a single coin for myself. What I received in gifts last year and at New Year—that is just the packet of silver from the other day.

If shimu wants three hundred cash a month, I am afraid that may not be possible—I must first ask the young master clearly before anything can be settled. But think about it for me, shimu—how could I possibly raise this matter with the young master myself?"

His shimu gave a cold laugh and said: "You have been living in his house for more than half a year—you cannot even bring this up? The closer the friendship, the more money should naturally flow. If it is the kind of friendship that produces no money, what is the use of it? Do not play the innocent with me. He has not yet spent three or five thousand taels to redeem you from your contract. If he had redeemed you, I would naturally have nothing more to say to you. Then again, there are those with a conscience who, even after being redeemed, still regularly show their gratitude to their master and mistress—that kind of person exists too. Otherwise, you could simply tell him to bring three thousand taels of silver to buy out your contract; I could use that to acquire some property, and that would be better than receiving it in dribs and drabs. There are two paths before you—choose whichever one you will. But you must settle this before you return to the city. If you go in without settling it, I cannot very well go in there after you, and that will create endless complications." With that she got up and left. Qinyan, assaulted by all this, could not argue back, yet his heart burned with anger and bitterness. He had thought that with his master gone, his own fate would be his own to manage—he had not known that his shimu would prove even more ruthless.

Seething with resentment, he lay back down and turned things over in his mind in every direction, arriving at no solution. After grieving for a while, he thought again: "Every time I cannot see my way through something, one word from Xiangwan makes it clear. I must go and talk this over with him." His mind made up, he called the young boy who attended him, changed into everyday clothes, and walked out the door.

He went to Sulan's lodgings, but found that Sulan had not yet come home. He considered going back but felt too restless for that. He thought: Baozhu's lodgings are not far from here—it would be just as well to go and find him for a chat. He had barely stepped out of Sulan's gate when he noticed two men standing in the middle of the street. He happened to glance up: one had a round, blank, bovine face, and on his feet—incongruously—a pair of black boots. The other had a weasel's sharp face and shifty eyes, and a long red cord dangling from his cap.

Qinyan kept his head down and walked on, but he could sense that the two men were following him. He heard one say in a low voice: "What a fresh bloom." Then the other: "Hmm—which household is he from? I don't recognize him. Let's trail him a while." Then the first again: "Now there's a fine-looking head." Qinyan was furious, but helpless—there was nothing to be done but let them talk. He could hear them right at his back, footstep on footstep, sleeve brushing sleeve, and with the wind at his back came a coarse, rancid smell. He quickened his pace and reached Baozhu's door. He sent the boy in to inquire—but Baozhu was not at home either. Qinyan saw that the two men had planted themselves behind him once more; anger boiling inside him, he turned and walked briskly back toward home, and the two men came hurrying right along after him. Qinyan reached his own gate, kept his head down, and went straight in.

At this hour the afternoon performances were just ending—where would any of the young entertainers be at home? Qinyan had made two wasted trips and on top of that had run into these two repellent creatures on the road, which left him feeling even more wretched. Back in his room, he heaved a long sigh, and found tears falling without his willing them.

At just this inopportune moment, his shimu's younger cousin Wu Mazi came wandering in without reading the mood in the room, sat himself on the edge of the kang, pinched his damp tobacco pipe, fished out a strip of paper for a spill, and puffed away twenty or thirty times, scattering paper-taper ash all over the floor.

He then started poking around, turning this over, peering at that. Qinyan found him unbearably tiresome and simply ignored him. After chewing on his damp tobacco for a while, Wu Mazi said to Qinyan: "I've heard that those senior gentlemen at the Hua household—needless to say about them—each man has a big house to himself, with eight or ten little concubines to share his bed.

And even the third young masters, fourth young masters, fifth young masters—even the coachmen, the stable-hands, and the grass-cutters—they were all wearing fox-fur jackets at New Year." He spread his hands to indicate the size: "Money-pouches this big, tied at their waists, and when they go to the gambling dens and undo them, they're full of silver ingots—they grab a handful and bet it all on a single throw. And then there's that troublemaker from last year, the Jin family's third son, Jin San, who went to look for trouble with you—he was in a wine shop drinking and had a band of entertainers hired to keep him company. It may be that he got his money by extorting it from your late master, but even so, he was doing well enough. Is that all true? How do these people get so rich?" Qinyan's mind was entirely occupied with his own misery and vexation; he had no heart for Wu Mazi's chatter and simply did not respond.

Wu Mazi went on: "I've heard that's not even the remarkable part. That household has so much silver it won't fit in the chests—it's piled loose against the walls. After two or three years without being used it gets damp and starts to mildew, so they have it brought out to air in the sun. They air it for a whole day and then someone swaps out the five-tael pieces for ten-tael ones, and the two-tael pieces for five-tael ones, and he doesn't even count them. Occasionally he notices and says: 'How have my silver pieces shrunk?' And his people say: 'They've been in the sun all day—they dried out and naturally contracted.' I find that rather hard to believe—does this young master really think silver can shrink in the sun?" At this, Qinyan could not help laughing and said: "Where did you hear that?" Wu Mazi replied: "There's a group of us fellows, and when we have nothing to do we gather together and talk about things like this. In the city there's a Young Master Hua, and outside the city there's an Old Master Xu in a big garden estate—between those two wealthy families you could talk for a year without running out of stories. They say that beneath the garden rocks on Old Master Xu's estate, there are ten urns of silver and ten urns of gold buried in the ground. The spirit guardian of the gold has a head of yellow fur, and the spirit guardian of the silver has a head of white fur. At midnight they sit on top of the garden wall and frighten people, and sometimes they even throw gold and silver ingots at them. If you have good fortune and they hit you, you pick up the gold and silver and go home rich. If you have no luck and they hit you, you pick it up and it's just a clod of yellow earth, and when you get home you fall ill. It seems to me that even the gods of wealth play favorites—they only oblige people who already have luck." Qinyan found this rather amusing despite himself.

Wu Mazi was just warming to his subject when someone called for him from outside, and he went out. It turned out that two visitors had come to take tea, and Wu Mazi showed them to the reception room. Sizing them up, he saw that one was very shabbily dressed, wearing old black boots, with a small cap on his head that was greasy and shiny and dusted with ash. He thought to himself: "He must be either a county-school student without prospects, or an official still waiting for an appointment." The other was somewhat better dressed, with a long red cord on his cap; though he did not look particularly prosperous, he might perhaps be a seasoned old hand at the pleasure quarters. Wu Mazi asked their names and offered them seats. And who were these two? One was Wu Dasha, the other was Ji Liangxuan—two men newly acquainted at the theater. That day both had been idling about the streets when they happened upon each other, had followed Qinyan to the gate, and now had come in. Liangxuan had a vague sense that he recognized the gate, and after thinking about it for a while it came to him—he guessed that the person he had seen just now was Qinyan. He then recalled something Xi Shiyi had said, that the previous month he had met Qinyan at Pincai's place and had him as a drinking companion. This confirmed his guess. He shared his thought with Dasha; Dasha, seeing that Liangxuan was eager and wishing to second his enthusiasm in order to cadge a few drinks on the side, said: "Whether it's him or not, since this is clearly an entertainer's lodging, we can certainly go in and sit a while and have a cup of tea." Liangxuan said: "If you want to go in, I'll keep you company." After conferring for a moment they went in together.

Inside, Wu Mazi was busying himself with arrangements when, conveniently, Tianfu and Tianshou returned from the day's performance. Tianshou thought he had seen Liangxuan somewhere before, though he could not quite remember where, and greeted him politely. As for Dasha, both boys had definitely seen him before—he was a regular audience member at the garden theater—and they greeted him as well. Dasha, in his vague and muddled way, said: "Today Lanbao's 'Stealing the Token' and 'Killing on the Boat,' and Guibao's 'The Tryst' and 'The Quarrel'—those are the true accomplishments of a master; no one else can do what they do." Liangxuan said: "Do you boys still remember me?" Tianfu replied: "Your face is familiar, but I cannot quite place you—I feel I have seen you somewhere." Tianshou stared at him for a moment, then asked: "Could you be the gentleman who came along last year with a gentleman who smoked opium? That opium-smoker started a fight with our master, and it was you who pulled them apart."

Liangxuan said: "What a good memory you have—Tianfu cannot remember at all." Now that Tianfu heard this, he recalled it too and said: "Oh! That was a frightening day. That opium-smoker was so violent—he turned over the table and kept on fighting all the way inside. Lucky I got out of the way fast, otherwise one kick from him and I'd have been half-dead." Liangxuan said: "Was it not so? And it was I who rescued you—are you grateful to me or not?" Tianshou said: "Where has that gentleman gone to now?" Liangxuan said: "He is ill at the moment." Tianfu said: "Heaven's justice! Heaven's justice! Let him be ill a good many more days." Dasha said: "Just now I saw an entertainer come in—what's his name?" Tianfu replied: "There is no one; it is just the two of us, brothers." Liangxuan said: "Someone came in just before us—a little taller than you, sixteen or seventeen years old." Tianshou said: "No, no—we only have our senior brother Qinyan, who came back from the Hua household some time ago. He is not really an entertainer any more—he has given up the stage. Perhaps it was him you saw." Liangxuan said: "Yes, yes—that's the one. Could he be asked to come out and meet us?" Tianfu shook his head: "He does not see visitors. No matter how many people know he is back and want to see him, he always refuses to come out. He only goes to Old Master Xu's Yiyuan garden—besides that household, he goes nowhere." Dasha said: "Since he won't come out, you could at least take us into his room for a sit." Tianshou shook his head: "He would scold us." Wu Mazi, standing in the corridor, said: "Our Qinguan is now the second master of the Hua household—he does not receive visitors. If the two gentlemen are in the mood, let Tianfu and Tianshou attend you."

Dasha looked at Liangxuan and said: "Since you are old acquaintances, it is only natural that you should exchange a word or two—there is no reason to simply sit here like this." Liangxuan demurred: "I have something I must attend to." Tianshou said: "You have nothing to attend to—you simply do not wish to honor us with your presence." Liangxuan said: "I truly do have something." Wu Mazi said: "Stay a while—even if you have something, there is no need to rush. Now that his master is gone, his mistress depends entirely on these two boys." Dasha said: "You rarely come out, and I am tired from walking—let us sit a while." He also asked Tianfu: "When did your master pass away?" Tianfu replied: "The twenty-fifth of last month." Dasha said: "Alas—I had no idea he was gone. You may not recognize me, but your master and I were on very good terms." Tianshou said: "I always see you at the theater, but you never stay long—you always leave before the end. Most of the time you are going, not staying." Dasha replied: "I have many friends; if I attend to one and neglect another, people will complain." Tianfu said: "I see you come in and go out, come out and go back in again—you seem very busy." Dasha said: "If I have come to this garden to look in on things, I naturally have to go to that garden to look in on things too—otherwise people will complain of me there as well." Wu Mazi had by now drifted off elsewhere.

Shortly afterward Liangxuan made to leave, but Tianfu held him back; Dasha, however, showed no sign of moving. Just then one of the servants came in and arranged a few dishes on the table. Tianfu said: "Master Ji, please stay." Liangxuan was anxious; he made eyes at Dasha and jerked his head, clearly meaning for him to go. Dasha pretended not to notice and stroked the few sparse, stubby hairs of his rat-like moustache. Seeing Dasha would not budge, Liangxuan thought he would slip away on his own, and said: "I need to use the lavatory." Tianshou pointed to the corner of the courtyard and said: "Over by the east wall is fine." Liangxuan walked out of the room, crossed to the middle of the courtyard, and broke into a run.

But he had not reckoned on Tianshou being right behind him; Tianshou gave his pigtail a sharp yank and Liangxuan's hat tumbled to the ground, leaving his scalp stinging from the pull.

Tianshou grinned; Liangxuan wheeled around, his face flushed, and said: "What kind of nonsense is that?" Tianshou picked up the hat, dusted it off, put it back on Liangxuan's head, and dragged him back inside.

Liangxuan said: "I really do have something to do—why must you torture me like this?" Dasha, whose throat had been itching at the sight of the wine, urged Liangxuan: "They are keeping you with such sincerity—do honor them a little. Since the dishes are already out, to refuse them would be making things awkward for them." Liangxuan had no choice; and seeing that Dasha refused to leave and was in fact the one keeping him there, he supposed Dasha intended to play the host. If Dasha was paying, then he could relax—and so he reluctantly sat back down. Tianfu and Tianshou each poured a cup. Liangxuan drank two cups and watched Dasha drink with cheerful abandon, grabbing handfuls of apricot kernels and tossing them into his mouth without pause, then working his way through three hawthorn berries and a persimmon cake.

Liangxuan was beginning to want a look at Qinyan. By now lamps had been lit, and he said to Tianfu: "Take me into your senior brother's room for a sit." Tianfu replied: "If you really must see him, let me go in and say a word first." Tianfu went in and found Qinyan reading, and said: "There is a Master Ji outside who would like to meet you—will you see him?" Qinyan said: "Why would I want to see him? Have you ever seen me receive a visitor?" Tianfu, rebuffed, was about to come back out when Qinyan thought to close the door—but before he could, Liangxuan and Dasha had already reached the doorway and were both squeezing their way inside.

Qinyan's face was full of anger; he had not yet spoken when Dasha made a deep bow, and Liangxuan bent forward in a half-bow, his face wreathed in smiles. Qinyan found he had no choice but to return their bow—he could not very well walk away. Without waiting to be invited they sat down.

Liangxuan narrowed his ratty eyes and opened his mouth to speak, but Dasha got in first: "Who would have thought that so many days had passed without my seeing your honored master, and it turns out he has gone to heaven—I had no idea whatsoever. Not only did I fail to send even a small contribution, I never even went to pay my respects at the bier. We were on good terms for many years; he treated me well in the past—it was no ordinary friendship." He shook his head: "A great lapse—a great lapse! I wonder whether my name appeared on the condolence banners or the contributed wreaths?" Liangxuan said with his most ingratiating smile: "I had the honor of calling on you last year—and you had just entered the Hua household, which meant I could not see you, though you were in my thoughts morning and night, until this very day.

A few days ago I also heard that you shared a table with my patron, and I did not have the good fortune to be there to attend you. My patron is a straightforward man—he lacks the gift of warmth and delicacy; I hope you will overlook his shortcomings and not hold them against him." Qinyan now recognized these two as the men who had followed him on the street, and felt a surge of renewed anger. But seeing them bow so respectfully, one claiming to have been a friend of his master's, the other saying he had dined at the same table with some patron of his—he could not quite work out what manner of creatures they were, and did not bother to investigate; he gave a vague reply and told the boy to bring them two cups of tea.

Dasha drank his tea, noticed a couplet hanging on the wall, and read it aloud—mispronouncing two characters. Dasha's classical education being decidedly thin, and his eyes being nearsighted into the bargain, he was nonetheless especially fond of reading out couplets and peering at paintings, so as to pass himself off as a man of culture. Qinyan saw the mistake and smiled inwardly; he took one look at their faces and already felt quite disgusted. He then heard Liangxuan say with his simpering grin: "My patron is really very easy to get along with. You do not yet know his temperament—once you are well acquainted, I daresay you will find it hard to be without him." Dasha said: "Whatever became of Chunlan?" Liangxuan replied: "Chunlan, certainly. Naturally, a good deal of money has been spent, so of course affections follow. And as for my Yingguan—I lent him for a couple of days and he has stayed till now and refuses to come back. The little creature has grown fond of him and thrown all my past kindness to the winds. But I cannot blame him—as they say, a white pigeon always flies toward the flourishing place; it is only human nature. Besides, my patron is considered one of the grand fellows of the capital—he is on a par with Young Master Hua and Young Master Xu, and indeed they are all on friendly terms. A few days ago Young Master Xu called and happened to find my patron not at home, so he came and sat in my study for quite a long time. When I saw him out, he pressed me again and again to come and take a tour of the garden." Dasha said: "Did you go?" Liangxuan replied: "At first I rather intended to—and indeed all the young gentlemen and men of letters who move in that circle are quite friendly with me as well. But then I thought: he has never yet held an official post outside the capital, so he may not fully appreciate the dignity of our station. When it comes to precedence at table, ours is the highest in every gathering—and I was afraid he might have other seniors and elders to consider and feel reluctant to yield to me, which is why in the end I did not go." Dasha said: "What a pity—what a pity! I have eaten at his table before; I suspect his dinners are among the finest in the capital." Qinyan, finding this altogether unbearable, was relieved that Tianfu and Tianshou were both in the room. He said to Tianfu: "Please take the two gentlemen to sit outside—I have something to attend to." And he walked straight out. The two visitors, put out, had no choice but to follow Tianfu and Tianshou out as well.

Liangxuan now resolved to make his escape and headed directly for the door, but was caught again by Tianfu and Tianshou. Four more small side-dishes and two bowls of congee had been added to the table. Liangxuan thought to himself: "What kind of entertainment is this—not a single proper dish in the lot. Can eight plates of dried fruit, four dishes of side-dishes, and two bowls of plain congee really count as entertaining guests? Unless Dasha has told them he is paying, and they are trying to economize. If Dasha is the host, I might as well eat my fill." Liangxuan picked up his chopsticks and ate for a while. Dasha had already drunk two pots of wine and polished off all four side-dishes, eaten two bowls of congee, and wiped his mouth. Seeing that Liangxuan was not very cheerful, Dasha said to Tianshou: "Master Ji likes his drinking to be lively—tell someone to bring more dishes, and get the wine warmed up nice and hot, and have a game of finger-guessing with Master Ji. I was the one who brought him here today; if you don't attend to him properly, he won't want to come again." Liangxuan, supposing he was being treated, immediately brightened, and said quickly: "What kind of meal is this anyway—it hardly counts as dinner." Tianshou said: "Of course it doesn't count as dinner—I assumed you had already eaten, and thought you were just sitting down for a casual cup of wine. Since Master Ji has not dined, we can perfectly well prepare a proper meal." Dasha said: "Right—and I haven't eaten either. Master Ji is fond of a puff or two—why not invite him in for a lie-down?" Tianfu said: "I did see you have a puff or two that one time—just a few draws, not much." Liangxuan, seeing Dasha playing the energetic host, began to feel quite pleased. Tianfu took them inside and meanwhile gave instructions to the kitchen to add dishes and prepare food. Liangxuan did not actually smoke—he only used the pipe as a diversion. Tianfu and Tianshou, who did have a few puffs of a habit themselves, fell to bickering over loading the pipe. Dasha took advantage of the moment when no one was watching and slipped quietly out. He was full anyway, and off he went, tramping in his battered black boots, and was gone.

Liangxuan chatted with the two boys for a while, asking about Qinyan's situation. Wu Mazi came in to announce that dinner was ready. Only then did Liangxuan start looking for Dasha—but Dasha had vanished without a trace, and Liangxuan's expression changed as he kept urgently trying to find Wu Dasha. Tianshou said: "He left. That man can never stay put—I see him at the theater and he comes and goes a dozen times a day. He will probably be back soon enough. Let us sit down—no need to wait for him." Liangxuan had no choice but to sit. He looked at the dishes—four plates and two bowls, plus two trays of steamed cakes—and ate a bit. He was entirely dispirited throughout, his mind occupied with how to make his exit.

Wu Mazi stood nearby, and noticing that Dasha had already left and that this remaining guest seemed distracted and uneasy—as though he had something on his mind—could not quite guess that the man was planning to bolt. Changqing's widow, since her husband's death, was treating this as the household's first time receiving guests, and had told Wu Mazi to look after the old regulars properly and not let them feel neglected; she was standing by the window quite frequently. The two young boys, for their part, had no knack for conversation, and their talk came out all jumbled. Liangxuan grew more and more unable to sit still; sooner or later he would have to go in any case, so when he had finished eating and rinsed his mouth he said to Tianfu: "I have imposed on you all today—I will simply have to make it up to you tomorrow. As for tonight, I happen not to have brought any money with me." Tianfu, hearing this, was struck dumb for a moment and dared not respond. Tianshou, being slightly quicker on his feet, said: "Since you did not bring money, your residence is not far—let someone escort you back, and the money can be brought here." Liangxuan replied: "That is not necessary—I will bring it tomorrow." Wu Mazi, overhearing this, thought: "Something is not right here—I did not expect these two gentlemen to be like this." He came inside and positioned himself by the window, watching Liangxuan. Liangxuan thought simply to stride out—but Tianshou grabbed him and said: "There is no hurry—sit a bit longer." Liangxuan paid him no heed and made to go; Tianfu also came and took hold of him. Liangxuan thought: I might as well borrow a trick from Xi Shiyi last year and bluster my way through. He thundered: "What is the meaning of this! Nobody walks around with payment ready in hand at all times! When gentlemen patronize entertainers, they settle the account over several visits in one lump sum. You ill-bred creatures—let go of me, before I lose my temper and do exactly as I did last year, and give you all a thrashing." The two boys were frightened and fell silent. Wu Mazi was a man who knew nothing of proper conduct and reckoned that since Changqing was dead, his cousin depended entirely on him. If the first piece of business after reopening went like this, it would look very bad for him—and he had been the one urging the visitors to stay. He heard what Liangxuan said and his temper flared. He said: "Master Ji, that is not a reasonable way to speak. You have not been here more than once or twice, and after being attended to for half the day—wine, food, tea, and tobacco, all purchased with real money—not a single cash appears, and on top of that you scold us for being ill-bred. Saying we should escort you home was not wrong either—surely you have somewhere you live? Whether it's a lodging-house or a temple, if you don't have it on you, naturally we go to your residence to collect it. Did those words really give offence?

And since you have no money with you, we are not detaining you or holding anything of yours as surety—naturally we accompany you back. Once we know your address, even waiting a day to collect is acceptable." Liangxuan had nothing to say in reply; he tried to think of another bluster but could not manage it. With that scrawny fishbone of a frame and that blockhead of his, whatever air of menace he tried to assume was not enough to frighten anyone. He had no choice but to say: "I was only trying to save you the bother of following me—that's all. If you don't mind the walk, then come and collect your reward." Wu Mazi thought those boys were useless for the job and decided to go himself, taking a lantern to light the way. Liangxuan made a few tentative zigzags, still hoping to give him the slip—but Wu Mazi kept the lantern trained on him without a gap, and Liangxuan was forced to go back to his lodgings. He asked Wu Mazi to wait at the door, inwardly cursing his luck and kicking himself for being taken in by Dasha's shameless scheming. He unlocked his document-chest and sorted through the banknotes, examining each one as though it were a piece of his own flesh being cut away. Steeling himself against the pain, he selected one note for two strings of cash, then rummaged through a stack of paper slips and found one for one string of cash, wanting to have someone take them out—but his attendant was not at home. He had no choice but to roll a paper spill, dip it in lamp-oil, and carry it out himself, handing the notes to Wu Mazi, then turning and walking straight back inside.

Wu Mazi could not read, but Changqing in his lifetime had sent him to fetch money against banknotes many times—too many to count. The characters from one to ten, however they were written, he recognized every one. He held the notes under the lantern: two strings of cash. He called out: "Master Ji, come back!" Liangxuan almost pretended not to hear, but Wu Mazi had already followed him through the door—Liangxuan had no choice but to respond: "What is it now?" Wu Mazi said: "Two strings of cash—what does this mean? Is this your gift to me? What about the young man's fee, and the cost of the meal?" Liangxuan replied: "I am not sure—it's meant to cover everything." Wu Mazi said: "Master Ji, please do not joke. If that is what you say, then please take it back." And he held the note back out. Liangxuan could do nothing—he had to add the one-string note as well, and said: "This is every last bit there is—take it or leave it." Wu Mazi was not about to accept this and said heatedly: "If you were going to be so sparing with your money, you should have thought it through beforehand and not come in at all. Just for wine alone, the minimum would be twenty strings of cash—never mind adding dishes and a full meal! Three strings of cash is not even enough to tip the kitchen boy and the servants. And you want that to settle everything." Liangxuan simply walked inside and ignored him.

Wu Mazi tried to follow him in, and a gatekeeper who had heard the commotion came out to ask what was the matter.

Wu Mazi explained everything in detail. The gatekeeper laughed: "Our master here has really been trying to get something for nothing—wants all the pleasure and won't part with the cash. You might as well let it go—chalk it up to a loss this time, and don't make a scene here. If our master happens to hear, it could get awkward." Wu Mazi saw that Liangxuan had already gone inside and he could not very well go in after him. With the gatekeeper's persuasion, and realizing this was Xi Shiyi's lodgings and that making trouble there could lead to worse trouble for himself, he had no choice but to turn back—though not without venting his frustration at some length on the way home to give his account.

Changqing's widow, when she saw only three strings of cash, said: "Who was ever paid off with three strings? You've been in this business long enough—have you ever seen three strings handed over? Why didn't you throw it back in his face and shame him a little? Reeking and putrid—two young entertainers keep two guests, wine, tea, liquor, food, the whole filthy mess, and on top of that you escort the man home like a lackey, carrying his lantern, and you come back clutching three strings of cash with your tail between your legs. A grown man who can't even read a number—have you never seen a banknote before?" She ranted until Wu Mazi's ears burned, and he shouted back: "As if I didn't know! I've seen thousands, tens of thousands, and I've never seen two such shameless creatures. One of them drank two bowls of congee and slipped away first. The other one was also looking to run at every moment—I had a time of it cornering him, then had to escort him back.

I figured ten strings of cash at least, no less. Who would have thought he'd first offer only two strings, and the extra one was added afterward. Who wanted to take it from that turtle-son? He crammed it into my hand and ducked inside. I thought about following him in, but a gatekeeper came out to calm things down, saying it was Xi Shiyi's lodgings. Is Xi Shiyi someone to trifle with? Last year he came out of nowhere to make trouble over Qinyan, grabbed his brother-in-law and threw him over in a sprawling tumble—the man couldn't get up for half an hour, and the tables and chairs were smashed to splinters. If I'd run into him again today, I might have gotten a beating for nothing and come away with even these three strings—which is why I took what I could and left. As if I didn't want thirty strings from him?" Changqing's widow said: "It's all you blind fools, who can't tell a person from a ghost. Obviously the man came in to take tea—and you held him there by force, taking a bedbug for a jeweled toy. In the future, whenever a shameless good-for-nothing like that walks in, throw him out immediately. This is not a soup kitchen, and he is not my son—eat and wipe his mouth and walk away. Now that the master is dead, today was our first day open for business, and we ran into two worthless wretches—just like that Yang the Eight from the Yang ginger-shop the year before last, who only got away with it because Yutianxian still calls him brother-in-law. When all is said and done, it's just eating and drinking for nothing. These shameless bastards—they are truly infuriating!" Changqing's widow ranted on for some time. The next day Wu Mazi went to exchange the notes for cash—only to discover that the one-string note added later was counterfeit. He went to Xi Shiyi's lodgings to find Liangxuan, and was roundly abused by Xi Shiyi's servant. Wu Mazi returned with his grievance unredressed, had to make up the difference from his own pocket to square the accounts, and could do nothing but curse Liangxuan.
