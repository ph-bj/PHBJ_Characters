Chapter 43 — Su Huifang uses keen wit to outmaneuver the widow; Xu Ziyun redeems Qinyan at great expense.

That evening, Qinyan endured a great deal of empty talk from Ji Liangxuan and Wu Dasha, leaving his spirit ever more clouded. Fortunately they soon took their leave. He waited until the second watch, yet seeing no sign of Baozhu or Sulan, he had little choice but to retire for the night in solitude. He lay restless through the long night. At the first touch of dawn, he dispatched a servant boy to summon the two of them.

That morning, Sulan had already been called away by Wang Wenhui at an early hour. Presently Baozhu arrived. "I missed you yesterday," said Baozhu. "I did not return until the third watch, and no one thought to tell me. Your Wu'er only just mentioned it just now. I haven't seen you these two or three days — why haven't you come out to take the air? Today Duxiang has invited us to enjoy the apricot blossoms. Shall we all go together?" "Very well," said Qinyan. "These past two days I have had a slight chill and felt rather weary. I too should like to go out for some air. Besides, my leave has run out, and I must soon think about returning to the city." "A few more days can't hurt," said Baozhu. "Once you go back, we shall be apart far more than we are together." "There is something that has been troubling me of late," said Qinyan, "and I am quite at a loss. I asked you and Xiangwan to come precisely so I might think of some plan with your help." "Tell me what the trouble is," said Baozhu, "though if you cannot think of a solution yourself, I am afraid I may fare no better." "Yesterday my master's wife questioned me about the day I entered the Hua household — what Master Hua's young lord said to my shifu, and whether my shifu had ever received money from them. She also said that the household costs two thousand four hundred strings a month, and demanded that I pay her two hundred strings a month, saying she would not let me return to the city until this was settled. As I recall, I was sent in last year because of the Hua family's matter concerning Xi the Eleventh; after I entered I never even met my shifu, and I still don't know the full story. Now, out of nowhere, my master's wife is demanding two hundred strings from me a month — how am I ever to manage that? She also wants me to speak to Young Master Hua myself. She says that since my shifu died I have grown faithless. She claims the Hua household has never spent three or five thousand taels on me. Now she wants me to ask the young master to pay three thousand silver taels to buy out my indenture, and only then will she stop demanding monthly support from me. Otherwise, she says, she will depend on me for the rest of her life. As things stand, I have no engagements and cannot go out to entertain; the only income I have is the occasional festival gift from the household — how could I possibly save two hundred strings a month? You are a sensible person — is it possible to say such things to the young master? Is it not a dreadfully difficult matter? My master's wife simply does not understand how hard this is, and does nothing but demand money. Please think of some plan for me; I am utterly without ideas." Baozhu, hearing all this, felt the difficulty keenly. After pondering a while, she said: "Two thousand four hundred strings a year comes to three thousand taels over three years. This 'support money' has no end to it. Young Master Hua has an uncertain temperament — one cannot rely on him. If you wished him to buy out your indenture, perhaps he might consent when in the right mood, but someone would need to approach him on your behalf. And there is another consideration: if he does buy out your indenture, you would in effect become his person, and could no longer act freely — you would likely spend the rest of your days in his household. This is something you must decide for yourself." "I have thought of all that," said Qinyan, "but even now, though I have not been formally released, I cannot act as a free person." "If someone were to buy out your indenture," said Baozhu, "what would you do afterward? Remain outside, or would you be willing to enter the Hua household?" "I cannot decide that just yet," said Qinyan. "Let me first be released, and then think about leaving the household." Baozhu laughed: "Most people have only one way out of a contract. You seem to have two, but mind you don't end up with seven causes for expulsion." Qinyan laughed as well.

Just then Sulan walked in, and Qinyan and Baozhu invited him to sit. "Where did you go so early?" asked Qinyan. "Lord Wang sent for me this morning," said Sulan. "I thought it must be something urgent, but it turned out to be quite a trifling matter. I had a chat with Jiantan and Yong'an, and only just got home. I heard you had sent for me. What is it you need?" Baozhu repeated everything that had just been discussed. Sulan clapped his hands and laughed: "Just as I thought! Just as I predicted! I truly admire him. My view is that release from indenture is the right course, and you should agree to it." "Easy enough to say," said Qinyan. "You settle the matter in a single breath. Where are the three thousand taels to come from? And could I possibly ask Young Master Hua for such a thing?" "Must it be exactly three thousand?" said Sulan. "What about two thousand — would that do?" "Now we're getting somewhere," said Baozhu. "We invite you to help us think, and your first word is 'agree to the release,' while your second is to slash the price. Clearly you already have a plan. Does it not seem manageable?" "Someone else would still need to do the asking," said Qinyan. "Whether it's three thousand or two thousand, I cannot say it to him myself." Baozhu asked Sulan: "Even if we say two thousand, what is your brilliant scheme? Do enlighten us." "This matter is one I had already thought through with someone more than ten days ago," said Sulan, "and we had even discussed it once. But it was all uncertain, so I said nothing about it to anyone." "And who is it you say you admire?" asked Baozhu. "One day I was chatting idly with Meixiang," said Sulan, "and Yunong's name happened to come up. Meixiang said his master's wife—" At this point Sulan glanced out the window and said: "Can they hear us from over there?" "No, they cannot," said Qinyan. Sulan continued: "Meixiang said his master's wife is as hard a taskmaster as his shifu was, and fears she will make Yunong support her for the rest of her life. Although Yunong does not perform anymore, he has still not been formally released. As for Yunong's money, it is not a small amount, but at this point he probably has little saved up. If four or five thousand strings could secure his release, we could pull something together among friends. A few of his close acquaintances might each contribute a share. I am speaking of you, the Wang brothers, Shoux­iang, Peixian, and the rest. Surely none of them would refuse. If we could gather half, the other half would be easier." "And what happens after the release?" asked Baozhu. "We did not get that far in our discussion," said Sulan. "So long as he is released, this person belongs to himself. After that, naturally he may do as he pleases." "If he remains in the Hua household, it is no different from not being released, as he still cannot act freely," said Baozhu. "Young Master Hua has not purchased him," said Sulan, "and his shifu never drew up a deed of sale to the Hua household. How could he not be free? Is he to stay there his whole life?" "Speaking here is awkward after all," said Baozhu. "Why don't we all go find Meixiang and discuss it? We can go to Duxiang's together and see the apricot blossoms. The ornamental peaches have opened splendidly too. I can't think why the season is so early this year; I remember the ornamental peaches blooming in the middle of the third month in past years. Duxiang is not hosting anyone today, so there is nothing wrong with a few of us dropping in for a chat." Qinyan was very glad to go, and changed into fresh clothes, and ordered the carriage to be harnessed. As Sulan and Baozhu had come on foot, the two of them sent their attendants back to fetch their carriages, along with instructions about which garments to bring, all to be delivered to the Su family's Peixiang Hall.

The two of them rode together in Qinyan's carriage to the place where Huifang was staying.

It happened that Huifang was at home. The three of them entered, and found Huifang at the writing table looking over several album leaves. He rose with a smile to welcome them, saying: "Today I have indeed received three uninvited guests." The three smiled, but did not sit down at once, turning instead to look at the albums. Baozhu was the first to seize the painted one, and the other two clustered round to look with him. There were landscapes and flower studies, all rendered with great skill, as Huifang had newly commissioned them from Master Qu Daoweng. On the last page was a painting of a beauty leaning on a balustrade in a mood of melancholy: fallen petals carpeted the ground below the railing, and a pair of swallows came flying through, as if evoking the lines:

"Fallen blossoms drift, and alone stands the solitary figure;
A gentle spring rain descends, as paired swallows take to flight."

The image stirred in Qinyan's memory the riddle from long ago, and a sudden vague emotion passed over him. He read the quatrain inscribed below, and softly recited it to himself:

"Spring's vivid hues stir the heart as swallows weave and fly,
Apricot blossoms in fine rain, yet my sleeves remain dry.

"Alone I lean upon the rail as melancholy gently grows;
Where fragrant grasses stretch to heaven, you have yet to come."

They looked through the calligraphy album as well. Huifang invited the three to sit, and asked: "Did you all arrive together by chance, or was this arranged?" "We came by arrangement," said Baozhu. "Let us all go together to Duxiang's to see the apricot blossoms." "Is there a banquet today?" asked Huifang. "No banquet," said Baozhu. "Call it three uninvited guests — what is the harm?" Huifang nodded and smiled his assent. Sulan's and Baozhu's clothes and carriages soon arrived; the two changed into fresh garments. Huifang went inside and changed as well, then asked: "You came all this way together without any purpose? Simply to see the flowers?" "There is one matter to discuss," said Sulan. "Let us talk of it at Yiyuan." "Why not say a little now?" said Huifang. "It is still early — there is no need to hurry to Duxiang's." Sulan then briefly outlined the matter of Qinyan's master's wife demanding that Qinyan be released from his indenture. "Indeed!" said Huifang. "I told you the other day, and you said it might not come to pass, yet here it is, just as I predicted. The matter is not so very difficult to arrange. The only fear is that a woman's word cannot be relied upon, as she may agree today and change her mind tomorrow. Or she may name a figure and then keep raising it. And who is to go and speak with her?" "That is no great obstacle," said Sulan. "We ourselves could go and speak with her." "In that case, let us discuss it further at Yiyuan," said Huifang. And so they all boarded their carriages and drove directly to Yiyuan.

Entering the garden, they passed beneath crimson peach boughs and emerald willows, beside green water and among blue hills. Crossing a red bridge and winding through ten layers of painted doors, they arrived at last at the East-Wind-Last-Night Tower. From above came the sound of clear singing and the tap of a clapperboard: someone was performing up there. The four of them halted to listen. It sounded like Duxiang's voice, singing a passage to the tune of "Lan Hua Mei." They listened carefully:

"Say not that on the moon-bright Jasper Terrace we chanced to meet,
Or that atop the Mountain of Clustered Jade we claimed the highest seat.
I endure night after night while stars tilt and moon sinks, the cold upon me keen,
And day after day the copper vase and paper curtained bed hold winter's sting between —
Let me but taste that first faint thread of fragrant tidings, and be serene."

None of the four could place the text in any known score, yet the flute harmonies were delicately mournful and pleasant to the ear. Then they heard Ziyun laugh and say: "That still won't do — let me come and play the flute instead." And then what seemed to be Cixian's voice singing:

"In this very pleasure-quarter, stars and moon dissolve in mist through the night,
I have heard every song and every flute that rides the river wind's flight.
It chimes with the curtain-ring's jade-clear striking, dingdong, dingdong bright,
Drawing me into the plum-blossom dream of parting-sorrow, parting-fright,
Until moon sets, stars wheel, while the bleak temple bell sounds the night's last rite."

The four were thoroughly absorbed in listening when the singing suddenly ceased. They heard Cixian say: "Truly, sung aloud, the rhythm is quite beautiful." Then Ziyun: "Why not transcribe the whole score with the gongche notation and have them learn it properly?" The four, realising no more singing was coming, walked inside together. A page boy scrambled up the stairs to announce their arrival.

Baozhu and the others climbed the staircase into the upper room. Cixian and Ziyun came forward with smiling faces; seeing Qinyan and Huifang among the visitors, they were all the more pleased, saying: "We hardly expected you today." "I brought them all," said Baozhu. And then, turning to Cixian: "Shouxiang is unwell and could not come."

This was Qinyan's first time ascending that tower. He saw that it curved and folded, rising in tier upon tier, spanning several dozen rooms, all wreathed in apricot blossoms. Some parts rose three storeys, others two; the passages branched in every direction, winding and opening unexpectedly, truly in the manner of a labyrinthine palace. Looking ahead, he could see the Peach Blossom Dell, set apart by a small hill.

A clear stream ran between. The peach blossoms were in full bloom, the ornamental peaches just half-unfurled, and together with the apricot blossoms beyond, the scene seemed as though clouds and rosy mists were rising together. Above the tower hung a plaque reading "East-Wind-Last-Night Tower," with a long couplet below:

"One night of delicate, lingering rain, and lo, the swallows have flown in.
The curtain lifts on the east wind, where Northern Song and Southern Tang judged the music of the pleasure-quarters;"

"Three parts of spring's allure: ask whether the apricot blossoms have yet opened.
Through latticed windows of carved jade, red castanets and ivory raps choose the songs of the singing-rooms."

Cixian and Ziyun observed that the four visitors were unusually well turned out that day. Some were resplendent and some elegant, as though each had taken pains to outshine the others. All four still wore their short fur outer jackets. Qinyan wore dark fox-ear velvet, Baozhu wore dark fox with a raked-claw pattern, Huifang wore cloud-fox with a raked-claw pattern, and Sulan wore cloud-fox cut in domino-block points. Seen together, they were like coral trees and jade branches intertwined, like rare orchids and spirit-flowers vying in colour, so that the thousand red apricot boughs outside the tower could not match the four jade figures within. Even Cixian and Ziyun, accustomed as they were to such company, could not help glancing at them again and again. "There are no elaborate dishes today — only a modest table," said Ziyun. "If you are hungry, do begin." "I confess I am rather hungry," said Huifang. Ziyun called for several confections to be brought first, and presently a few courses were set out, and they ate and drank lightly together. "What was the piece you were just performing?" asked Baozhu. "The rhythm seemed quite familiar, but the words I did not recognise."

"A close friend of mine from years ago," said Cixian, "composed a twenty-act drama score called Dream of the Plum Blossoms. The other day I found it at the bottom of a book-chest, and using the Nine-Palace Notation, I worked out the gongche for his lyric modes. It came out quite well in pitch. But I have only notated this one act, 'Entering the Dream'; whether the rest can be sung, I cannot say. Tomorrow I shall consult the musicians in your troupe and see if the whole can be scored." "What a shame," said Huifang. "I thought the text very fine. You had better score it yourself. If you give it to our musicians, they will smear and alter it until it conforms to their own mouths, cutting here, adding there, corrupting it past all sense. Unless one could ask Master Qu to advise." "He has little interest in musical theory," said Cixian. "Beyond playing the qin, nothing else engages him. If you speak to him of it, he simply says that since the Three Dynasties music has perished, and that is why the Book of Music was absorbed into the Rites." All four laughed. "I have obtained some dried river scallops today," said Ziyun. "Dried ones, of course, as they make a finer broth than common dried seafood, though not equal to fresh." "I have heard," said Qinyan, "that fresh lychees paired with fresh river scallops have a flavour unlike anything else in the world. Dried lychees lose all their charm and are no better than longans. What can dried river scallops be like?" Huifang suddenly fell into a deep reverie, silent and bowed in thought. Ziyun was rather surprised. Huifang was habitually lively and witty, so this sudden gravity was unexpected. "Does Meixiang have something on his mind?" asked Cixian. "No," said Huifang. "You were quite merry just now," said Ziyun. "What has come over you?" Baozhu and the others also noticed that Huifang seemed ill at ease. Huifang said nothing for a while, then spoke: "How many days can a flower bloom?" "Seventy years," rejoined Cixian. "How can that be?" "A person lives, by our reckoning, seventy years, and blooms a year for every year alive." "This year's flowers are not last year's flowers." "From last year's flowers come this year's flowers." "Can this year's flowers last until next year?" "That depends on those who tend them." "You have all suddenly taken up Chan riddles," said Sulan. "In my view," said Qinyan, "it would be better if flowers never bloomed at all." "Why? I say it would be better if they never fell," said Baozhu. "A flower that cannot fall is a flower that cannot bloom," said Huifang. "Listen to what Yunong just said. How wondrous is the fresh lychee, and yet once dried, it turns sour and repellent. How is it that a thing's appearance and its flavour both change? Perhaps it is that people, too, after some years grow impure where once they were pure, coarse where once they were fine, sour where once they were sweet." "Even if a little sour," said Baozhu, "the finest things still surpass common flavours." These words drew sighs from the three young performers together. Cixian and Ziyun were rather gratified. "We must see," continued Huifang, "whether Jingyi will be the same at seventy." "Spring blossom, autumn fruit have each their season," laughed Cixian. "Even the lychee in its fresh prime was worthy of the consort Yang Yuhuan. Now that it has dried, it is still worthy of Master Qu Daoweng, remaining superior to dates and chestnuts." The whole company laughed at this.

"The comparison is apt, but rather unfair to Daoweng," said Ziyun. "He at least is not sour. Let him be compared to dried river scallops instead." "That is even more unfair to him," said Cixian. "You are a man from Zhejiang, so naturally you sing the praises of river scallops. If you ask me, dried scallops are like an aging courtesan who has retired to respectable life: all the grace of her prime is gone, yet a faint smell of the old trade lingers still." "Among all edible things," Baozhu asked Cixian, "what would you rank first?" "My palate differs from most," said Cixian, "and I dare not pronounce. Personally, I would put fish above all else." "Well said," agreed Qinyan and Huifang together. "Among foods, as among people," Cixian continued, "there are distinctions: celestial rank, divine rank, transcendent rank, exquisite rank. Some are fit to simmer dragon and boil phoenix, others fit to drink moonlight and dine on dew. Force them into contrary roles, and neither is at its best. Hence it is that what one person praises, another disparages. Mencius says: 'In the sense of taste, all mouths are alike.'

"He was speaking in general of the seasonings blended by Yi Ya, which could suit all palates. As for today's cooks, they suit only their own palates." "Exactly," said Ziyun. "Take that bear's paw dish last year. What a waste it was. No wonder Duke Ling of Jin had his cook put to death. He must have had only the one paw left. Had he kept back a few more pairs, his resentment might not have reached quite that pitch." The whole table laughed.

"Last time you were matching opera titles," Baozhu said to Qinyan, "and you gave a set of four characters. Since then I thought of a couplet of my own." "What is it?" asked Qinyan. "'Touring the Lake to Borrow an Umbrella'" said Baozhu, "matched with 'Searching the Mountain and Smashing the Cart.'" "Excellent. Perfectly balanced," said Qinyan. "Then there is 'Parting from Mother Under a Hail of Arrows,'" said Huifang, "which matches 'Admonishing the Son with a Single Sword.'" "In that vein," said Sulan, "'Making a Ruckus in Court by Killing the Dog' matches 'Robbing the Inn and Stealing the Chicken.'" "They clearly know the repertoire through and through," laughed Ziyun, "and recall it without the least effort." "Naturally," said Cixian, "— we know a few titles too, but only half by heart." "I have a little diversion with dice," said Ziyun, "to test your wits." He called for three dice. "Is this the 'flying lyrics' game?" asked Huifang. "No, that is far simpler than this. You arrange three dice to form a line of poetry based on whatever they suggest. For instance, a four can represent a person, or a flower, or water, or wind. It stands for whatever you imagine it to represent. Is that not easy enough? Jingyi and I shall drink, and you shall set the dice." Baozhu took the dice and said: "Let me try. I may manage or I may not." She set a one, a four, and a five, and recited: "'Red apricot trees lean over the clouds by the sun's edge.'" Both Cixian and Ziyun praised it: "Excellently done! The five is perfect for clouds, which is quite refined. We shall drink to that." Sulan snatched the dice and said: "My turn." She set three red faces and recited: "'On apricot branches, the urgency of spring calls out.'" Ziyun praised her as well, for the three red faces yielding that one word, 'urgency,' was a stroke of real inventiveness, and said to Cixian: "We drink again." "The phrase 'on the branches' seems to lack its proper ground," said Huifang, who then set a four and two fives, and recited: "'A whole unbroken flush of apricot-red for ten li around.'" "Even better," said Ziyun, "'Triumphant, the new laureate rides home swift as a flying horse.' This is a happy omen for Xiangfan. Let us all drink, Meixiang included." Pleased by Ziyun's apt remark, Huifang smiled warmly and drank. Qinyan took the dice, set a four and two threes, and said: "You have all spoken of apricot blossoms, but I shall speak of the peach." He recited: "'Peach blossoms upon the current, gone beyond all trace.'" "Very good," said Ziyun. "There was no restriction to apricot blossoms, as anything goes." He and Cixian each drank. Baozhu set two threes and a one, and recited: "'Side by side to roost, side by side to fly, through a whole lifetime."' Ziyun and Cixian praised her and drank. Huifang then snatched the dice back and set two sixes and a four placed at an angle. "Look how eager he is," laughed Sulan, "grabbing them from me. And now he sets out that arrangement. Surely a splendid line is coming." Huifang recited: "'Beyond the pearl-screen curtain, slanting toward those within.'" Everyone praised the line at once — "What a perfect line, 'Beyond the pearl-screen curtain, slanting toward those within.' The dice truly look just so; let us all drink a cup together." Sulan set two sixes and a four and recited: "'In each of the twelve towers, flowers are in their prime.'" Cixian and Ziyun drank a cup apiece. Qinyan set two ones and a three and recited: "'One by one they return to the nest, and how I envy the crows.'" Cixian glanced at Qinyan with a flicker of thought: Yunong's lines today all carried an air of desolation, so why did he keep choosing such verses? Afterward the group played on in a more relaxed spirit, some placements fanciful, others not. On the whole, Huifang was judged the finest player of the afternoon.

After the meal they descended and strolled for a while, crossing the little hill and the stone bridge to reach the Spring-Lingering Dell. There, over brewed tea, they fell into easy conversation. Baozhu recounted to Ziyun the matter of Qinyan's master's wife demanding the release of his indenture, together with the plans that Huifang and Sulan had proposed. "If that is truly the case," said Ziyun, "it would be very good indeed." He turned to Huifang and asked: "Do you have the means to carry out this righteous act?" "In terms of means," said Huifang, "it would be a stretch, but gathering fur from many pelts, one can still make a coat. The three of us, Yaoqing, Xiangwan, and I, could put together six hundred taels; the Wang brothers, Peixian, and Genxiang could manage four hundred." "I shall contribute my share of two hundred taels," said Cixian, "and Qianzhou can put in three hundred; Yong'an and Zhujun together can provide three hundred. We need not call on Genxiang, Xiangfan, or Jiantan. All told we would have one thousand eight hundred. If three thousand is the target, we are still short one thousand two hundred, which means, it goes without saying, Duxiang makes up the rest." "Surely Hua Xingbei is not let off entirely without spending a single coin?" said Ziyun. "In my view," said Huifang, "there is no need to involve him. If we go to him and demand the full sum all at once, he might agree, but then Yunong would be in his household for the rest of his life." Ziyun nodded. "Quite right. But I worry that none of you is particularly flush at the moment, and if you all squeeze out these sums at once, you may find yourselves in difficulty afterward. Do not trouble yourselves at all. Leave the whole of it to me. You need only go and negotiate. Once the terms are agreed, the silver is ready; they need only come to collect it. But we must act quickly: first, Yunong's leave has run out and he should not remain outside the city much longer. If he goes back in, drawing him out again would be troublesome. Go tomorrow and offer what is reasonable; everything can surely be settled." The three young performers all said yes. Qinyan, moved by Ziyun's generosity, was overcome with gratitude and, before he knew it, tears ran down his face. He dropped to his knees in thanks. Ziyun quickly raised him up; seeing Qinyan so overcome, he felt a pang of compassion. "Yunong, there is no need to be so distressed," he said. "I can see you were never meant for the dust of the entertainment world. It is the smallest service and not worth a word of thanks." The three young performers, seeing Qinyan's grief spring from genuine gratitude and Ziyun's generosity from genuine tender feeling, were themselves stirred to the core; their own tears trembled on the edge of falling. "Who is to go and speak with her?" asked Ziyun. "It must be someone mature and persuasive. I fear you yourselves may not carry it off." "The one indispensable person is Ye Maolin," said Huifang. "He was the one who brought Yunong to the city, and he is the one who taught him to perform. Furthermore, he is steady and knows how to talk." Qinyan nodded several times: "Only he will do." "In that case, return early," said Ziyun. "This very evening, go and engage Ye Maolin. Once the matter is settled, send word to me tomorrow, and we shall see what fortune holds for Yunong. I have some business at home and cannot accompany you further." Ziyun left the garden with his attendants and returned to his residence.

The four young performers departed in high spirits, took their leave of Cixian, and went together to find Ye Maolin and inform him of the matter. Maolin agreed at once, then said to Huifang: "Come with me in a little while. I am old, my tongue is clumsy, and I fear I may not manage the delicate turns of speech. Please be on hand to back me up. That Madam Qing — her tongue runs like a birdsong, and I confess I find her somewhat formidable." "I have heard she is a direct sort of person," said Huifang. "Stroke her the right way and she will be quite easy to deal with." Qinyan, Baozhu, and Sulan went home ahead.

Huifang and Maolin rehearsed what they would say, agreed to go together after supper, and Huifang then returned home.

It happened that Tian Chunhang came to call on Huifang, and the two of them dined and talked for a while before Chunhang left. Maolin had by then been waiting outside for some time. After the first watch, Maolin took up a lantern, lit the way for Huifang, and the two arrived at the Changqing household. They did not seek out Qinyan but found Wu Mazi and asked for the widow, the wife of Changqing. Huifang saw a woman emerge who wore a white mourning kerchief and a white hemp jacket, below which she had on a long green silk skirt with white cloth bound shoes. Her face was sallow and thin, her build tall and willowy; she looked to be around thirty, with the look of someone sharp-tongued and quick-witted. Not recognising Huifang, she asked Maolin: "And who is this?" "This is Master Su of the troupe," said Maolin. Huifang stepped forward, paid his respects, and addressed her as 'Auntie.' The widow returned the greeting, invited them to sit, and asked Ye Maolin: "What wind has blown two of you to this chilly doorstep?" Maolin replied cheerfully: "We come sincerely to pay our respects to you, sister-in-law. With our Master Cao gone, you are after all a woman who does not venture far from home. If at any time you need something done outside, do send word through Old Wu Mazi. I am at leisure and quite happy to be of service." "Oh, you are too kind!" said the widow. "Thank you for your kind thoughts. When my husband was alive, our room had at least a dozen people in it every day, gathering round that lamp, one getting up as another lay down, just like the Ministry of Personnel handing out posts, each waiting his turn. Since his death, not a single soul has crossed our threshold, not even a dog. Those two children of his are useless, and Mazi is a muddlehead who doesn't know the trade. I see this household cannot go on, and have been thinking of making other arrangements. My family is in Yangzhou; my mother is only fifty this year. My younger brother runs a second-hand clothing shop there, and I hear it is doing well. I have been thinking of going back, but I have no money in hand. When my husband was alive, money came in one hand and flew out the other. The lamp alone cost more than a thousand strings a year, to say nothing of all the other expenses and the household's upkeep. Those two foolish children cost money in food and clothing, yet how much do they earn a month? Just yesterday two strangers came for tea, and the children asked them to stay for wine and dinner. They ate and walked out. Mazi followed them, and in the end they paid only three strings. I ask you, is this any way to run a business? It makes me want to die of shame." "Things are difficult all round these days," said Ye Maolin. "It is not as it used to be, as everyone is calculating carefully now. Look at any respectable establishment in the evening: seats full, drinkers few. The idea you mentioned about returning south is a fine one. After all, a woman living alone in the capital, with few relatives and few friends, will find it genuinely hard to keep a household going. Better to take a few thousand taels and go into a large business with your brother, which is a much wiser course." "Oh, easy for you to say!" laughed the widow. "If I had a few thousand taels, I wouldn't be anxious. It is precisely because my hands are empty that I am in this difficulty. The other day I discussed it with Qinyan. I told him I would be depending on him, and asked him to go to Young Master Hua and arrange for a monthly payment of two hundred strings. He said he could not, and dared not raise it himself. I told him: if you cannot bring money back, do you expect to leave me dangling in the autumn wind? Besides, the young master has not spent much on him. I said: why not ask someone to make the case to him, get three or five thousand taels to buy out his indenture, and from then on do whatever you like. With that lump sum I could manage my old age, and naturally I would not ask him for support year after year. But he was evasive about that too, with no clear word either way." "Auntie," said Huifang, "so you do truly want to release him from his indenture? As it happens there is someone who has taken an interest. We came precisely to discuss this matter with you." "What sort of person is he, and what office does he hold?" asked the widow. Huifang improvised: "He is a district magistrate from the south of the Yangtze. He is a very good sort, simply not a man of great means. He saw Qinyan the other day, praised him highly, and wishes to take him as a son, which is why he is willing to pay for his release.

"He discussed it with us yesterday. If three or five thousand taels were needed, he could not manage it, though three thousand strings might perhaps be possible." "Three thousand strings, and you want to release him!" The widow exclaimed several times in astonishment. "Do you realise that when Qinyan was performing last year, he earned a clear ten thousand strings in half a year alone? This person pays three thousand strings and gets Qinyan for his own, and then in a few months he will have recouped his investment. Dear me, dear me! If it were this easy a bargain, I would go into the business myself." "Sister-in-law, that is not quite how it works," said Maolin. "Consider: if he were still performing, yes, he might earn that much. But if he is taken as a son for study, schooling, and a proper marriage, there is nothing but spending and no earning at all. Besides, this gentleman is a kind-hearted man, and helping Qinyan is in itself a good deed." "I don't care about any of that," said the widow. "So long as the person is willing to spend the money and meet my figure, I will let him come and arrange the release." "Auntie," said Huifang, "what is your actual figure? Name a definite sum, so that I may go and negotiate. Whether they can meet it or not, we can speak again..." "Plain and simple," said the widow: "three thousand taels of good silver, nothing less, and I will agree. Can he manage it or not? If he cannot, I will wait for Young Master Hua. He has a reputation as a free spender, so ten thousand or eight thousand might not be beyond him. Or there is still Lord Xu, who is fond of Qinyan, and would be easier to approach. I am in no hurry!" "Auntie, you have heard of Young Master Hua's reputation," Huifang said with a cool smile, "and three or five thousand taels is nothing to him, to be sure. But Young Master Hua has not been particularly pleased with Qinyan of late. Not only is he unlikely to buy out the indenture, he may well send him away entirely. How would Auntie know the inner workings from outside? We visit the household regularly. Now a single spare room has been allotted to him, and they don't often find uses for him. At New Year when we went to pay our respects, the young master gave each of us a silver ingot, yet Qinyan received nothing. One day I happened to see what was in his box. It was a mere sixty-odd taels of silver all told, accumulated from last year's Mid-Autumn Festival right to the present. As for Lord Xu, he has cooled somewhat from before, and besides, Qinyan has always kept a certain reserve with Lord Xu. If Lord Xu had truly been willing to release him, he would have done so long ago, not waited until now. Apart from these two, consider: two hundred strings a month, or three thousand taels for a full release, which is truly possible? Auntie is a sensible woman. Has more than a month at home not been enough to see through his situation? This opportunity has presented itself; we have come to speak for him, and perhaps you might add a little yourself. Auntie, see it clearly: treat him as you would your own child, and come down a little. Between the two sides we can make it work. Three thousand strings is indeed too little, but two thousand silver taels I can guarantee." "Since you have come to speak," said the widow, "you should be looking after my interests too, but not to the point of making your respected performer lose face. Very well then. Let us cut to the chase. I am a woman who binds her hair and wraps her feet, yet I dislike all this haggling and dragging on. I will come down two hundred taels. Two thousand eight hundred is the absolute minimum." Maolin, sensing the wind shifting, said to Huifang: "Let us put it this way: go and tell that gentleman to consider it as an act of charity to a widow and orphan, a store of virtue and merit, and ask him to put up two thousand four hundred taels. We as middlemen will charge not a single cash in commission; every bit goes to the principal sum. Sister-in-law, you need not hold firm any longer. Speed is of the essence in this matter. If it is settled in a day or two, sister-in-law too can put her mind at rest. Qinyan is a straightforward person who cannot manage roundabout dealings." The widow weighed the matter inwardly: "If the Hua household sends him away, and the boy is stubborn and refuses to perform again, that too will not do. As for Lord Xu, his heart is already divided among many. Better to accept the offer. Two thousand four hundred taels, which is six thousand strings, is not a small sum."

Her mind was made up, though her mouth still pressed for more. But Ye Maolin, that old head, was as a soft rope, offering sweet words of 'sister-in-law' this, and 'sister-in-law' that. He worked the widow like a headstrong mule until she was bound fast and could only consent. "I have committed to this," said Huifang, "yet I do not know whether the other party can truly manage it. If in the end they fall short, I will have put auntie to all this trouble for nothing." "You are losing your nerve," laughed Maolin. "Even if they come up a thousand eight hundred short, your own connections can fill the gap, which is a small matter for you. And he is not likely to leave you unrewarded for it." "That is the only course," said Huifang. "We shall see what fate decides." They agreed that a reply would come the following morning after breakfast; if the matter succeeded, the silver would be sent over at once, and a written receipt would be drawn up on this side. The whole negotiation had stretched to the third watch. Huifang then took his leave of the widow and withdrew with Maolin. The widow retired within; she thought to herself: "That young Mr. Su is a clever little rascal! Starting from three thousand strings and working up to two thousand four hundred taels. If I had softened even a little, I would have been completely outmanoeuvred. How infuriating!" Muttering to herself, she went to sleep. Huifang and Maolin went to Qinyan's room and told him the terms that had been agreed. Qinyan was overjoyed, needing only to wait until the morrow to fly free of his cage. Huifang and Maolin then took their leave.

Early the next morning, Huifang went to Yiyuan. Ziyun had not yet arrived. He waited at Cixian's rooms, and after two rounds of messengers had been sent, Ziyun came over. The matter was explained to him. Ziyun was very pleased, and summoned his chief steward, instructing him to go and draw a bank draft for two thousand four hundred taels, together with a separate draft for fifty taels as a gift for Maolin. Huifang wasted no time. He hurried back, ate quickly, found Maolin, and sent him the fifty taels first. Maolin was deeply grateful. They then went together to the widow's home. "With great effort," said Huifang, "I managed to gather one thousand nine hundred taels, and borrowed five hundred on his behalf. The whole has been made into a single draft, so please accept it." Maolin drew up a written receipt for Qinyan to keep. Seeing the matter concluded, the widow set out a few dishes and invited Maolin and Huifang to take a light meal, asking Qinyan to sit with them. "I have already eaten," said Huifang. "Please do not trouble yourself. Master Ye, I leave you to dine alone." He then turned to Qinyan: "Go and gather your things, take your leave of your shifu's spirit, thank your master's wife for her care, and come with me to the other side. Then we shall go into the city together to thank Young Master Hua, as there is no time to lose." Qinyan did as instructed. His things were few; he called someone to help his young attendant pack and bind them. Huifang sent them all back ahead in a carrying-load, and took out a draft for ten strings as a parting gift on Qinyan's behalf to the household's servants. Qinyan dressed and donned his hat, and knelt before his shifu's spirit-tablet. He wept for a while, genuinely moved. He then made his farewell to the widow with a formal bow. The widow herself wept a good deal, and added a few words of instruction. Maolin, seeing the scene, had no heart for the wine remaining in his cup, and walked out with them. The widow accompanied them all the way to the gate; Qinyan departed with tears, and returned with Huifang to his lodgings.

The following day the widow thanked Maolin with a gift of a hundred strings. Maolin had not been expecting it, and was well satisfied. But Qinyan's fate was riddled with afflictions: though he had now been released, a fresh source of trouble was about to arise. What came next, the following chapter shall reveal.
