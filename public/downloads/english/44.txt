Chapter 44 — Listening to rumors, three households erupt in conflict; seeing vicious letters, two young masters sever ties.

Now, on the very day Qinyan was released from his indenture, Young Master Hua was hosting a flower-viewing gathering. The next morning Young Master Hua ordered Shanzhi to send someone to summon Qinyan back to the household. Shanzhi dispatched an outer attendant named Yao Xian, who set out early for the city gate. Arriving at the Changqing lodgings, he found Wu Mazi and delivered word that the leave period had expired and Qinyan was to return to the city.

"Qinyan?" said Wu Mazi. "Yesterday someone arranged his release from indenture. He has already moved out and is unlikely to return to the city." Yao Xian was startled. "What does that mean? How could someone in our household be simply let go without permission? Where has he moved to?" "I don't know," said Mazi. "I only heard that the one who secured his release was a man from the south of the Yangtze — he has probably moved into that man's home." "Preposterous! Even if you wanted to arrange a release, you should have reported it to our young master. Our young master will never stand for this. If he finds out, there will be serious consequences." "It is none of my affair," said Mazi. "This was the mistress's decision — who could stop her?" "Where is he now, exactly?" said Yao Xian. "I need to find him and get a straight answer." "I truly don't know his whereabouts. I only heard it said that he still intends to enter the city — he has things there he can't abandon, so naturally he will have to come back. " Mazi's account was muddled and inconclusive, and left Yao Xian frantic. He also asked: "Where is your mistress? I should like to question her in person." "She is not in," said Mazi. "She went to tend a grave at daybreak." Having no other option, Yao Xian left and wandered to the entrance of a theatre, pausing to look about idly. Suddenly he heard the rumble of carriage wheels behind him, bearing straight down on him. He stepped aside; looking up, he saw what appeared to be two young performers in the carriage, and the one in the back looked rather like Qinyan. By the time he thought to hurry after it for a closer look, the carriage was already far away. "So that's it," thought Yao Xian. "There he is, out in the world enjoying himself — he must have gone off to attend some wine party." He walked on, still thinking, when ahead of him two acquaintances appeared: one was twenty-nine-year-old Meng Qi, a household man of Xu Ziyun's; the other was thirty-nine-year-old Hu Ba, a household man of Xi the Eleventh's. Both were natives of the capital. Hu Ba was Yao Xian's first cousin on the maternal side; all three were on friendly terms. That day Hu Ba, whose master was ill and had no errands, had come out to find Meng Qi to watch a performance, and then intended to dine at a restaurant. Running into Yao Xian — who had come out from the city — Hu Ba pulled him by the arm; they exchanged greetings and retired to a restaurant, where they ordered several dishes and two pots of wine, and settled in for a leisurely chat. Meng Qi asked why Yao Xian happened to be out strolling; Yao Xian said: "Strolling? I have a specific duty to perform and cannot be far from my post even when there is nothing to do. Today the young master sent me to fetch Qinyan — his leave has expired and he is to come back. But now I cannot find him anywhere." Meng Qi, hearing this, gave a start and said: "You still intend to call him back to the household?" "Precisely. I went to his shifu's home just now and ran into a fellow called Mazi, who gave me a muddled account. He said that yesterday a man from the Yangtze south had arranged Qinyan's release from indenture and taken him away.

"As I see it, Qinyan is currently in our household — how could an outsider simply arrange his release and take him away? That is extraordinary news. And given how generously our young master has treated Qinyan — paying his shifu two hundred silver a month, on top of extra gifts — who in the household, besides Lin Shanzhi, enjoys a better position than he did? And yet he shows not a shred of gratitude, makes no farewell, and just walks off with someone else. I tell you, there are truly people in this world devoid of all conscience. When I report back to our young master, he will certainly have Qinyan brought back — it will come down to whether Qinyan is lucky or not." Meng Qi heard all this and laughed. "Where did you hear such a thing? Qinyan is right here, perfectly well — when has he left the capital with any southerner? This is all rumour and not to be believed." "It is not rumour," said Yao Xian. "His own household told me." "Never mind that," said Meng Qi. "Have a cup of wine, and I'll tell you: ever since Qinyan's shifu died and he came out on leave, he has spent every day at our master's garden. Our master has been hosting banquets for him these past two weeks and more. As for the matter of releasing his indenture — I don't know whether Qinyan asked our master, or whether our master offered of his own accord. Yesterday our chief steward sent me to the Rixin Bank to draw a draft for two thousand four hundred taels and another for fifty taels, which was handed to Su Huifang to arrange the release on Qinyan's behalf. Just now, on the road, we saw Qinyan and Huifang sitting together in the same carriage, on their way back to our master's garden. From the look of things, our master intends to keep him close.

"We assumed he was no longer in your household, and so our master stepped in to take care of him. Had we known he was still in your household, our master is on such good terms with your young master — they are always exchanging fine antiques and valuable objects as gifts. If he wanted this person, he had only to send a note to your young master — surely your young master would have given him up? Why spend two thousand four hundred taels? What a needless waste." "So that is the story," said Yao Xian. "Even so, even if your master wanted him, he should have informed our young master first — Qinyan has not yet formally left the household. Not to put too fine a point on it, your master was a little rash."

Hu Ba then put in: "I have never seen this Qinyan — I don't know what he looks like. As for our master, last month at the Hongjisi Temple, at the gathering hosted by Master Wei, Qinyan attended our master for a whole day, and our master gave him a pair of emerald bracelets that had belonged to his consort. Those bracelets were purchased in Guangdong for one thousand four hundred silver coins; in the capital they would be worth even more. Now our master has taken ill, and Qinyan has not come to inquire after him even once. This person seems to have no conscience at all. He was with your young master and now wants to be with our master — clearly a man of no constancy. Who knows where he will land next?" The two of them, ignorant of the true state of affairs, rattled on with talk that was plausible enough on the surface yet wide of the mark.

Yao Xian finished his meal, offered his thanks, and returned to the city, where he reported to Shanzhi. He relayed Qinyan's recent doings — first as Mazi had told them, then as Meng Qi and Hu Ba had reported — without leaving out a single word, and with his tongue loosened, he added a good many embellishments of his own. He also said that on the road he had seen Qinyan sharing a carriage with another young performer, apparently going off to attend some wine party. Shanzhi listened, fell quiet for a moment, and then said: "What is all this? Is it true or false? If I pass it on to our master exactly as you have told it, and there is any false element, things will go very badly."

"How would I dare to tell lies?" said Yao Xian. "These are words spoken by Meng Qi of Lord Xu's household and Hu Ba of the Xi family's household — with witnesses to what was said. Would I dare add a word? Is it something to jest about?" Shanzhi turned it over in her mind: "How could Qinyan be so ungrateful? Not only has the young master loved him for nothing — I have taken care of him for nothing as well." Then the thought shifted: "Looking at his heart, he has always been reluctant to be here, always pining after some young Master Mei. Naturally he is happier out there. To be at Lord Xu's is one thing. But how could he also attend wine parties for Wei Pincai and Xi the Eleventh? Has he no self-respect, or does he care nothing for the young master's face? The jade bracelets are not worth all that much, and yet he could sink so low. And here in the household he always put on such airs — the manner of a superior being, a cold hauteur about him. So that is the reality of it, now plain to see. At this point I can no longer help conceal things for you — better to tell the truth outright. A person of true character acting out of true principle — perhaps it will shame someone too. His manner is refined and doesn't look like a performer's; there seems to be not a trace of vulgarity in him. Yet now, it turns out — it is those with a trace of vulgarity who do not dare to take unauthorized leave, while this one makes a spectacle of himself. Shall I just tell it straight?" Her mind made up, she walked to the outer study and called softly to little Xiang'er through the whitewashed wall. She heard Xiang'er's silvery laughter from within, and only after several calls did the girl emerge. "What is it?" Xiang'er asked. "I need to report something." "The young master has gone to the garden," said Xiang'er. "Did the young master go alone, or with the mistress?" "The young master went from here accompanied by Sister Bao, Sister Zhen, and Sister Rui to the garden, to see the peach blossoms again. The mistress did not go." Then Shanzhi heard a voice inside the room: "Listen — who is that?" The voice replied: "Lin Shanzhi, of course — who else?" Shanzhi recognized it as Huazhu and Hezhu, and went off quickly toward the garden. She passed through a blaze of crimson and pink, in a languid and gentle breeze, and went straight along to the Liu Xian Courtyard. Reaching the rear of the garden, she could hear laughter everywhere, and what sounded like verse recitation — in girls' voices. Shanzhi lightened her step and circled to the western side, concealing herself behind a Taihu rock, peering through a gap in the stone from a distance. She saw Rui Zhu, dressed in a peach-pink silk jacket and green silk vest, kneeling under the peach trees and reciting the "Song of Everlasting Sorrow." She had arrived at the lines:

"She grasps her gown and pushes back the pillow, rises and wanders restless through the night;
Pearl blinds and silver screens roll open one by one in slowly shifting light.

Her cloud-piled hair is half-undone from sleep just ended;
She descends the hall with her headdress and her garments not yet mended.

The wind sweeps her immortal robes, billowing and lifting high,
As if she danced again the 'Rainbow Skirt and Feathered-Robe Dance.'

Her jade face in its solitude, tears crossed upon the sill—
A spray of pear blossoms in spring rain, trailing its tears still."

At the line "a spray of pear blossoms in spring rain," Rui Zhu stumbled, repeated two lines, and could not go on. The young master burst out laughing. "Still kneeling, and you still can't recite it," he said. "You deserve a smacking." Rui Zhu's face had turned deep red. She tried and tried again but could not recall the words. Beside her, Ai Zhu was laughing at her, while behind the young master, Bao Zhu was scratching her own cheek in mock shame. The teasing drove Rui Zhu nearly to tears. Over these past two days, the young master and the mistress had devised an entertainment for the ten Pearl-maids, teaching them Tang poems and having them recite from memory, imposing penalties for any errors.

For many mistakes, a kneeling penalty could be followed by a rapping of the palms. Today Bao Zhu had recited the six "Untitled" poems of Li Yishan, with only one wrong character and no demerit recorded. Ai Zhu had recited the "Song of the Pipa" without a single error. Rui Zhu, reciting the "Song of Everlasting Sorrow," had already accumulated many errors, and so was kneeling on the ground; now she could not recite further, while the other three Pearl-maids chipped in with a word or a phrase to tease her. Half beside herself with vexation, she did not dare simply stand up and walk away.

Young Master Hua was enjoying the scene thoroughly when he suddenly noticed that through the aperture of the Taihu rock something seemed to be spying on them. He called out: "Who is behind the Taihu rock?" Shanzhi gave a start and quickly stepped forward, standing with hands at her sides. "Why did you come and then not come forward — what were you doing hiding behind the rock?" said the young master. "I arrived just now," said Shanzhi, "and hearing the young master in conversation, I paused to glance from behind the Taihu rock before coming forward." "What is it?" said the young master. "You sent Yao Xian early this morning to summon Qinyan; Yao Xian has returned," said Shanzhi. "And Qinyan?" "Qinyan did not come back." "Why has Qinyan still not come back? Has something detained him?" "I fear Qinyan will not be able to come." The young master was startled. "What do you mean — is Qinyan ill?" "No." "If there is nothing wrong, why can he not come?" Shanzhi made a show of hesitation. The young master grew deeply suspicious and urged her: "Just tell me exactly what Yao Xian reported — don't be evasive." "I fear the young master will be angry when I tell him." This made the young master still more uneasy; he pressed her again, and Shanzhi then recounted in full what Yao Xian had told her.

The four Pearl-maid attendants, hearing all this, were also astonished. And Rui Zhu, still kneeling on the ground, had been staring blankly at Shanzhi as she spoke, lost in a dream of her own: a shower of petals had fallen all over her head, and she was idly chewing a petal in her mouth, which she then spat out onto Ai Zhu's hand. Ai Zhu gave her a look.

Hearing all this, Young Master Hua was overcome with fury; the colour drained from his face. He kept saying: "Can this be true! Outrageous! Outrageous! Qinyan has utterly lost all human conscience — the like of it is rarely seen. And Duxiang — smiling on the outside, a blade within — he has gone too far in insulting me. Do you think I will simply let this pass? Go at once and send Yao Xian back — he must bring Qinyan here, no matter what. I must question him face to face and hear his reasons. I care nothing for this Xu Duxiang — I intend to have a reckoning with him and see who is in the right. Is there such an outrage as this in the world? If we were strangers, that would be one thing; but as intimate friends, he had no right to set out to deceive me. Why did he not arrange this release earlier? He waited until I had Qinyan here with me, and then made a show of his private resources and arranged the release. Qinyan himself is truly detestable. In what particular was he treated poorly? And yet his heart was always with that other quarter!" Shanzhi gently soothed him: "Young master, please calm your anger. Qinyan came to the capital only recently, and his shifu was the sort who let him have his own way and spoiled him. He spent half a year here, and I can say not only was he not badly treated — he could travel the whole world and not find a finer situation. One cannot understand why he was always knitting his brows and shedding tears behind closed doors. He has his own inner sorrow that he cannot put into words. Such a faithless person — why should the young master keep him in mind? In my view, one need not be angry: watch — he will not stay long at Lord Xu's either. Lord Xu's garden has eight or ten people about every day; if Qinyan is treated the same as all the others, he will certainly be uncomfortable. There is no turning a wild pheasant into a barnyard hen. His conscience ruined, what good can come of it? Heaven itself may not permit it. Besides, this Xi the Eleventh — I do not know him personally, but he is said to be a thoroughly disreputable man. To attend wine parties for such a one — is that not a disgrace? In my view, that single vulgarity alone, even setting aside the matter of Lord Xu, is enough reason not to want him." The young master, hearing Shanzhi's words, felt his anger subside a little.

Shanzhi then caught Bao Zhu's eye, and Bao Zhu also sought to console him: "Shanzhi speaks rightly. If Qinyan's heart had truly been with the young master, then even if someone had arranged his release, he would not have concealed it but would have come to inform the young master in person. If he had come to inform the young master, would the young master not have been willing to release him? This person is both confused and faithless — what use is such a one? Besides, last year it was he who chose to come; this year it is he who chooses to leave. The young master's kindness to him — who in the world does not know it? This is his own ill fortune — he is not able to receive such a blessing. If the young master were determined to have him brought back, he would certainly not dare disobey; but that would make it seem as though one cannot do without him, and he would think himself all the more valuable. In our humble opinion, from now on let him come if he will and stay away if he will — the household loses nothing by his absence. As for Lord Xu, he was certainly wrong; but we counsel the young master not to press the matter with him. Over one person of no great consequence, to damage the friendship of two generations of families is not worth it. And people will naturally say Lord Xu was in the wrong — poaching another's favourite — while praising the young master's magnanimity."

These two between them had coaxed the young master considerably; his anger had partly subsided, but he could not entirely release it. He sat in silence. Rui Zhu had been kneeling all this time — though a cushion had been placed beneath her — and her knees had grown quite painful. Nature was also pressing her with some urgency; her face flushed crimson in that expression somewhere between the desire to laugh and the desire to cry, which was rather pitiable. The young master had worked himself up into a rage and then been calmed by Shanzhi's and Bao Zhu's words, and had quite forgotten that Rui Zhu was still kneeling.

In desperation Rui Zhu finally said: "I could kneel until tomorrow and still not remember it. You had better just give me my punishment." The young master laughed in spite of himself. "Get up," he said. "I had forgotten you were still kneeling." Rui Zhu stood, bent over, rubbed her knees, and sauntered off saying: "What bad luck — kneeling all that time for nothing." She went off to find a secluded corner to relieve herself. Young Master Hua rose and went to the mistress's quarters; Bao Zhu and Ai Zhu followed him in. Zhen Zhu waited for Rui Zhu, and the two walked together. Shanzhi slowly escorted the young master out of the garden, and was just turning to leave when a handful of petals was thrown over her head. She spun around to see Rui Zhu and Zhen Zhu scolding her: "Someone was kneeling right there, and you were spying through that stone-hole! A pox on your eyes!" "And you'll be getting your hands slapped again tomorrow," said Shanzhi, and walked away.

The young master returned to his rooms, and seeing the mistress, he tried not to broach the subject — but could not hold it back, and told her of Ziyun's arrangement of Qinyan's release from indenture. "What does 'release from indenture' mean?" asked Madam Hua. "In the old days his shifu paid to have him, so all earnings went to the shifu," the young master explained. "With his release, he is no longer considered the shifu's person and may act for himself. Yesterday Duxiang spent two thousand four hundred taels to release Qinyan's indenture." "So Qinyan now belongs to Duxiang," said the mistress. "Precisely. I am truly furious — Duxiang shows contempt for everyone, and never even told me. I intend to pay him a personal visit tomorrow, and I want to send Qinyan out of the capital entirely and forbid him from remaining in the city." The mistress smiled: "Is this worth getting upset over? If someone else chose to release his indenture, what concern is that of ours? After all, Qinyan cannot really be counted as one of our household members. If he does not wish to remain here, let him go.

"Duxiang's lord and our lord were the closest of friends — why damage a friendship of generations over Qinyan? My advice is that you need not act on this at all. When all is said and done, Qinyan is a performer — if this becomes a quarrel, both families will earn an unpleasant reputation for keeping company with such people." The young master, who had always held his wife in great esteem, heard her speak with calm and reason, and felt more than half his anger dissipate. He murmured: "You are right." Yet he could not quite let Qinyan go from his heart. He went back and forth in his mind — unable to act, unable to desist — and drifted through the day in a mood of empty restlessness. The mistress soothed him further. The young master could only try to put the matter aside for the time being. Through the night his grievance lay unresolved, and the next morning it flared up again without warning. He ordered Shanzhi to have Qinyan's clothing-chests and belongings loaded into a cart, wrote a letter, and directed Shanzhi to deliver it in person to Yiyuan and hand it directly to Duxiang, to see what he would make of it. Shanzhi had no choice but to carry out the command.

This was the second day after Qinyan's release. Qinyan had originally intended to return to the Hua household that day, but Ziyun was planning a banquet on the sixth day of the month — to send off Nanxiang and Chunhang, and to host Master Qu Daosheng, with Ziyu, Zhongqing, and others invited to keep them company. As today was only the fourth, they resolved to wait until the seventh for Qinyan to go back, and to draft a note to Young Master Hua explaining that after his leave expired, Qinyan had felt unwell and had been kept a few days for some fresh air. So Qinyan was quite at ease and enjoyed these extra days of leisure.

That day Huifang had gone out, and Qinyan came to Yiyuan on his own. The pear blossoms were now in bloom; Ziyun and Cixian were chatting with Baozhu in the Pear Blossom Courtyard when Qinyan arrived and greeted them. "Yunong," laughed Cixian, "now that you are your own master, why not resign from the Hua household and come to live here?" "I would truly like nothing better," said Qinyan with a laugh. "But how would one go about resigning?" "Heaven forbid!" laughed Ziyun. "Hua Xingbei would say I had stolen his favourite — that lawsuit might never be settled. Watch out it does not end up in an imperial complaint. By the seventh day you can go back. When did you leave in the first place?" "The twenty-seventh of the first month," said Qinyan. "So it has been forty days already — how time flies!" said Ziyun. "When I was at the household, the days seemed to crawl," said Qinyan, "and out here they seem to race." Ziyun said to Cixian: "These past two days both Zhujun and Xiangfan have been cramming for the examinations. It is understandable for Xiangfan — he wants to make a name for himself under Meixiang's admiring eye. As for Zhujun, the fact that he can sit still long enough to write and compose shows how keen his ambition is — which is something no man can entirely escape." "This year there are two paths open," said Cixian. "If one fails the metropolitan examination, one can still sit for the Erudite Scholars examination. Passing the Erudite Scholars examination — is that not finer in its way than passing as a jinshi?" "Far harder to pass, actually," said Ziyun. "A good birth by that route is not something I can hope for. Hoping to pass as a jinshi is not entirely unreasonable, but then I have gone and filled a vacancy, which takes the wind out of one's sails — this year I can only watch others compete. Of the two, Zhujun and Xiangfan, who do you think has the better chance?"

"Their abilities are about evenly matched," said Cixian. "Xiangfan's style is exceptionally polished and in the right vein; Zhujun's gifts are more wide-ranging and bold. If they meet with an examiner who is conservative, he will likely be undervalued. In palace examination composition, Zhujun falls short of Xiangfan; but in the Erudite Scholars examination, Zhujun would have the advantage. My view is that Genxiang is certain to pass this year, and Jiantan and Zhuoran each have a nine-in-ten chance." "And you yourself," said Ziyun, "are a foregone conclusion." "I am not entering," said Cixian. "I know my own lack of fortune in such things." "What sort of talk is that?" said Ziyun. "If you choose not to sit for the regular examinations, that is one thing — one might at least say you have no interest in advancement. But this Erudite Scholars examination is meant to identify the finest talent in the realm; even the most eminent elders who have retired to private life still come to sit for it. Could a man of your complete gifts truly not participate? I will commission someone to submit your name by force — you will have no choice but to go." Cixian smiled but said nothing. "If one passes," asked Baozhu, "what office does one hold?" "Compiler in the Hanlin Academy," said Ziyun. "Can Genxiang take the examination?" asked Qinyan. "He is only a xiucai." "He can," said Ziyun. "You yourself will surely go," said Qinyan. "Active officials are not permitted to sit," said Ziyun. "I have already taken up a post. And as for Qianzhou — I fear he cannot enter either; he will likely receive his assignment sometime around the fifth month."

While they were talking, the gatekeeper came in to announce: "Young Master Hua has sent someone who wishes to see the master in person, and has also brought several trunks." Ziyun was puzzled. "What trunks? Have the visitor come in." Before the words were out, Shanzhi had already walked into the Pear Blossom Courtyard. Qinyan caught sight of Shanzhi and immediately retreated behind the house to listen in concealment, wondering what business had brought him. Shanzhi greeted Ziyun and Cixian formally, and said: "The young master sends his respects to both gentlemen, and asks me to deliver this letter." He offered it with both hands. Ziyun took it; on the envelope he read: "Four leather trunks, to be delivered to and receipted by Second Lord Xu." He acknowledged greetings to Young Master Hua, opened the letter, and Cixian read it together with him. It read:

"On the twenty-seventh day of the first month, my household page Qinyan, following the death of his shifu Changqing, was granted one month's leave to manage the funeral arrangements. As this leave has now been exceeded by several days, I sent my man Yao Xian to the city outskirts yesterday to summon him back, and only then learned that my elder brother had arranged Qinyan's release from indenture and has already received him into his household. I therefore send his chests and belongings herewith, requesting that you kindly receive and forward them; I presume Qinyan is in no position to come and collect them himself. I have however heard that this young man has conducted himself in a very base manner, having attended wine parties at various establishments indiscriminately, caring for nothing but advantage. I was enraged to hear it. I had considered recalling and severely disciplining him, but feared this would be contrary to my elder brother's wishes. Nevertheless, I trust that in future you will keep him under strict oversight and prevent him from repeating his former transgressions. My elder brother's broad-minded generosity is beyond dispute, yet I for my part shall remain vigilant, and should I have occasion to hear further reports, I shall relay them in full so that you may act on my behalf and expel this person — so that your noble garden may not be brought into disrepute. In haste, this brief note, with wishes for your continued wellbeing."

Ziyun read through the letter and did not know where to begin: the injustice was total, the wrong impossible to argue away. His hands turned cold with anger. He and Cixian looked at each other in silence, and exchanged a few cold laughs. Cixian asked Shanzhi: "What did your young master say to you?" "Nothing in particular," said Shanzhi. "He only asked me to deliver Qinyan's trunks clearly to his lordship and to ask whether there was any reply." Ziyun was too angry to speak. Cixian said: "Strange — how did all this come about? There is no time to write a reply at this moment; tomorrow I will come with Lord Xu and speak to your young master face to face." Shanzhi replied with a "Yes," withdrew, had the trunks brought and left with the gatekeeper, and departed without further ceremony.

In the courtyard, Qinyan remained unaware of the exact reason for the visit, yet he gathered that his trunks had been delivered. Hearing Shanzhi's departure, he hurried out, only to find Ziyun pale and slumped in his chair while Baozhu and Cixian pored over the letter. Qinyan stepped forward to read it. Cixian instinctively moved to hide the pages, but Ziyun intervened. "Let him see," he said bitterly. "Where do such unfounded claims even originate? Hua Xingbei proves himself devoid of all honor, blindly swallowing slanders to heap abuse upon an innocent. It is utterly outrageous." Qinyan had maintained his composure until his eyes met the cruel words. Reading the letter line by line, a violent surge of fury rose within him. Darkness suddenly swept across his vision, and a heavy blockage lodged in his throat, cutting off his breath entirely. His eyes rolled back, and he collapsed to the floor. Struck dumb with fright, Ziyun, Cixian, and Baozhu rushed to catch him. Ziyun pressed urgently on Qinyan's philtrum while Cixian supported his back with one hand and vigorously massaged his chest with the other. A terrifying rattling sound emerged from Qinyan's throat. Cixian quickly lifted the boy, propping him up against his own body. After the span of time it takes to steep a cup of tea, Qinyan's head gave a slight nod. He bent forward, coughing up a dark clot of phlegm, followed by a violent bout of retching. "He is better, thank heavens," Baozhu murmured, gently patting the boy's back. As Qinyan slowly began to revive, he fluttered his eyes open, and tears poured forth like an unsealed spring. Watching his profound misery, Ziyun and the others were stricken with deep sorrow, and Baozhu's own tears fell in a ceaseless stream. Together, they helped Qinyan to the couch in the side parlor, arranging pillows behind him. "Do not grieve so bitterly," Ziyun comforted him. "Tomorrow I will bring you there myself, and we will confront him. We shall make the truth plain." At these words, Qinyan burst into a sudden, agonized sobbing. It was a wail of profound desolation, carrying the deep injustice of a three-year drought, the piercing cruelty of a midsummer frost.

Ziyun and the others felt their own hearts wrung within them; the three could find not a single word of consolation. He wept for a full hour, until at last there were tears but no sound, and he wept on in silent, desolate grief.

Seeing Qinyan in such distress, Ziyun felt an ache of sympathy. "Hua Xingbei has gone too far," he thought to himself. "To hurl such insults without verifying a single fact is intolerable. I had intended to go and clear the air, but to defend myself now would only look like groveling. Better to leave things as they are. Severing this friendship would be no great loss anyway." Turning his attention back to the weeping boy, he spoke aloud. "Yunong, please stop your crying. Your pure character is clear to everyone, so who could possibly believe these venomous words? Lin Shanzhi must have stirred up trouble from within, twisting the truth until even I am painted with the same brush. As I see it, the Hua estate is no place for you to find peace. Why should you depend on him any longer? Since your trunks have already been sent here, there is no need for you to see him again. Returning would only subject you to further humiliation. Stay here for a few days, and we will work out a proper plan." Still quietly weeping, Qinyan expressed his gratitude. "You treat me with such grace and unwavering loyalty," he murmured. "I will not forget this kindness for as long as I live. Yet, because of me, you are forced to endure this anger and trouble. My miserable fate brings nothing but harm to those around me. I truly do not want to live anymore. Death would be a clean escape, putting an end to all this bitter grief. Every day I draw breath is another day of suffering. I have already lived two years past my time, and now it seems my true end has finally come." With that, he dissolved into tears once more. Cixian gently intervened. "When you first entered the Hua household, I warned Duxiang that no good would come of it. Now that you have left, it is actually a blessing. From this moment on, you are free of all fetters and can do exactly as you please. Naturally, you will leave your old profession behind. Why not live here in this garden with me as a young friend, forgetting the years between us? I will teach you the qin, chess, calligraphy, painting, and the arts of poetry and prose until you are accomplished in every discipline. In time, you will be known as a refined scholar. Is that not infinitely better than serving as a mere page in the Hua household? You should be rejoicing, not grieving. Besides, his letter was penned in the heat of mindless anger, which naturally breeds ugly words." "Jingyi speaks the truth," Ziyun agreed warmly. "In fact, I intend to one day invite your entire troupe here to settle in the garden. None of you will ever need to perform again. With a few years of study, I will cultivate an exceptional circle of refined minds, far superior to those half-educated examination candidates and third-rate provincial scholars." Catching himself, Ziyun turned to Cixian with a laugh. "Ah, a slip of the tongue! Forgive me. You hold the elevated rank of a tribute scholar and are far beyond an ordinary xiucai." "I am a xiucai nonetheless," Cixian chuckled. "And you are a juren." "A most unaccomplished one," Ziyun replied self-deprecatingly. Wishing to lift Qinyan's spirits further, Baozhu laughed and addressed Cixian playfully. "Tribute scholar, tribute scholar... we performers of the youyi actually outrank the tribute scholars! When we apply ourselves to study, we truly embody the saying, 'learning and, when opportunity arises, excelling.' And should any of us become an official, we would embody 'governing and, when opportunity arises, excelling.'" "This is too much!" Cixian laughed heartily. "Not only do you insult me, but you drag Duxiang into the jest as well!" Baozhu made a deep, theatrical bow of apology. "Please forgive my thoughtless words." Ziyun joined in the laughter, and at last, Qinyan's tears came to a halt.

Just then, Huifang arrived at the garden. Seeing Qinyan's red, tear-streaked face, he was thoroughly surprised. However, upon hearing the full story, he clapped his hands in absolute delight. "There is no better fortune in all the world!" he exclaimed. "One could not beg for such luck, yet here you are weeping over it?" Cixian then relayed Ziyun's generous proposal to end their performing days and harbor them all safely within the garden. "Nothing could be more wonderful," Huifang sighed, "though I fear our lowly born fate may never allow us such leisured grace. I have desperately wanted to change my way of life these past two years, but there is simply no viable livelihood to turn to. Becoming an official's companion is little different from acting on a stage. As for starting a trade, I have no head for business. Furthermore, if I were to change professions here in the capital, people would still recognize my face. I would have to leave the city entirely before a fresh start could ever be possible. Do you truly suppose I enjoy performing? I am trapped within this world and cannot claw my way out. Even if it brought in ten thousand silver taels a year and made me a wealthy man, what would it amount to? I would never escape the degrading label of 'young female impersonator.' Yet, I am absolutely resolved to change my path." "My heart beats the same as yours," Baozhu agreed softly. "Only heaven knows whether our deepest wishes will be granted." That night, the three performers lingered in the garden, exchanging confidences and gentle banter that helped ease Qinyan's lingering sorrow. Later, Ziyun spoke privately with Huifang. "Yunong is not entirely comfortable staying at your residence, and you cannot be at home to keep him company all day. Instead, let him move here for a few days. We can take our time to formulate a proper, long-term plan, for we must secure a sound and lasting arrangement for him." "My original intention was to host him for a day or two before sending him back into the city," Huifang admitted. "For him to stay with me indefinitely would be highly inconvenient. And his circumstances have drastically changed. It is much better for him to move here, where there is room to breathe and pleasant distractions to ease his mind. I wonder what Yunong thinks?" Possessing no firm opinion of his own, Qinyan simply agreed, "It would indeed be more convenient here." So Baozhu and Huifang remained for the night, keeping Qinyan company in the garden's Pear Blossom Courtyard. Ziyun retired to his own quarters, and Cixian returned to his rooms. The three boys lay close in the same bed, whispering long into the night until the fifth watch finally brought them sleep.

Meanwhile, Shanzhi returned to the Hua household. Young Master Hua eagerly demanded to know how Duxiang had reacted upon receiving the letter at the garden. "When I arrived, I found them gathered in the Pear Blossom Courtyard," Shanzhi reported. "As I entered, I saw Qinyan and Baozhu standing there, but the moment Qinyan spotted me, he quickly moved away. Lord Xu formally inquired after the young master's health. He then opened the letter and read it for a long moment in utter silence, offering only a single, chilling laugh. Lord Xiao stepped in, saying there was no time to pen a reply, and instructed me to return and convey his respects. He promised they would come in person to speak with the young master tomorrow. I handed the trunks over to their gatekeeper, saw them accepted, and took my leave." After dismissing Shanzhi, Young Master Hua brooded over the news. He had firmly believed that Ziyun would recognize his own fault and personally escort Qinyan back, a gesture that would have dissolved his lingering resentment. Instead, not a single word of apology was uttered. The trunks were coolly accepted without so much as a written reply. It was sheer, unmitigated impudence, softened only slightly by Xiao Cixian's hollow diplomatic pleasantries. This fresh affront struck Hua Xingbei with the agonizing frustration of Zhou Gongjin outmaneuvered by Zhuge Wuhou. His brows drew together in a fierce, heavy scowl, and a dark chill settled over his features, leaving him entirely speechless. What events would unfold next, the following chapter shall reveal.
