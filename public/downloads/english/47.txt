If he truly can, I will give him three hundred strings. But strictly warn him not to tell anyone.” Tianxiang left and returned two days later, bringing the man to Pan San’s small account room. Pan San was deeply embarrassed. The man said: “Third Master, I know everything about your matter. But so much time has passed, extracting it will not be easy.” Pan San could not bring himself to speak, leaving Tianxiang to negotiate the terms: if cured, he would be paid three hundred strings; one hundred tomorrow, and the remaining two hundred if there was no itching after ten days. The man agreed and said to Pan San: “Third Master, your cavern is deep, and my finger is short; I cannot reach the bottom. Have a silversmith beat two taels of gold into an earpick, seven inches long and as thick as a brush shaft. Tomorrow, after breakfast, I will come. I guarantee I will scrape you completely clean; you will not have to suffer a second time.”

Pan San asked: “Must it be gold? Will silver not do?” The man replied: “It must absolutely be gold. Silver will not do under any circumstances.” With that, he left. Suspecting the man was trying to swindle him out of the two taels of gold, Pan San used two taels of base silver, had it gold-plated, and waited for him. The next day, the man arrived as promised, inserted the earpick, and scraped him clean. Pan San, finally tasting relief, paid the initial one hundred strings. The man naturally kept the earpick, and Pan San thought his trick had succeeded. Ten-odd days passed, and miraculously, the itching ceased entirely. Pan San then paid the remaining two hundred strings. Tianxiang used this leverage to borrow money from Pan San, who, wanting to buy his silence, gave him several dozen strings more.

The man was a barber. Receiving three hundred strings of cash made him wealthy overnight, and the two taels of gold brought him unspeakable joy. One day, he decided to take the gold earpick to a silversmith to have two rings made. The silversmith told him it was only gold-plated. Refusing to believe it, the man had him test it on the touchstone, and indeed, it proved to be silver underneath. Enraged that Pan San had cheated him, malice filled his heart. He sought out Tianxiang and demanded he tell Pan San that he should not have deceived him. Now, he would use the earpick as proof and tell everyone he met that Pan the Third had hired him to dig out his ass—how would Pan San ever face the world again? Tianxiang relayed the threat. Left with no choice, Pan San had to beg Tianxiang to intercede, asking him to keep quiet and offering him more money. After much haggling, the man refused to yield until Pan San paid another three hundred strings. From then on, the barber and Tianxiang colluded; whenever they were short of cash, they would seek out Pan San. If Pan San refused their demands, they would viciously bring up that incident. Pan San was trapped, as if he had signed a deed selling himself to them; he ended up paying out bits and pieces for several years until the man finally died.
