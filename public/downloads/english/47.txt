The day those scholars had matched scrolls at the Garden of Ease, Qu Daosheng’s praise left them glowing; after they read the preface text as well they were utterly awed—only prostrate respect would do. Even Nanxiang and Chunhang, who seldom let anyone overtop them, knelt in earnest this time. The next day Ziyun invited Jin Jifu to the garden to review the plaques and scrolls; Jifu was full of admiration too. Ziyun had Daosheng brush the sixteen fan-screens of the great paravent in regular and running script, and the carver Ji the Tenth Dwarf—introduced by Jifu—set to work engraving them in the garden.

On the eleventh day of the fourth month, Chunhang and Nanxiang were posted to the rolls of new *jinshi*: Nanxiang placed twenty-first and Chunhang thirty-fourth. Their joy went without saying, and Huifang and Lanbao could hardly speak for delight. Nanxiang was lodging at Wenze’s on this stay in the capital—last year, when his father took up his new post, the family residence had been sold—so Huifang, though he and Chunhang got on peaceably enough, still had to pass the gatekeeper’s lodge when he came and went, which he found a constant bother.

Learning that Nanxiang had also passed, and sure that both men would be selected for the Hanlin compilation bursary, Huifang proposed renting a house together with Chunhang and Nanxiang; they were glad to agree and asked Huifang to look. He in turn put out feelers here and there, but nothing suited. One day he called on Ziyun and mentioned it. Ziyun said: “Why not move into my garden—it would be livelier? Besides, Daosheng has been appointed assistant prefect of Nanchang Prefecture and will leave for his post before long; Yu’nong goes with him. If you move in, would that not serve?” Huifang said: “I am not moving in yet.” Ziyun said: “You must move in too.” Huifang answered: “If I do, it will be another month or two—not now.” Ziyun said: “There are thirty-odd rooms at the **Clustered Cassia Mountain Lodge** by Cassia Ridge—more than enough for Zhongqing and Xiangfan. Tell them from me they need not look elsewhere; when their families arrive they can hunt again. I will pick a day tomorrow and invite them myself.” Huifang agreed. He spent a while with Cixian and Qinxian too. Hearing he would leave the capital soon, Qinxian thought of old friends—who knew when they might meet again after parting—and felt tenderly reluctant; he kept Huifang half the day and they talked at length. Huifang said: “We shall of course give you a send-off—but my father is at home; I cannot be quite at ease—I must see you outside the house.” Qinxian agreed. Only after long talk did Huifang leave. He explained everything to Chunhang, Nanxiang, and Wenze: Duxiang asked the two of them over. Chunhang said: “Zhongqing may go—I have been meaning to fetch my mother and wife these few days; I still need a house—better than shifting twice.” Nanxiang said: “Whether I go or not stands fifty-fifty.” Next day Chunhang took formal leave of Ziyun, citing bringing dependents—Ziyun could hardly insist. Huifang found a house that suited very well in Mingke Lane, close to Ziyu; he also arranged new carriage and horses for Chunhang and took on several stewards—the driver was Zhou Xiaosan; once inside he recommended his brother-in-law Xu Laosan, renamed Xu Gui, as a footman—and the whole arrangement improved at once. Meanwhile Xi Eleven, once recovered from his illness, had convalesced well over a month. His temper had softened somewhat, his frame had thinned somewhat, and his opium appetite had grown considerably. Yet for all he had been through, this reprobate ought by rights to have reformed and guarded his health—but the moment his strength returned he would not keep order. With Ju Hua within and Bayingguan without, front gate and back gate could never be sealed; civilities were unavoidable. Worse, his male organ behaved very badly: at rest it hardly troubled him, but it was now only half a stump, blunt and unsightly; when arousal came, an inner sinew swelled painfully, and even a token effort at congress was impossible. Ju Hua fretted in silence, weeping only in secret. Xi Eleven felt ashamed too. At thirty years of age, how could matters stand so? At home he might contrive repairs; here in the capital he feared there was no master hand to “shift the beam and swap the pillar” for him.

One day he went to Hongji Temple to thank Monk Tang and had Yingguan carry a gift of fifty taels in silver. At the gate he noticed a new inn next door with a signboard reading **Hall of Quiet Blessings**; he paid it no mind. Inside he saw Deyue; malice flared and he twisted the boy’s shoulder hard. Deyue cried: “Why pinch so hard?” Xi Eleven laughed: “You made me suffer—more than a month ill, and you sawed off half my heir-pillar so I’m a cripple—I do hate you.” Deyue shot him a fierce look and sneered: “Who knows where you picked that up—you blame me! There was nothing wrong with me—only look to my master—” He stopped. Xi Eleven sat and pulled him close: “Where is your master?” Deyue said: “In the inn next door. Master Yang the Eighth wanted a word—he’ll be back soon.” They jested a while longer; Wen Pinchai was out as well.

Presently Monk Tang returned drunk, saw Xi Eleven, and beamed: “Congratulations, congratulations—you are sound now.” Xi Eleven answered: “My thanks—it was your doing. Though I’m a crooked stump of a half-man, half is still left. If I had taken that fellow’s remedy, they would have rooted it out clean and gelded me for good. I haven’t much to thank you—take this as repaying your drug cost.” He bowed and took the packet from Yingguan with both hands. Monk Tang hurried to decline: “How could I accept? We’re brothers—do you treat me like a stranger and offer fee? Take it back.” Xi Eleven said: “Too little?” Tang forced a smile: “Of course not,” and pushed the packet away. Xi Eleven said: “Brother Tang, spare the ceremony—our bond is not about money—but your Eight-Treasure Elixir is costly to compound, not bought for coppers. If you refuse, you put me in an awkward spot.” Tang still protested; Xi Eleven insisted, and Tang had to accept.

They talked a while; Monk Tang said: “By now you’re off restrictions, I take it; this inn of mine has a few dishes worth trying—let us taste them today.” Xi Eleven asked: “Who opened the place, and how long ago?” Tang said: “The house belongs to our temple; year before last my senior brother leased it—two people hanged themselves and the tenants moved out. Since then it was haunted and stood empty a year. Last month Steward Huang of the Chunyang Hall came, saw it would make a fine inn, and partnered with me; I put in four thousand *diao* in cash—we’ve been open three days. The cook has several specials; the roast duck already tops anything in the capital, and his stir-fried shark’s fin is a trick no one else knows. Thanks to your luck, trade has been packed; a few town friends even ride out. There is another edge we have over other places: a back door opens straight to the front court of Master Wei’s residence—if after wine a guest wants a pipe or two he can come to me; we also have two private chambers on that side—actors or women, all can be sent for. Enter from my side and no one is the wiser—handy as the You Monk Temple by the canal, only tighter—what do you say?”

In former days Xi Eleven would have thrilled to hear it; now he was a different man. Out of sight, out of mind was easier; if talk and company stirred him, that “head” below seemed ready to thrust out, yet the sinew cramped and would not straighten—unbearable—so he dared not stir desire. He was not ashamed to confess the misery to Monk Tang, who roared with laughter and said: “Bear one more month of illness and I’ll cure you.” Xi Eleven asked: “How?” The monk laughed: “I’ll put corrosive paste on that sinew and rot it away—no more pain—wouldn’t that be best?” Xi Eleven said: “No good—suppose it all rots off; can it grow back? In our Guangdong we graft trees—some use seal genital; is there anyone in the capital who knows how?”

Monk Tang clapped his hands: “Perfect, perfect—of course there is. Just now one Master Yang the Eighth, Mei Chuang, and a clerk surnamed Zhang, Xiao Mei, favourites of Master Wei—come here often, and I’m friendly with them. They were dining next door; I carried over the pipe and talked half the day. Xiao Mei has a Suzhou relation who specialises in this line—fits false eyes, noses, teeth—false, all of them. Only for *that* part, he uses anesthetic, splits it in four petals, embeds dog kidney, packs medicine, binds with medicated thread—after a month, good as new. The dog organ must come from dogs coupled in heat, both killed at one stroke, the organ taken from the bitch—then it works; not the so-called seal genital they sell. And they say there is neither pain nor itch. The man is called Yang Shanxiu—lives outside the walls; yours can likely be grafted too. By your account it is short—I don’t know if length can be added.” Xi Eleven’s heart lifted; he forced Monk Tang to send for him at once.

Tang had already learned the lodgings and sent someone to invite Yang Shanxiu.

Yang lived close; he arrived almost at once. Monk Tang saw to seating him in the outer room first. Xi Eleven eyed him from inside: the face was unlovely, the clothes ragged—he looked down on him and did not come out, letting the monk speak for him. Tang explained the complaint. Yang said: “Grafting methods differ: one must first judge a man’s native condition, then his equipment. For instance, the old and weak in sinew cannot be grafted—even if you try, it is wasted. If the root is sound, even half-rotted off, so long as a stump remains it can be raised. But I must see the patron’s constitution and then choose the method.”

Monk Tang brought him in. Xi Eleven loosened his sash a little and sat. Yang saw a man barely past thirty, tall and still virile in bearing, yet the formidable air unnerved him; he stood ill at ease. Xi Eleven beckoned him to sit. Yang had overheard the talk; Xi said: “This trouble has one queer point—inside it feels like a sinew catches; when it swells there is pain. That sinew must be cured straight before anything else.” Yang said: “Let me see first how it lies.” Xi Eleven felt awkward; Monk Tang withdrew. Xi Eleven stood and opened his trousers. Yang leaned close, probed twice with an ivory spatula, told him to dress, and said: “Indeed—we straighten that sinew first, then graft.” He sat with the monk to fix terms: two hundred taels for a guaranteed cure—if anything went wrong, not a cash charged. Tang relayed this; Xi Eleven said: “Two hundred is fair if it works—don’t let him swindle me.” Tang said: “He takes pay only if it succeeds.” Xi Eleven agreed. Tang stood as middleman; all three set it in writing. Tomorrow fifty taels for drugs first. Yang handed a packet of medicine and a silk band: “At home mix this with clove oil, spread thick, bind with the band—loose at first; when arousal rises, bind tight—the harder, the tighter. Three quarters of an hour and the sinew will lie straight forever. Tomorrow I come to your house for the rest.” With that he left.

Xi Eleven went home too impatient even for Tang’s meal. He told Ju Hua; she was even gladder. She found clove oil, they ate early and after his pipe she urged him to bed, mixed the salve thick, painted him full length, and bound the band. He felt cold, then heat, then a blaze as if on fire—swelling hurt; he told her to tighten the ties—easier tight. Three tightenings and the pain eased; he slept. Ju Hua woke and felt him—longer than before—and rejoiced. Next morning she meant to unbind for a look while he wished to urinate; she loosened him, he passed yellow water, and she rebound him.

After breakfast Monk Tang brought Yang to the study. Xi Eleven kept them company. Yang asked how the night had gone. Ju Hua peeped through a crack at the doctor: sallow puff-faced, about thirty, scraggly whiskers, short in stature, greasy old satin jacket with sleeves in tatters. Tang’s head shone bald purple under a blue lined jacket, fanning himself. She heard Yang order a charcoal stove, a large jug of boiled water, a small drug pot, and a piece of old satin. Xi Eleven had all brought—the stove and water stood ready. Yang unpacked herbs: “This is ginseng—this cow bezoar—this pearl,” and tossed more in; he brewed a while, poured a cup, cooled it half a beat, and told Xi Eleven to drink first. Xi Eleven said: “I cannot wait—I need my pipe.” Yang said: “Better apply medicine first; smoke with Master Tang while the dose rises—then we operate.” Chunlan and Yingguan stood in the doorway watching.

Ju Hua saw Yang mix half a bowl of paste, loosen Xi Eleven’s band, rinse him clean, and dry him with the cloth. She thought the plank crack too narrow—dust grit still lodged—so she took her gold ear-pick and scraped the crack clean until her sight followed the bend. Yang spread salve, slipped on a satin sheath, lit a stick of incense five inches long. Xi Eleven and the monk lay down to smoke. Yang opened another bundle at the window desk—a bamboo tube and an oil-paper packet—wet-looking threads laid straight in four strands. She heard him ask: “Your majesty seems short—do you want added length?” Xi Eleven said: “Longer would be better.” Yang said: “Not very long. How long are you erect now?” Xi Eleven said: “Other day barely two and a half *cun*; after the sinew straightened, three.” Yang said: “I can repair you to four *cun*—that should suffice.” Xi Eleven had a pipe in his mouth and could not answer. Outside, Ju Hua thought he wanted only four *cun* and cried without thinking: “Even the least must be five!” Monk Tang snorted a laugh. Xi Eleven heard her voice and coughed. Ju Hua knew she had blundered and fled inside. Hearing “five *cun*,” Yang looked up—two boys at the door—took them for the speakers and smiled. Chunlan flushed; Yingguan sniffed.

The anesthetic had been on a long time when Ju Hua stole out again. Yang said: “The incense is ash; the drug has spread.” He took green crepe and smiled at Xi Eleven: “There is no pain—but if you watch, I cannot work; you must close your eyes.” Xi Eleven bound the crepe twice across his face, sat on the kang edge, legs wide on two stools. Yang set the threads aside, crouched, and drew two small steel knives from the tube. Ju Hua’s heart thudded; she shook, teeth chattering against the plank. Yang removed the sheath; the paste was half dry. He brushed with feather and lotion, then cut once—blood welled—and packed a thread. Four cuts, four threads. Ju Hua watched, trembling—Chunlan and Yingguan stuck out tongues and could not draw them in. Monk Tang could not bear to look and lay smoking. Yang opened a tin box and took a piece of red meat with a hole pricked through; he scored the glans several times, more blood, laid the graft, packed salve, bound more threads, pressed the head smooth with ivory, slipped the sheath, and unwound the blindfold. Xi Eleven rubbed his eyes—no pain—and Ju Hua breathed again.

Monk Tang asked: “How is it?” Xi Eleven said: “Not painful—only numb below; I can hardly move my legs.” Yang lifted his legs down, laid him flat, and said: “Drink one decoction daily—I leave the prescription—you fill it yourselves. I will change dressings each day at noon; seven days should close the wound. After healing, do not use the organ at once—wait two or three months or you will reinjure it. Remember—no cockerel, carp, or mutton for a hundred days. When fully well, dog meat helps if you can stomach it.” Xi Eleven said: “We Cantonese call dog *earth-lamb*—I have eaten plenty.” Yang said to Tang: “The drug money we agreed—give it so I can compound.” Xi Eleven sent Chunlan to the concubine for a sealed packet of silver. Ju Hua opened the chest, took silver, handed it to Chunlan for delivery. Yang packed his kit, told Chunlan and Bayingguan to help Xi Eleven inside, and left with Tang. Xi Eleven took the draught daily; Yang came at noon for dressings—ten-odd days and he was healed. Later Ju Hua stopped hiding and watched when Yang came—the organ looked much stouter, yet though the cuts had closed, ridged scars mottled the head like brand-marks on a face—worse to look at—green patches and red. Urination still smarted at times. Enough of that thread.

Meanwhile Pan San, after Zhou Xiaosan’s cruelty that day, went home and collapsed in a serious fever—not upright for twenty-odd days. The resentment lodged in his chest with nowhere to vent; that was still the lesser harm.

Xu Laoer’s intrusion had left something inside; Pan San could have sought remedy sooner had he not stayed sick a whole month—and now so much time had passed that within him it seemed worms crawled—itch unbearable. He could not speak of it to his wife; when the itch came he could only scratch through his trousers—no relief. He wanted someone to treat it but was this age, this face—how broach the shame? One day the itch overmastered him and he resolved to swallow pride in old age. No one in the household could be told—only the night-watchman Jiao the Fool, a simple dull fellow in his twenties. Confide in him, charge him with the “fine office,” swear him to silence—surely he would keep quiet. He called Jiao to a small account room, gave him a bowl of wine and three black buns, explained the ailment, and warned him not to speak. Jiao nodded, understood nothing, finished his buns, and turned to go. Pan seized him—“What now?” Pan could not repeat the words; if he let go Jiao might bolt. He pulled him to the kang, released him, bent over the edge himself, hitched up the back of his gown, hoisted his rump high, and said: “Come! Come!” Jiao looked about, grabbed a sheet of tea-paper from the table, held it out: “Third Master, wipe yourself—I only beat the watch—I don’t wipe arses,” and walked straight out. Pan San was furious and ridiculous both—and gave up.

A few days later the itch worsened; he suddenly saw plain: “I need a professional—what use a dolt?” He thought of actors he had sported with: “Pretty young ones would never do—there is one called Guizhi, mid-thirty now, hard up, splitting take in the troupe—we have some history.” He found Guizhi at the playhouse, took him to a restaurant, promised him clothes. Guizhi thought Pan was reviving old favours and fawned eagerly. When Pan saw him well disposed, the itch surged; he confessed the ailment and asked a cure. Guizhi laughed long and said: “There is no prescription—even if there were, you would never agree.” Pan said: “I would agree—only others might not. If you’ll cure me I’ll thank you handsomely.” Guizhi smiled and studied him. Pan, seeing consent, sat on his lap, fondled him—and felt something. Guizhi needed his help and yielded; belts loosened, Guizhi expected what he knew himself—only at the gate one thrust found bramble-thatch, pathless, prickling; his heart jolted; his member withered like a silkworm at death and would not rise. Pan’s face turned: “Well?” Guizhi’s flesh crawled, gooseflesh rose, and he stammered: “Not today—tomorrow.” Pan saw how it lay and dropped the matter—still hoped for the morrow—gave him four *diao* cash. Guizhi poured out woes and borrowed ten *diao* more; Pan paid.

Next day Guizhi came to the little account room and they tried again as before—still no use—not all the temple offerings could rouse him. Pan San was desperate; they sat idle half the day and parted. Pan was fretting when a clerk entered: “The Zhou household’s change silver—twenty-nine taels seven mace—the messenger came for it.” Pan said: “I weighed it long ago,” opened the scale drawer—only weights, no purse; searched everywhere—gone. Pan knew Guizhi had stolen it but, since he had come to treat him, held his tongue; he had the clerk remint—noticed the wall clock gone too—and fumed silently, hating Zhou Xiaosan and Third Sister for ruining him.

Several days later, after an extra cup of wine, the itch flared worse. He thought of Zhuo Tianxiang, seventeen or eighteen and an old patron—“call him—sound him out.” Tianxiang came, bowed. Pan was delighted; they sat in the account room with boxed dishes, smoked fish, melon seeds, and old papaya wine, and talked. Tianxiang saw Pan’s merry squinting twist—unlike usual—and supposed today meant bother; after a while he slid onto Pan’s stool. Pan embraced him, felt his member—barely larger than a peanut—and started: “You’re eighteen—why haven’t you matured—like a child of seven or eight?” Tianxiang laughed: “Who knows—he won’t grow—no sense of such things—never stirred.” Pan said: “I don’t believe you,” rolled the “peanut” in both hands—no stir; stroked—nothing. Furious, Pan pushed him off. Tianxiang giggled, threw himself back, tugged Pan’s whiskers: “Why vexed, Third Master? I never needed that—you picked the wrong door today.” Pan pouted, silent. Tianxiang groped Pan’s parts—they hung limp as opium withdrawal, not frisky as before. He toyed a while; something stirred faintly, yet when Pan’s thoughts stirred the back itched worse. He tried to push Tianxiang away; Tianxiang thought him coy and gripped hard. Pan clenched teeth, clamped buttocks, bit Tianxiang’s shoulder through his jacket—Tianxiang yelped and shielded it—saw Pan slump against the chair, grinding down his seat. Tianxiang stared, bewildered—Pan flushed and said nothing. Tianxiang said: “Third Master, why cold to me today—did I serve wrong and earn your anger?”

Pan said: “I am not angry—I’m not in humour for this today.” Tianxiang moved aside and said: “Shall I comb your hair?” Pan said: “Fine—comb it.” As Tianxiang combed, Pan asked: “When you please gentlemen inside—what pleasure do you feel?” Tianxiang laughed: “What pleasure—it’s service—the gentleman’s pleasure is the pleasure.” Pan said: “That isn’t what I mean. I hear some men were fooled young into ‘red-fuzz itch’—hair grows inside and itches until they must find partners—even when old it goes on—true?” Tianxiang said: “Indeed—in Dongguang county two—Liu the rice-shop master, Fox-Immortal Li—both past forty—prowl playhouses, feel men’s parts—if the man stays silent they haul him off, feast him, pay him, beg on their knees for one turn—meet a prude and they earn slaps. Tianxiang said: “No method—” then paused: “Yes—yes—a man in our trade cured one—dug it out by hand.” Pan laughed: “How can a hand fit?” Tianxiang said: “The hand won’t—the finger will.” Pan said: “If there is hair—the finger cannot dig.” Tianxiang said: “He has ways. Hair grows only after a man’s essence—it does not sprout without that—only the thing cannot leave.” Pan said: “So if it is three months along, maybe it can be cured?” Tianxiang said: “You must ask him.” Pan, hearing a cure existed, told the truth. Comb done, Pan begged him to find that man; Tianxiang left.

Next day Tianxiang sought the fellow and explained. The man laughed: “Pan San sent you? I knew. In the first month Pan used this trick on Xu Laosan—Third Sister schemed, Xu Laoer used the same on Pan—Laoer told me. San ate buckwheat flour, took purgatives—couldn’t void. Tianxiang pleaded repeatedly. The man said: “If I must cure him, tell him three hundred *diao* cash for me—that ailment cost him three hundred to get; to cure should pay double.” Tianxiang told Pan; Pan said: “If it can really be extracted I’ll pay three hundred—but he must not tell.” Tianxiang went; two days later brought the man to the account room. Pan was ashamed. Today have two taels of gold beaten into an ear-scoop seven *cun* long, brush-thick—come after breakfast tomorrow—I will clean you clean—no second ordeal.”

Pan said: “Must it be gold—won’t silver do?” The man said: “Gold only—silver will not do.” He left. Pan suspected a swindle on the gold but used low silver plated with gold and waited. Next day the man came, inserted the scoop, cleaned him out. Pan tasted relief, paid one hundred *diao*; the man kept the scoop—Pan thought he had outwitted him. Ten-odd days later the itch truly stopped; he paid the two hundred *diao*. Tianxiang borrowed money on the strength; Pan bought his silence with several dozen *diao* more.

The fellow was a barber; three hundred *diao* made his fortune at a stroke—two taels gold pleased him beyond words. One day he took the gold ear-scoop to a silversmith for two rings; the smith said it was plated—he scraped the test stone and found silver underneath.
