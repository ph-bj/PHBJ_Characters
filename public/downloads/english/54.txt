Chapter 54 — A talented scholar enters the Hanlin through lyric examination; in the ladies' chamber, beauties discuss Tang poetry.

After receiving Qinyan's answering lyric, Ziyu remained in sorrow for days. He also kept recalling that dream in which Qinyan looked haggard, and he could not tell whether it boded good or ill. Depressed and unsettled, he drifted through his days listless, with tears constantly wetting his kerchief.

One day a family letter arrived from Academician Mei. He told Lady Yan that all was well at his post and that he had selected many genuinely learned scholars. One newly successful jinshi, recommended for the Broad Learning and Grand Diction exam, was bringing back three thousand taels on his behalf. Mei said Ziyu was now nineteen and could marry; if they waited for his return at end of term, it might be next winter or later if transfer came. Since Wang Zhifu was a close kinsman and trusted friend, everything could be entrusted to Zhongqing. The wedding should be done appropriately, neither lavish nor mean. He sent Ziyu a separate instruction: Ziyu's recent writings showed progress; with the autumn special exam approaching, Ziyu should judge himself honestly—seek recommendation only if confident, and avoid chasing empty reputation otherwise. Lady Yan asked Ziyu his view. He said he could not guarantee success, but many of his level would try, and failure would be no disgrace. Lady Yan consulted Wang Wenhui and showed him the letter. Wenhui said Mei had written him similarly. He had long prepared and expected the marriage in the eighth or ninth month anyway. As for the exam, Ziyu should absolutely sit; confidence talk was irrelevant, and Wenhui would handle recommendations. Zhongqing and Wang Xun would also sit, and he would secure all their nominations together. Lady Yan replied that she was inexperienced in marriage arrangements and had no one to manage details, so she asked him to specify everything clearly. Wenhui said such ceremonies had no single formula; with their status they could not be too frugal, and outer display was required. Zhongqing understood these matters well, so Lady Yan should direct through him. Wenhui then departed and had Liu the Minister recommend Ziyu, Zhongqing, and Wang Xun.

Three days before the exam, chaos erupted: seals and guarantee papers, purchase of exam sheets, crowds of scholars from all regions, retired grandees and commoners alike—seven to eight hundred in total. Ziyun had arranged recommendation for Cixian, but Cixian suddenly fell ill and could not sit; Ziyun sighed deeply.

On the ninth, examining ministers were assigned: Su the Marquis again chief examiner, Huagongzi inspection officer, Ziyun receiving officer, Wenze sealing officer, and Zhang Zhongyu patrol officer. On the morning of the tenth they entered the hall. First session had eight questions, including drafted Han edicts, Tang memorials, Five Classics analysis, dynastic era-name studies, river-control policy, Six Ministries regulations, salt-law reforms, and essays on rectifying fundamentals. Second session had three grand fu compositions. Third required imitative poems in Du and Han meters and original rhymes. Ziyu performed strongly across all three. From about eight hundred in session one, five hundred were cut; after session two, only three hundred remained; after session three, only sixty. Wang Xun was eliminated; Gao Pin placed forty-ninth, Zhongqing twenty-seventh, and Ziyu second. At palace-level final selection, Ziyu's star burned bright: he took top place and, among thirty-two selected, was appointed compiler. Zhongqing and Gao Pin, talented but unlucky, were both dropped. Ziyu was the youngest candidate in this cohort. Lady Yan was overjoyed—this success was said to be even harder than a standard zhuangyuan. Yet seeing his close friends fail, Ziyu felt uneasy rather than proud, and became more modest. He paid respects to recommender Liu and then to exam reader Su the Marquis. Seeing Ziyu, Su remembered Ziyun's words and thought him truly auspicious. Wang Wenhui and Madam Lu felt mixed joy and frustration: glad for Ziyu's success, vexed at Wang Xun and Zhongqing's failure, though the coming daughter wedding shifted them toward joy.

One day Wang Xun's wife Sun Peiqiu and Zhongqing's wife Ronghua came to congratulate Qionghua. Ronghua said with warmth that her future husband had overwhelmed the empire's talent and now entered jade-hall ranks more glorious than the current year top-three. Peiqiu added that even prior zhuangyuan and Hanlin men had sat this exam and failed against him. Hearing this, Qionghua blushed. She was pleased, but being not yet married in, she could not openly accept such praise, so she fingered her sash and lowered her head in silence. Seeing her embarrassment, the sisters-in-law stopped pressing.

Ronghua admired Qionghua's refined dressing table and found a poetry volume: the verses of Lady Huanbai, wife of General Du of Nanhai. She asked how those poems were. Qionghua replied they were good yet retained a boudoir tone and lacked larger structure. Ronghua asked whose prefatory poems were best. Qionghua said Yaoyin's ten seven-character quatrains were strong, and the seven-character regulated poems by Huanxiang and Huanlan were brilliant—she wondered who those two were.

Ronghua said she had met both sisters in the seventh month but had not read carefully then. She found the poems and confirmed they were indeed by Su the Marquis's daughters: Huanxiang, married into the Hua household, and Huanlan, newly married to Tian Chunhang. Both were exceptional in talent and beauty. Qionghua said that explained why her mother had praised them so much. Peiqiu said the sisters looked almost like twins, even more alike than she and Ronghua. Ronghua replied she and her own sister were not that alike; Qionghua's beauty seemed to glow beneath skin and features like peonies behind blue gauze, all charm hidden rather than exposed. Ronghua joked she herself was now dry and angular. Peiqiu said it was only recent thinness from child-rearing fatigue. She then compared the women poetically: Huanxiang like white peony, Huanlan like pink peony, Yuan Qixiang like lotus—fragrance reaching far, graceful and unworldly.

Ronghua asked about Liu's daughter-in-law. Peiqiu said she resembled green peach blossom. Qionghua laughed that she was more like cherry blossom—small and deft—and also like smiling magnolia because she laughed so sweetly. Qionghua then asked Ronghua whether she had contributed a poem to Lady Huanbai's volume; Qionghua planned to write one too. Ronghua said her mood had been poor and she preferred silence to weak verse. She asked what Qionghua wrote—surely a long ancient-style piece, her best mode? Qionghua smiled that she had dashed off one yesterday and asked her brother to polish it; he said it was fine, but she felt it poor and wanted to redo it with Ronghua's help. Ronghua joked that asking her to polish was like asking a blacksmith to turn gold into iron. Peiqiu said writing poetry seemed difficult: everyone quoted 'Read three hundred Tang poems and you'll at least recite,' and she knew that anthology well, yet still could not compose.

Ronghua said Peiqiu could compose but hid her work. She praised Peiqiu's recent Qixi poem and asked why she kept such talent private. Peiqiu denied writing it and teased maybe Ronghua's brother had written poorly and blamed her. Qionghua asked which poems in the anthology Peiqiu favored most. Peiqiu listed many: Du Mu's famous quatrains, Li Shangyin's lines, Wen Tingyun's dream verse, several beloved seven-character regulated poems by Li Shangyin, Shen Quanqi, and Yuan Zhen, many five-character regulated poems, and in longer forms especially *Song of Everlasting Sorrow* and *Song of the Pipa*, plus two Li Bai pieces.

Ronghua replied she loved many of those too—especially lines of hard sorrow from Meng Jiao and Du Fu; grand seven-character ancient poems by Li Bai, Cen Shen, Du Fu, Han Yu, and Li Shangyin; key five-character regulated lines of deep feeling and image; and seven-character regulated masterpieces by Cui Hao, Cui Shu, Li Bai, Du Fu, Liu Zongyuan, Liu Yuxi, and Li Shangyin. Peiqiu said she appreciated those but found 'small and subtle' poems easier to grasp than giant powerful ones. Qionghua said *Tang Poems Three Hundred* was the concentrated essence of all Tang poetry and a bridge for beginners, though one poem's merit could not simply rank above another. She observed that people most liked writing seven-character regulated verse because its structure seemed neat and serviceable for scene and feeling, yet this form was actually among the hardest; she feared it and preferred seven-character ancient style.

She said the finest seven-character ancient poems were too many to list; even within the anthology, Cen Shen's famous snow poem was exemplary:

"The north wind bends the white grass down; in eighth-month Hu skies falls the snow..."
She explained its brilliance: in frontier cold it interwove 'pear blossoms,' bead curtains, fox furs, brocade quilts, bows, armor—creating a clear frozen world whose force lay beyond literal words.

She then cited Gao Shi's *Yan Ge Xing*:

"Soldiers in the border wastes half-living and half-dead; in beauty-tents the women still sing and still they dance."
She said it perfectly showed misery for those in misery and pleasure for those in pleasure. She quoted Wang Wei's *Luoyang Girl* at length, praising its natural feminine glamour, unlike hired-maker ornament. For deepest tragic coldness, she said nothing surpassed Du Fu's war-lament poems.

On the claim that Li and Du had different styles, she argued Du sometimes sounded like Li too, citing Du's poem to Han and reading the lines.

She asked whether those lines did not strongly resemble Li Bai. She added Han Yu's *Visiting Hengyue Temple* and *Mid-Autumn to Zhang Gongcao* felt strikingly Du-like, and wondered whether such likeness was conscious imitation or natural convergence.

Ronghua praised her criticism as exact and said these ideas could form a proper poetry notebook. Peiqiu said she sensed such things too but could not articulate them with precision and feared rebuttal. Qionghua said five-character regulated verse prized sincerity above all, then quoted Du Fu's famous eight-line war poem as the crown of the form. For seven-character regulated verse, she said flowing heroic qi was supreme and offered Zu Yong's *Looking toward Jimen* as ideal model.

She recited Zu Yong's poem and said its pattern was exquisite, widely imitated later. She listed corresponding masterpieces by Du Fu, Liu Zongyuan, Liu Yuxi, and Li Shangyin as examples of the same structural spirit.

She added that seven-character regulated verse also had deeply sincere peaks, especially Bai Juyi's *Looking at the Moon with Feelings*.

She recited and praised it as blood-and-bone language, near pure natural sound, calling it Bai's best. Ronghua said this proved the saying 'poverty brings craftsmanship.' Qionghua replied adversity helped, but many suffered exile and still could not write this truly; many wrote only artificial scenery and stock sorrow-weather. Those could move readers, but remained hollow compared to poems where each word rang true. Peiqiu joked she disliked such near-prose poetry; would learning it not become plain speech? Qionghua answered that good poetry could be plain and still beautiful; over-carving often looked worse than plainness. She quoted Cen Shen's brief courier poem:

"Looking eastward toward my home the endless road goes on..."
and then added Wang Wei's famous festive homesickness lines:
    "A lonely stranger in a foreign land..."
She said these were almost plain speech yet superior to ornate carving. Otherwise, one must achieve deep conception with richly fragrant diction, every word with dew-light and flower-air, as in Wang Changling's palace and grievance poems. She then cited Wen Tingyun:

"By silver bed and screen of ice, the dream refuses form..."
and Gu Kuang:
    "At mid-sky's jade towers rise piping and song,
The immortals above laugh and play in the heights.
They look down on the earth as an ocean of dust,
and they see not the myriad cities of men."

She said those words were like petals with dew beads, irresistibly lovely. Ronghua asked whether even such excellent poems had points open to critique. Qionghua laughed that she dared not critique Tang sages—people would mock or scold her. Ronghua said this was private discussion only. Qionghua conceded there were discussable points and targeted even Du Fu's famous *Dreaming of Li Bai*: after brilliant opening lines, she questioned two middle lines about soul coming and going through dark forests and passes, saying they shifted from dreaming of living Li to ghostly dead Li too abruptly.

She proposed deleting those two lines and jumping directly to later lines.

She said ending there without the final warning line might yield fuller implicit feeling. Ronghua and Peiqiu laughed and agreed deletion seemed cleaner; the dark lines indeed carried ghostly chill. They asked for more critiques. Qionghua named the monk Jiaoran's poem on visiting Lu Hongjian as weak and structurally confused—neither properly ancient nor properly regulated—and wondered why anthologists included it:

"Though moving near the city wall, the wild path enters mulberry and hemp.
The new chrysanthemums welcome autumn festival, the hidden pavilion delights in the evening glow.
Through the bamboo the dog's barking sounds distant, across the trees the cicada's song sounds late.
Who could know that the one who has retired here,
Is still a person of the dusty world?"

She said the poem lacked flavor and even repeated characters awkwardly, making it misleading for learners. As for five-character quatrains, she said they were another art, often fit for music and hardest to master; she cited Jin Changxu:

"Shoo away the yellow orioles..."
and Bai Juyi:

"Green-ant new-fermented wine,
A small red-clay stove.
Evening approaches and the sky threatens snow,
Can you drink a cup with me or not?"

She said these seemed casually tossed off yet each became profound. Peiqiu joked that with such command, Qionghua should sit the Broad Learning exam herself and become another Huang Chonggu.

Qionghua laughed that a few memorized lines were useless alone. Peiqiu said Qionghua used to ask her brother to revise poems, yet in truth brother likely could not write them so well himself, or he would not have been cut after three sessions. Ronghua laughed that if her brother heard this, he'd object loudly. Peiqiu said she had looked up Du's *Northern Expedition* and Han's *South Mountain* after hearing men discuss their difficult rhymes, and now she would test them: how many rhyme positions in *Northern Expedition*? Ronghua said she had never counted and could not remember fully.

Qionghua guessed about seventy rhymes. Peiqiu asked how many repeated rhymes. Qionghua said only one clear repeated 'ri' rhyme; Peiqiu pointed out additional apparent repeats with *zu* and *que*.

Qionghua argued those were not true repeats: one *que* was 'gate,' the other 'omission'; same sound, different word-value. For *zu*, one meant soldier, another an adverbial 'suddenly' sound value—thus effectively separate rhyme categories. Ronghua praised her memory, citing favorite lines and saying their political praise of Minghuang's moral stance surpassed later poets' treatments. She admitted she could barely remember *South Mountain* at all—its diction was too craggy and rare. Peiqiu said she had only looked it up for testing and could not memorize such a long, difficult poem either.

Qionghua corrected that *South Mountain* had one hundred and two rhymes with no repeats, requiring enormous power like grand fu writing. Peiqiu challenged with two apparent repeated *cou* characters. Qionghua laughed and explained one was different graph/semantic class despite similar sound, then gave a side philological question about removing radicals and reading shifts. Peiqiu joked through it. Qionghua explained a gloss where the character meant 'offspring.' Ronghua said it was a pity Qionghua could not sit the exam; with such mastery she would surely pass. Qionghua smiled and admitted she had only recently read these poems closely because those two pieces happened to be this year's set texts. Ronghua asked to see *South Mountain* again.

Qionghua fetched it. Ronghua read a little and asked which word was the seventh rhyme. Qionghua laughed that no one remembered by position alone, then counted through and answered it was 'shou.' Peiqiu praised such memorization and said Qionghua could certainly compose in those rhymes. Qionghua said the one hard rhyme in *Northern Expedition* was actually the 'da' of Daji; she wondered how examinees used it, since her brother and brother-in-law had also used that same allusion and there seemed little alternative. Peiqiu laughed that asking second brother-in-law directly would reveal usage. Qionghua blushed and fell silent. Peiqiu then joked that on the first bridal night they should make him recite full rhyme tables before entering, otherwise lock him out kneeling—so he would know women could be broadly learned too.

Ronghua laughed. Qionghua blushed harder, paused, then said perhaps Ronghua's husband had already knelt once. Peiqiu joked she herself was unworthy; if worthy, Ronghua's husband would kneel too. Ronghua sighed that their sisters' time together was ending and Qionghua was the blessed one, unlike her. After saying she'd return with her child, she and Peiqiu left. Qionghua reflected privately: Ronghua's resentment was understandable, though her husband was truly talented and would rise eventually. She then thought of her own future husband: only nineteen yet already so accomplished, truly rare. But she had heard long ago of someone named Qinyan who had caused him several illnesses, and now she wondered what had become of that Qinyan. Meanwhile Wang Wenhui fixed the wedding date for the nineteenth day of the ninth month; Lady Yan sent family letters saying Ziyu had passed and marriage was now imminent, all arrangements entrusted to Zhongqing.

Zhongqing roused himself. Being naturally broad-minded, he did not brood over gains and losses and resumed normal life. Soon the wedding date arrived. Both families hosted celebratory banquets, with famous performers woven throughout, delivering operas and feasts for several days. On the wedding night, Ziyu saw the bride and his heart bloomed. The narrator insists this was no fabrication: perhaps heaven, pitying Ziyu's long suffering, had fixed this fate in advance. Qinyan, being male, could never truly become spouse despite deep attachment; thus heaven had made Qionghua with the same styled beauty as Qinyan in stage makeup. Lady Yan had recognized this long before and once said so on first seeing Qinyan. Seen this way, even two families' likenesses were set five hundred years before. Ziyu felt another wave of emotion but inwardly resolved himself and completed the marriage-fruit in proper love, nothing like the ridiculous dream seduction scene. Had that dream become reality, Qionghua would have died of outrage. Next day Yuan Qixiang, Huanxiang, Huanlan, Ziyan, Ronghua, and Peiqiu came to accompany the bride—a gathering of near-immortals that lasted another full day. Lady Hua came gladly because Ziyu was her father's prized disciple and Lady Yan had invited her personally. The scale and excitement matched the Tian wedding and cannot be fully told. Thereafter Ziyu's chambered happiness became immense: she led among talented women and he among gifted men. They watched flowers at dawn, played with moon at night, matched rhyme themes, discussed history and textual doubts, becoming intimate scholarly companions. Naturally his obsessive longing for Qinyan faded somewhat.

One day Ziyu was talking privately with Qionghua when business from the academy called him out suddenly. In his haste he dropped a small old-brocade pouch on the floor. Qionghua picked it up and opened it, finding two folded sheets. One, in Ziyu's hand, was a *Jinlü Qu* and clearly a painful parting lyric. The other, also *Jinlü Qu*, looked like a response. She saw Qinyan's name on the letter paper and felt an inward thrill: several times she had asked about this Qinyan and Ziyu always dodged with jokes, saying only they resembled each other; now she had evidence. Their depth of feeling—calling each other in dreams, speaking of repeated heartbreak—was rare indeed. She recalled hearing her brother and brother-in-law long ago discuss Qinyan as a uniquely strange emotional case. Reading these two lyrics proved such passion and grievance were no ordinary thing. She put away the sheets and hung the pouch to one side.

After a while Ziyu returned. At first he forgot the pouch; then he saw it hanging and started. Qionghua pretended not to notice. Ziyu hovered, wanting and fearing to take it. When he finally took it down and felt it empty, panic rose—he knew Qionghua had removed the papers. Other matters he might explain, but Qinyan's name on the letter was hard to deny. He wanted to ask but could not directly, and kept stealing glances at her. Qionghua smiled despite herself. Ziyu took this opening and asked why she laughed. She answered playfully that she didn't wish to laugh, yet laughed anyway. He joked with poetic line about rare open laughter in life. She countered with 'How many times does one break the intestines?'—hitting his sore point directly. Ziyu dared ask no further and thought to walk away, but Qionghua held him and said she had found two excellent lyrics and would read them aloud. After reading, she asked if they were good. Ziyu evaded interpretation, saying poetry cannot be linearly explained and cited philosophical analogy. Qionghua said allegory and plain statement differ, and she would explain plainly. She proceeded line by line through the first lyric, praising its layered turns, emotional openings/closures, and intricate movement from immediate pain to imaginative strategy of mutual name-calling across distance if souls could not fly. She ended that 'ink mixed with tears' needed no gloss beyond itself, then asked if her reading was right. Ziyu said entirely right. She then asked the core question: if this person had lived in constant intimacy with him, what place had he held? Ziyu answered only as attendant in books and ink. Qionghua shot back that attendants are not called by souls in dreams. Ziyu, slightly agitated, said he had thought her his true confidante, able to see his heart unlike others. Qionghua said she had interpreted thoroughly, so how was that not understanding? Ziyu said others could speak muddle, but she should not; he began to say their appearances were similar and stopped. Qionghua started a sharp retort and also stopped. They looked at each other. Ziyu smiled awkwardly and said no need to keep arguing—time would show truth. He changed topic: Duxiang had invited her to view chrysanthemums, also inviting wives of Yong'an and Jiantan and other familiar ladies. Would she go? Qionghua said probably not. Why? She said first she had not yet completed a month after marriage; second she should await Madam Yan's instruction—if Madam Yan went, she'd follow. Ziyu said this invitation was to younger wives only, not elders, and Duxiang had said date would be chosen only after confirming everyone could attend and weather was calm, with no absences allowed. Qionghua said she'd decide by what her sisters and sisters-in-law did: if they went, she'd go; if any declined, she'd decline. Fearing renewed Qinyan discussion, Ziyu kept steering to trivial topics. Qionghua, understanding his heart, spared him further embarrassment. The narrator closes with praise of their deep marital pairing and notes that the next volume will detail Qinyan's journey onward.
