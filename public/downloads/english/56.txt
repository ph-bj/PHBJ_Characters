Chapter 56 — Qu Fangzheng appears in a dream after becoming a god; Historian Hou feigns righteousness in aiding an orphan.

When Qinxian came aboard and heard that Daosheng had fallen and injured himself, he hurried into the cabin to check on him. Daosheng murmured: "I feel a little clearer now. Though half my body refuses to move, I imagine it will mend in time. I have already taken a good deal of medicine. Where did you wander today?" Qinxian explained that he had strolled along Mochou Lake, discovering a Du Fairy's tomb just as the spirit-planchette had foretold. Daosheng, taken aback, asked: "So there truly is such a grave. Does it bear an inscription?" Qinxian replied that there was none, and he went on to describe the ethereal vision of the girl in red. Daosheng surmised it was guidance from the lotus spirit, and father and son conversed warmly for a while longer.

Qinxian then showed Daosheng the poem Old Shi Weng had gifted him. Daosheng's temper flared as he read it, and he scoffed: "That old man treats life as a trifling game and scatters his mind to the wind. His habits have calcified into his very nature, and even in his twilight years, he remains unchanged. I have always despised the man himself, admiring only his raw talent. Tear that fan to pieces." Qinxian immediately ripped the fan into shreds. The rest of the night passed in silence.

The next morning they were just about to pass the customs barrier when a fierce headwind suddenly sprang up. The anchor dragged across the riverbed as white waves towered high, driving the boat backward all the way to Swallow Bluff before their momentum could finally be checked. The crew hurried to drop the anchor, drive mooring stakes deep into the bank, and double the cables against the howling wind. Daosheng called Qinxian to his side and instructed: "You ought to write letters of thanks to my friends in the capital. My arm aches terribly, so you shall write while I dictate. A flowing running script will suffice; it need not all be perfectly regular." Leaning heavily against a bolster, he dictated while Qinxian dutifully wrote. They composed over a dozen similar letters, followed by numerous poems, working tirelessly for most of the day. Toward evening, the wind eased slightly. Knowing Qinxian was exhausted from his labors with the brush, Daosheng told Liu Xi to take the boy ashore for a leisurely walk. Liu Xi led Qinxian on a stroll around Swallow Bluff, then onward to Hongji Temple to gaze at the famed "Cliff-Edge Letting Go." They continued to the "Iron Chain Mooring the Lone Boat," taking in countless breathtaking sights until darkness finally fell. That night, Qinxian worked out a lyric poem to match Ziyu's earlier composition, tossing in his bed for half the night before he was finally satisfied with the phrasing. The next day, the wind remained too high for them to safely sail. Taking advantage of the delay, Qinxian formally wrote out his new lyric alongside a personal letter, then drafted two more—one addressed to the circle of scholars, and the other to his stage brothers. He sealed all of them together with Daosheng's official correspondence. Daosheng then dispatched a servant into the city to deliver them to the garrison office for secure, sealed dispatch.

Throughout his entire life, Daosheng had poured his heart's blood into brush and ink, while simultaneously destroying his health with heavy wine. This recent, devastating fall had struck at his very core. He drifted in and out of awareness—sometimes lucid, sometimes terribly muddled. Whenever phlegm rose in his throat, his mind clouded over completely, robbing him of speech. He was an old man now, and even the simple act of relieving himself was an awkward, humiliating ordeal. Why should common servants deign to wait upon such a burden? Only Liu Xi faithfully cooked and saw to their meals, leaving Qinxian to go without sleep night after night, standing vigil in the mid-cabin. On top of this misery came day after day of relentless headwinds; boats traveling up and down the river were wrecked in endless succession. Daosheng reflected bitterly: "This illness may never fully heal. And even if it does, I will be left half-paralyzed. The journey ahead is not long, but how could a paralyzed cripple present himself to his superiors at the provincial seat? It is far better to recover here before pressing on." With his mind made up, he sent someone into the city to rent lodgings, eventually taking quiet rooms in Huguo Temple inside Hanxi Gate to convalesce. They moved their baggage ashore and paid off the boatmen. Daosheng and Qinxian rode sedan-chairs into the city to their new lodging, which consisted of clean, simple guest chambers rented at three taels of silver a month. Daosheng and Qinxian took rooms facing each other, separated by two empty chambers in between. Qinxian observed that the four rooms were exceptionally neat. In the front courtyard, two massive locust trees stretched their branches, completely blocking out the sky. To the rear lay another large yard where the wild grass grew a foot deep, and downstairs rested a hauntingly empty coffin. On one side stood four or five wing rooms—one serving as the kitchen, the others as quarters for the servants. On the opposite side stood a high wall with a secondary gate leading out to the street. Because they had only just moved in, they were far from settled, and heavy trunks remained piled in chaotic heaps. Liu Xi and the others first busied themselves making up the beds and hanging the curtains for Daosheng and Qinxian. Qinxian then carefully set out his writing implements and precious curios, hanging a few elegant scrolls to brighten the space. From that day onward, they lived quietly in the temple, continually calling in doctors to tend to the ailing old man.

But fate had marked this as Daosheng's ninth year of profound ill fortune, and his allotted span on earth was rapidly drawing to a close. He did not improve; instead, a dark new ailment manifested. A lifetime of expending his heart's blood had finally run him dry. He had long suffered from Li He's dreaded disease—the vomiting of blood—which had lain dormant for several years but now raged to life once more. He suffered several violent bouts a day until his spirit grew agonizingly dim and all color drained from his face, leaving him entirely confined to his bed. After more than twenty days of this torment, his condition grew even graver. Seeing this tragic decline, Qinxian felt his own heart boiling like hot oil in his chest. Day and night he burned fragrant incense before the gods, desperately praying to take his foster father's place in death. Daosheng knew the end was inevitably near, and witnessing Qinxian's pure devotion only deepened his profound grief. He thought: "If I die halfway along this journey, what will become of him? Upon whom can this fragile boy possibly rely?" At that agonizing thought, he wept without cease. In the midst of this heavy grief, Qinxian gently brought in the medicine bowl. Seeing Daosheng's frail state, he dared not look up, only standing quietly to the side with brimming tears. Daosheng weakly beckoned him to come closer. Qinxian set down the bowl and sat gingerly upon the edge of the bed. Daosheng took his trembling hand, calling out "Qin'er," but his throat immediately blocked, and he could not go on. Qinxian's tears fell like a string of pierced pearls, and he could only bury his face deep in his sleeve to hide his sorrow. Daosheng gathered a final, desperate thread of breath and murmured: "I have ruined you. You were so well and whole before you met me." Qinxian swallowed his bitter sobs and pleaded: "Father, please take care of yourself. Bad months come to everyone, they will pass." Daosheng only sighed again. Qinxian urged gently: "The medicine is ready now. Please, drink it." Daosheng shook his head slightly. "The sickness is past all cure now. What possible use is medicine? None. But after my death, you must still..." He paused, struggling for air. "You must still return to Beijing. I see that your mind is firmly set on it, so I can rest easy. I had no true home in life, and I shall have no grand tomb in death. When I pass, wrap my body in a leather sack like the tragic Grandee Wu, and sink me deep below the waters of Swallow Bluff. Do not waste your efforts giving me a formal burial."

Hearing this final, bleak command, Qinxian felt his liver and bowels tear with grief. He fell to his knees by the bed in a flood of desperate tears, his trembling hands still gripping the porcelain medicine bowl. Daosheng forced down a single mouthful, but immediately broke into a violent cough, bringing up a great deal of dark blood once more.

Evening was rapidly closing in, and Qinxian's mind descended into absolute chaos. From the ancient cypress outside, old crows cawed endlessly, while up on a ruined upper story, an owl relentlessly worked its beak and tongue until the eerie sound made Qinxian's hair stand on end. It was early autumn—still oppressive by day, but sharply cool by night. Qinxian suddenly felt a deep chill and retreated to his room for heavier clothes. In the central hall, a single, solitary lamp burned as small as a bean, its little glass shade trembling as if it too were ready to die. Outside the window, the crescent moon hung blurred and indistinct. Suddenly, beside a twisted tree, a shadowy human shape flashed past and vanished into the darkness.

Qinxian shook violently with fright and cried out for help. Liu Xi happened to be away on errands, and the other three servants were nowhere to be seen. Terrified, Qinxian crept slowly back toward his room. Without warning, a large, black mass shot out from beneath the bed, slamming hard into his legs. He cried out "Aiyo!" and fell completely flat on the cold floor.

The dark thing whisked away into the night, leaving Qinxian trembling from head to toe. After a long, paralyzed moment, he finally forced himself up. The fragile lamp had gone out in the commotion. He crept out to relight it and returned to his room, where he discovered a small wooden lid discarded on the floor. By the flickering lamplight, he saw a large bowl overturned right before his bed. Seeing Qinxian unable to eat day after agonizing day, Liu Xi had specially steamed him a rich, hundred-day fat duck with lotus seeds and barley. Qinxian had managed only a few meager pieces. Fearing the other greedy men would steal the precious leftovers, Liu Xi had carefully covered the bowl and slid it deep under the bed for safekeeping. Instead, a massive, shaggy stray dog had caught the rich savor, sneaked in to lick the bowl entirely clean, and violently knocked Qinxian down in its frantic escape.

Qinxian pulled on a short, half-sleeve vest and sat in the gloom for a while. Hearing a strange noise behind him, he called out nervously for Zhang Gui, but received no answer. He dared not go look for the source. Liu Xi was still out fetching the doctor. He asked, "Who is there?" but met only with dead silence. Suddenly, a loud, cracking noise echoed—sounding like a heavy coffin violently bursting open, followed by the chilling wail of ghosts. He was utterly terrified. He desperately wanted to go pray before the Buddha, but his heart was hammering so violently in his chest that his legs refused to move. Yet, remaining still unsettled him even more. He checked on Daosheng's room and found the old man sleeping fitfully. Finally gathering a sliver of courage, he lit a small censer and knelt out in the desolate courtyard. He kowtowed repeatedly, praying in fervent silence for nearly three quarters of an hour before finally rising to his feet. As he stood, something dropped from the dark tree above directly into his hair and began to crawl. Suppressing a shudder, he quickly brushed it off with his sleeve, grabbed the censer, and hurried back inside. He sat down heavily, his heart still thudding against his ribs. Suddenly, out of the corner of his eye, he spotted a three-inch gecko perched on his shoulder, crawling menacingly toward his chest. His soul nearly leaped from his body. Daring not to touch the creature with his bare hands, he violently shook his vest, but the gecko only scrambled backward along his bare neck. His bones turned to absolute water. Frantic, he peeled off the vest and threw it to the ground, but the gecko had already crawled from his neck up into his hair. Overwhelmed with horror, Qinxian broke down, screaming and weeping uncontrollably.

Just at that moment, Liu Xi returned to the room. Seeing the creature, he swiftly knocked it to the floor with a fan and crushed it underfoot. Qinxian was left paralyzed with fright. Every hair on his body stood on end, tears streamed endlessly down his face, and his entire body burned with a feverish heat as his eyes felt as though they were on fire.

Liu Xi quickly hung the mosquito net around the bed to comfort him. Noticing only an empty bowl discarded beneath the frame, he asked in confusion: "Where is the duck?" Qinxian sniffled and replied: "I was out of the room. A massive black dog sneaked in and ate the whole thing." Liu Xi swore in frustration: "Where on earth did this plague of a dog come from? I didn't dare leave it in the kitchen for fear those greedy fellows would pick it clean. Instead, we ended up preparing a feast for a stray cur!" Changing the subject, Qinxian asked: "Why were you gone for so incredibly long?" Liu Xi explained tiredly: "Doctor Wang traveled to Yizhen today and will not return for three or four days. I had no choice but to track down Doctor Li. He is also famous, but lives very far away—a full twenty li there and back." He then asked how Daosheng was faring. Qinxian shook his head sadly: "He is exactly the same." Liu Xi's face fell. "If the master should... if the worst happens, what will we do?" Qinxian began to weep anew: "If anything happens to him, I will surely die as well." Liu Xi let out a heavy sigh, looked quietly in on the sleeping Daosheng, and then trudged exhaustedly to the rear rooms.

Qinxian went again toward Daosheng's room and heard Liu Xi shout: "Disaster—where are those trunks?" Qinxian hurried out. In the rear wing only a few chests of paintings and books remained; everything else was gone. Zhang Gui, Wang Sheng, and Qian De's luggage bundles were nowhere to be seen—still not back? The door stood open—anyone could walk in—what were they to do? The travel money had been in those chests too. In the master's room a small flat trunk held only a few gauze garments. "Young master, your things are all stolen—that little chest of yours was only gauze clothes. I have less than twenty taels on me. If something happens—what then?" Qinxian was frantic but said: "We must not tell the master—don't break his heart. Wait until Zhang Gui and the others return; we'll decide."

Qinxian and Liu Xi waited anxiously until the cold light of dawn, yet there was not a single trace of the missing servants. They finally realized the bitter truth: the three men had stolen their goods and fled into the night. Qinxian did not mourn the goods; seeing Daosheng as he was, if the worst came he would have no money for funeral—what then? He wept half the day. Only Liu Xi was left, and he could not split himself to search.

Suddenly they heard Daosheng call. Qinxian ran over. The old man had turned on his side—Qinxian thought he needed the pot; asked; he shook his head; he wanted to sit up. Qinxian called Liu Xi to help raise him and propped his back with two large bolsters. Daosheng said: "Fetch my poetry and prose collections." Qinxian opened the chests and carried the volumes over one by one. Daosheng asked the titles, looked them through, and said to keep one recent poetry manuscript and one painting album; the rest were to be burned. Qinxian wept: "These writings hold your life's blood—they should be handed down—why burn them?" Daosheng said: "You don't understand. Without these things I would not be as I am today—they ruined me. If they remain, they will harm others who imitate me and, like me, drag through life cursed by heaven. They must not stay—take them and burn them all." Qinxian's grief knew no bounds; he burned several kinds outside and secretly hid several others. Daosheng handed him the poems and paintings he had spared: "Keep these for remembrance." Qinxian could not hold back his tears. Daosheng called for brush and inkstone; Qinxian ground the ink and brought paper, straightening him. Liu Xi moved a small table to the bedside while Qinxian attended. Daosheng panted a while; Liu wrung a towel for his face and he rinsed his mouth.

Daosheng took the brush, trembling, and wrote large and small characters on a sheet—then stopped and panted. Tears fell from his eyes. He called: "Qin'er—I have something to tell you." Qinxian listened through tears. Daosheng said: "Though you lost your way while young, your resolve is uncommon; I need not say much. After you return to Beijing you will not go back to your old trade—you may lodge with Xu Duxiang." At that Qinxian wept and could not answer. Daosheng went on: "Keep this testament; when you reach the capital give it to Duxiang—he will know what to do." Qinxian took it and read:

On the eighth day of the sixth month I climbed Liang Mountain with Hou Shi Weng, reached a perilous summit, and was blown off the cliff by a squall; I struck the ground and injured my waist and legs. Since returning I have lain unable to rise and vomited many dou of blood. I lodge now in a humble temple at Baixia; I am at the last gate—matters of a day or two. Alas! Alas! No white carriage comes to mourn me; who will tie the green cord? My bones are light as a cicada's shell; my soul chokes in the tide. Where shall a handful of earth lie—who will shelter this six-foot orphan? Qinxian has long been in my favor; I bid him come and rely on me. Ah—Duxiang knows me; he will comfort me in the grave. If my lingering soul does not fade, may it be the snake that repays a kindness; if the boy has sense, let him be the sparrow that remembers grace. Our hearts have seen each other clear; spirits may meet. What words remain—please read with care. Seventh day of the seventh month: Qu Benli's final brush.

Qinxian read and collapsed to the floor; Liu Xi wept too. Daosheng told Liu Xi to raise Qinxian; Qinxian leaned on the bed and wept. Daosheng said: "No more crying—I've burdened you. After the funeral bury me on the riverbank; do not wait the full hundred days—hurry to Beijing. Take my credential certificate to Shi Weng and ask him to surrender it at the governor's office and to write my biography. The man may not deserve a biography, but few have known such hardship as I." Qinxian only sobbed into his hands and could not answer; Liu Xi's tears would not stop either. Suddenly a fragrant breeze seemed to stir through the room. Daosheng had Liu Xi wash him and change his clothes, lit incense on the table, and sat cross-legged. Qinxian stole a glance: the look boded ill. Daosheng took up the brush again and wrote four lines on paper:

A lifetime of bitter grievance stretches to my white-haired end;
My devotion to literature has cheated me of all rank and seal.
The old realm's rivers and hills stand empty of my hollow craft;
Revisiting the southern courts, I can only mourn my old wanderings.

When he finished writing the final character, he cast the brush aside and peacefully breathed his last. At the horrifying sight, Qinxian fainted dead away once again. Liu Xi panicked, weeping openly as he gently slapped Qinxian's face to wake him. Qinxian knelt by the bed, desperately clasping Daosheng's cold feet, and wept until he swooned, woke, and swooned again, trapped in his grief for half the day. Liu Xi urged him again and again: "Young master, it is done. The dead do not return to us, and the aftermath must be urgently seen to. In this terrible heat, we cannot afford to delay." Qinxian refused to listen, weeping until his tears ran dry and his voice cracked, leaving him unable to rise from the floor. Liu Xi firmly helped him up and brought a basin of water to wash his face; only then did Qinxian dare to look up at the bed. Daosheng's face wore a serene smile; twin jade pillars of mucus hung from his nose, and the strange, unearthly fragrance still lingered thick in the room. Qinxian whispered in awe to Liu Xi: "Look. The master has become an immortal." Liu Xi nodded solemnly: "The master was upright and true all his life. How could he possibly fail to become an immortal?" He then turned to discuss the grim practical arrangements with Qinxian: "We previously held back twenty taels for the boat fare and spent four, leaving us with sixteen. The thieves left my trunk behind out of some small sliver of conscience; if we pawn the rags inside, it might fetch twenty or thirty thousand cash. Together, we may just have fifty strings. We can only manage a very plain, simple funeral. Perhaps, after the death notices are sent, some charitable contributions will come in. But how on earth are we to carry all this out alone?"

Qinxian shook his head helplessly: "I know absolutely nothing of these practical things. I must rely entirely on you." Liu Xi squeezed his shoulder: "There is no need to even say it." They first laid Daosheng's body down reverently; after the bedding was changed, they lit tall candles and began to burn paper money. Doctor Li, whom they had fetched yesterday, had only just arrived; upon hearing that Daosheng was already dead, he turned on his heel and hurried away. Liu Xi was forced to go out into the city to manage everything alone, without a single soul to help him. Not a single decent coffin could be bought on such short notice, so he negotiated to purchase the empty one stored in the rear tower from the monks for twenty-two thousand cash. Securing mourning clothes, hiring musicians, arranging for monks to chant the sutras, and hiring a cook—he was completely run off his feet. Qinxian could do nothing but kneel by the corpse and weep; for two full days he took neither food nor water. Liu Xi pitied him deeply but had no spare moment to coax him to eat. After the grim encoffining, they laid the bier in the main hall. Qinxian, clad in rough hemp, kept a ceaseless vigil inside the mourning curtain, while Liu Xi spread his own bedding close beside him. It was the solemn time of the Ghost Festival—Zhongyuan, the basin rite meant to feed hungry ghosts. Across Nanjing, offerings were generously set out for the wandering spirits; paper money was burned in heaps, and sutras were chanted through the night. Delicate oil-paper lanterns were lit and floated down the river, meant to light the dark path to the nine springs below. All day long the wind broke fiercely and cold rain scattered; the sky would be brilliantly sunlit one moment and aggressively cloud-black the next. In the desolate old temple, dead leaves covered the stone steps, blowing with a dry, mournful rustle. At night, the pale moon shone through the torn paper windows, and autumn insects chirped a melancholy tune. Even a truly happy soul would feel deeply moved in such a place, let alone Qinxian, who was already so tender to tears. Left entirely alone with his solitary shadow, the road ahead stretching into a terrifying void, how could his fragile heart not feel as though it were being sliced to ribbons? He found himself caught in a trap: he could not rightly die, nor could he rightly live. If he chose to die, what would become of Daosheng's coffin? Would he not be betraying his great benefactor? But if he chose to live, how could he possibly endure these agonizing, grief-filled days? Over the course of a few short weeks, a face once as fair and flawless as a spring flower and carved jade grew gaunt and haggard. He ate less and less. For over a month, he forced down only two bowls of thin porridge a day, spending his hours either violently weeping or falling into exhausted sleep, looking for all the world as if he were fatally ill himself.

That night, a biting, mournful wind stirred the soul, and fine rain tapped insistently against the paper window. Qinxian could not find sleep, a hundred conflicting feelings crowding into his exhausted mind. He stood and walked a few steps across the room; his feet felt as light and insubstantial as air. Hearing Liu Xi's snores rumbling like thunder, he went over to check on him. Liu Xi's pillow was shoved aside; he lay face up, mouth wide open, nostrils pointed toward the ceiling, snoring magnificently with one hand resting heavily on his breast. A bold rat scurried across his bedding and paused to sniff right at his nose. Fearing a sudden bite, Qinxian shouted sharply, and the startled rat jumped away into the shadows. Qinxian turned to head back to his own mat. Suddenly, Liu Xi's nose snorted several times; he muttered a curse in his sleep, then violently bolted upright and sprinted toward the door. Qinxian's bones crawled with primal dread, having no idea what was happening. He hurried out to pull the man back. Still fast asleep, Liu Xi threw open the long lattice window, ran straight into the courtyard toward a massive tree, and hugged its trunk tightly.

Qinxian did not understand this bizarre behavior and stood frozen with fear. After a tense while, hearing no further commotion, he cautiously ventured closer. Liu Xi was still fiercely hugging the tree, snoring away once again. Realizing the man was merely sleepwalking, Qinxian called his name and shook his shoulder until Liu Xi finally woke up and asked groggily: "What is it?" Qinxian asked incredulously: "What were you doing, running out in your sleep to hug a tree?" Liu Xi rubbed his blurry eyes, paused to gather his wits, and laughed sheepishly. "It was a dream. I dreamt Zhang Gui was pulling at my quilt. I tried to catch the scoundrel and demand to know where our trunks were! I chased him all the way out here and grabbed him... I suppose I ended up hugging the tree and just fell asleep again." He laughed at his own foolishness. Qinxian felt a mix of lingering fright and genuine amusement. Together they went back inside, securely closed the window, and Liu Xi lay back down, instantly falling asleep again.

Qinxian had no choice but to lie down as well. In a hazy daze, he dreamed that he walked out of the temple and saw a bookshop situated directly opposite; the wooden sign read "Hua Zhengchang," and an elderly shopkeeper warmly greeted him. Qinxian entered the shop, but suddenly, loud gongs rang out, followed closely by majestic music. Looking back outside, he saw pairs of vibrant banners and silken canopies, accompanied by a bustling, magnificent retinue. Figures clad in golden helms and gleaming armor lined the way with drawn blades; fragrant incense smoke curled upward to form auspicious characters in the air. Jeweled canopies coiled with misty clouds drifted past, alongside jade boys and golden girls in splendid, otherworldly dress. The grand procession continued for over half an hour. Last of all came a seven-scented jeweled palanquin bearing a resplendent goddess, her majestic features partially veiled by strings of luminous pearls. He watched the entire procession, including the deity, enter the temple gates. Curiosity taking hold, he followed them inside—but it was no longer the dilapidated temple he knew. Instead, it was a vast, towering palatial hall. After the celestial attendants announced themselves and bowed deeply, two rows stood at strict attention like a disciplined army—their gowns tight, scabbards resting at their belts, an awe-inspiring sight. Qinxian hid safely behind a large tree and peeked out. Behind the seated goddess stood many beautiful maids in elaborate palace dress: some gracefully holding ruyi scepters, some bearing towels and combs, some carrying books, and others elegantly wielding fans. The goddess spoke a few words that Qinxian could not quite make out. From the gathered crowd stepped a young boy of perhaps twelve or thirteen. He possessed fine brows and remarkably bright eyes, radiating a commanding, proud, and crystal-clear air despite his youth. He confidently mounted the steps and offered a long bow, yet he did not kneel. The goddess seemed immediately angered; she slapped the table repeatedly and scolded the boy fiercely, while the boy appeared to stubbornly argue back. They spoke heatedly for a long while. Eventually, the goddess's color softened, and the boy stood quietly with a bowed head. She murmured something to a maid standing on her right, and the maid quickly disappeared into the rear hall. Presently, she emerged carrying an ancient, heavy brocade bag, walked over to the boy, and shook it open. Brushes of every conceivable kind—large and small, new and old, in every color of the rainbow, numbering well over a hundred—poured out into the boy's lifted lapels, though he seemed to receive them with mild reluctance. The boy thanked her respectfully, stood a while longer as she spoke a few final words, and then withdrew slowly from the hall.

Qinxian watched him all the way until he passed out of the temple gate, thinking to himself: "What manner of place is this? That boy is unbearably proud. Even here, before a goddess, he refuses to kneel, standing as cold and unyielding as ice. He must be someone of incredibly deep root and no small origin." Just as Qinxian was about to leave his hiding spot, a god wearing a gold official's cap and a brilliant red robe entered the hall. Looking closely, Qinxian realized with a shock that it was his foster father, Qu Daosheng. Qinxian started, but curiously did not feel that the man was dead. The overwhelming strangeness of the celestial hall kept him from stepping forward to reveal himself; instead, he remained hidden behind the tree. He watched his foster father mount the steps and bow respectfully with joined hands. The goddess did not return the formal greeting, merely lifting a hand slightly in acknowledgment. His foster father then stood respectfully to one side.

The goddess asked a few questions, followed by the strike of a cloud-clapper and music rising from both sides. As she withdrew to the rear hall, her retinue scattered. His foster father paced alone beneath the steps, gazing up at the great hall. Only then did Qinxian remember that Daosheng had passed away; grief swept over him, and he seized the old man's robe, weeping bitterly. His foster father looked upon him with sorrow and offered comfort: "Qin'er, you have suffered, yet this was ordained by your fate. It will be but a hundred days of hardship. Wait patiently, and a good person will come to take you home." Qinxian wanted to ask of many things but could recall none, save for the boy who lingered in his mind. He asked: "There was a boy who just entered. The goddess bestowed many brushes upon him, yet first she scolded him. Who is he?" Daosheng replied: "That boy's former life was no small matter. He has been cycling through rebirth since the Six Dynasties, and she was likely reprimanding him for his past transgressions. He is destined to become a great writer whose name will endure eternally. In thirty years you will see a small book of his, and forty years later, a monumental work will appear." Qinxian then asked for the goddess's title. "Speak softly," Daosheng warned. He looked left and right before tracing two characters onto Qinxian's palm with his finger. Qinxian read them as "Dian'e," though he did not fully understand. Before he could inquire further, Daosheng was already walking away, and Qinxian hurried after him. Outside the gate, Daosheng mounted a horse, trailed by two shadowy attendants. Pointing with his riding crop, he said: "Hanlin Mei approaches yonder." Qinxian turned to look, and the rivers and hills unfurled like a painting: it was the bank of Swallow Bluff. He found himself on a boat once more, and Daosheng had vanished. Another boat drew alongside, where Ziyu sat in the cabin, sighing deeply.

Qinxian's old sorrows stirred, and he meant to call out, but the boats gently collided. A beauty stepped from Ziyu's cabin, arrayed in gorgeous dress, and sat beside him. Qinxian looked closely and started in alarm: she was wearing his exact stage costume, and her face was identical to his own. He looked at himself and could not fathom the mystery. The pair sat shoulder to shoulder, murmuring to one another in deep, tender affection. The beauty held a mirror, and they both gazed into it. He heard her say softly: "One mirror reflects two people, yet the heart's true matters remain unclear." Ziyu laughed in response: "What is there to be unclear about?" Qinxian could bear the sight no longer and cried out: "Yu Xiang, are you well?" Ziyu did not hear him. Qinxian called again, and he heard Ziyu murmur: "My ears are burning today; someone must be cursing me." The beauty suddenly caught sight of Qinxian and exclaimed: "Who is spying on us there?" With that, she flung the mirror at his face. Qinxian dodged, and the mirror fell into the cabin. In a blink, the other boat had vanished completely.

Qinxian picked up the discarded mirror and looked into its surface. To his absolute astonishment, he saw that his reflection had become that of the mysterious girl in red from the lotus boat at Mochou Lake. His heart leaped with profound wonder. Then, many fleeting shadows passed rapidly across the mirror's surface, revealing the familiar faces of the circle of scholars and celebrated actors. He turned the mirror slightly, and the dim figures shifting within seemed to be Wei Pincai, Xi Shiyi, and other men of their ilk. As he watched in utter fascination, the mirror suddenly began to spin. It grew as radiantly bright as the full moon, magically transforming into a single, massive pearl—a truly marvelous sight. But suddenly, a terrifying blue arm, covered in thick scales from shoulder to elbow, thrust violently into the cabin. Five sharp claws spread wide and ruthlessly snatched the glowing mirror away.

Qinxian cried out "Aiyo!" and bolted awake. It had all been a vivid dream. When he finally opened his eyes, the morning sun already stood high in the sky, and Liu Xi had been up for quite some time.

Qinxian rose, and Liu Xi waited on him as he washed. Qinxian sat in a stunned daze, turning the dream over in his mind, remembering every detail with striking clarity. He kept the beginning and the end to himself, telling Liu Xi only the middle: that his master seemed to have become a god, though not one of very high rank. Liu Xi replied: "As long as he has become a god, that is blessing enough. Surely the heavens grant promotions in due time." Presently, he brought food and mentioned he must go to Lord Hou to report the matter and retrieve the credential. Qinxian protested: "The credential was in that clothes trunk, and it was stolen along with the rest. What can we do?" Liu Xi frowned: "Stolen too? Then we have no choice but to ask Lord Hou to explain it to the governor. Since the master has already passed, it shouldn't matter too much." After serving the meal, Liu Xi shed his mourning jacket and went to Hou Shi Weng at Phoenix Mountain. Ever since Daosheng's fall, Lord Hou had been deeply uneasy. Finding the boat gone the very next day, he had assumed they sailed for the official post, never imagining that Daosheng had perished. His thoughts had lingered on Qinxian and the poem, wondering whether the boy would be pleased or annoyed, though he reasoned his praise would surely bring delight. Yet for such rare beauty, the boy was so cold, lacking in charming wit. He pitied the fair youth, wishing he could take him as a pupil to bring joy to his twilight years. While his mind was lost in these wandering thoughts, Liu Xi entered and kowtowed upon the floor.

Hou asked: "Why have you returned? Did you not go with them?" Liu Xi recounted the tragedy of Daosheng's passing in agonizing detail: his final wishes, how Zhang Gui and the others had absconded with clothes, silver, and the vital credential, and how the young master now kept a bleak vigil at the temple, nearly destitute of food and clothing. Lord Hou was visibly shocked: "Such a thing! I assumed he had already taken up his official post. I never dreamed it would come to this!" He shed a few sorrowful tears. Liu Xi pleaded: "We desperately need the master's help now." Lord Hou nodded gravely: "Qu had many devoted friends, but none reside here. I shall write several letters. Have the mourning notices printed and bring them to me to distribute; contributions will surely follow, and we can arrange a proper burial. I was intimately close to Qu for many years, and he has left an orphan behind. Naturally, I will do all within my power. Tomorrow I will see the governor and compel Jiangning and Shangyuan counties to hunt down Zhang Gui. I must also send word to Jiangxi, lest they use the credential to swindle the officials there. Leave those matters to me. Tomorrow I will visit the temple to mourn, and I will speak with your young master regarding the rest." Liu Xi kowtowed in deep gratitude and returned to inform Qinxian, though the youth felt little thankfulness in his heart. The following day, Lord Hou met with the governor, explained the dire situation, and filed a formal petition at Shangyuan county. The magistrate swiftly forwarded it, issuing warrants for the runaway servants and dispatching a formal warning to Jiangxi.

A day later, Lord Hou prepared a sacrificial table adorned with a poetic couplet and an honorific plaque. He came in person to burn incense and pour libations, weeping loudly until old tears streamed down his face, appearing deeply moved. Inside the mourning curtain, Qinxian wept as well, thinking to himself: "This old man possesses some measure of honor. That crying sounds genuine enough." Hou dried his tears, directed his men to hang the plaque, gazed upon it with a heavy sigh, and stepped into the curtain. Qinxian kowtowed his thanks. Lord Hou crouched down, seized the boy's arms to raise him up, and then sat cross-legged close beside him, taking Qinxian's hand in his own as his old eyes grew misty. To Hou, it felt as though he were seated in a lush bed of fragrant grass; a delicate breath of perfume drifted into his nostrils, and his heart melted into an intoxicating swoon without a single drop of wine. Qinxian resembled a fragile pear blossom. Though his radiant glow had faded in grief, he appeared all the more pitiable and entirely lovable. Qinxian felt profoundly displeased and shifted his body away, but Lord Hou only pressed closer, saying: "I never imagined you would suffer such a devastating loss. I only learned of it when Liu Xi came to me yesterday. Otherwise, I would have assumed you were already bound for Jiangxi. On the tenth of last month, I went down to the river and saw that you had sailed. I never dreamed this would happen. What do you plan to do now?" Qinxian's heart churned with fierce annoyance, yet he was forced to answer politely: "I am deeply grateful, sir, for all you have done for my late father. My intention is to complete the hundred days of mourning, find a suitable plot of land to bury my ancestor, and only then decide on my future."

Lord Hou protested: "What kind of plan is that! Your late father was a Hubei man, and the Miluo River was his ancestral home. For generations, his family line ran single and thin, leaving no kinsmen behind. If you were to return there, not a single soul would recognize you. You are utterly alone now, and everything of value has been stolen. You must put Hubei entirely out of your mind. As for the capital, it is a place brimming with snobbery, and your father had no truly close friends there either. As the old saying goes: 'When the man departs, the favor dissipates.' Forgive my bluntness, but you are far too delicate and far too young to suffer the harsh indignity of begging for a living. Between your father and me lies thirty years of deep literary friendship. If I do not raise you up, who else will? My turn has come, and I simply cannot refuse the duty. In a day or two, I will fetch you to my home. The coffin can safely rest here; in a month or two, when a proper plot of land is found, we can bury him. There is no need to hurry. Set your mind completely at ease. With me here, you will never lack for support. Your natural gifts must be exquisite; when you eventually make a great name for yourself, you will honor your father in heaven and bring immense credit to me as well. Settle your heart on this path, and do not waver."

Seeing Lord Hou's flower-bright old eyes fixed intently upon him, Qinxian felt a cold chill. Though the man's words sounded high and fair, his lingering manner was distinctly not that of a good and honorable man. Qinxian was seized by a mixture of anger and fear; his face flushed deeply as he looked down, unwilling to offer a reply. Lord Hou clasped Qinxian's hand firmly between his palms, rubbing it lightly with a creeping smile. "That poem on the fan the other day," he whispered. "Did you truly understand its meaning?" Qinxian's anger flared hotter. He wrenched his hand back, on the verge of tears, and attempted to stand up and walk away. Lord Hou grabbed him to pull him back down. "Wait, I have more to ask you. Whom exactly did you know in the capital?" Qinxian desperately wanted to ignore him, but knowing he could not, he swallowed his rising temper and muttered: "I knew a few people." "Whom?" Lord Hou pressed. "Serious men. They were not false friends. Besides Xu Duxiang and Mei Yuxiang, I knew a few other respectable scholars." Lord Hou smiled knowingly. "Xu Duxiang, the esteemed son of Minister Xiaoshan. Are you two close?" Qinxian nodded stiffly. "Yes. My late father left a letter specifically asking him to look after me."

Lord Hou laughed aloud. "Terrible, absolutely terrible! You must not go to him. How could you possibly keep company with those idle, silk-trousered sons of privilege? They may appear fond of you on the surface, but in the depths of their hearts, they do not truly count you as a friend. Please do not take offense, I am only speaking the truth. You are young and far too handsome. The manners in the capital are vicious, and the tongues there are razor-sharp. You absolutely must not go. You would live to deeply regret it. Stay with me, and your father down in the nine springs would rest easy. You can call me your adoptive father, or perhaps your teacher. I am over seventy years old; what scandalous gossip could people possibly invent? I have several concubines at home, many who would love to dote on you. Women are all of a kind; there will be endless, tender care for you there. If you insisted on going to the capital, the road is long and treacherous, and I would be consumed by worry. People would rightfully blame me for abandoning the vulnerable orphan of a dear friend, letting him wander off alone into danger. How could I ever bear the weight of such a charge?" Qinxian pretended not to hear a single word. He pulled himself entirely free and stood at a far distance. Lord Hou was visibly put out. His triangular eyes widened sharply as he stared at the boy, and then he sighed. "I only mean well. Please do not mistake my intentions." With that, he rose to leave. Qinxian formally kowtowed twice on the floor. "I do not know the ways of the outside world," Qinxian said coldly. "Please accept this as my final thanks in mourning."

Lord Hou tried to help him rise, but Qinxian was already on his feet, stubbornly keeping his distance. Lord Hou stepped outside the window to bid a lingering farewell, hoping he might still slip in a final word, but Qinxian had already withdrawn completely behind the mourning curtain. Left with no other choice, Lord Hou departed. He brooded for half the day, and the very next morning, he sent over a load of rice, a load of charcoal, and four taels of silver to test whether Qinxian's heart would soften and accept them. He reasoned that if the boy accepted the gifts, he might in time be coaxed into coming to his house. But Qinxian refused the offerings outright. Liu Xi dared not make the decision for him, and so he returned the gifts entirely intact. Lord Hou was furious, bitterly cursing the boy for not knowing his betters. Afterward, his pride left him too ashamed to ever visit the temple again. Yet, in the quiet of his own home, his heart was a turbulent mixture of hate, lustful love, and deep pity. All day his mind waged a bitter war with itself, and he poured his frustrations into writing many poems. Luckily for him, the governor of Suzhou soon invited him away, and he lingered there for two full months before finally returning.
