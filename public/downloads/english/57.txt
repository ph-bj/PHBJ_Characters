Chapter 57 — Yuan Qixiang leads drinking-game banter among beauties; Wang Qionghua becomes alliance leader through poetry cards.

The previous chapter recounted how Qinxian fell on hard times in Jiangning, tasting every bitterness. Now our story returns to affairs in the capital. It was already early in the tenth month. The weather felt like a 'little spring' with mild sunshine and gentle light, as a hundred blossoms flourished anew. Yiyuan was about to bustle with life once again.

Xu Ziyun's wife, Yuan Qixiang, possessed a gentle, quiet grace and unparalleled virtue. With a heart of brocade and lips that embroidered verse, her talents rivaled Xie Daoyun's famous 'willow-fluff' poetry. On the tenth day of the tenth month, she invited Hua Gongzi's wife, Su Huanxiang; Tian Chunhang's wife, Huanlan; Liu Wenze's wife, Wu Ziyan; Yan Zhongqing's wife, Wang Ronghua; Mei Ziyu's wife, Qionghua; and Wang Xun's wife, Sun Peiqiu. By this time, the garden's chrysanthemums were in full bloom, a brilliant tapestry of five colors. The day's sunlight was gentle, the wind stirred no dust, and light fur garments were unneeded as padded clothes provided ample warmth. Early in the morning, Qixiang led her twelve 'Red' maids, along with several married serving-women, into the garden to await her guests. On this day, Ci Xian, Gao Pin, and Nan Xiang had tactfully avoided the inner grounds. The twelve Red maids were all about fifteen or sixteen years of age. Some already wore their hair in towering cloud-buns like crows' wings, while others still wore hanging tresses brushed with kingfisher green. Yet, they all shared the same limpid, autumn-water eyes and dainty, narrow bow shoes. Madam Yuan and her bevy of maids waited at Baoxiang Hall. Today, the hall was transformed with an entirely new array of furnishings. There were unbroken swathes of brocade cushions and embroidered rugs, kingfisher-feather curtains and silver screens, with seven tiers of magnificent chrysanthemums banked in the center.

At the first quarter of the *si* hour, Liu Wenze's wife, Wu Ziyan, arrived first. Her carriage entered the garden gate, and she immediately transferred to a shoulder-sedan, which carried her directly to Baoxiang Hall. Resplendent in pearls and kingfisher ornaments, she was accompanied by four maids. Qixiang welcomed her into the hall, and they exchanged warm courtesies. Qixiang smiled, saying, "You are early today. I have been here since the *chen* hour." Ziyan replied, "I rose exactly at the *mao* hour. Yesterday, elder sister, you said we should all be gathered by the end of *chen*. It is already early *si*, and not one of the other sisters has arrived."

Qixiang said, "It is nearly time. Huanxiang will likely be the latest; she will naturally have gone to Huanlan's first so they could arrive together." Just then, a serving-woman announced, "The elder and younger Wang ladies, along with the Mei family's young mistress, have all arrived." Before she even finished speaking, the sedan chairs had drawn up before the hall. The three sisters-in-law alighted, followed by a large train of matrons and maids. Qixiang greeted each of them in turn. Qionghua's attire today was especially dazzling, possessing an even more bewitching and graceful charm than on the day she visited after her wedding. The sisters seated themselves according to age. Ronghua said, "We waited a very long time for our second sister. We thought every guest had already arrived, yet the two Su ladies are still not here." Qixiang asked, "Rong-mei, Pei-mei, why didn't you bring the little boys?" Ronghua replied, "The children are afraid of strangers. They cry the moment they see someone new, so we did not bring them." She then asked, "Why didn't you bring your nephew and niece over to play?" Qixiang answered, "Your nephew has only just recovered from a chill, and I was afraid he might catch the wind again. I meant to bring your niece over, but she simply refused to come." As they were speaking, another announcement came: "Madam Hua and Madam Tian have arrived." It was as if a swarm of butterflies were escorting two queens of flowers from their chairs. Before their lotus steps even stirred, their fragrance had already arrived.

Yuan Qixiang stepped down to meet them on the stairs. The Su sisters stepped forward with radiant smiles and curtsied, then exchanged greetings with Peiqiu, Ziyan, Ronghua, and Qionghua. Each took another's arm, their faces bright with joy, and they conversed warmly for a while.

The Su sisters felt a special fondness for Qionghua at first sight, and Qionghua, in turn, was exceptionally warm toward Huanxiang and Huanlan. This circle of sisters might well have all been immortals from the Jade Pool banquet, as each pair seemed linked by deep, preordained karma. Qixiang said, "Today, we sisters are all from families with generations of friendship. The two Hus of the Su clan and the twin 'huas' of the Wangs are blood sisters, which goes without saying. There are seven of us in total. Today, let us emulate the Seven Sages of the Bamboo Grove and make a Peach Garden oath. We will bow to one another in a circle, and from now on, when we meet, we must never stand on formal ceremony. I am the eldest in foolish years. What do my sisters think?" The beauties all responded, "An excellent idea." Huanxiang said, "I have had this very thought for days and was just planning to discuss it today. I never expected that you, elder sister, would anticipate my mind. After we order ourselves by age today, may we use that ranking for our future forms of address?" Ziyan agreed, "That is even better. Sister Qixiang and I have no blood sisters, and I have always disliked being called 'eldest young lady.' Now it is perfect; I can finally change my rank." Qixiang laughed, "What rank will you change to? 'Eldest young lady' has already become 'young mistress.' Are you now aiming to become 'madam'?" Everyone burst into laughter. Ranked by age: Yuan Qixiang was twenty-five; Wu Ziyan twenty-three; Sun Peiqiu and Wang Ronghua both twenty-two; Su Huanxiang twenty-one; Huanlan nineteen; and Wang Qionghua, at eighteen, was the youngest. Qixiang had the maids burn a censer of hundred-blend incense and spread a large brocade carpet. The seven beauties bowed in a circle according to their years. With pearls swaying at their shoulders and cloud-like robes brushing the ground, it was a truly breathtaking sight. Thereafter, among the seven, Qixiang was 'eldest sister,' and Qionghua was 'seventh sister.' Ziyan ranked second, Peiqiu third, Ronghua fourth, Huanxiang fifth, and Huanlan sixth. They seated themselves in that exact order.

Qionghua addressed Qixiang, saying, "Eldest sister, we came today not simply to feast, but to stroll through the garden. If we merely sit here, the day is short, and I fear we may not manage a walk at all. What do all of you sisters think?" Qixiang smiled warmly and replied, "I only used a 'stroll in the garden' as an excuse to gather you all together. A true tour of this garden covers five or six *li* in a single sweep, and the paths are jagged with rocks and difficult to walk. Besides, your golden lotuses are not even three inches long; how could you possibly manage?" Huanlan added, "To my mind, even a full day would not be enough to wander the entire place. We would be better off climbing to a very high spot and gazing out over it all."

Huanxiang said, "The highest spots require going uphill, which I am afraid would be too difficult." Ziyan suggested, "I hear there is a Pimiao Pavilion that is the absolute highest point. Let us go there." Ronghua countered, "In my opinion, facing the water is better than climbing a mountain, and I hear that every path connects by boat. It would be better to take a boat around once. The views from the water seem even finer than those from the shore." Peiqiu agreed, "That makes sense, and it saves our strength. If we climbed, we would be exhausted before we even began our tour." Qixiang concluded, "Since that is the case, let us go to the top of Yinqiu Belvedere. We can take in the entire prospect and simply sit there." And so, the whole bevy of beauties left the rear courtyard of Baoxiang Hall, boarded at the Wind-Dew Clear-Chant Lodge, and poled a fleet of small boats toward Yinqiu. There were only seven mistresses, but the maids and matrons of the seven households numbered over forty, requiring more than a dozen boats to glide together toward the Yinqiu Belvedere. The ladies gazed upon hibiscus blooming like brocade, clear water as bright as glass, craggy hills folding like screens, and winter woods scattered in picturesque patches. Even Qixiang could not remember every landmark. The twelve Red maids frequently came here to pluck flowers and fruit, so they pointed out the names of each place, leaving the beauties with hardly enough time to take it all in.

Arriving at Yinqiu, they toured all three stories and had the feast arranged on the second level. The ladies' capacity for wine was modest, yet each could manage a few cups. The strongest drinkers among them were Wu Ziyan and Wang Ronghua.

Qixiang ordered Hongxue, Hongyun, and Hongyu to tune the strings and pipes, accompanying light, clear songs with small clappers. Qixiang remarked, "It is a pity our limits with wine are so constrained. During the New Year, having some idle hours, I compiled a drinking game with my husband. It is quite lively when played, and any number of people can join in." Huanxiang laughed, "By that description, it's not a wine game at all—it is a battle chart!" Qixiang smiled, "There is indeed something of a battle chart to it." Ronghua urged, "Tell us how this game works. If we need more people, we can easily call up all these 'soldier girls' of ours, and we will have plenty."

Qixiang explained, "To run this game, we must do exactly that. The game is called 'Qin Destroys the Six States,' and also 'The Six States Attack Qin.' Today, the seven of us fit perfectly, representing the seven states of Qin, Chu, Qi, Zhao, Han, Wei, and Yan. There are seven large lots to draw, one for each state, as the six states unite their forces against Qin. There are also dozens of smaller lots naming various historical figures from the seven states. Whoever draws a figure belongs to that corresponding state."

Before she even finished speaking, the beauties beamed with delight, all eager to try the game.

Qixiang continued, "First, we must select those who can actually drink. Anyone unsuited for it will be useless. If you are routed in battle, you will only burden your mistress with penalty wine." She began by choosing from her own maids, picking Hongxiang, Hongyu, Hongxue, Hongwen, Hongwei, Honglian, Hongyan, and Hongjuan. Her other four maids could not drink. Huanxiang's Ten Pearls were all capable and were chosen in their entirety. Out of Huanlan's four maids, she selected only Xiao Cui, a pretty and quick-witted thirteen-year-old, along with Miss Xu Sanjie. From Qionghua's four maids, Qingqin was chosen. Ronghua picked Qiulian from her two maids. Ziyan selected Shixiang. Peiqiu chose Jinfeng. In total, there were twenty-four capable maids; the rest were ordered to drink on behalf of others when necessary. Qixiang then had the lots brought out. The seven mistresses drew first, in order of age: Qixiang drew Qin, Ziyan drew Chu, Peiqiu drew Yan, Ronghua drew Zhao, Huanxiang drew Wei, Huanlan drew Qi, and Qionghua drew Han. Huanxiang playfully taunted, "Elder sister, you are vastly outnumbered today. The six of us states will join forces and wipe you out to the very last scrap of armor." Qixiang shot back, "Save your boasts. Very soon, I will have one of your states surrender and another paying tribute. Just watch." Ronghua declared, "If I draw Lian Po and Lin Xiangru, you will never dare take a step beyond the Xiao Pass." Qionghua added, "And if I draw Zhang Zifang, that Bolang hammer blow will certainly not strike a mere decoy carriage."

Peiqiu chimed in, "If I draw Jing Ke, my dagger will not miss the bronze pillar." Huanlan said, "I will unleash Tian Dan's fire-oxen against you. Let us see how you counter that." Ziyan remarked, "Even if my state lacks a champion, drawing the double-pupiled Xiang Yu would be more than enough." Qixiang responded confidently, "Do not celebrate just yet. My Qin fields strong generals; there is not a single weak soldier among them. Let me balance the numbers first. We have twenty-four servants. I will use six, and each of you will use three." She then called out to Huanxiang's maids, Aizhu and Huazhu, "Come to my great kingdom and win true merit. Do not moulder away in your petty state." Aizhu and Huazhu laughed and stepped over to her side. Qixiang chose Aizhu, Huazhu, Hongxiang, Hongyu, Hongxue, and Hongyan for her own forces. Huanxiang kept Baozhu, Mingzhu, and Zhangzhu. Huanlan retained Sanjie and Xiao Cui, and added Hezhu. Ziyan kept Shixiang, taking Hongwei and Zengzhu to fill her ranks. Peiqiu kept Jinfeng and added Honglian and Hongjuan. Ronghua retained Qiulian, bringing in Hongwen and Yazhu (the 'Painting Pearl' maid). Finally, Qionghua kept Qingqin and rounded out her forces with Zhenzhu and Ruizhu.

With the assignments set, Qixiang called for the seven small lot-tubes and drew for Qin first. Aizhu drew Bai Qi; Huazhu drew Lord Shang; Hongxiang drew Han Feizi; Hongyu drew Lü Buwei; Hongxue drew Li Si; and Hongyan drew Zhao Gao. Qixiang laughed triumphantly, "You see? Both civil and military talents are perfectly complete." They put away Qin's tube and drew for Chu. Shixiang drew Minister Ling Yin Zilan; Hongwei drew the Gaotang Goddess; and Zengzhu drew Song Yu. Ziyan groaned with a laugh, "We are done for! A flatterer, a dream goddess, and a romantic ghost. How can we possibly fight with them?" The others laughed and set Chu's tube aside. Next came Peiqiu's Yan lots: Jinfeng drew Jing Ke, Honglian drew Tian Guang, and Hongjuan drew a fine horse. Peiqiu sighed, "Not good. It feels ominous all the same." Ronghua teased, "Before the army even marches, you have already lost two heads!" Laughter erupted again, and the tube was put away. For Zhao, Qiulian drew Lian Po, Yazhu drew Lin Xiangru, and Hongwen drew Lord Pingyuan. Ronghua beamed, "My three draws are excellent; we can easily blunt Qin's edge." For Wei, Baozhu drew Lord Xinling, Mingzhu drew Hou Sheng, and Zhangzhu drew 'wine and woman' (the drunk-woman lot). This sparked even more laughter. Qixiang noted, "That is an awkward one; it counts as both wine and a woman. Whichever element rides out first will surely cheer everyone up." Zhangzhu pleaded, "Let me swap the lot." Hongxiang quickly interjected, "An easy bargain!" and snatched the chip away. Zhangzhu had no choice but to keep her lot, her face burning red. Next was Qi's draw. Huanlan murmured, "My state will be quite ordinary. We probably have no strong lots inside. Nothing like Ronghua's Lian Po and Lin Xiangru."

When Xiao Cui drew her lot, the ladies were already smiling. But when Sanjie's lot was revealed, everyone shook with laughter like trembling flower branches, barely able to sit upright. Xiao Cui had drawn the rooster's crow, while Sanjie had drawn the dog-thief. Fortunately, Hezhu drew Lord Mengchang, which was just enough to laugh off the shame. They then drew for Qionghua's state of Han: Ruizhu drew Zhang Liang, Qingqin drew the Bolang hammer, and Zhenzhu drew the old man at the bridge. Qionghua laughed, "I warned you, Sister Qixiang! You had best mind the Bolang hammer and Jing Ke's dagger. Let the Gaotang Goddess and the wine-woman deal with you instead." Hongwei cautioned, "Madam, do not rejoice too soon. Your own side might have a joke coming as well!" Qixiang declared, "There is no need for idle talk. Bring out the rule book." They began to read:

"The six states attack Qin: regardless of who wins or loses, all six states must take the field. The game begins with the drum and the passing of the flower. Whichever state holds the flower when the drum stops will open the battle. The ruler does not fight in person; instead, a general is dispatched. If the three generals defeat Qin, the King of Qin must lead his ministers in surrender, kneeling to offer three cups of wine to the victorious ruler and court. Conversely, if a state loses to Qin, its ruler and ministers must likewise kneel and offer three cups to Qin. Other scenarios follow similar rules. If a state has three champions, after all three have fought, win or lose, they must yield the turn to another state. Each state's ministers have specific stories and actions to follow, but since the lots are drawn at random, the order will vary. After two champions engage in battle, each drinks according to their respective story as annotated in the book. Check the manual, and all will be clear. The six states will take their turns based on the passing of the flower. Within a given state, the generals march forward based on a dice roll; whoever the count lands on will strike first."

The beauties laughed, "The jokes will flow thickly today! Who will go first? Let us examine the rule sheets. Will there be any truly ridiculous moments?" Qixiang advised, "There certainly will be, but it is better not to read ahead. Reading the rules now will only make some gleeful and others cross. It is better to establish the players first." And so, they broke off a chrysanthemum branch and had a young maid beat the drum. The flower was passed around, and when it reached Ronghua, the drum abruptly stopped. Ronghua laughed confidently, "My three champions are perfectly fit to lead the vanguard and demonstrate their skill." Qiulian, Huazhu, and Hongwen stepped forward to the staging area, where another table filled with wine and dishes had been set aside. Qiulian cast the two dice, rolling a four, meaning she would fight first. Qin's forces—Aizhu, Huazhu, Hongxiang, Hongyu, Hongxue, and Hongyan—stepped up to meet her. Aizhu cast the dice and rolled a two, signaling that Huazhu would take the field. Huazhu was playing Lord Shang, while Qiulian represented Lian Po. Qixiang opened the rule book to Lian Po's entry and read: "If Lian Po meets Lord Shang, as both are bold generals, they must use the finger-guessing game to decide the outcome. If Lian Po loses, it proves he has become useless in his old age. For one loss, he must wear a false white beard. For two losses, he must drink one large cup of penalty wine. For three losses, he must eat a full bowl of rice." The beauties erupted into laughter again. Qiulian sighed, "Madam, it seems this Lian Po is not so grand after all." Ronghua smiled, "Just win the game, and you can keep the beard off your face."

They then turned to Lord Shang's rule sheet: "Lord Shang is cunning and capable of opening new fields and ditches. If defeated, he must hide an object in his hand for the victor to guess. If the guess fails, the round is a draw. If the guess succeeds, Lord Shang must 'fly' a poetic line featuring that object as the theme. If he fails or the line mismatches, he must drink one cup." Huazhu remarked, "That is tolerable enough, not overly fussy." The two generals faced off. Qiulian, having heard her punishing rules, lost her nerve and quickly lost three consecutive rounds.

Ronghua sighed playfully, "Brave Lian Po, the very first battle is lost." Qiulian attempted to flee, but Aizhu and Huazhu swiftly caught her and tied on the false white whiskers, which fluttered comically in the air. The maids vividly painted the scene, fining her one cup of wine and heaping a large bowl of rice before her. Qiulian laughed, "You still have some heart! But wearing this, how can I possibly eat? Unless we use golden hooks to tie the whiskers back." Hongxue replied, "The hooks are already prepared," and from a small box, she produced two gold hooks to hang from Qiulian's ears, parting the whiskers neatly to the sides. Peiqiu, suddenly recalling a joke her husband had told, blurted out, "She looks rather like a fly-whisk." Ronghua shot her a sharp look and asked, "May I ask whose fly-whisk she resembles?" Peiqiu's cheeks flushed a delicate pink; fortunately, the others missed the subtle implication, and the moment passed. Qiulian begged someone to eat the bowl of rice on her behalf, pulled off the ridiculous beard, and pointed directly at Huazhu, declaring, "Now I will watch you become the joke!" The dice fell to Yazhu, representing Lin Xiangru. Ronghua announced, "Lian Po has failed us; now it is time for Xiangru." Qixiang read Lin Xiangru's entry: "If defeated, all three penalty cups must be drunk by the King of Zhao in his stead." Ronghua laughed nervously, "Miss Yazhu, please be careful! Do not disgrace the army and force me to drink." Yazhu reassured her, "Rest easy, Madam. I will win." However, the chosen game was guess-the-fingers: Yazhu hid three melon seeds in her hand, and three times in a row, Huazhu guessed perfectly. Yazhu was mortified and offered, "I will just drink the wine myself." Qixiang interjected, "That is not allowed. If you show favoritism, the penalty is thirty cups." Ronghua laughed in defeat, "I will drink! I will drink!" and she downed the three cups in a single breath.

Next up was Hongwen, playing the role of Lord Pingyuan. The rule book dictated: "Lord Pingyuan uses a silk thread, engaging in a game of cat's-cradle. If Pingyuan loses, his hands are slapped, and he must drink ten large cups of wine, echoing the line, 'Only wine can soak Zhao's earth'. He must drink until he vomits before stopping." Hongwen had the weakest capacity for wine and a naturally timid disposition; upon hearing this severe rule, she immediately quailed. She stretched the thread taut between her hands, but her ten slender fingers trembled ceaselessly. The sight made the beauties laugh, and she herself began to laugh. The harder she laughed, the worse her hands shook. Qixiang observed, "This 'claw-wind' affliction will simply not do. Rongmei, perhaps you should just kneel with your troops and offer the three cups of surrender." Ronghua laughed stubbornly, "We can still fight with our backs to the wall!" They crossed the string for a round, and miraculously, Hongwen managed to win one turn. She endured only two light hand-slaps and drank two small cups, while others graciously drank the rest of the harsh penalty on her behalf.

Huazhu then hid a lotus seed in her hand for Hongwen to guess. Yazhu saw it and subtly kicked Hongwen's foot to give her a hint. Puzzled, Hongwen looked at Yazhu, who then pointed discreetly at a dish of lotus seeds resting on the table. However, Hongwen's gaze drifted sideways, and thinking of duck feet, she confidently guessed, "Duck webs!" Yazhu burst out laughing. Hongwen flushed with embarrassment and scolded, "You are teasing me on purpose!" Huazhu replied, "I am not teasing you; you are scolding me!" She then opened her hand and recited, "Cold dew descends, the lotus pod sheds its pink." Hongwen turned to Yazhu and complained, "If it was a lotus seed, why did you kick my foot? How was I supposed to guess from that?" Yazhu shot back, "Is what you hide under your skirt not a golden lotus? Must it automatically be duck feet?" All the ladies dissolved into laughter.

Qixiang smiled at Ronghua and said, "Your three generals have taken the field and lost eight rounds. While it is not quite a total rout, you are certainly on your last gasp. Look at the rule book: 'If, out of nine rounds, you win only one, you escape the humiliation of a kneeling surrender, but you must still lead your line forward to offer submission.'" Ronghua laughed, "That is not difficult." She poured a cup of wine, walked over to Qixiang, and gracefully curtsied. Qixiang returned the bow and accepted the cup with a warm smile. Yazhu, Qiulian, and Hongwen were then forced to curtsey to Huazhu, who had emerged victorious as Lord Shang. Huazhu laughed, "I have my armor on; I cannot return the courtesy." Yazhu retorted, "Do not spend all your swagger so quickly. You may well be kowtowing in the next round." They beat the drum once more, and this time the flower stopped with Ziyan. Shixiang, Hongwei, and Zengzhu stepped forward. Zengzhu cast the dice, and it pointed to Hongwei, who was playing the Gaotang Goddess. Everyone laughed. Ziyan smiled, "Look at our Miss Hong. With her high chignon and wide, flowing sleeves, she truly resembles a goddess." Hongwei blushed deeply. On Qin's side, Aizhu, Hongyu, Hongxiang, Hongyan, and Hongxue stepped forward. The dice landed on Aizhu, representing the fearsome general Bai Qi. Qixiang noted, "This match is 'the heartless meets the divine.'" The rule book read: "If the delicate goddess meets the brutal Bai Qi, how can she possibly fight? She must mobilize the entire nation's host to aid her in battle. If civil ministers are involved, they must draw lots by hiding objects in their hands. The ruler will name an opera and assign roles: Bai Qi will act as the painted-face general, and the goddess as the female lead (*dan*). The helpers will take on colors matching their respective figures." Furthermore, it specified: "Minister Ling Yin Zilan will play the clown; Song Yu will play the male lead (*sheng*)." Qixiang had the four of them each hide a hazelnut in their hand and declared, "You may either hold the nut or leave your hand empty. Hold out one hand. I will call out roles based on the play I name. If a character should be present but is absent, they drink. If a character should be absent but is present, they also drink."

Qixiang named *Liu Tang*, a solo performance for a painted-face character. Every outstretched hand held a nut. She laughed, "The *sheng* and *dan* should not appear in this scene. Each of you must drink one cup." She then named *Naozhuang*, another painted-face piece where the *sheng* and *dan* were not required. Yet Hongwei's hand held a nut again, earning her another penalty cup. Hongwei protested, "Our king should be the one to pick the play!" Ziyan agreed, "You are right. We fell right into their trap." Ziyan then quickly named a *sheng-dan* piece in order to fine Aizhu, but Aizhu's hand was perfectly empty, meaning Shixiang was forced to drink the penalty instead.

The drum beat and the flower was passed again, stopping this time with Huanxiang. The dice sent Baozhu forth. Huanxiang smiled, "Here is our lucky general, the true champion among the Four Lords. Let us see who dares to meet him." On Qin's side, the lot fell to Hongxue, representing Li Si. Qixiang said, "A worthy foe indeed." The rule book stated: "Lord Xinling utilizes the 'plan and deploy' rule." They brought out a tube filled with poetic wine lines. Baozhu drew a slip: "Candlelight half-holds kingfisher gold." The annotation read: "Whoever wears gold armlets or jade bracelets must drink one cup." Qixiang gasped, "That line might make everyone in the room drink!" All seven ladies drank, with the sole exception of Huanlan. Qixiang asked why, and Huanlan replied, "This cup is not mine." Qixiang pulled her hand open to reveal bracelets made of tourmaline. The others grumbled, "She got off cheap." However, of the twenty-four maids present, all wore either gold or jade, meaning the entire hall was forced to drink. Peiqiu remarked, "Fifth sister's lucky star has made the whole room drink." Hongxue then drew a slip: "Jade hairpin sways; phoenix pair in flight." The annotation read: "Whoever wears gold-thread soft phoenix hairpins must drink." Hongxue looked carefully around the room; many easily qualified. She spotted Aizhu and Hongwen playing cat's-cradle. Aizhu missed a tricky cross, and Hongwen slapped her hand. Aizhu giggled so hard that her golden twin-phoenix pins shook violently. Hongxue poured a cup, stepped right up to her, and said, "Here." Aizhu complained, "Why chase your forfeit abroad?" Hongxue replied, "Look at your head; that line was written specifically for your twin phoenix pins." Aizhu touched her pin, glanced around the room, and scoffed, "Pooh, who isn't wearing them? Why pick on me?" before laughing again. Huanxiang smiled gently, "Aizhu, just drink. You cannot dodge this." Aizhu looked at her mistress and took a small sip. Hongxue pressed her to finish it. In response, Aizhu shoved Hongxue, causing half the wine to spill. Qixiang laughed, "This darling truly deserves her 'beloved' name." Baozhu drew another slip: "Softly she knits kingfisher brows, shows bright teeth." She looked about the room and announced, "Found her. I toast our Shixiang. With those pinched brows and teeth half shown, is she not the very image of Xishi clutching her heart?" Shixiang flatly refused the drink. Baozhu playfully pinched her nose and poured the wine in; Shixiang laughed and accidentally sprayed the wine all over Baozhu's dress, sending the beauties into roars of laughter.

Qixiang praised her, "Miss Bao is formidable indeed, a true champion." Hongxue then drew a slip: "In the dark, only the scent of sleeping shoes." She frowned, "This line is too difficult." Qixiang instructed, "Sniff each one in turn. Whoever's scent matches must drink." Hongxue laughed mischievously, "If we are truly to sniff, then..." she trailed off, smiling slyly. "I know exactly what to do. I shall toast someone," she declared.

She poured a cup and purposefully approached Hongwei. Hongwei recoiled and said, "Have you truly sniffed my feet? How absolutely absurd, to pester me for nothing!" Hongxue replied, "I have never sniffed your feet, but I often see you powdering your foot-binding bands with pine powder. Are they not fragrant?" Hongwei flushed a deep crimson and quickly drank the cup. Baozhu drew another slip: "Ten fingers slender, like red jade shoots." Looking around, she noticed that only Xiao Cui's fingernails were currently painted red, so she made her drink. Hongxue then drew: "Heaven gifts a smear of rouge upon the cheeks." Hongwen had already consumed two cups, and her cheeks were burning bright red. Consequently, she was forced to drink yet another.

They cast the dice once more, and Mingzhu was called up to represent Hou Sheng, competing in a "linked-tail hemp" verse chain. The rules stated that if Li Si lost, the penalty was wine; if Hou Sheng lost, the penalty was soy sauce. Mingzhu groaned, "Soy sauce is incredibly hard to swallow." Huazhu whispered a helpful hint, "Try vinegar instead; it is far better than soy." The beauties stifled their laughter. Mingzhu ignored her and boldly began the verse: "'In the tenth month,'"

Hongxue said, "Yellow birds cry *jiao-jiao*." Mingzhu answered, "Birds sing *ying-ying*." Hongxue countered, "*Ying*—they cry." Mingzhu was stumped, "How do I link to *yi*? Aren't you making it impossible?" Hongxue was fined a cup, drank it, and said, "I'll switch to *yi*, meaning 'already': 'Already it is ended.'" Mingzhu said, "Another fine." Hongxue challenged her, "You've only memorized the *Book of Songs*, not the other classics. So you say nothing begins with *zai*." Mingzhu fiercely disputed this. Hongxue teased, "Drink a cup of soy sauce and I will tell you." Mingzhu refused to yield. Hongxue said, "If you can't continue, why not just drink the soy?" Everyone laughed. Mingzhu proposed, "If you invent a line that the ladies cannot verify, I'll drink. But if you trick me into drinking soy and cannot prove it, you must eat my spit." Hongxue agreed, "Done. Drink." Mingzhu swallowed the soy sauce in a huff. Hongxue smiled victoriously, "From the *Book of Documents*: 'In the third month, on the day *zai sheng po*... *zai sheng ming*'. *Zai* can certainly open a sentence. How can you say no line begins with it?" The beauties agreed, "That holds true." Qixiang laughed, "The spit-wine penalty is remitted." They exchanged a few more linked lines, and Hongxue ultimately lost one cup.

Next was Zhangzhu, drawing the wine-woman lot. She had to cast dice; if she lost, she must kneel and ask both her own king and the enemy king to issue a command. Zhangzhu threw a one-two-three; Hongxue threw a four-five-six. Defeated, Zhangzhu knelt before Huanxiang, begging for a command, and Madam Hua could not stop laughing. "What command?" she asked, turning to Qixiang. "I have a rhyme made of lyric titles: two three-character lines and one seven-character line, tightly fitted." She recited, "*Feast in Clear Capital*, *Clear Peace Joy*, *Eight-Tone Ganzhou*, *Golden-Thread Song*. Sister, see if you can match it." Qixiang sighed, "That is hard. I'm not fluent in lyric titles like you, who write them constantly. You have stumped me. I will drink."

Huanxiang urged, "Sister, do not be modest. Go on." Qixiang thought for a moment and recited, "*High Sunny Terrace*, *Yuchi Cup*, *Sable Coat Exchanged for Wine*, *Drunk in Penglai*." Huanxiang was impressed, "I bow to you! I bow! You fitted it so neatly and still claim unfamiliarity!" The other five raised their glasses, "Both are splendid. We drink together to our two 'league leaders.' Give us more to hear." Huanxiang mused, "Seven-character titles are the hardest; there may not be many." She recited again, "*Long Regret*, *Twelve Hours*, *Candle-shadow sways red—jade clepsydra drags slow*." Qixiang nodded, "Even better," and answered, "*Besotted Maiden*, *Sash at Waist*, *On Phoenix Terrace Remembering the Flute*." The beauties cried out in delight, "Wonderful! The seven-character line only appears once, and Qixiang found it." Huanxiang conceded, "Truly a brocade heart and an embroidered mouth. I kneel." She then added, "*Young Wanderer*, *Pass Qin Tower*, *West-River Moon*, *Bright Moon*, *Lone Boat on the Stream*." The last line was eight characters. Qixiang thought carefully and replied, "*Red Maiden*, *Brocade Tent Spring*, *Like a Dream*, *Wu Mountain*, *One Stretch of Cloud*." Praise rang through the room, and the entire hall drank another round in celebration.

Then the drum beat and the flower passed again, coming round to Peiqiu's kingdom of Yan. The dice count sent Jinfeng forth as Jing Ke, while on the other side the count fell to Hongyu, as Lü Buwei. Jing Ke's appointed game was pitch-pot. Huanlan said, "This game, at least, should yield no jokes." Jinfeng threw a shaft in the 'Su Qin Shoulders His Sword' style, and Hongyu one in the 'Lord Jiang Angles for Fish' style; neither shaft found the pot, and each drank two cups. Then it passed to Honglian's Tian Guang, who came out for the mute game: each player puts forth one finger, the thumb standing for metal, the forefinger for wood, the middle finger for earth, the ring finger for water, and the little finger for fire. If one throws the thumb and the other the forefinger, that is metal overcoming wood: the thumb wins and the forefinger loses. If one throws the thumb and the other the little finger, that is fire overcoming metal: the little finger wins and the thumb loses. The three maids threw out their fingers with great speed, winning some and losing some.

Next Hongjuan's fine steed came forward; the rulebook showed a chart for madiao. The thumb counted as a 'reward,' the middle finger as a 'shoulder,' the little finger as an 'extreme,' and the forefinger as a 'hundred sons'; the ring finger was not used. Both hands might be thrown at once: if this side put out two fingers and the other side one, and together they completed a scoring pattern, the pattern belonged to the side that threw two, while the side that threw one toasted wine according to the schedule of congratulations. If the other side put out three fingers on two hands and this side two fingers on one hand, completing a pattern, it belonged to the two-handed side—the smaller number always completing the pattern for the larger, and so on throughout. When the toasts to be drunk grew numerous, the whole company divided them. Ronghua said, "This wine is no trifle. If we drink by the congratulation schedule—say a pattern worth a hundred toasts—surely we are not to drink a hundred cups?" Qixiang said, "A hundred cups is not so many. There are more than thirty of us here now; each would get no more than three cups apiece. What is there to fear?"

Hongjuan said, "I cannot keep these madiao patterns straight—my lady must remember them for me." Huanxiang agreed. Hongjuan put out a forefinger and a little finger, and Hongyu, as it happened, put out a little finger: together they made exactly 'a hundred with two extremes'—the Double-Tailed Scorpion. Huanxiang said, "That is sixty toasts." Qixiang said, "Excellent—let every attendant in the hall drink a cup as well." Hongyu threw with both hands—one forefinger and two little fingers—and Hongjuan put out a little finger: 'a hundred with three extremes,' completing the Jade Carp's Back, worth another hundred toasts. Peiqiu said, "This wine truly mounts up; whatever the count, let the whole company drink one cup and be done." So another general toast was drunk. Hongjuan put out two thumbs and a forefinger, and Hongyu put out one thumb, making 'three rewards and a hundred'—the Flowered Stomacher, worth twelve toasts. Qixiang and the others each drank a cup, Hongyu drank two, and Hongjuan three. All told, that round they drank one hundred and seventy-two cups of wine.

Then the flower passed on to Huanlan, whose commission sent forth Hezhu as Lord Mengchang, while the other side appointed Hongyan as Zhao Gao. Huanxiang laughed, "How is Zhao Gao any match for Lord Mengchang? Let us see the rules." Lord Mengchang kept three thousand table-guests, so the two were to cast six dice against each other: whenever reds or aces turned up, coins were thrown into the basin—six reds meant six coins, two reds and two aces four coins—while a throw with no reds and no aces won the whole pot. If Lord Mengchang won, he asked the other: 'What do you have? What do you lack? What do you want? What do you not want?' To each question the other must answer with a line of Tang poetry: a good line excused the drink, while a poor line, or none at all, was fined in wine. If Lord Mengchang lost, he was questioned in the same fashion.

Hongyan and Hezhu cast for a while, and Hongyan lost. Hezhu asked, "What do you have?" Hongyan said, "What I have is: 'An embroidered sandalwood pillow, fretted and carved like jade.'" Hezhu asked again, "What do you lack?" Hongyan said, "What I lack is: 'A precious mat spread fresh in a kingfisher-jade tower.'" Hezhu asked, "What do you want?" Hongyan said, "What I want is: 'Within the red-pearl canopy, the cherries ripening.'" Hezhu said, "And what do you not want?" Hongyan said, "What I do not want is: 'Spring entering the brow with two points of sorrow.'" The beauties all cried, "Well said!" Huanxiang said to Qixiang, "Sister, it is plain that under a strong general there are no weak soldiers. That your very maids should have such embroidered mouths and brocade hearts—it makes one envious beyond measure." Qixiang said, "They may remember a few lines of verse, but how could they compare with your maids, accomplished in every art?" Hezhu, hearing her own mistress praise Hongyan, felt a prick of rivalry and said, "Those four lines were well spoken indeed—but you forget you are Zhao Gao, an old eunuch. What business have you with such things?"

Then she said, laughing, "What you have is: 'Fine grasses, spring fragrance, a small secluded grotto.' What you lack is: 'A winsome, tender mood that cannot master its shyness.' What you want is: 'Beneath the mandarin-duck curtains the fragrance still warm.' What you do not want is: 'To marry a young Xiao who loves to roam afar.'" At this Huanxiang laughingly scolded Hezhu: "He'er, how can you be so saucy?" Qixiang was still laughing and had not yet spoken when Hongyan, in a fury, made to strike Hezhu; Hezhu apologized over and over, the other maids pleaded for her, and only then did Hongyan let her off. The beauties laughed, "Miss Hezhu's lines were far too cutting. Lucky for her there were so many present, or a beating from Miss Hongyan was a certainty." Then came Xiaocui's cock-crow, and Xiaocui stepped forward already rather timid. The rulebook showed a tile-joining game.

The two were to join dominoes end to end—an ace head against an ace, a deuce head against a deuce—and whoever was blocked drank a forfeit. Xiaocui secretly rejoiced. The two began joining tiles there on the floor, and Xiaocui was blocked three times running. She began to fret, not knowing what would be done with her. Qixiang said, "Now comes a fine rule," and turned up the book: for the cock-crow to pass the barrier—three cups; the player must imitate a rooster's cry and crawl under the bench, through and back, three times. The beauties hid their laughter behind their hands. But this, as it happened, suited Xiaocui exactly; without the least difficulty she set up a shrill imitation of a rooster's crowing, and after a few cries crawled under the bench three times, setting everyone laughing. Huanlan said, "How kind of you, sister—you appointed her on purpose to make a joke of her." Qixiang laughed, "She drew it herself. And you had better not laugh at her: had it been anyone else, they could never have wriggled through so nimbly." When Xiaocui had finished her crawling, her little side-chignon knocked askew, she grinned at Huanlan. Huanlan rolled her eyes at her: "Aren't you pleased with yourself." The wine was drunk on her behalf by Sanjie.

When Sanjie came forward, Hongyan set up a dog-calling noise with her mouth. Sanjie said, "You are in luck—don't you beat me. If you beat me, I will truly give you a bite." The book, turned up, showed the Five Venoms game: the thumb was the toad, the forefinger the snake, the middle finger the centipede, the ring finger the gecko, and the little finger the spider. Victory ran thus: the spider devours the gecko, the gecko devours the centipede, the centipede devours the snake, the snake devours the toad, and the toad devours the spider. The two began to guess. Sanjie thought, "If she reckons I will throw the spider, she will throw the toad; better I throw the snake." Who could have known Hongyan would throw the centipede? Sanjie lost, and said, "As it happens, I could do with a drink." Hongyan laughed, "Look at the book first, then drink."

Qixiang said to Huanlan, "Sister, your rooster and dog thieves have provided excellent entertainment. What next?" Huanlan replied bitterly, "Enough, enough! Today, elder sister's pride has had its day. I'll take my revenge slowly. Next time I draw Xiang Yu at Julu, I will breathe again." The beauties laughed, "Han still waits; strategy is not yet deployed. We only hope they win." Sanjie's rule dictated: "First cup—bark three times. Second and third cups—crawl on the floor for two steps and bark three times." Sanjie spat, "What kind of rule is this? I thought 'dog thief' meant something else. It turns out I must actually be a dog. I won't do it!" and tried to run. The beauties howled with laughter. Aizhu, Huazhu, Hongxiang, Hongyu, Hongxue, and Hongyan chased her down and surrounded her, "However fierce you are, where can you go in our garden today? Bark nicely and we will spare you; otherwise, we'll pin you down and skin you!" They pinched her sides, knowing Sanjie was incredibly ticklish. Caught, she shook like a crape-myrtle blossom, begging, "Stop, stop! I'll bark, I'll bark!" The six still doubted her; five held her securely while one brought the wine—only after a bark would she be allowed to drink. Outnumbered, Sanjie yapped three times, and the hall roared like a hundred birds. Her face burned. Hongyan made her guess again; she wanted revenge but lost yet again.

Sanjie pleaded, "Sisters, spare me this round." But the two Pearls and four Reds would not yield. Huanlan laughed at Qixiang, "Your lawless Qin—what more do you want? You've swallowed five whole states and still set wolves on people." Qixiang laughed so hard she bent over the table, entirely unable to answer. Sanjie, pinned mercilessly, hatched a desperate plan. "These minxes are too vile," she thought. "I'll risk a joke and give them some jokes in return."

She strategized, "The worst are Aizhu and Hongxue. I will pay them back." Smiling sweetly, she said, "Sisters, don't. Step aside and I will get on the floor." They still doubted her; Hongxue said, "We'll stand clear, but you cannot run." Sanjie slowly bent down. Spotting Aizhu and Hongxue's three-inch golden lotuses, their flower-edged trousers barely showing beneath their skirts, she let out a cry. Everyone laughed. Suddenly, she whirled, seized Aizhu's ankles, hiked up her trousers to bare a calf as white as frost, yapped once like a dog, bit down lightly, and scratched with both hands. Aizhu shrieked like a ghost, went limp, and collapsed. Five maids rushed to save Aizhu, while Sanjie bit Hongxue's leg and scratched her as well. Four others fled, bent double with laughter. Hongxue, too, collapsed, falling laughing onto Aizhu. Aizhu thought Sanjie was still atop her, ready to bite again, and shrieked with laughter until she was near tears. Sanjie covered her mouth and quietly slipped away. Mistresses and maids alike laughed until their powder streaked and their eyes watered; hairpins fell, and wine spilled. Aizhu and Hongxue sat on the floor for a long time before finally rising. Sanjie giggled from a safe distance; Aizhu pointed at her and cursed, "Short-lived thing! May a mad dog bite you yet, and may puppies grow in your belly!"

Hongxue added, "No need to wait for later. She'll be bitten the moment she leaves the gate." Sanjie laughed triumphantly, "Who told you to be so wicked? I was actually merciful. The other four ran fast, or else all six of you would be rolling in one heap." The six berated Sanjie roundly before the beauties finally stopped laughing—though Ziyan still had a few giggles left in her. Qixiang said to Huanlan, "Sister, your Sanjie is wonderful. I would trade you a maid for her." Huanlan replied, "What would you do with her? She only plays the dog." Ziyan laughed, "Sister, why recruit these desperadoes? Are you playing the First Emperor of Qin?" Laughter erupted once more.

Qionghua declared, "I will destroy Qin. They only have Han Feizi—a man of law and names, not of war." The dice fell to Ruizhu, playing Zhang Liang. Her challenge was the "Golden Gate examination": she must propose an idiom, cover three dice under a cup showing the phrase's meaning, and have the opponent match it with three dice of their own. If different, a judge would pick the better and worse, and the loser would drink. Ruizhu set three dice, covered them, and handed Hongxiang three dice, saying, "Match *Ninefold spring hues drunk on fairy peaches*." Hongxiang thought for a moment and set a three, a six, and a four. "Three and six make nine. Four stands for peach. Is that right?" Ruizhu lifted the cup—it was correct. She offered another: "*Thirteen zither pillars; wild geese slant*." Hongxiang set two fives and a three. Correct again. A third: "*Word-flood pours backward; Three Gorges' water*." Hongxiang hesitated, then set three threes. "Right?" Ruizhu smiled, "No," and lifted her cup to reveal three fours. Hongxiang clapped her hands, "Clever! That is backward flow; I missed it. I drink." Han Feizi's penalty was severe: "The lawmaker traps himself. Light penalty: tattooed face. Heavy penalty: feet cut off."

Ruizhu demanded, "Bring the inkstone. We are painting faces." Hongxiang pleaded, "Sister, spare me. Painting means washing, which is a bother. I will kneel and drink instead." Ruizhu was about to relent, but Zhenzhu refused, "I fight her next. Show one mercy, and everyone will want mercy." She took the brush and drew spectacles on Hongxiang's face, causing the hall to roar with laughter again. Hongxiang fumed, drank her penalty, and hurried off to wash.

Luckily, she wore no powder, or her makeup would have been ruined. Fuming, she pointed directly at Zhenzhu, "You're next! You're first! If you lose and beg, you are not human—you are a dog!" Zhenzhu laughed confidently, "Afraid of you? Does begging still make one a hero?" The old man at the bridge used a chess-manual game called "Eight Formations"; the old man played the red pieces.

Zhenzhu played chess quite well but knew the manual poorly; she unfortunately met Hongxiang, who absolutely loved memorizing traps.

Zhenzhu tried hard but missed the subtleties. After only a few moves, she was completely lost. Baozhu wanted to offer a hint, but Hongxiang warned, "Whoever teaches, loses. Same penalty." Qionghua burned with worry but dared not speak. Hongxiang hung a corner checkmate—dead. Hongxiang smiled sweetly, "Got you this time." The old man's book dictated: remove a shoe, fill it with wine, and toast the whole table. Zhenzhu cried out in horror, "Impossible! Filthy! What kind of game is this?" Hongxiang insisted, "It is perfectly fine," and reached for Zhenzhu's shoe. Zhenzhu tried to run, but Hongxue tripped her from the side. Zhenzhu fell, and Hongxiang pinned her down, forcefully pulling off one shoe. Zhenzhu's face flamed; she clutched at Hongxiang to get it back, but Hongxue took the shoe to fill it with wine. Huazhu swiftly snatched it away and threw it back to Zhenzhu. Laughter filled the air everywhere. Zhenzhu put the shoe back on, tied it, rose indignantly, and pinched Hongxiang twice. And with that, the round closed.

Only Qingqin remained, playing the Bolang hammer. The rule book stated: "The ring match has 'dull thunder' and 'split thunder'—you may strike anyone in Qin, even the king." Qionghua commanded, "This general must succeed." Ronghua cheered, "Qin'er, win honor for your mistress!" Qingqin smiled confidently, "My hammer strikes true with every swing." Huanlan added, "Beat them, and you avenge us all." Huanxiang urged, "Dull and split thunder may strike wildly. Do not spare them—not even their king." Peiqiu warned, "Do not be Qiulian's Lian Po." Ziyan chimed in, "Nor our Jing Ke's dagger." Banter flew across the room. Qixiang laughed heartily, "This lone army, deep in enemy land—can it possibly return alive?" She confidently ordered six of her maids forward at once against Qingqin.

Strangely, Qingqin's dull and split thunder-strikes left the two Pearls and four Reds trembling in defeat. Qionghua glowed, nodding and smiling, "One general's merit; ten thousand bones dry." The beauties cheered wildly. Qixiang laughed, "How dare you! The youngest little sister bullying the eldest sister—it is outrageous! Will you face me?" The five cried out, "Why not? If seventh sister flinches, we will all help!" Qionghua smiled gracefully, "I would willingly yield three camps, but if commanded, I will feed the horses, sharpen the spears, and spar with my sister." Qixiang laughed in surrender, "Many hearts make a city unbreakable. I yield." Qingqin's ring match had already won plenty of victories; Aizhu, Huazhu, Hongxiang, Hongyu, Hongxue, and Hongyan had all drunk deeply.

Huanxiang, seeing the approaching dusk, wished to return to the city. Huanlan urged her to stay, but she gently refused. Qixiang saw that the sun had set and could not insist. She saw Huanxiang off first, then said to the others, "The rest of you need not rush to the gate. Stay until the third watch if you like." The Ten Pearls tidied up; they all boated across the river, walked to the foot of the hill, boarded their sedans, and left the garden.

The remaining sisters took each other's hands and walked to the nearby Chunfeng Zuixuan—the "Spring Breeze Drunken Pavilion"—and sat down. The maids followed, brewing tea and talking until the lamps were finally lit. Ziyan wanted to play ma-diao and pulled Ronghua and Peiqiu into a game. Qionghua spotted a box of poetry cards and played a hand with Qixiang and Huanlan until past the second watch. Qionghua completed one beautifully regulated octet; Qixiang missed one rhyme and could not finish; Huanlan's draw was poor and lacked many essential characters. Qionghua proudly spread her cards on the table, and Ronghua's group came over to look. The poem read:

Parting spring—half a year now gone;
Little-spring sky most stirs pity.
Wine apportions thwart-and-ally stratagems;
People rival Peach Pool and fairy isles.
Though morning after morning men lean on jade trees,
Step after step must yield to golden lotus feet.
Bright clouds and bright moon, jealous perhaps,
Light towers and terraces doubly clear.

The five beauties said together, "This might have been composed—how could it possibly be 'drawn' from random cards? Every word sits firmly, and it perfectly fits today." Qixiang laughed, "I love most the couplet

'Though morning after morning men lean on jade trees,
Step after step must yield to golden lotus feet'

—it truly vents our women's breath. Otherwise, this garden might have become nothing but an opera yard for those famous actors." Ronghua asked, "Sister, have you met those actors? I hear second brother brings them here daily." Qixiang replied, "Those *dan* are handsome enough. I have seen five or six of them; at festivals, they come to pay their respects. Not to boast, but several among us today resemble them." Some could not guess whom she meant; others had heard rumors. Huanlan and Qionghua, fearing Qixiang would name names, quickly cut in with small talk. Near the third watch, they finally prepared to leave. Qixiang could not keep them any longer and said, "The waning moon still shines. Sisters, are you willing to walk to the garden gate?" All agreed. Lantern pairs lit the way; arm in arm, laughing, they passed through stone gates and bamboo paths. Truly:

Perfume on sleeves and gleam in hair left lingering charm;
They plucked kingfisher green while spring yet lingered.

At the gate they boarded their carriages, exchanged farewells, and rolled down the embroidered curtains as the wheels turned. The maids mounted carriages behind them, and Qixiang, accompanied by the twelve Reds, rode safely home. What followed—hear the next chapter.
