That day Shi had arranged for Deyue to come after breakfast; she waited long without him and feared Pan would intercept. Quietly she went to the study: door shut—more suspicion. Listening, she heard whispers she could not make out; she crept to the window. The blind was down; she licked a hole in the paper and saw Pan holding Deyue in his lap, cheek to cheek, moving—rage flared; she meant to curse, then thought: "Better wait at the door and beat the old rabbit when he comes out." She set a stool by the door and sat. She heard Deyue say: "Let me go—godmother will worry and scold me," and Pan sucking his mouth two or three times—Shi's fury peaked. The door opened; Deyue stepped out, saw Shi, face crimson, feet nailed.

Shi's face was wrath; she glared. Pan stepped out; Shi rose and seized his beard full—Pan's soul fled; he hung his head, motionless.

Shi cursed: "Shameless old turtle, old rabbit—your own backside wormed till you paid to have it dug clean—now you want more—why not bend your own rod into yourself? He's my godson—you dare touch him?" She slapped with all her might—Pan cried "Aiyo!"—blood streamed; he could not mind his beard and wrenched free—half the whiskers tore away. Shi still raged; she rapped Deyue's head, twisted his cheeks. Deyue trembled, knelt, begged; pitying him, she dragged him by the ear inside.

How could one slap from Shi make Pan bleed so? She wore two silver fingernail guards; one swipe caught Pan's nose—the guard was thin and sharp and snapped, slicing off the tip of his nose—hence the blood. Pan could not bear the pain, fled, and sent for Yang Shanxiu—the same who had repaired Xi Eleven's kidney—to fit a false nose. Pan could not heal at home and feared his wife's fists, dared not return, lodged at his boot shop in the city, and had Yang come daily for two months before he mended. Tales spread; people gave him a fine name: "Scratch Third Lord," or "Big-Eye Third." The days Xi lost his member were the days Pan lost his nose—so Pan could not see Xi off, and Xi did not blame him. Strangely, the two were always ill in sympathy: one rotted member, the other "hip wind"; one took a dog-kidney, the other had his gate dug; one lost a glans, one lost a nose—odd, and no logic to it. Only Deyue the little bald wretch profited—crippled two men and still gained a fine godmother. What followed—hear the next chapter.
