Chapter 59 — Vice-Minister Mei independently builds the Qu shrine; Young Master Qu returns to the capital.

Meanwhile, Qinxian had kept vigil for Qu Daosheng at the Huguo Temple in Nanjing for over a hundred days. Although the master and servant lived frugally, the daily offerings of rice and paper money, along with the room rent, still cost them ten taels of silver a month.

By the third month of their stay, they had pawned or sold all of Qinxian's remaining clothes. The early winter chill was setting in, yet Qinxian had no padded garments, let alone Liu Xi. One day, Liu Xi offered a gentle word of advice: "Young Master, you are still young, and you shouldn't be so rigid. I believe Lord Hou was sincere in his intentions. For him to personally invite you over and even send money and rice is quite rare. You shouldn't misjudge this gentleman; princes, nobles, and prime ministers all respect him. He has countless disciples—serving officials, jinshi, and juren beyond reckoning—and many ladies and young misses also bow to him as their teacher. I remember one year when he celebrated his birthday, the longevity screens and poetic scrolls filled every room in his garden, with some left over because there was no place to hang them. I reckon his social circle is even grander than Master Xu's at the Yiyuan. If you were to go, you could make some useful acquaintances, and surely some good would come of it. If we stay here, with no relatives in sight, how are we to survive? Putting everything else aside, the old master's coffin must eventually be laid to rest in the earth. The weather is turning cold, and you don't even have padded winter clothes. In this situation, we need to make a decision sooner rather than later. We can't go on like this." Qinxian replied firmly, "I would rather starve to death than go to Lord Hou's estate." Liu Xi pressed, "But why? I truly don't understand." Qinxian instructed him, "Keep an eye out outside for any travelers heading to the capital. I need to send letters and borrow some silver so we can properly bury the old master."

Liu Xi replied, "There are couriers leaving every day—imperial dispatches and relay reports—when is there a day without one? Just write the letters, and I will go and send them." With a sorrowful heart, Qinxian poured his grief into several letters addressed to Ziyu, Ziyun, Huifang, and the others, pleading with them to send someone specifically to take him back. In the letter to Ziyun, he also enclosed Qu Daosheng's dying testament. After a full day of writing, Liu Xi entrusted the letters to a courier. Later on, the temple began hosting Buddhist rituals, drawing large, mixed crowds of men and women that packed the grounds. People frequently peered into Qinxian's room, leaving him feeling deeply stifled. Seeing their livelihood dwindling, Liu Xi reasoned that even if someone were sent from the capital to fetch them, the journey would take at least two months, dragging them into the end of the year. So he devised a plan. He sold two pieces of clothing and used the money to set up a small stall right at the temple gates, selling fruits, dried nuts, and the like. Earning a hundred or so cash a day, they managed to scrape by. Qinxian remained quietly in their quarters, content with his simple gruel, spending his idle time writing calligraphy and painting. His only hardship was the thinness of his clothes, with no means to afford anything warmer.

One day, Hou Shi Weng returned from Suzhou and learned that Qinxian was still staying at the temple, now struggling with inadequate food and clothing. His thoughts immediately turned back to the boy. Although he still harbored some resentment from being rebuffed when he previously sent rice and charcoal, the root of his affection had not been severed. Steeling his old face, he took twenty taels of silver, had his servant boy carry them, and rode his sedan chair to the temple. Arriving at the gate, he saw Liu Xi managing a small stall stocked with nothing more than water caltrops, water chestnuts, melon seeds, and peanuts. He also noticed a few paintings hung on the wall—floral pieces done on raw paper, their colors bright and quite striking. He asked Liu Xi, "Who painted these?" Liu Xi answered, "The young master painted them. The paper costs twenty cash a sheet, and it seemed a shame to throw them away, so I brought them here to hang. Yesterday, someone actually praised them and bought two; a peony sold for two hundred cash, and a plum blossom for a hundred and fifty. There's even someone who wants to commission a set of eight folding screens. If he provides the paper, he's willing to pay two thousand cash! If this painting business takes off, it'll be much better than running this stall." Shi Weng offered a faint smile and walked inside, where he found Qinxian mixing rouge and powders. Squinting his aged eyes, Shi Weng felt that the boy looked even more captivating than before. In the past, he had resembled a delicate, ailing sunflower; now, he still held the fragrant grace of a plum blossom, exuding an ethereal beauty like cool jade sleeves and snow-white silk.

Qinxian's heart gave a violent lurch at the sight of Shi Weng, but he forced himself to step forward and offer a polite greeting. Shi Weng, choosing to forget their previous unpleasantness, grasped the boy's hand once more, exchanged a few pleasantries, and sat down. Qinxian reluctantly kept him company, his expression remaining cold and detached.

Shi Weng began by offering lavish praise for Qinxian's paintings, secretly formulating a plan to win him over. He then said, "My dear young friend, there is no need for you to worry so much. You should be receiving replies from various places in the next day or two. When I was in Suzhou, I spoke of your late father's situation to others, and everyone expressed a willingness to help. However, staying in this temple is ultimately quite inconvenient for you. If you were to move into my home, my close friends—who were also close friends of your late father—would surely come to visit. Meeting face to face fosters a natural sense of camaraderie, which makes everything much easier to discuss. If you remain here, it's so far away that people simply won't want to make the trip. Moreover, how can you possibly withstand the cold in your current state? While the plum blossom may endure ice and snow, your delicate jade-like frame will surely suffer in the harsh winds. Besides, this temple is a public place, teeming with all sorts of idle people. Seeing that you are a stranger from another land with no one to rely on, someone is bound to take advantage of you. I don't mean to pry, but with you hanging a few paintings at the temple gate for sale, what's to stop those local ruffians and scoundrels from using 'viewing the art' as an excuse to barge right in? Here in Nanjing, you'll find people from all eighteen provinces, including a certain type who make their living through deception—they are known as kidnappers. When they spot someone young and beautiful, they flick a drugged pellet onto them, which clouds their mind and makes them follow the kidnapper willingly. Once lured away, these captors will dress the person as a woman to deceive others and exploit them for pleasure, subjecting them to every conceivable indignity. And that is the better outcome. Some will even maim the person, twisting their body into grotesque shapes, and force them to beat a gong at busy crossroads to beg for money. Such things happen all the time, which is why I worry about you constantly and keep you in my thoughts. A clever person like you must surely be able to foresee good fortune and disaster, right? You should show a little appreciation for the affection I hold for you. Who knows, perhaps we share some destined bond; otherwise, why would I hold you so dearly in my heart? I do not mean to boast, but beneath my roof, there is no shortage of elegant and beautiful companions; my household is quite lively. Yet, seeing that you possess the beauty of He Yan and the bearing of Pan An, clearly blessed by the heavens, I feel you should be cherished all the more. The firefly cannot illuminate its own path; it must borrow the light of the candle-dragon. A fly cannot travel far on its own; it must attach itself to the tail of a noble steed. Being the scion of a talented scholar, you are already poised to leap the Dragon Gate; residing in the Hou family gardens would be better than dwelling in the Moon Palace itself. It would be a beautiful tale for a lifetime, a romance lasting a thousand years. A fair youth strolling alongside an old man like myself—who could possibly look upon that and deem it unseemly? When the crabapple and the pear blossom are planted side by side, they can face each other without a hint of suspicion. Furthermore, there is no lack of cherry-lipped singing boys or peony-like little maids; you would find no shortage of amusement here. In all matters, one should think thrice before acting, and avoid clinging stubbornly to a single path."

Hearing these words, Qinxian's face burned crimson with outrage. Observing Shi Weng's demeanor, he noticed a purplish sheen radiating from the old man's face, exuding an undeniable aura of wickedness. Qinxian desperately wanted to unleash a torrent of curses to vent his anger, but mindful of the man's seniority, he forced himself to hold back and ignored him. Seeing Qinxian's flushed face, Shi Weng mistook it for bashfulness, assuming the boy was simply too shy to agree outright, and thus believed there was a genuine change of heart. He ordered his servant boy to bring forth the silver, personally handing it to Qinxian with the words, "Here are a few taels of silver. First, buy back a few pieces of clothing to wear, and tomorrow I will send a sedan chair to fetch you." Qinxian offered a curt word of thanks and replied, "I dared not accept the gifts you bestowed upon me previously, and I certainly dare not accept this reward now. As for freezing and starving, those are matters dictated by fate. If, for instance, my late father were still alive, I would not have to endure such insults, let alone the threat of freezing and starving. Even if I were reduced to begging from door to door, the great heroes of antiquity suffered similarly, so what right have I to consider it a disgrace? Even if I were to freeze to death or starve to death, I would die with a clear conscience. I will absolutely not make a mockery of myself by doing things that betray a cowardly clinging to life and a complete loss of honor and shame." With that, he turned and walked away without looking back. Shi Weng, still clutching the bundle of silver, felt those words slice through his thick, old hide like a steel blade. He trembled with such rage that his beard and eyebrows bristled; truly, he had never experienced such an indignity in his entire life. His shame quickly morphed into a furious wrath, and he was on the verge of an explosive outburst. However, realizing Qinxian had disappeared to who-knows-where, and seeing Liu Xi occupied with his stall and unable to enter, Shi Weng had no choice but to pocket the silver and storm out in bitter resentment. Before leaving, he unleashed a vicious tirade at Liu Xi, scolding Qinxian for being ungrateful and refusing to be uplifted. He declared that he would no longer concern himself with their future affairs, then boarded his sedan chair and departed, leaving Liu Xi completely bewildered.

When the stall closed for the day and Liu Xi came in to cook rice, he found Qinxian still weeping in his room. Liu Xi offered more clumsy words of comfort. Unable to speak of Shi Weng's wicked intentions, Qinxian could only bottle up his sorrow, quietly choking on his sobs. We shall leave their plight there for now.

Meanwhile, Mei Shixie, during his tenure as the Education Commissioner of Jiangxi, had proven exceptionally skilled at selecting scholars, greatly revitalizing the literary culture of the region. Furthermore, he praised virtue and honored martyrs, bringing hidden merits to light, and submitted memorials on over a dozen crucial matters. This deeply pleased the Emperor. Consequently, within a single month, Mei Shixie was promoted three times: first to Chief Supervisor of Instruction at the Supervisorate of Imperial Instruction, then to Left Vice Censor-in-Chief of the Censorate, and finally to Left Vice Minister of the Ministry of Personnel, with orders to return to the capital for duty. Lu Zongyuan was appointed to replace him as the Education Commissioner of Jiangxi. Recently, Vice Minister Mei had also received letters from home, learning that Ziyu had successfully passed the Erudite Scholastic examination, had been appointed as a Compiler, and had even taken a wife. Overjoyed by this news, he set out for the capital that very day. The endless official farewells require no detailed recounting; naturally, throngs of colleagues crowded the roads to see him off. Vice Minister Mei departed on the first day of the eleventh month, his journey smooth, like a fair wind sweeping past the Pavilion of Prince Teng. After ten days of travel, he arrived in Nanjing. He planned to stay there for a few days to sweep the ancestral tombs, inspect his fields and estates, and offer assistance to his relatives. Two days into his stay, on the third day, he paid a visit to the Governor-General, and they spoke for quite some time.

During their conversation, the Governor-General asked, "There was an Assistant Prefect in Jiangxi named Qu Benli; were you acquainted with him?" Vice Minister Mei replied that they had been close friends. The Governor-General then explained that Qu Benli had passed away in Nanjing, and that his luggage and travel funds had been stolen by three of his senior servants, who had subsequently fled. Hou Shi Weng had reported the incident on behalf of Qu's adopted son and had dispatched an official dispatch to Jiangxi. Just yesterday, a reply had arrived from the Governor of Jiangxi, stating:

"The constables of Ji'an Prefecture have apprehended the thieves, Zhang Gui and Qian De. A search yielded one fragment of a credential belonging to the Assistant Prefect of Nanchang Prefecture, two leather trunks containing three hundred and seventeen taels of silver, one gold bracelet, and several garments. These have all been forwarded under escort. However, these clothes and belongings must be returned to his adopted son for claiming. The two culprits are currently imprisoned in Jiangning County; an accomplice, Wang Sheng, has already died. But as the whereabouts of his adopted son are unknown, we must ask Shi Weng to ascertain this."
Upon hearing this, Vice Minister Mei felt a profound sense of sorrow. He thought to himself, "Daosheng had no heir; this adopted son must be a recent development." He took his leave of the Governor-General and headed to Phoenix Mountain to visit Shi Weng. Shi Weng hurriedly welcomed him in, first offering his congratulations and reminiscing about their long separation, before inquiring about the financial success of Mei's tenure. Shixie laughed and replied, "Relying on the blessings of my ancestors, I have a few mou of modest land—enough to keep me fed—so I have no need to amass a separate fortune. As for Jiangxi, while it may not be a land of extraordinary wealth, it is certainly fertile enough. If one were to ignore justice and act unscrupulously, this journey could easily have lined my pockets with a hundred thousand taels, allowing me to strut about with pride. I won't hide it from you, senior: I actually eliminated two-thirds of the customary illicit fees in each examination hall. Yet, in truth, I still fared several times better than I would have in a capital post." Shi Weng remarked, "Your integrity and rectitude are known to all. With this journey, you have already secured a ministerial appointment, and soon enough, you will hold a provincial governorship. Moreover, your son is young and brilliantly talented, admired by all the empire's scholars. This truly is a double celebration of virtue in your family." Shixie offered a few modest demurrers, then brought up the Governor-General's question regarding the whereabouts of Daosheng's son. Hearing Qinxian's name, Shi Weng's initial impulse was to speak ill of him and advise Shixie to pay him no mind. Yet, a sudden flicker of conscience stayed his tongue, and he blurted out, "The boy is quite exceptional. He is currently at the Huguo Temple inside the Hanxi Gate, not far from here." Shixie chatted about a few other trivial matters before taking his leave to return home.

The next day, Vice Minister Mei first sent someone to the Huguo Temple to announce that he would be coming in person. He then dispatched a servant with a monetary condolence gift in Daosheng's name and prepared a sacrificial table before making his way to the temple. Flustered, Liu Xi paid a young monk to watch the stall and hurried inside to serve. Qinxian, dressed in mourning white, knelt behind the curtain. Knowing this was Ziyu's father, his heart swelled with joy, yet a nagging apprehension lingered, fearful that the man might have caught wind of past rumors and would question his origins. With great trepidation, he watched as Vice Minister Mei entered, offered incense, poured the libation wine, performed the customary bows, and then requested Qinxian's presence. Qinxian stepped forward and kowtowed in gratitude. Mei helped him up, looked him over carefully, nodded, and sighed, saying, "Daosheng can truly be said to have had a son." He then asked, "How old are you, my young friend?" Qinxian replied, "Seventeen." Vice Minister Mei then inquired about the circumstances of Daosheng's passing and Qinxian's current situation, which Qinxian detailed thoroughly. Mei sighed heavily. "When your father was alive, his name was renowned throughout the empire, and everywhere he went, he was met with a warm welcome. Even in this very place, he had many close acquaintances. How is it that after his passing, not a single person came to look after you? The fickleness of human nature is truly detestable. But there is good news: some of your stolen belongings have been recovered. They are currently at the Governor-General's office. Because they didn't know your whereabouts, they asked me to look into it. Tomorrow, you can go and claim them." He continued, "Leave all matters concerning your father's funeral to me. When I return, I will send men to find a suitable plot of land. We will ensure he is properly laid to rest first, and discuss everything else later." Qinxian thought to himself, "Rather than burying him elsewhere, it would be better to bury him beside Fairy Du's tomb by Mochou Lake; after all, they were father and daughter." Fearing Vice Minister Mei might not believe him, he meticulously recounted the entire intricate story.

Half-believing, Vice Minister Mei replied, "I will go and take a look tomorrow. I'll inquire about the location, and if it can be purchased, that specific plot will do." As they spoke, Qinxian quietly studied Vice Minister Mei's features, noting that he bore not the slightest resemblance to Ziyu. Mei was tall and lean, his face weathered like autumn frost, projecting an awe-inspiring, formidable presence, appearing to be nearing fifty years of age.

At that moment, Qinxian was addressing Shixie as 'Your Excellency' and referring to himself as 'your humble junior.' Vice Minister Mei corrected him, saying, "Your father and I were close friends for twenty years, and our ancestors also shared long-standing ties. You should call me 'Uncle' and refer to yourself as 'Nephew.' The way you addressed me just now feels far too distant." After a bit more conversation, Mei departed. Qinxian felt a deep sense of relief and immense gratitude, hoping to implore the Vice Minister to take him along to the capital. Knowing Mei would be delayed for a few days, he decided to broach the subject gradually. The next day, he took Liu Xi with him to formally offer his thanks. Vice Minister Mei ordered a servant to write out a receipt on Qinxian's behalf, retrieved the lost items, and returned them to the boy. From then on, Qinxian finally had a means to survive. Seeing that the two trunks contained all his clothing, and that there were still three hundred and seventeen taels of silver, along with a gold bracelet and a few miscellaneous trinkets, he felt a newfound sense of security. He told Liu Xi that the funds for the funeral were now secured, which brought Liu Xi great joy. Since his silk and fine fur garments were inappropriate for mourning, Qinxian took out several dozen taels of silver and personally went with Liu Xi to a tailor shop to purchase two well-fitting sets of plain lambskin clothing for himself, as well as a set for Liu Xi.

Over those two days, Vice Minister Mei had entrusted someone with finding and purchasing a burial plot, but he had yet to receive a reply. That night, as he slept, he dreamed of Qu Daosheng, dressed in a black gauze cap and red robes, approaching him with a joyful expression. Shixie was greatly surprised and asked why he was dressed in such a manner. Daosheng didn't explain, but grasped Shixie's hand and said, "Your Honor has not forgotten your old friend, and you have righteously taken pity on this orphan. Though I am in the netherworld, I could never repay such kindness even if I were to offer a jade ring. My daughter currently resides by Mochou Lake; I beg you to entrust my bones to her side, and I shall remain eternally grateful even in death. My son is wandering destitute with no place to rely on; I hope that within your vast estates of ten thousand mansions, you might spare a single branch for him to rest upon. I entreat you to show him mercy." With that, he bowed deeply, prompting a flustered Shixie to return the bow. Daosheng rose and took his leave, but suddenly reappeared holding a single lotus blossom. Presenting it to Shixie, he said, "This flower emerges from the mud yet blossoms over clear water; how could it consider the mud a disgrace? Let the past remain in the past. I pray Your Honor will not disdain the origins from which this flower bloomed."

With those words, clouds and mist swelled beneath his feet, and he slowly drifted upward into the sky. When Shixie awoke, he carefully pondered the words spoken in the dream, gradually coming to an understanding. The phrases "emerges from the mud yet blossoms over clear water" and "let the past remain in the past" suggested that this adopted son must have originated as a young opera singer. He decided it wasn't worth dwelling on; as long as the boy possessed good character, that was all that mattered. He also thought, "It seems Daosheng has become a deity. Could it be that his daughter's grave truly is located by Mochou Lake? If so, Qinxian's account of the planchette session and his encounter with Fairy Du must actually be true." He lay awake for the rest of the night. At the crack of dawn, he rose and sent someone to invite Master Qu over, indicating he had matters to discuss.

Shortly thereafter, Qinxian arrived and shared breakfast with him. Vice Minister Mei did not mention the dream, but instead invited Qinxian to accompany him on an excursion to Mochou Lake, which Qinxian happily accepted. Vice Minister Mei and Qinxian each took a sedan chair, while the servants rode on horseback. They exited the city and followed the city wall; after about two li, they arrived at their destination. It was the dead of winter, and several heavy snowfalls had already blanketed the area. Fearing the biting cold of the open wilderness, Vice Minister Mei wore a black fox-fur cloak inside his sedan. However, as they entered the mottled bamboo grove, they were surprisingly greeted by a warm, gentle breeze, reminiscent of the second month of spring, without a hint of chill in the air. The bamboo, trees, flowers, and grasses remained vibrantly green and lush, exuding their familiar, fragrant scents. Most astonishing was the sprawling trumpet creeper, which had burst forth with hundreds of blossoms. On the ground, orchids and sweet basil bloomed in unison, and the horse-chestnut tree—which typically flowered in high summer—was also budding and blossoming, displaying a profusion of flowers all at once. This miraculous sight left Vice Minister Mei profoundly shaken, filling him with an overwhelming sense of awe and reverence. Qinxian noticed four small trees standing by the tomb entrance, already three or four feet tall. Looking closely, he recognized them as the apple, pear, peach, and plum trees that Fairy Du had planted. Each tree bore a single, incomparably vibrant blossom, leaving him utterly astonished. "How can they have blossomed already?" he wondered. Vice Minister Mei, witnessing this, repeatedly expressed his amazement, praising it as a truly blessed realm of immortals. He turned to his servants and asked, "This area is likely government land; it has no private owner, correct?" The servants replied, "Everything along the city wall is government property." Vice Minister Mei finally made his decision. As he paced the area, a flock of kingfishers suddenly flew out from the clusters of trumpet creeper, chirping and trilling melodiously. They flew back and forth, circling Vice Minister Mei and Qinxian countless times before finally darting off toward the lake. Vice Minister Mei offered continuous words of praise, turning to Qinxian to say, "This place is truly a realm of immortals. I have never believed in tales of the divine, but seeing this with my own eyes, I have no choice but to believe. I intend to build a shrine here for your late father, and also enshrine the tablet of this fairy maiden. What do you say?" Hearing this, tears streamed down Qinxian's face, and he knelt to kowtow his deepest gratitude. Moved by a surge of emotion, Vice Minister Mei quickly helped him up, saying, "I will delay my journey by a few days for this matter. Even though I won't be able to wait for the construction to be completed, I must have everything planned and arranged before I can set off." He then sent Qinxian back to the temple while he proceeded to the Jiangning County yamen to discuss the construction of the shrine with the county magistrate. The magistrate readily agreed, immediately dispatching men from the Works Office to measure the land, summoning foremen, gathering laborers and materials, erecting a work shed on the site, and commencing construction. Shixie selected the twenty-fourth day of the month for the burial. He composed an epitaph for Daosheng and had it hastily carved, and also wrote a spirit-path inscription, which was engraved onto a stone stele.

On the twenty-fourth, having heard of Shixie's righteous undertaking, hundreds of Jiangning's gentry came to attend the funeral. Some composed poems, some wrote songs and ballads, others drafted essays, and some offered couplets and plaques for the shrine. Shixie reviewed each one carefully, planning to have them all carved within the shrine once it was completed. By that day, the pillars and beams of the shrine had already been erected, outlining the structure: a front gate, a second gate, a main hall spanning three bays, three-bay rear buildings for the side wings, and six additional single-story rooms. With the general layout established, Shixie, unable to wait any longer, provided two thousand taels of silver to his seasoned chief steward, Mei Cheng, to oversee the remaining construction. He also sketched a portrait of Fairy Du, ordering a painted clay statue to be modeled after it. Once every task was meticulously assigned, he invited Qinxian over that very day to discuss taking him along to the capital. Overjoyed, Qinxian offered his profound thanks once more. He immediately settled the rent for his room and moved directly onto Vice Minister Mei's boat. He then offered the silver he had recovered to Vice Minister Mei, but Mei insisted he keep it. This time, Qinxian's gratitude was truly boundless. Influenced by Daosheng's words in the dream, Vice Minister Mei firmly refrained from inquiring into Qinxian's background. Noting that Liu Xi addressed him as "Young Master," Mei ordered his household servants to respectfully refer to him as "Master Qu." Although Vice Minister Mei wished for them to adopt the familiar address of uncle and nephew, Qinxian didn't dare, continuing to address Mei as "Your Excellency" and referring to himself by his given name; Vice Minister Mei had no choice but to let it be.

On the day of the funeral, Hou Shi Weng had been dragged along by the gentry and made a brief appearance. Seeing that Qinxian was still holding a grudge and refused to speak a single word to him, Shi Weng felt deeply displeased. Harboring hatred for Qinxian and jealousy toward Shixie, he left almost immediately without even offering a single bow of respect. Later, several enthusiastic members of the gentry stepped forward to spearhead a fundraising effort; one donated ten taels, another twenty, and bit by bit, they amassed several thousand more taels. With these funds, they expanded the Qu Shrine, constructing numerous pavilions, terraces, and towers. Within Mochou Lake itself, they built a mid-lake pavilion and a winding red bridge with nine turns, and even commissioned a few boats for the enjoyment of spring and summer excursions. In front of the tombs of Master Qu and Fairy Du, they erected stone memorial archways, ornamental pillars, and stone guardian statues. Furthermore, they enclosed the area to create a garden, planting various flowers and trees, and piling up artificial rockeries, transforming the site into a renowned scenic landmark. The Qu Shrine ultimately achieved immortal fame, standing on par with the Sun Chu Pavilion and the Jiang Ling Residence.

Vice Minister Mei set sail on the twenty-eighth. Feeling lonely on the boat, he treated Qinxian just as he would Ziyu, keeping him by his side from morning till night. Observing Qinxian's steady demeanor and sharp intellect, Mei grew exceedingly fond of him. Testing the boy's knowledge, he found that although Qinxian hadn't devoted much effort to formal classical studies, he displayed remarkable cleverness in poetry, lyrics, and various other arts. Mei resolved that once they reached the capital, he would slowly teach him the classics and instruct him in the art of prose. However, Qinxian absolutely dared not mention his acquaintance with Ziyu, terrified that it would lead to questions about his background. If asked, he was prepared to spin a few lies and vaguely cover it up, completely unaware that his late father had already imparted the truth to Mei in a dream. When the boat reached Wangjiaying, they continued their journey by land; it was already the eighth day of the twelfth lunar month. Calculating the days, they wouldn't reach the capital until the twenty-sixth. With the days growing shorter and the nights longer, they had no choice but to press forward day and night. But we shall leave them on their journey for now.

Meanwhile, Ziyu was overjoyed to learn of his father's exceptional promotion to Vice Minister. He had already received letters sent from Jiangxi, and by his calculations, his father should have reached the capital long ago. Why was he still not here? Lady Yan's anxious anticipation goes without saying, and Wang Wenhui also came by frequently to ask for news. That day was already the fifteenth of the twelfth month. In the morning, a letter was delivered, and Ziyu saw the inscription on the envelope: "For the personal attention of Young Master Mei Yuxiang at the Mei Residence, Jiangxi Education Commissioner. Sent by Qu Qinxian." His heart leapt with joy, realizing Qinxian had reached the post in Jiangxi. He hurriedly tore it open, noticing there were also letters addressed to Ziyun, Huifang, Sulan, and Qiguan, which he temporarily set aside. Opening his own letter, he saw a plain sheet of paper bearing the words, "A Notice of Bereavement." He was deeply shocked, thinking to himself, "Could something have happened to Daosheng?" He read on carefully, and before he knew it, teardrops began to fall one by one.

When he read further, learning that all of Qinxian's clothes and belongings had been stolen by runaway servants, that he was keeping vigil over the coffin in a desolate temple entirely bereft of food and clothing, and that he had been repeatedly harassed and humiliated by Hou Shi Weng, Ziyu's heart shattered. The letter stated that Qinxian had intended to end his own life, but because the traveling coffin remained unburied, he was temporarily clinging to his fleeting breath, desperately praying that they would find a way to send someone to his aid. Ziyu's tears suddenly gushed forth like a spring; it felt as though ten thousand arrows were piercing his heart. Utterly bereft of a plan and unable to bear reading any further, he immediately ordered a carriage to take him to the Yiyuan to find Ziyun. To his dismay, neither Ci Xian, Ziyun, Nan Xiang, nor Gao Pin were at the garden. Ziyu grew even more frantic. The footmen, unaware of what had happened, didn't dare to ask Ziyu and drove him on to the Nine-Fragrance Tower. Going inside, he found that all the famous dans were gathered in the garden, and Nan Xiang, Gao Pin, and Jin Su were there as well. Bypassing any pleasantries, his face a mask of sorrow, Ziyu showed them the letters Qinxian had sent. Every single one of them shed tears. It was truly unimaginable that Qinxian would suffer such a massive calamity the moment he left the capital. Huifang said, "There is nothing else to be done right now; we must find Duxiang quickly to discuss this." They immediately sent people out to search for Ziyun. When they found Ziyun and he arrived at the Nine-Fragrance Garden, he saw Ziyu's state, hurriedly tore open the letter, and felt his own tears begin to stream down.

When they tore open and read Daosheng's dying testament, their tears fell even more heavily, like rain. After Ziyu and the others finished reading, every one of them wept bitterly, their cries making the base of the Nine-Fragrance Tower ring with grief. Once their weeping subsided, Ziyun spoke up: "Leave this matter to me. Tomorrow, I will dispatch men to Jiangnan to bring Yunong back and to handle Daosheng's funeral arrangements. However, they won't be able to reach him before the year's end." Ziyun returned immediately to inform Ci Xian and discuss the matter with him. With no heart to remain at the Nine-Fragrance Tower, Ziyu also went straight home. Gao Pin, Shi Nanxiang, Jin Su, and the famous dans were left feeling melancholic and devoid of any joy. When Ziyun returned to the garden and recounted the events to Ci Xian, Ci Xian was even more profoundly grief-stricken; over the course of a single night, he traced and painted a spirit portrait of Daosheng. The next day, he invited the various renowned scholars to the Nine-Fragrance Tower, where they established a spirit tablet and wept before it, holding a memorial ceremony that lasted for three days. Upon hearing the news, Young Master Hua also came to offer his condolences and weep. The Nine-Fragrance Garden had essentially transformed into Qu Daosheng's mourning hall, lacking only people wearing mourning white.

Ziyun issued a thousand taels of silver and dispatched his servants to travel to Jiangnan by the light of the stars. Ziyu was steeped in endless sorrow day after day. During the daytime, he didn't dare enter the inner quarters; firstly, he feared Lady Yan would question him, and secondly, he was afraid Miss Qionghua would see through him—his suffering was, after all, several times more profound than anyone else's. However, harboring such heavy matters in his heart, how could he possibly maintain a composed facade? Lady Yan merely suspected that he was afraid of seeing his father, assuming he was anxious because his father would soon return. Only Miss Qionghua, possessing an exceptionally perceptive mind, deduced that he had something else weighing on his conscience. She questioned him repeatedly until Ziyu had no choice but to tell her the truth. Miss Qionghua could only offer a few words of comfort; seeing his current state, she couldn't bear to tease him any further.

A few days later, a letter arrived from Vice Minister Mei. The advance party had returned, reporting that he would arrive on the twenty-third, which made Ziyu even more frantic. If his father returned and kept him under strict confinement, he would suffocate to death. It was truly a case of sorrow reaching its limit and turning into joy; on the twenty-second, Ziyu and Zhongqing rode out thirty li from the city to welcome him, staying overnight at an inn. They waited until the first watch of the night before the advance party finally arrived. However, these were newly employed servants whom Ziyu didn't recognize. After the innkeeper explained the situation to them, they came in to kowtow and pay their respects, stating that the master's sedan chair would arrive shortly, as they had pushed through an extra travel stage that day. Ziyu waited until past the second watch. Hearing the clamor of voices and the rumble of carriages outside, he knew they had arrived and went out with Zhongqing to receive them. Shixie stepped out of his sedan chair; Zhongqing and Ziyu stepped forward and kowtowed. Shixie offered a few words of comfort, inquired after Zhongqing's well-being, and then they all proceeded to the main room. Shixie had risen at midnight the previous night and was utterly exhausted. He quickly sat down, leaned against a pillow, and asked Ziyu about matters at home. He then asked Zhongqing if his wife and children were well, and inquired about Wenhui's recent situation. After the three men had chatted for a while, Shixie remembered Qinxian and asked his servants, "Why hasn't Master Qu's carriage arrived yet?" A servant replied, "It should be here any moment." Shortly after, the rumble of carriage wheels echoed outside the door. Zhongqing and Ziyu wondered to themselves, "Which Master Qu could this be? It must be someone he brought back from his post." They saw a servant illuminating the way with a lantern, and a beautiful young man walked in. Zhongqing and Ziyu were greatly astonished; under the dim lamplight, his features weren't entirely distinct, but they could sense that this youth possessed an extraordinary bearing. Qinxian, however, had already seen them with perfect clarity. A wave of sorrow washed over him, but he forced himself to hold it back and stepped forward to offer his greetings.

Shixie said, "This is my youngest son, and that is my nephew by marriage, Yan Jiantan." He then turned to Ziyu and Zhongqing and said, "This is the son of the scholar Qu Daosheng, who traveled with me to the capital. There are reasons behind this that I don't have time to detail right now. You should greet him; you will be spending a lot of time together in the future." Ziyu was initially terrified, but this quickly gave way to immense joy, so much so that he nearly burst out laughing. Seeing Ziyu, Qinxian's face lit up with a smile, and he too felt a surge of happiness. He stepped forward and formally greeted the two of them. They stared at one another, their hearts completely clear on the situation, yet none of them could utter a single word. Believing they were naturally stiff because this was supposedly their first meeting, and completely unaware of the underlying truth, Shixie said, "I am utterly exhausted today and need to lie down. You all go over to the other room." Overjoyed, Ziyu pulled Qinxian into the adjacent room.

The three of them stood there in a daze, looking at one another; one didn't dare to ask, and the other didn't dare to speak. Zhongqing didn't know if his uncle was aware of Qinxian's detailed background, and he felt it inappropriate to ask, so he could only silently ponder the situation in his heart. It was as if three mutes had gathered in one room. Ziyu and Qinxian could only communicate with their eyes. One moment, they recalled the bitter pain of the past, bowing their heads, knitting their brows, their eyes welling with tears; the next moment, realizing that this reunion, which they hadn't even dared to dream of, had actually come to pass, they would beam with smiles. It was like the weather during the yellow plum season—shifting unpredictably between overcast and bright. Shortly after, food was brought in, and Qinxian ate.

Meanwhile, Shixie had already gone to rest. Qinxian was exhausted beyond measure and couldn't hold on any longer, so he lay down on the kang, while Ziyu and Zhongqing sat beside him. The servants left the room; fortunately, Yun'er had not come along today, and Zhongqing was accompanied by newly hired men who didn't recognize Qinxian, so the night passed quietly without incident. Eventually, all three grew tired and lay down. Once the night was entirely quiet, they began to speak in hushed tones. At that moment, Ziyu and Qinxian were lying side by side on the same pillow, fully clothed, having forgotten all need for caution. In a soft murmur, Qinxian recounted how deeply he had missed him upon leaving the capital, how they had toured Nanjing, how he had personally visited his former life's grave at Mochou Lake, the miraculous manifestations of Fairy Du, the heartbreaking sorrow of Daosheng's final moments, the suffering he endured after the senior servants stole their belongings and fled, how Liu Xi had served him with utmost devotion, the lecherous harassment of Hou Shi Weng, and finally, Vice Minister Mei's visit and how he had righteously arranged the burial and built the shrine. He recounted it all in meticulous detail, leaving Ziyu alternating between grief and joy.

Watching them lie head to head, whispering intimately, Zhongqing felt a deep sense of happiness for them, thinking to himself, "These two have suffered countless heartbreaks and shed countless tears over the past two years, all leading up to this single night's conversation. It has truly turned into a tale of sorrow and joy, parting and reunion; it's utterly extraordinary." Later, Qinxian spoke of dreaming that the divine maiden had bestowed brushes upon them, how Daosheng had become a deity, how their boats had drawn alongside one another and they had reflected each other's fronts and backs in mirrors, and how the pearl had turned into a dragon and been snatched away. Ziyu and Zhongqing marveled repeatedly at these tales. Ziyu then shared how he had fallen ill after their parting, his devastating grief upon receiving the letters, how everyone had offered sacrifices to Daosheng, and how Duxiang had sent men down to Jiangnan to fetch him and bury Daosheng, right up to the shock of seeing him arrive today when he had least expected it. Once again, the two were overcome with a mixture of sorrow and joy. Qinxian felt a renewed wave of gratitude towards Ziyun and the others, repeatedly kowtowing on the pillow to Ziyu and Zhongqing. Zhongqing asked, "Along the way, did my uncle know about your past?" Qinxian replied, "Probably not. His Excellency never once asked about my background, and I was constantly on guard, terrified he would ask, wondering how I should answer if he did." Ziyu pondered this but couldn't think of a solution. "If he does ask about it in the future, how will you reply?"

Zhongqing said, "This matter can't be kept hidden anyway. Once we arrive home tomorrow, won't there be people in the household who recognize you? From my perspective, trying to hide it will only make things awkward. If outsiders end up telling my uncle, it will cause you even greater embarrassment. It would be better if tomorrow we ask my aunt to gently explain everything to my uncle. Since my uncle values him highly today, she will just have to explain his past clearly so that both sides can be at peace. Besides, my aunt has spoken very highly of him. Now that he has survived this tribulation and turned a new leaf, no one will ever bring up the past again." Ziyu agreed, "That is a very good plan, but it's not convenient for me to say it. You should be the one to go and speak with them." Zhongqing agreed, and after that, everyone finally drifted off to sleep. When dawn broke, Zhongqing woke first and saw Qinxian resting his head on Ziyu's arm, still sound asleep and breathing softly, while Ziyu remained asleep as well. Zhongqing smiled to himself and woke them up. Realizing he had been sharing a pillow with Ziyu and resting on his arm, and that Zhongqing had seen them, Qinxian felt profoundly ashamed. Ziyu's arm had become incredibly sore from being used as a pillow, though he hadn't noticed while asleep; when he tried to lift it, he found he couldn't move it at all, prompting a burst of laughter from everyone as they got up to wash and rinse their mouths.

Shixie rose and urgently called for the carriages to head into the city. The thirty-li journey went very quickly, and they arrived in a little over two hours. Vice Minister Mei didn't go straight home; instead, he lodged at a temple first, preparing to attend court and report back to the Emperor at the fifth watch the next morning. Ziyu first settled Qinxian in the study. As soon as Mei Jin and Yun'er saw Qinxian, they were utterly astonished, guessing first that it was him, and then that it wasn't. If it was him, why did the master treat him as an equal? And why was he wearing plain mourning clothes, like someone bereaved? If it wasn't him, how could there possibly be two faces that looked so exactly alike? Everyone was filled with doubts and suspicions, unable to guess the truth. But when they heard him being addressed as 'Master Qu,' they concluded it couldn't be him. Taking advantage of this free moment, Ziyu asked Zhongqing to explain the situation clearly to Lady Yan. Qionghua listened from the side, smiling at Ziyu as she watched him flush with embarrassment and shame, squirming uneasily. Lady Yan found the whole thing quite strange as well, remarking, "This child was always inherently good. Now that he has become a son of the Qu family, there's no need to ever bring up his past background again. We can just consider it as him having passed through a tribulation and moved on to a new life."

Zhongqing said, "It's best if you and Uncle explain this clearly to everyone, Aunt; otherwise, when outsiders see him, they are bound to talk, which will only cause Qinxian embarrassment." Lady Yan agreed, saying, "Your uncle highly values old friendships, and seeing that the boy is of good character, he certainly won't look down on him." Seeing that Lady Yan had agreed, Zhongqing took his leave.

Miss Qionghua entered the room, and Ziyu followed her in. Qionghua said, "Well, things are fine now. You won't have to keep dreaming and calling out for him every single day." Ziyu laughed, "I'm going to bring him in to meet my mother. Why don't you step outside and see if he looks the part?" Qionghua scoffed, but then added, "Bring him in; I really do want to take a look at him." Ziyu indeed led Qinxian inside to pay his respects to Lady Yan in the inner hall. Seeing him, the lady felt a deep sense of pity and called out, "Master Qu, you have suffered!" Qinxian had felt uneasy upon entering, but seeing Lady Yan treat him with such courtesy and address him as Master Qu, his heart settled. Miss Qionghua peeked from the doorway, confirming that it was indeed him; she marveled inwardly, watched for a moment longer, and then slipped away. Lady Yan asked about Qinxian's recent situation, and after briefly summarizing his experiences, Qinxian excused himself.

The next day, Shixie had his audience with the Emperor and returned home, where the entire family gathered to welcome him. Qionghua paid her respects to her father-in-law, and Shixie was extremely pleased. Lady Yan chatted with him for a while, and then gently and tactfully recounted Qinxian's entire story, explaining that he had once sung in operas, and that Qu Daosheng, seeing his excellent character, had adopted him as his son. She deliberately hid the details of Ziyu's illness, making no mention of it. Shixie remarked, "I had already guessed as much." He then shared the words Qu Daosheng had spoken to him in his dream, adding, "There's no need to dwell on the past. This child is very good, without the slightest trace of the habits of an actor. If you hadn't revealed the truth, one honestly wouldn't be able to tell." Lady Yan replied, "Looking at this child, it's quite possible he'll achieve some great fortune in the future." Shixie nodded in agreement. He then simply called Mei Jin into the room and explained Qinxian's situation to him clearly: "Everyone is to address him as Master Qu, and no one is permitted to show him the slightest disrespect. If anyone slights him, I will absolutely not forgive them." With Shixie's strict orders, the servants below dared not disobey. From then on, all the servants treated Qinxian with strict propriety, not daring to overstep their bounds in the slightest, which allowed Qinxian to live in peace. Shixie immediately ordered a bed prepared for Qinxian, instructed him to study alongside Ziyu in the library during the day, and told Ziyu to teach him with all his heart and not to look down on him. Vice Minister Mei really didn't need to add that last sentence; how could he possibly know what was in Ziyu's heart? Lady Yan let slip a faint smile, while Ziyu felt immensely proud; the situation was utterly perfect, bringing him a joy several times greater than when he passed the Erudite Scholastic examination. The next day, people lined up to host welcome banquets for Shixie, creating quite a bustling scene.

At first, Qinxian was reluctant to go out. One day, however, Ziyu took him to visit the various renowned scholars. Everyone met with him and offered their congratulations to Ziyu in unison. They then went to the Nine-Fragrance Tower and met with the nine famous dans, each encounter filled with a mix of sorrow and joy. Qinxian was also delighted that all of them had managed to escape the sea of sin and preserve their purity. They all poured out the details of their separation, holding onto each other's clothes and hands, talking continuously for an entire day. It was truly a case of:

The golden crow and the jade rabbit fly swiftly by;
As the twelfth month ends and spring returns, another year begins.
There is no need to detail the various New Year preparations of every household. To know what good tidings the New Year will bring, you must listen to the next chapter's unraveling.
